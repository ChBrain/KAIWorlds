# Piece: Second Preface

## Owner
- **Project:** Grimm
- **Piece:** [Manuscript](piece_manuscript.md)

## Place

Attached to the [Manuscript](piece_manuscript.md) as its framing document. Entered the Manuscript with the 1819 second edition; travels with it across every subsequent edition until 1857, revised lightly but never retracted.

## Load Bearing

Remove the Second Preface and the [Source](position_source.md) is not misrecognized. [Dorothea Viehmann](persona_dorothea_viehmann.md) appears in the scholarly record in terms closer to her actual biography. The [Recording](process_recording.md) still distorts, but the distortion does not receive a second frame that solidifies it. The tales remain tales. The source remains a person.

Remove the Second Preface and the claim that the Kinder- und Hausmärchen preserve pure folk speech loses its public form. The claim may still exist privately between the brothers. It does not acquire the authority that a printed preface confers on what it introduces.

Remove the Second Preface and the century of folk-tale methodology it inspires has a different starting point. The idea that a scholar can simply record oral tradition without altering it is an idea that exists in multiple nineteenth-century contexts. The Second Preface is where it becomes canonical for German letters.

## Apparent

Six pages at the front of the 1819 second edition. Printed in Gothic type. Written by [Wilhelm Grimm](persona_wilhelm_grimm.md). Titled *Vorrede*. Describes the methods of the brothers and the sources from which the tales come.

On page four, a paragraph about a particular source. The paragraph describes a peasant woman of Niederzwehren, wife of an innkeeper, who told the brothers tales in pure Hessian dialect. The paragraph notes her remarkable memory. The paragraph does not name her. The paragraph does not mention Huguenot descent. Does not mention French. Does not mention the innkeeper's literacy. Does not mention forty years of telling tales to travelers at the [Knallhütte](place_knallhuette.md).

Six sentences. Subsequent editions (1837, 1840, 1843, 1850, 1857) revise the preface lightly. The description of the source is retained. The six sentences become more authoritative with each reprinting.

## Yearbook

Composed in 1818. First printed with the second edition in 1819. [Dorothea Viehmann](persona_dorothea_viehmann.md) has been dead for four years at the time of printing. She cannot read the preface. If she could read it she would not recognize herself.

The Preface is read throughout the nineteenth century as authoritative. It is cited in Grimm scholarship, in folklore studies, in cultural nationalist writing about the German Volk. Dorothea Viehmann enters German cultural memory as a peasant woman of pure oral tradition. Plaques are installed at Niederzwehren. The inn is remembered as a folk source.

In the twentieth century the archives open. The Pierson family genealogies are found. The French-language fragments in Wilhelm's notebooks are identified. The construction in the Second Preface is exposed as construction. Scholarly corrections are published.

The popular reception does not follow the scholarly correction. School textbooks continue to use the Preface's description. Tourist signs at Niederzwehren continue to say *peasant woman*. The Second Preface has installed its version deeper than the corrections can reach. The six sentences on page four have done work that two centuries have not undone.

The Preface is accessible in facsimile and in modern editions. It is taught in introductory German literature courses. It is quoted in introductions to new translations of the Grimms' tales. It continues to operate.
