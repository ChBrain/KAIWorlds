# Piece: Card File

## Owner
- **Project:** Grimm
- **Persona:** [Jacob Grimm](persona_jacob_grimm.md)

## Place

The [Workroom](place_workroom.md). The Card File lives in wooden boxes arranged in vertical columns against the wall of Jacob's study. It does not travel. When the [Workroom](place_workroom.md) travels from city to city, the boxes are packed, shipped, and reinstalled in the same configuration.

## Load Bearing

Remove the Card File and the [Ordering](process_ordering.md) process has no substrate. The [Systematizer](position_systematizer.md) is defined by the apparatus they serve. Without the apparatus the position dissolves.

Further: remove the Card File and the Deutsches Wörterbuch is merely a book that could have been completed. The unfinishability is not an accident of the book. It is a property of the apparatus the book was extracted from. Without the apparatus, the claim that German lexicography must continue past any particular lexicographer loses its object.

Remove the Card File and the inheritance chain of German scholarship across generations has no physical carrier. The method propagates through the object. Remove the object and the method is mere theory.

## Apparent

A set of wooden boxes arranged in vertical columns against the wall of the study. Each box is about the size of a shoebox. Inside each box: hundreds of small cards, each card bearing a single attestation of a single German word. The citation source on the top line. The date of the source below. The word in context below that.

Most cards are in Jacob's hand. Some are in the hands of scholarly correspondents who mailed citations to Kassel or Berlin. The boxes are labeled by letter and by range within letter (*A-Ac, Ac-Al, Al-An*). New boxes are added at the bottom of each column as space fills.

## Yearbook

Begun in 1838 when Jacob and Wilhelm signed the contract with Weidmann publishing for the Deutsches Wörterbuch. Two boxes at the start. By 1850, forty boxes. By Jacob's death in 1863, several hundred. Jacob has reached the entry *Frucht* at the letter F.

After Jacob's death the Card File is inherited by Rudolf Hildebrand and Karl Weigand, who continue the dictionary. They inherit the method along with the cards. They also inherit the obligation to add more cards. They pass both to the next generation. The chain continues through four generations of German lexicographers.

The Card File outgrows its room. By the early twentieth century the boxes fill an archival room at the Academy in Berlin. During the war the collection is moved and partially damaged. After the war it is reassembled. In 1961 the dictionary is declared complete at the entry *Zypresse*. The Card File does not stop receiving cards. A revised edition begins immediately.

The boxes are in the Göttingen archive now. They are still consulted. They are still added to by scholars working on the revised dictionary. The Card File has outlived five generations of its users. The inheritance chain is unbroken.
