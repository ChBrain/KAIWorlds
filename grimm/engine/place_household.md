# Place: The Household

## Owner
- **Project:** Grimm

## Shown

[Wilhelm Grimm](persona_wilhelm_grimm.md) and [Dorothea Wild-Grimm](persona_dorothea_wild.md)'s home, with [Jacob Grimm](persona_jacob_grimm.md)'s rooms attached under the same roof. From the outside it looks like one house. Inside it is organized as three zones.

Zone one: the common rooms. Entrance hall, dining room, parlor, kitchen. This is where the family eats and where guests are received. Dorothea's zone. The children's zone. The visible life of the house.

Zone two: Jacob's quarters. A bedroom, a small study of his own, and access to the shared [Workroom](place_workroom.md). His rooms face the north side of the house. They are furnished plainly. He asked for nothing when he moved in and he has not asked for anything since.

Zone three: Wilhelm and Dorothea's quarters. Their bedroom, Wilhelm's dressing room, and the children's rooms. These face the south and east. This is the zone of the marriage.

The [Workroom](place_workroom.md) sits between zone two and zone three on the ground floor. It has two doors. Jacob enters from the north. Wilhelm from the south.

## Holds

- [Manuscript](piece_manuscript.md): travels with Wilhelm throughout the house. On the desk in the Workroom by day, occasionally at his bedside when he is ill.
- [Empty Chair](piece_empty_chair.md): in the Workroom between zones two and three, after December 1859.
- The daily meals, produced in the kitchen by [Dorothea Wild-Grimm](persona_dorothea_wild.md): the unmarked repetition by which the [Enduring](process_enduring.md) process sustains itself.

## Offers

The house offers a solution to a problem that was never stated. The problem: how do two brothers continue a lifelong collaboration after one of them marries? The solution, invented without discussion: one house, three zones, one [Workroom](place_workroom.md). Wilhelm married a woman who agreed to this without it being explicitly proposed, because it was already the shape of the brothers' lives before she entered. The shape had been visible to her since childhood in Kassel. She did not ask for a different shape.

The house offers the condition under which the [Hearth](position_hearth.md) can operate without being renegotiated. The [Enduring](process_enduring.md) process runs on absence of naming. The house refuses to provide a name. The house therefore provides the silence inside which the process can continue.

The house offers the possibility of the work. The Kinder- und Hausmärchen seventh edition, the Deutsches Wörterbuch, most of the Deutsche Grammatik's later volumes, the Deutsche Mythologie: all produced in this house or its several incarnations. The house is the precondition for thirty years of published work by both brothers.

## Withheld

The house does not hold the name of its own arrangement. No word is used for it. It is not called *Wohngemeinschaft* or *Dreierbeziehung* or anything else. It is called *our house*. The absence of a name is the condition that allows the arrangement to continue without requiring anyone to explain it.

The house does not hold the ambivalence of the arrangement. There is no visible strain. Letters between the brothers never refer to it. Dorothea's letters to her sisters never refer to it. The children grow up in it without describing it in any of their own later memoirs beyond saying that Uncle Jacob lived with them. The withholding is total. The withholding is also the operating condition.

When Wilhelm dies in 1859, the house holds four people: Dorothea, Jacob, and two adult sons still in residence. Jacob continues to work in the Workroom. Dorothea continues to run the house. The arrangement loses a person but not a shape. The withholding continues until after Jacob's death in 1863, and arguably until Dorothea's in 1867.
