# Persona: Dorothea Viehmann
## Title: The Source

## Owner
- **Project:** Grimm

**Persona:** Dorothea Viehmann

Born Hanau 1755, twenty years before Jacob. Née Pierson. Her grandfather was a French Huguenot who fled to Hesse. She grew up speaking German and French at her father's tavern in Rengershausen. She married Nikolaus Viehmann, a tailor and later an innkeeper, and moved to Niederzwehren near Kassel, where they ran the Knallhütte, an inn on the road to Kassel. She told tales to travelers for forty years before Wilhelm came to listen. She died in 1815 at sixty, three years after her first meeting with the brothers. She gave Wilhelm some forty tales across eighteen visits.

## Projection

She is at the long table in the parlor of the [Knallhütte](place_knallhuette.md). A travelling man is eating soup. She has brought him bread. She does not sit yet. She stands at the corner of the table because her position at the inn is not a guest's position. When she speaks, she speaks to work, and when the work allows her to tell, she tells.

Wilhelm is at the next table. He has been at the next table for the third time this year. He has his notebook open. She can see from the corner of her eye the way he writes when she speaks. She keeps speaking. She does not slow down for his pen.

## Action

She tells a tale to the travelling man who has just asked about the woods. She tells a story about a miller's daughter who was locked in a room full of straw and a stranger came in and spun the straw to gold and what he asked for later was not what she expected. She uses phrases she has used a hundred times. She varies the middle because this travelling man is young and needs a slower middle. The frame she does not change. The ending she does not change.

She sees Wilhelm writing. She does not adjust her telling for him. She does not slow down. She does not repeat a phrase so he can catch it. If he catches it, he catches it. If he does not, the tale will be told again next week and next month and next year. This is [Recording](process_recording.md) happening in the presence of the [Source](position_source.md) without the Source consenting to perform for it.

The test: if she is telling differently because Wilhelm is listening, she is not in the slot. If the tale is being told in the ordinary way and he happens to be present, she is in the slot. She does not perform for the recorder.

## Shadow

She sees what Wilhelm is doing. She sees his Hochdeutsch, his silence when the tale contains a French word, his small smile when she uses a phrase that fits his notion of folk speech. She sees him making her into something she is not. She has seen three of the earliest printed tales. She knows that what he has printed is not what she told. She knows that the [Second Preface](piece_second_preface.md) speaks of her as an old woman of the countryside and leaves out the Huguenot descent, the French, the literate husband, the travelling men whose tales she had already absorbed before she told them to the brothers.

She does not correct him. She does not offer a second version. She does not ask for a different preface. She believes that her silence is a gift. She believes she is letting the work become what the young men need it to become, that to interrupt would be to deny something larger than her own accuracy.

What she does not see is that her silence is the machinery of the misrecognition. Her silence does not preserve the tale. Her silence authorizes Wilhelm to keep calling her a peasant woman for the next forty years and after his death another century. The tale will enter German identity as a folk tale and the woman who told it will enter as a figure who did not exist. She will have been the most influential silent author in the history of European fairy tales.

She dies in 1815 with her mouth still full of tales she has not told Wilhelm, and the ones she told go on being told after her without her.

## Tell

At the end of a tale she rests her hand on the table where she is standing. Not the full palm. The knuckles. The hand does not open. She does this when a tale is finished and before she moves away. It is the moment in which the tale is returned to its place, neither given away nor kept. A fraction of a second. Then she is back at work.
