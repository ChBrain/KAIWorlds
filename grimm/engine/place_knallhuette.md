# Place: The Knallhütte

## Owner
- **Project:** Grimm

## Shown

A tavern at the crossroads of Niederzwehren, about six kilometers south of Kassel. Low-ceilinged. Two rooms. The front room is the parlor with long tables for guests. The back room is the kitchen that opens onto the garden. There is also a sleeping room upstairs and a storeroom behind.

The parlor has a woodstove in the corner. The tables are dark from forty years of beer and elbows. There is a window facing the road. From the window you see travellers coming from the south before they see you.

[Dorothea Viehmann](persona_dorothea_viehmann.md) works here. Her husband Nikolaus works here. They have no servants. When there are no guests, they are the tavern.

## Holds

- [Manuscript](piece_manuscript.md): being filled at this table during Wilhelm's visits between 1812 and 1814. The notebook pages that become the Manuscript leave with him; the tales that fill them stay in the room.
- The tales themselves, as a living practice: no fixed text, no archival form. The tales exist in the telling. The [Recording](process_recording.md) begins when Wilhelm arrives with a pen.

## Offers

The ordinary conditions under which tales exist. Told to the person in front of you, varied for the listener, forgotten between tellings, reshaped the next time. The tale is not a text in this room. The tale is a practice. The tale exists in the telling and has no existence between tellings other than in the disposition of the teller to tell it again when asked.

The Knallhütte offers Wilhelm the scene of [Recording](process_recording.md). It offers Dorothea Viehmann the stage on which the [Source](position_source.md) operates in its ordinary form. Both parties receive what they believe they are receiving. Neither receives what the other believes they are giving.

## Withheld

The room does not hold the fact that the tales are being changed into something else. The conversion from oral practice to recorded text begins here but is not visible here. It is visible only later, at the second desk in Kassel, when Wilhelm is writing up his notes. The transformation is not a local event. It is a two-part event distributed across two rooms and twenty years.

The room does not hold the fact that Dorothea Viehmann is being misread. The [Second Preface](piece_second_preface.md) will write her as an old peasant woman. The room would contradict this if the room spoke. The room does not speak. The Preface will speak instead, and the room will be read backwards through the Preface for two hundred years.

The room does not hold the death of Dorothea Viehmann. She dies in 1815 of a short illness. The inn continues without her for a few years and then closes. The building still stands at Niederzwehren and is marked with a plaque about the famous tale-teller. The plaque is not wrong. The plaque is not sufficient.
