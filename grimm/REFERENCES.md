## Primary sources

**Kinder- und Hausmärchen**, gesammelt durch die Brüder Grimm. Berlin: Realschulbuchhandlung.
- First edition: Volume 1 (1812), Volume 2 (1815)
- Second edition: 1819 (contains the preface that rewrote Dorothea Viehmann)
- Third edition: 1837
- Fourth edition: 1840
- Fifth edition: 1843
- Sixth edition: 1850
- Seventh edition: 1857 (the edition that entered the canon)

**Deutsches Wörterbuch**, begun by Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm in 1838. First volume (A through Biermolke) appeared 1854. Jacob died in 1863 at the entry *Frucht*. The project was completed in 1961 by successor scholars across four generations.

**Deutsche Grammatik**, Jacob Grimm. Four volumes, 1819 to 1837.

**Deutsche Mythologie**, Jacob Grimm. Göttingen, 1835.

**Briefwechsel der Brüder Grimm**. Edited correspondence between the brothers and with contemporaries. Multiple volumes across the Grimm-Gesellschaft publishing history.

## Scholarly reconstructions

Heinz Rölleke's editorial work on the Kinder- und Hausmärchen, particularly his restoration of the *Ölenberg manuscript* (the earliest pre-publication manuscript version of the tales). Rölleke's *editions* of the 1812 first edition and of the *Urfassung* are the standard reference for anyone tracking what Wilhelm's smoothing changed.

Maria Tatar's work on the *Hard Facts of the Grimms' Fairy Tales* addresses the systematic softening across editions.

Ruth Bottigheimer on the gendered patterns of Wilhelm's revisions.

Jack Zipes on the ideological and editorial shaping of the tales across the nineteenth century.

## On Dorothea Viehmann

The reconstruction of Viehmann's actual biography is owed primarily to twentieth-century archival work. She was born Dorothea Pierson in 1755 to a Huguenot family in Hesse. She married Nikolaus Viehmann and ran the Knallhütte in Niederzwehren. She was literate. She spoke French as well as Hessian German. She died in 1815 shortly after her last meetings with the brothers.

Wilhelm's 1819 preface described her as an old peasant woman of pure oral tradition. This description was maintained in scholarly reception until approximately the mid-twentieth century, at which point archival evidence began to correct it. The correction did not reach popular reception until much later and is still incomplete.

## On the Göttingen expulsion

The Seven Professors (*Göttinger Sieben*) protest of November 1837: Wilhelm Eduard Albrecht (law), Friedrich Christoph Dahlmann (history), Georg Gottfried Gervinus (literary history), Jacob Grimm, Wilhelm Grimm, Wilhelm Eduard Weber (physics), Heinrich Ewald (oriental studies).

Ernst August, King of Hanover, annulled the 1833 constitution upon his accession. The seven professors signed a letter of protest on 18 November 1837. All seven were dismissed from their university positions. Three (Jacob Grimm, Dahlmann, Gervinus) were ordered to leave Hanover.

The brothers returned to Kassel. Bettina von Arnim intervened with the Prussian court. King Friedrich Wilhelm IV called them to Berlin in 1840, where they remained until death.

## On the brothers' cohabitation

Jacob Grimm did not marry. He lived in Wilhelm and Dorothea's household from their marriage in 1825 until his death in 1863. This arrangement is documented in household records, letters of family members, and the memoirs of the Grimm children. It was not discussed explicitly in print by the brothers themselves, and it was not described as unusual by visitors. The arrangement is factual. Its interpretation in this world as a configuration unnamed by design is the world's interpretation, not an established scholarly reading.

## On Goethe and the Grimms

Goethe met Jacob Grimm briefly and knew the brothers' work by reputation. He wrote approvingly and briefly about the Kinder- und Hausmärchen in correspondence in 1815. He did not become their patron. His relationship to their project is documented in his letters and in the letters of intermediaries. This world's reading of Goethe as the pressure under the world rather than a figure within it is structural rather than evidentiary: it follows the pattern of the Faust world in the KAI Worlds corpus, where Goethe plays the same structural role for his own work.

## Scope note

This world is built from the documented historical record supplemented by the interpretive choices of the KAI Worlds framework. It is not a biography. It is not a work of scholarship. It is a structure for engaging the patterns the brothers' lives and work made visible: the recording that composes, the smoothing that reveals, the ordering that invents, the enduring that sustains.

Any of the brothers' scholarly or literary productions may be consulted as primary material while inside this world. The world is built to receive that consultation and to convert it into scenes inside the workroom, at the Knallhütte, in the household, or in the lecture hall.
