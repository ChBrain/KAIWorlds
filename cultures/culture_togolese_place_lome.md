## Shown
- The Grand Marché. Lomé.
  The women who run it: *Nanas Benz*.
  The *Nana Benz* — the market women
  who built fortunes trading Dutch wax cloth
  and were wealthy enough to drive Mercedes.
  The first indigenous African businesswomen
  in West Africa to achieve
  this specific kind of economic independence.
  They funded their children's educations in France.
  They negotiated with governments.
  They are the city's most powerful informal institution.
  The dynasty has not displaced them.
  They predate the dynasty.
- The Monument de l'Indépendance.
  The memorial to Olympio and independence.
  In a country where Olympio was assassinated
  and his assassin's son now governs.
  The monument remains.
  The contradiction is present.
  Lomé contains both.
- The Ghana border.
  200 metres from the centre.
  The crossing that is not a crossing —
  for Ewe people on both sides,
  the border is an administrative line
  they cross for family, for market, for normal life.
  The state's border and the people's border
  do not coincide.
  Lomé understands this intimately.
- The beach.
  The Gulf of Guinea.
  The fishing pirogues at dawn.
  The hotel strip.
  The sand that is eroding —
  Lomé is losing its beach to sea level rise
  faster than most coastal cities in the region.
  The coast that defines the city
  is becoming a different coast.
  This is the most concrete long-term threat Lomé faces.
  The political dynasty is the most visible long-term problem.
  The climate is quieter and more permanent.
- The re-export warehouses.
  Lomé as a transit hub.
  Goods from Asia, from Europe,
  destined for Burkina Faso, Niger, Mali.
  The port that serves landlocked neighbours.
  The economy that runs on location
  regardless of who governs.
  The dynasty did not build the port's logic.
  The port's logic survived the dynasty.
