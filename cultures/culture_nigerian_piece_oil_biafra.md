# Piece: The Oil and the Wound
## The resource that was supposed to build Nigeria — and the war fought over who it belonged to.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Lagos / Abuja](culture_nigerian_place_abuja.md)

## Place
The Niger Delta.
The creeks. The flares burning at night
visible from the sea.
Gas flared continuously since 1958.
The mangroves black in places.
The fishing boats still going out
because there is nothing else.
Port Harcourt: the oil city.
Also the city where Biafra ended.
Both facts present simultaneously.

## Load Bearing
Remove the oil and Nigeria is a large,
complex, multi-ethnic West African federation
still negotiating what it is.
With it: Nigeria is the country
that went to war over the question of who the oil belonged to,
and has been answering that question
every day since the war ended in 1970.
The oil funded the federal victory.
The oil funded the military governments.
The oil funded the corruption that became
the permanent operating condition of the state.
The oil also built the infrastructure
of the Nigerian middle class —
the universities, the hospitals, the roads
that were built in the 1970s
and have been degrading since.
The position carries both:
the proof that Nigeria can be built,
and the evidence that it wasn't.
Remove the piece:
a federation managing ethnic pluralism without a single resource war.
With it: a federation whose central question —
who controls what flows from the ground —
has never been finally answered,
and whose answer when it comes
determines whether the south funds the north
or the east funds everyone
or the Delta burns again.
The oil is the wound and the prize simultaneously.
The position knows both.

## Apparent
The Oloibiri well. Niger Delta. 1956.
The first commercial oil discovery in Nigeria.
Shell-BP.
The flares began that year.
They have not stopped.
The Biafran War: 1967–1970.
The Eastern Region, oil-rich,
declares independence as Biafra.
The federal government blockades it.
One million dead — estimates range up to three million —
mostly from starvation.
Kwashiorkor. The distended bellies
on the front pages of European newspapers.
The war ends January 1970.
Gowon's address: *no victor, no vanquished.*
The oil revenues flow to the federal government.
The Igbo, who had held the east,
receive the terms of the defeated.
1973: OPEC oil embargo. Oil price quadruples.
Nigeria's revenue explodes.
The boom. The construction cranes everywhere.
*Oil doom* — what Nigerians call what followed:
the Dutch disease, the abandoned agriculture,
the cocoa and groundnut exports
that had made Nigeria solvent before oil
and which collapsed under the currency
that oil inflated.
1995: Ken Saro-Wiwa.
Ogoni writer, activist, hanged by the Abacha government
for his campaign against Shell's environmental destruction
of the Niger Delta.
Nine men executed.
International condemnation.
Shell's presence in the Delta continued.
The flares continued.
1999: return to civilian rule. Obasanjo.
The oil still flowing.
The Delta still burning.
2021–2025: Rivers State,
the heart of the Delta,
in intermittent armed conflict.
Pipeline attacks. Federal emergency declaration.
The question Biafra asked in 1967
still not fully answered in 2025:
whose ground is this?
