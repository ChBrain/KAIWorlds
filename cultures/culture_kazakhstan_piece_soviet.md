# Piece: The Soviet Inheritance
## What the USSR left in Kazakhstan — the cities, the tests, the trauma, and the opportunity.

## Owner
- **Place:** Astana

## Place
The Semipalatinsk Test Site — the Polygon.
18,500 square kilometres of steppe
in northeastern Kazakhstan.
456 nuclear tests between 1949 and 1989.
The nearest town: Semey. 150,000 people.
Radiation-related illness persists in the population.
The site is closed. The steppe absorbed it.
The steppe absorbs.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Soviet inheritance
and Kazakhstan is a nomadic steppe civilisation
that had not yet become a state.
With it: Kazakhstan is a country
that received, all at once,
the infrastructure of a Soviet republic —
the cities, the railways, the industrial plants,
the universities, the bureaucracy —
and the debts of a Soviet republic:
the environmental destruction,
the demographic manipulation,
the suppression of Kazakh language and culture,
the 1.5 million Kazakhs killed
in the collectivisation famine of 1931–33,
the Gulag sites across the steppe,
and the nuclear test site
that irradiated 1.5 million people
who were not told what was being done
to the land they were living on.
Remove the piece: a newly independent Central Asian state.
With it: a country managing the simultaneous inheritance
of extraordinary infrastructure
and extraordinary damage —
and deciding which to lead with
in every room it enters.
The oil that funds Astana's glass towers
was found in Soviet geological surveys.
The Baikonur Cosmodrome, leased to Russia, launches satellites.
The nuclear testing produced the anti-nuclear movement
that helped Kazakhstan become
the first country to voluntarily give up
nuclear weapons and a nuclear test site.
The inheritance is not only burden.
It is also the material
from which the position is building something
it did not have a blueprint for in 1991.

## Apparent
1731: the Kazakh khans accept Russian suzerainty. The beginning of incorporation.
1860s: Russian colonisation intensifies.
1916: Kazakh uprising against Russian conscription. Suppressed.
1920: the Kazakh Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic.
1931–1933: forced collectivisation. The Kazakh nomads' herds destroyed.
Famine. 1.5 million Kazakhs dead — 40% of the Kazakh population.
The Kazakhs become a minority in their own republic.
1937–1938: the Stalinist purges. The Kazakh intelligentsia decimated.
1949: the first Soviet nuclear test at Semipalatinsk.
456 tests over forty years.
1986: Jeltoqsan — the December protests in Alma-Ata.
The first major nationalist uprising in the Soviet Union.
Russian is appointed head of the Kazakh Communist Party.
Kazakhs protest. Soviet troops used.
1991: independence. Nursultan Nazarbayev.
The oil fields: Tengiz, Kashagan, Karachaganak.
1997: the capital moved from Almaty to Astana — the steppe.
The new capital built. The argument made in architecture.
2019: Nazarbayev resigns. Tokayev becomes president.
Astana renamed Nur-Sultan for Nazarbayev.
January 2022: the Qandy Qantar — Bloody January.
Protests. Russian troops. Suppression.
Astana renamed back to Astana.
Nazarbayev's networks partially dismantled.
The oil continues flowing.
The glass towers continue rising.
The Polygon is still in the ground.
