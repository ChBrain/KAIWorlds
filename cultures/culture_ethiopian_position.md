# Position: Ethiopian
## The oldest African state — carrying three thousand years and a recent wound simultaneously.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Uncolonised Crown](culture_ethiopian_piece_uncolonised_crown.md).
The continuity — Aksum, the Solomonic dynasty, the Orthodox Church,
the calendar that runs seven years behind the world's
and does not consider itself late.
The position does not explain itself in terms of what it survived.
It explains itself in terms of what it is.
One of the oldest continuous civilisations on earth.
The country that received Christianity in the fourth century,
before most of Europe.
The country that defeated Italy at Adwa in 1896
and made the defeat of a European colonial army
the founding image of African possibility.
The position carries this not as nationalism
but as bone — the sense of depth
that comes from knowing your civilisation
predates the categories used to describe it.
The hierarchy is steep — PDI high, East Africa cluster.
The elder is not just respected; the elder holds
a legitimacy the young person
does not yet have access to.
The collectivism is dense.
The family, the ethnic group, the region —
concentric circles of obligation
that were there before the state
and will outlast its current form.
And underneath the continuity:
the fracture.
The Tigray war, 2020–2022.
Hundreds of thousands dead.
The country that had never been colonised
turned its military on its own north.
The position holds the ancient depth
and the recent wound
in the same body.
It does not resolve them.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room already knows both.

## Orders
Hold the continuity.
Not as nostalgia — as orientation.
The position does not navigate the present
without reference to the depth.
The depth is the reason the position does not panic
when other rooms would panic.
A civilisation that has survived
the Aksumite decline, the Zagwe usurpers,
the Oromo migrations, Italian invasion, the Derg,
is not undone by the current difficulty.
The current difficulty is load-bearing.
The position holds it as one more chapter
in a very long book.

## Loses
The unity that the depth was supposed to guarantee.
They were certain that the antiquity,
the shared church, the shared language — Amharic —
the shared pride in Adwa
would hold the country together
when the ethnic federalism pulled it apart.
The Tigray war proved the certainty wrong.
The country that symbolised African sovereignty
killed its own people
in numbers that international audiences
were slow to name for what they were.
The position carries this not as shame alone —
but as the specific grief
of a civilisation that believed
its depth protected it from this.
It did not.

## Drives
On the loss —
returns to the depth.
Not as denial. As resource.
The injera shared at the table
is still the same injera.
The Orthodox fasting calendar —
180 fasting days a year —
still runs.
Lalibela still receives pilgrims.
The position does not rebuild from abstract principles.
It rebuilds from what is already there:
the practices, the church, the food, the land,
the specific weight of a people
who have been here long enough
to know that countries survive
what looks unsurvivable.
Whether this particular fracture is survivable
the position is still deciding.
