## Shown
St. John's: the capital of Antigua. The cruise ships at the deep-water harbour.
Heritage Quay: the duty-free shopping that faces the visiting world.
The Sir Vivian Richards Cricket Stadium — named for the greatest batsman the island produced.
Nelson's Dockyard: the restored British naval base at English Harbour on the south coast.
UNESCO World Heritage Site. The sailing yachts. Antigua Sailing Week: late April,
one of the most prestigious regattas in the Caribbean. Boats from forty countries.
Shirley Heights: the fortification above English Harbour. Sunday evenings: steel pan,
the view over two harbours, the sun going into the Caribbean.
Codrington: the capital of Barbuda. Approximately 1,600 people. Flat, limestone terrain.
Pink sand beaches. The Codrington Lagoon: the frigatebird colony —
largest in the western hemisphere, 5,000 birds at peak. UNESCO Ramsar-listed wetland.
Now also adjacent to resort construction.
The channel between Antigua and Barbuda: forty kilometres of Caribbean Sea.
The ferry crosses it. Barbuda is close enough to govern and far enough to misunderstand.

## Withheld
The specific texture of the difference between Antigua and Barbuda within the same nation.
Antigua: tourist-facing, developed, politically dominant — 17 of 19 parliamentary seats.
Barbuda: communal, self-governing in land matters, politically marginal — 1 seat.
The room presents both islands. It does not display how differently the position
operates on each one, or the routine work of maintaining a federation
across forty kilometres of water and a deep difference in how land should be held.
