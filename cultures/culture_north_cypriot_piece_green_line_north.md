# Piece: The Green Line (Northern Side)
## The same line, the other account.

## Owner
- **Place:** Nicosia (North)

## Place
Nicosia. The northern side of the Green Line.
The same city. A different administration.
The buffer zone between them: UN-monitored.
The Ledra Street crossing: open since 2008.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the North Cypriot position
loses its defining physical condition.
The Green Line is not a border — it is a ceasefire line.
Not internationally recognised as a border.
But it separates two administrations,
two currencies, two legal systems, two communities
who share a city.
The North Cypriot position administers one side.
The Greek Cypriot position administers the other.
Both positions are shaped by the Line.
For the North, the Line is the proof of the 1974 intervention's permanence.
For the South, it is the wound.
Same line. Different weight.

## Apparent
A line through Nicosia.
UN Buffer Zone: 4 kilometres wide in places, narrower in the city.
UN peacekeepers: present since 1974.
Abandoned buildings in the buffer zone.
Varosha — the resort area of Famagusta, sealed since 1974.
Partially reopened 2020 — contested.
The Ledra Street crossing.
Pedestrians cross.
Goods cross with restrictions.
The administrations do not recognise each other.

## Yearbook
1974: Turkish military intervention. The line drawn.
1983: Northern Cyprus declares independence.
Recognised by Turkey only.
2003: the crossings open for the first time since 1974.
People cross. Communities see each other again.
2004: Cyprus (south) joins the EU.
The whole island joins legally.
The northern administration outside EU governance in practice.
2008: Ledra Street crossing opens in the city centre.
2020: Varosha partially reopened.
The Greek Cypriot government objects.
The UN objects.
Turkey proceeds.
The line is still there.
The crossings are open.
The political status is unchanged.
Both sides continue.
