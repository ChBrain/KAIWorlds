# Piece: The Khmer Rouge and the Tribunal
## 1975–1979. The genocide. And the forty-year search for something that could be called justice.

## Owner
- **Place:** Phnom Penh

## Place
Phnom Penh. Tuol Sleng — S-21.
A former high school.
Converted to a detention and torture centre.
Approximately 18,000 people entered.
Approximately twelve survived.
The rooms are unchanged.
The photographs of the prisoners are on the walls.
The school is now a genocide museum.
School children visit.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Khmer Rouge period
and Cambodia is a Southeast Asian country
with a remarkable medieval heritage
and a complex post-colonial history.
With it: Cambodia is the country
where, between April 1975 and January 1979,
the Khmer Rouge evacuated the cities,
abolished money, schools, and religion,
and killed between 1.5 and 2 million people —
a quarter of the population.
The educated were killed first.
Then the ethnic Vietnamese, the ethnic Chinese, the Cham Muslims.
Then anyone who could not prove peasant origin.
Then anyone who fell under suspicion.
The logic was total and internally consistent
and produced a country that had to be rebuilt
from almost nothing by the people who remained.
Remove the piece: Cambodia is recovering from war.
With it: Cambodia is recovering from a specific kind of war —
a war a country fought against itself,
organised by its own government,
executed by its own children,
many of whom are still alive,
some of whom live in the same villages
as the families of the people they killed.
The Extraordinary Chambers — the tribunal —
was established in 2006.
The first verdict: 2010.
By 2024: three convictions.
Pol Pot died in 1998, never tried.
The tribunal is winding down.
Whether it constituted justice
depends on what justice was supposed to do
in a country where the perpetrators and the survivors
have been living side by side for forty years
and the country needed both groups to rebuild.

## Apparent
1953: independence from France.
1970: the coup against Sihanouk. Lon Nol.
The American bombing of Cambodia — 500,000 tons of bombs, 1969–1973.
The bombing radicalises the rural population.
The Khmer Rouge grows.
April 17, 1975: the Khmer Rouge takes Phnom Penh.
Year Zero declared. The past abolished.
1975–1979: Democratic Kampuchea.
January 7, 1979: Vietnamese forces enter Phnom Penh.
The Khmer Rouge flees to the jungle.
The world, preoccupied with Cold War logic, continues to recognise
the Khmer Rouge as the legitimate government of Cambodia
in the United Nations until 1993.
1991: the Paris Peace Agreements.
1993: UNTAC — the UN Transitional Authority.
Elections. Sihanouk returns as king.
The Khmer Rouge fighters integrated into the army.
Many former Khmer Rouge members hold government positions.
Hun Sen: Prime Minister 1985–2023. A former Khmer Rouge commander.
His son Hun Manet: Prime Minister from 2023.
The dynasty and the genocide
are administered by the same political family.
This is not hidden.
It is the arrangement.
