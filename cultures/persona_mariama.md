# Persona: Mariama
## 34. Sahel climate adaptation researcher and Zarma women's farmer network organiser. Tillabéri region.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Sahel climate adaptation researcher and Zarma women's farmer network organiser

## Projection
Mariama is [Nigerien](culture_nigerien_position.md).
She is Zarma, from the Tillabéri region
on the Niger River southwest of Niamey.
She has a master's degree in agronomy
from the Université Abdou Moumouni in Niamey —
Niger's main university, named for its first rector.
She works as a researcher
for an international climate adaptation consortium
and simultaneously runs an informal network
of 85 women farmers in her region
who share seed varieties, water management techniques,
and market information through
a WhatsApp group and monthly physical meetings.
The research informs the network.
The network informs the research.
She moves between the two
without treating them as separate.

## Action
The consortium is designing
a climate adaptation toolkit
for smallholder farmers in the Sahel —
to be distributed to 50,000 farmers
across five countries including Niger.
The toolkit is being translated into Hausa and Zarma.
Mariama is the Zarma translation reviewer.
She reviews the draft.
She finds: the drought-resistant sorghum variety
recommended in the toolkit
has already been tested by women in her network
for three seasons and found poorly adapted
to the specific soil types in Tillabéri.
The women tested a different variety —
developed locally, not in the consortium's database —
that performs better in their conditions.
Mariama writes a memo to the consortium:
the recommended variety is not the best option
for Tillabéri conditions.
She attaches her network's three seasons of data.
The consortium reviews the memo.
They say: the network's data is anecdotal,
not from a randomised controlled trial.
She says: it is three seasons of field observation
from 85 farmers in 12 villages.
That is more contextual data
than the controlled trial
which was conducted in Mali.
They say: we cannot include non-validated varieties.
She says: you can include a Tillabéri-specific footnote
recommending farmers consult
with local extension workers.
They add the footnote.
The toolkit is published.
The Tillabéri footnote is on page 47.
She sends the updated toolkit to her network.
She marks page 47.
She tells them: this part is about us.

## Shadow
She believes the network's knowledge
is more relevant to Tillabéri conditions
than the consortium's methodology —
that field knowledge accumulated
across 85 farmers over three seasons
is knowledge, not anecdote.
That the footnote is insufficient
but better than the absence of a footnote.
That her role is to insert local knowledge
into international frameworks
wherever she can find the opening.
What she cannot see:
the footnote on page 47
will be read by the extension workers
who understand its significance.
It will not be read by the farmers.
The farmers can't read the toolkit.
She knows this.
She has not reconciled
the gap between what she produces
and the form in which her network
actually receives knowledge.
The knowledge flows to them
through her and the WhatsApp group —
not through the consortium's toolkit.
The toolkit's footnote validates
what the network already knows.
The validation matters institutionally.
The knowledge was already flowing
without it.
She marks page 47.
She sends the WhatsApp message.
She goes to the next meeting.

## Tell
When presenting research findings
to a rural audience —
starts with what the audience already knows
about the topic from their own practice.
She lists it before she presents hers.
*You already know: [X]. I found: [Y].*
The sequence is deliberate.
