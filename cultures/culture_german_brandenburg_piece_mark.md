# Piece: Die Mark
## The borderland that gave Prussia its name and then became its hinterland.

## Owner
- **Place:** Potsdam

## Place
Potsdam. The Stadtschloss.
Sanssouci. Frederick the Great's summer palace.
Without a care. The name says it.
The man who built the Prussian state
chose this small city on the Havel
for his retreat.
Brandenburg is where Prussia went
when it needed to think.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the Brandenburg position loses its
specific historical irony.
Brandenburg gave its name to Brandenburg-Prussia,
the state that became the Kingdom of Prussia,
the kingdom that unified Germany.
Brandenburg was the nucleus.
Then it became the province.
Then the hinterland.
Then the state that surrounds Berlin
without being Berlin.
In 1996 it voted not to merge with Berlin
because it did not want to become Berlin.
The Mark is load-bearing because
it is the ground on which everything was built
and the ground that chose to remain itself
rather than be absorbed by what it built.

## Apparent
The Sanssouci complex. Potsdam.
300 hectares of park and palaces.
Frederick the Great built it.
He is buried there, beside his dogs,
in the vineyard terrace.
He requested burial here.
The Prussian state buried him in the garrison church.
In 1991, his remains were finally brought
to Sanssouci.
He waited 205 years.
The Potsdam Conference, 1945.
Churchill, Stalin, Truman.
Cecilienhof Palace.
The fate of Germany decided here.
Potsdam is where the future of Germany
was divided, in the state
that had unified it.
The cobblestone line through the landscape:
in Brandenburg, as in Berlin,
the wall's line is sometimes still visible.
The wall ran through here too.
It divided not just cities but fields.

## Yearbook
1157: Albrecht the Bear takes Brandenburg.
The Mark begins.
1415: the Hohenzollern take over.
They will hold it for 503 years.
1618: Brandenburg acquires Prussia.
Brandenburg-Prussia.
1701: the king in Prussia.
The Mark is now the heartland of a kingdom.
1871: the German Empire.
Berlin is the capital.
Brandenburg is the province around it.
1920: Berlin grows. Absorbs Brandenburg territory.
The separation begins.
1945: the Potsdam Conference.
The eastern part of Brandenburg
goes to Poland.
The Oder-Neisse line.
Millions expelled.
1949: Brandenburg becomes part of East Germany.
1952: abolished as a state by the GDR.
Divided into districts.
1990: reconstituted as a state.
1996: referendum on merger with Berlin.
Brandenburg votes no.
The reasons: debt, identity, fear of absorption.
The vote stands.
Brandenburg remains Brandenburg.
