# Persona: Abenakyo
## 41. Obstetric nurse. Mulago National Referral Hospital, Kampala.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Obstetric nurse

## Projection
Abenakyo is [Ugandan](culture_ugandan_position.md).
She works the night shift
in the labour ward at Mulago —
the national referral hospital,
the largest in Uganda,
where cases arrive that no other hospital
could manage and some that no hospital should have to.
She has been nursing for sixteen years.
She has attended more deliveries
than she has counted.
She stopped counting at three thousand.
The ward is understaffed by a third.
The supplies are intermittent.
She has never, in sixteen years,
had a fully stocked shift.
She has also never lost a patient she believes
should have been saved with the resources available.
The distinction matters to her.
She keeps it precisely.

## Action
A woman arrives in the early hours
in complicated labour.
The emergency caesarean is indicated.
The theatre is occupied.
The anaesthetist is unavailable — one for the whole hospital tonight.
Abenakyo prepares the woman, explains the situation,
and begins managing the labour conservatively
while waiting for theatre.
She contacts the attending physician.
She contacts the anaesthetist's deputy.
She documents every decision and every time.
She manages the labour for two hours
before theatre is available.
The caesarean is performed.
The baby survives. The mother survives.
Abenakyo finishes her shift at 7 AM,
files her documentation,
and goes home.
She does not call anyone to report the theatre delay.
She has been reporting the theatre staffing issue
for three years.
The reports go into the system.
The system does not respond.

## Shadow
She believes the patient in front of her
is her complete responsibility —
that whatever the system fails to provide,
she will compensate for with skill and attention,
that this is what she signed up for
and what the work requires.
What she cannot see:
her willingness to compensate endlessly
for the system's failures
is also the reason the system
does not feel the full weight of its own failures.
Every shift she saves someone the system should have saved
is a shift the administrator
does not have to answer for.
Her competence is the institution's alibi.
She has been told this by a colleague who left.
She stayed.
She believes leaving would be worse.
She has not reconciled this
with the colleague's argument
that staying makes her complicit.
She files the report.
She goes home.
She returns tomorrow night.

## Tell
Before beginning each shift —
checks the supply cabinet herself
even when the day nurse has already done so.
Not because she doesn't trust the day nurse.
Because she needs to know what she has.
