# Piece: The Friendly Islands and the Constitution
## The king who outmanoeuvred colonialism by writing a constitution before Britain could write one for him.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Nuku'alofa](culture_tongan_place_nukualofa.md)

## Place
Nuku'alofa. The capital.
The Royal Palace on the waterfront.
Built 1867. Still the royal residence.
The palace that Cook saw the precursors of
when he named these the Friendly Islands.
The Sunday morning: empty streets.
The church bells.
The constitution observing itself.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 1875 Constitution
and Tonga is a Pacific archipelago
that follows the trajectory of Fiji, Samoa, Hawaii —
European pressure, cession or partition,
colonial administration.
With it: the specific Tongan manoeuvre.
Tupou I, advised by the Wesleyan missionary Shirley Baker,
wrote a constitution that gave Tonga
the legal structure of a Christian constitutional monarchy
before any colonial power could impose one.
The 1900 Protectorate agreement with Britain
left Tonga self-governing in internal affairs.
Britain managed foreign policy.
Tonga managed Tonga.
The protectorate ended 1970.
Full independence.
No colonial administration.
No Treaty dispossession.
The land system — constitutionally protected,
no land to foreigners —
is the product of Tupou I's constitutional work.
The 2006 riots are the counter-piece:
November 2006, Nuku'alofa.
Pro-democracy protesters
burn the CBD.
Eight people die.
The trigger: the pro-democracy movement's frustration
with the pace of democratic reform
and the economic dominance
of a small Chinese business community.
Both currents — democracy and anti-Chinese sentiment —
in the same event.
The 2010 constitution reform follows.
Commoners gain majority in parliament.
The king retains cabinet appointment power.
The reform: real but bounded.
The monarchy: intact.

## Apparent
950 CE: Tongan Empire at its height.
Controls trade routes across Polynesia.
1616: Dutch contact.
1773: James Cook. Names them Friendly Islands.
1797: London Missionary Society arrives.
1820s–1830s: civil wars.
1831: Tupou I converts to Christianity.
Methodist Wesleyan. The conversion as political decision.
1845: Tupou I unifies Tonga.
1862: emancipation of serfs. Significant social reform.
1875: Constitution. Tonga's magna carta.
Land law: no sale to foreigners.
Every male entitled to land allotment.
1900: British Protectorate.
External affairs only.
1970: full independence.
Commonwealth member.
2006: riots. CBD destroyed.
2008: King George Tupou V initiates democratic reforms.
2010: constitutional amendments.
First commoner-majority parliament elected.
2012: Tupou V dies. Tupou VI succeeds.
The land law: intact.
The monarchy: intact.
The Sunday law: intact.
The remittances: arriving.
