# Piece: The Iraqi Invasion and Liberation
## August 2, 1990 – February 26, 1991. The seven months that made the Kuwaiti identity.

## Owner
- **Place:** Kuwait City

## Place
Kuwait City. The Liberation Tower.
372 metres. Built to commemorate the liberation.
Visible from everywhere in the city.
The argument the city makes:
we came back.

## Load Bearing
Remove the invasion and Kuwait is a wealthy Gulf emirate
with a functioning legislature
and a merchant tradition
and a Bidun problem
and too much oil money
and a population that is 30% Kuwaiti
and 70% expatriate.
With it: Kuwait is all of the above
plus the country that was erased —
the oil wells set on fire,
the cultural property looted,
the Kuwaiti citizens tortured and killed,
the resistance that formed in basements
and communicated through *diwaniyyas* —
and came back.
The liberation is load-bearing
because it is the Kuwaiti position's evidence
of what it means to be Kuwaiti:
not just the oil wealth or the merchant family
or the National Assembly —
but the people who hid their neighbours
and maintained the networks
and came back to a country
whose oil wells were still burning.
Remove the piece: a small oil state.
With it: a small oil state that knows
what it means to almost not exist
and has built its politics around the knowledge.
The Bidun who fought in the resistance
and remained stateless after the liberation:
also in the piece.
The liberation was for Kuwaitis.
The Bidun were there too.
This is also load-bearing.

## Apparent
1899: Kuwait enters British protection.
1938: oil discovered.
1961: independence. The Al-Sabah dynasty confirmed.
1963: the National Assembly elected for the first time.
The Gulf's first elected legislature.
1976, 1986: the Assembly suspended by the Emir.
Restored under public pressure each time.
August 2, 1990: Iraq invades. 100,000 soldiers.
Kuwait City falls in hours.
The Emir flees to Saudi Arabia.
The occupation begins.
Seven months.
The oil ministry looted.
The National Museum looted.
The oil wells: 700 of them set on fire on retreat.
The fires burned for ten months.
The sky above Kuwait was black for a year.
August 1990 – January 1991: the diplomatic phase.
The UN resolutions. The coalition assembled.
January 17, 1991: Operation Desert Storm begins.
February 24: the ground offensive.
February 26: Kuwait liberated.
February 26 is National Day.
The liberation: the founding of modern Kuwait.
The Bidun who fought in the resistance: still stateless.
The National Assembly: reinstated and active and gridlocked.
The oil wells: capped and producing.
The Liberation Tower: visible from everywhere.
