# Piece: The Ehrbare Kaufmann
## The honourable merchant. The word that was already higher than a title.

## Owner
- **Place:** Hamburg

## Place
Hamburg. The Speicherstadt.
The warehouse district built on oak piles in the Elbe.
Brick. Red. Still standing.
Now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
The coffee and spice warehouses
became museums and design studios.
The function changed. The building did not.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the Hamburg position loses its self-image.
The Ehrbare Kaufmann - the honourable merchant -
is not a job description.
It is the social contract
on which Hamburg's republic was built.
Your word is your bond.
Your reputation is your capital.
The Hanseaten considered ennoblement
by Prussian kings
an insult to their standing.
A title could not elevate a Hanseatic family.
The family was already what it was.
Remove the Ehrbare Kaufmann
and Hamburg becomes merely a large port city.
With it, Hamburg is a republic
that has been running its own affairs
since 1189.

## Apparent
The Handelskammer Hamburg. Since 1665.
One of the oldest chambers of commerce in the world.
The Speicherstadt. Built 1883–1927.
Workers displaced from the warehouses' footprint:
24,000 people moved.
The trade required the space.
The space was taken.
The transaction was recorded.
The record remains.
Thomas Mann's *Buddenbrooks*, 1901.
Set in Lübeck. Read in Hamburg.
The decay of a Hanseatic merchant family
over four generations.
The Germans bought it.
The Hanseaten recognised it.
Nobel Prize, 1929.
Hamburg joined the German Federation in 1815.
It remains the Freie und Hansestadt Hamburg.
Free and Hanseatic.
Both words still in the official name.
Bremen and Hamburg are the only federal states
that have never created their own orders of merit.
The Hanseatic rejection of decorations
was never formally abolished.
It was never needed.

## Yearbook
1189: Hamburg granted trade privileges by Frederick Barbarossa.
The city begins.
1241: alliance with Lübeck.
The precursor to the Hanseatic League.
1356: the Hanseatic League formally constituted.
Hamburg at its centre.
1510: Hamburg becomes a Free Imperial City.
Answerable to the emperor, not to local princes.
The independence is structural from this point.
1815: Hamburg joins the German Federation.
The Senate considers the terms carefully.
The joining is on Hamburg's terms as far as possible.
1842: the Great Fire of Hamburg.
A third of the city destroyed in three days.
Rebuilt within years.
The Speicherstadt begun forty years later.
1943: Operation Gomorrah.
British bombing. 34,000 dead in one week.
The city rebuilt again.
The port operating within years.
1949: Hamburg becomes a federal state of West Germany.
The Senate continues.
The Handelskammer continues.
The Ehrbare Kaufmann continues.
Today: Europe's third-largest port.
The world still comes through here.
*Moin.*
