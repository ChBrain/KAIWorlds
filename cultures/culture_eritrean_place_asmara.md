## Location: Eritrea

## Shown
- The Fiat Tagliero Building. 1938.
  A functionalist service station shaped like an airplane,
  cantilevered wings with no supporting columns.
  The Italian engineer who designed it
  reportedly told the builder
  that he would shoot him if he used columns.
  No columns were used.
  UNESCO World Heritage since 2017.
  The only UNESCO site in Eritrea.
  The world came to see the architecture.
  The world is not permitted to see much else.
- The main boulevard. *Harnet Avenue.*
  The Art Deco buildings.
  The cafés serving macchiato —
  the Italians left the coffee culture
  and the Eritreans kept it.
  The evening passeggiata —
  the Italian habit of walking the main street at dusk,
  also kept.
  The city that looks like the 1930s preserved in amber
  because there was no investment to change it
  and the government had other priorities.
- The Cathedral of Our Lady of the Rosary.
  Catholic. Italian colonial construction.
  The Orthodox churches. The mosques.
  The visible coexistence —
  the Christian and Muslim communities
  that have shared Asmara for centuries
  and which the government invokes as proof
  of Eritrean unity
  while restricting both communities' freedom
  to organise independently.
- The Ministry of Information building.
  The only broadcast media in Eritrea:
  state television, state radio.
  No independent press since 2001.
  No internet café operating freely.
  The satellite dishes on the apartment buildings —
  technically restricted, practically present.
  The city that knows more than it is supposed to know
  and knows that knowing is a private matter.
