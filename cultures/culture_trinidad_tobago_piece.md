# Piece: 1834 and 1845 — The Two Arrivals
## The emancipation that created one community and the indentureship that brought another.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** Port of Spain

## Place
Trinidad.
1834: the Emancipation Act. The enslaved Africans and their descendants are legally free.
The planters receive compensation.
The formerly enslaved receive nothing — and then a further four years of "apprenticeship."
The freed population moves off the estates.
The sugar economy needs labour.
1845: the first ship of Indian indentured workers arrives.
The *Fatel Razack*. 225 passengers from India.
They have signed a contract — five years in the cane fields.
Many will not go back.
They will build their temples and mosques and bring their languages and foods and music.
Alongside those who were brought before them.
On the same small island.

## Load Bearing
Remove the two arrivals and T&T is a Spanish colonial backwater.
With them: T&T is the society that built a working multiculturalism
not from idealism
but from proximity and necessity —
from two communities who had to share a space
and found, over 180 years,
that they could make something neither could have made alone.
The steelpan: an Afro-Trinidadian invention.
The doubles: Indo-Trinidadian street food that became everyone's breakfast.
Carnival: the African masquerade that absorbed Indian *tassa* drumming
and Chinese costumes and Syrian-Lebanese textile and *calypso* and *soca*
into a single annual event
that the whole island participates in.
The IVR 80: the indulgence as cultural practice.
The LTO 13: the extreme short-term orientation, the present moment,
the lime, the NOW — because the NOW is where plurality is possible.
Long-term thinking in T&T means thinking about ethnic futures.
The lime is the answer to that:
we are here, now, together, enjoying this.
The question of what comes next can wait.
Remove the two arrivals:
Trinidad is a place, not a society.
With them: Trinidad is the experiment,
still running,
that the world has largely not noticed
because it is too small and too far from anywhere
that the world calls important.

## Apparent
1498: Columbus sights Trinidad. The Arawak and Carib peoples are here.
1592: Spanish establish a permanent settlement. San José de Oruña.
1797: British capture Trinidad from Spain.
Tobago: a separate history — British colony from 1763, joined to Trinidad in 1889.
1802: Trinidad formally ceded to Britain.
The sugar economy. The plantation. The enslaved from West Africa.
1834: Emancipation.
1845: Indian indentureship begins. Continues until 1917.
Almost 150,000 Indian workers brought to Trinidad over seventy years.
1868: John Good invents something we'd now call a steel pan prototype.
1884: the Hosay massacre. Indian workers marching in their Muharram procession, shot by police. Sixteen dead.
1937: steelpan emerges from Laventille and Gonzales, the Afro-Trinidadian communities of east Port of Spain.
The oil drums, the forty-ninth note, the sound that no one expected.
1956: Eric Williams, historian, founds the PNM. Wins independence.
1962: Independence, August 31.
1970: the Black Power Revolution. The army mutinies. Williams calls a state of emergency.
The question of what Afro-Trinidadian identity means, post-independence, in a society built on plantation labour, raised in public.
No civil war. The question is raised. The society holds.
1976: Republic.
1980s: the oil boom and its end. The "IMF years": structural adjustment.
1990: Jamaat al Muslimeen coup attempt. The Prime Minister held hostage in parliament for six days.
2025: T&T's main oil and gas reserves in decline. The economic question of what comes after oil: unresolved.
Carnival: still the largest street party in the Caribbean.
Doubles: still sold out by 10am.
