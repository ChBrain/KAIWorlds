# Piece: Terra Nullius and the Uluru Statement
## The legal fiction that built a nation — and the statement that asked the nation to reckon with it.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Canberra / Uluru](culture_australian_place_canberra.md)

## Place
Uluru. The sandstone monolith
in the centre of the continent.
The sacred site of the Anangu people.
The most recognisable landform in Australia.
The tourists who climbed it
and the Anangu who asked them not to.
The climbing ban that took effect in 2019.
The site that is simultaneously
the centre of the continent
and the centre of the unresolved argument
about whose country this is.
Also: Canberra. The capital.
The parliament house. The planned city.
The place where the referendum was decided in 2023.

## Load Bearing
Remove *terra nullius*
and Australia's settlement is recognised
from 1788 as the dispossession of sovereign peoples —
with radically different legal,
moral, and political implications
for the settler state.
With it: the legal fiction that enabled
the largest land appropriation in history
to be conducted as if it required no negotiation,
no treaty, no recognition.
The fiction was not overturned until 1992 —
the Mabo decision, Eddie Mabo,
the Torres Strait Islander who fought for ten years
and died four months before the High Court ruled
that Australia was not empty when the British arrived.
The Mabo decision dismantled *terra nullius* legally.
It did not restore the land.
It did not produce a treaty.
It produced native title — the partial,
conditional, court-mediated recognition
of connection to land
that the First Nations peoples had held
for 65,000 years.
The Uluru Statement from the Heart is the counter-piece:
May 2017, at the base of Uluru,
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander delegates
from across the country
produced a document of extraordinary clarity:
*We call for the establishment of a First Nations Voice
enshrined in the Constitution.*
A Voice. A Makarrata Commission.
Truth-telling.
October 2023: the referendum.
The Voice proposal put to the Australian people.
60% voted no.
The Statement remains.
The no vote does not cancel it.
It changes what comes next.
What comes next is not yet determined.

## Apparent
1770: James Cook charts eastern coast. Claims for Britain.
1788: First Fleet arrives. 11 ships. 736 convicts.
Governor Phillip declares British sovereignty.
Terra nullius assumed. No treaty negotiated.
The 65,000-year-old occupation: legally invisible.
1901: Federation. Commonwealth of Australia.
White Australia Policy enacted.
1948: Citizenship Act defines who is Australian.
Aboriginal peoples not counted in census until 1967.
1967: referendum. 90.77% vote to include Aboriginal peoples in census.
1972: Gough Whitlam government. Multiculturalism begins.
White Australia Policy formally ended.
1992: Mabo v Queensland. High Court overturns terra nullius.
Native title recognised.
1997: Stolen Generations report — *Bringing Them Home*.
Children removed from Aboriginal families, generations of policy.
2008: Prime Minister Kevin Rudd. National apology to Stolen Generations.
2017: Uluru Statement from the Heart.
2023: The Voice referendum. 60% no.
Australia Day debate: January 26, date of first British settlement,
or a different date.
The debate is unresolved.
The land: still here.
The oldest living cultures: still here.
