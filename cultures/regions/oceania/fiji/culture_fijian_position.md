# Position: Fijian
## The coup-prone paradise — where two peoples share one country and have not yet decided on which terms.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Deed of Cession and the Indentured Indians](culture_fijian_piece_cession.md).
The iTaukei — the indigenous Fijians —
and the Indo-Fijians, descendants of indentured labourers
brought by the British from India 1879–1916.
57% iTaukei, 37% Indo-Fijian.
The British gave the iTaukei communal land rights —
87% of Fiji's land is iTaukei-owned communally.
The British brought the Indians to work the sugar.
The Indians stayed. The Indians prospered.
The prosperity became the tension.
Four coups: 1987 (twice), 2000, 2006.
The 1987 coups explicitly about indigenous paramountcy.
The 2006 coup claiming to end ethnic politics.
The 2013 constitution making all citizens equally Fijian
and banning ethnically-based parties.
The tension: still present,
differently named.
The *sevusevu* — the kava ceremony —
the iTaukei ritual of welcome
that must be performed for any significant encounter.
The kava bowl, the clapping, the *bula*.
The Indo-Fijian who has lived beside this ceremony
for four generations
and is still navigating
whose country this is.
The Islam, Hinduism, Christianity simultaneously —
the Hindu temple next to the Methodist church
next to the mosque.
The Pacific collectivism of the iTaukei:
the clan, the village, the vanua (land-people-tradition).
The South Asian collectivism of the Indo-Fijians:
the family, the caste network, the temple.
Two collectivisms. One country.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows immediately.

## Orders
*Bula* — life, health, greeting.
The warmth is the real thing.
The Fijian smile is not performance.
It is the genuine expression of a culture
that has hospitalised strangers for centuries.
The warmth and the political tension
operate on different registers.
Both are real.
The position performs both
without apparent contradiction
because the daily life does not require
the political tension to be present in the kava circle.

## Loses
The young Indo-Fijians.
After each coup, more leave.
Australia, New Zealand, Canada.
The skill drain.
The sugar industry declining.
The professionals who built Fiji's institutions —
lawyers, doctors, engineers —
who left after 1987 and did not return.
The position carries the knowledge
that the coups cost the country
a generation of its most capable people
and made the poverty that justified the coups worse.

## Drives
On the loss — tourism and resilience.
Fiji is extraordinarily good at tourism.
The beaches are real.
The welcome is real.
The dive sites are among the best in the world.
The tourism economy rebuilt
after each disruption.
The iTaukei villages that benefit
from the resort on the reef
and maintain the *sevusevu* beside it.
The country that keeps finding
a way to be open to the world
while the internal argument continues.
