# Position: Samoan
## The Pacific culture that absorbed Christianity without surrendering to it — and the diaspora that kept the *fa'asamoa* alive across the ocean.

- *

## Has
[Fa'asamoa and the Church](culture_samoan_piece_faasamoa.md).
*Fa'asamoa* — the Samoan way.
The matai system: the titled chiefs
who govern the village,
hold the communal land,
make the decisions that affect the family.
The matai title is earned, not simply inherited —
it is conferred by the family
on the person judged most capable
of carrying the obligation.
The obligation runs both ways:
the matai serves the family,
the family serves the community,
the community maintains the *fa'asamoa*.
The Christianity: arrived 1830 with John Williams.
The Samoans adopted it so thoroughly
that the church is now the second pillar
of Samoan society alongside the matai.
The pastor (*faifeau*) and the chief
are the two authorities of the village.
Both are fed first at every gathering.
The collectivism: total.
The individual is constituted through the group.
The family *aiga* as the primary unit —
not the nuclear family,
the extended family including the diaspora.
The diaspora: Samoa has more Samoans outside it
than inside it.
200,000 in Samoa.
200,000+ in New Zealand.
150,000+ in the United States.
The *remittance* economy:
the diaspora sends home.
The *fa'asamoa* travels with them.
*Tautua* — service.
The Samoan value: you serve
before you are served.
The youngest serve the oldest.
The people serve the chief.
The chief serves the village.
The service is the identity.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows your family,
your matai, your village.
The room waits for the *tautua*
to establish your position.

## Orders
Serve the *aiga*.
The position does not navigate
Samoan society independently.
Every significant decision —
where to live, whom to marry,
what to do with money —
runs through the family network.
The Samoan in Auckland
who receives a matai title from home
carries new obligations across the Tasman.
The title is not honorary.
It is functional.
The *aiga* expects the service.

## Loses
The land.
Customary land: 81% of Samoa.
The land belongs to the *aiga*,
governed by the matai.
It cannot be sold.
This protects the community.
It also limits individual economic development
in the way that investors require.
The tension between the communal land system
and the capital requirements of development
is the permanent economic constraint
the position carries.

## Drives
On the loss —
the rugby.
Samoa produces rugby players
at a per-capita rate
that no country on earth matches.
The All Blacks, the Wallabies, the English team —
the Samoan names on the backs of jerseys
across the Pacific diaspora.
The rugby is not just sport.
It is the Samoan physical expression
of *tautua* — the service,
the collective effort,
the willingness to give the body
for the group.
The rugby is the *fa'asamoa*
played out on a pitch in Auckland or Cardiff
and watched by the *aiga*
in Apia and Pago Pago and Porirua.
