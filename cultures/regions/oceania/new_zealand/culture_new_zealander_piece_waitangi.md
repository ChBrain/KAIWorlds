# Piece: Te Tiriti o Waitangi
## The treaty that founded the bicultural nation — and the argument about what it promised that has never ended.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Wellington / Waitangi](culture_new_zealander_place_wellington.md)

## Place
Waitangi. The Bay of Islands.
The grounds where the Treaty was signed.
February 6, 1840.
The Treaty grounds: now a national historic site.
Every year on February 6 — Waitangi Day —
the Prime Minister comes here.
The protesters also come.
The speeches are made.
The protesters make their case.
The day is New Zealand's most complicated public holiday.

## Load Bearing
Remove Te Tiriti
and New Zealand is a British settler colony
that dispossessed its indigenous people
without any formal acknowledgment of prior rights —
a much more straightforward injustice,
with a much less complicated reckoning.
With it: New Zealand is the country
that founded itself on a compact,
however incompletely honoured,
that named the obligation to the first people
in the founding document.
The obligation named in the founding document
cannot be erased from the founding document.
This is why the argument never ends:
the Treaty is load-bearing for both the injustice
and the possibility of redress.
Without it: no basis for the Waitangi Tribunal.
No basis for the Treaty settlements.
No legal or constitutional mechanism
for the Māori to bring their case.
With it: 185 years of argument,
ongoing settlement,
the river Whanganui granted legal personhood (2017),
the mountain Taranaki / Mount Egmont
recognised as an ancestor,
te reo Māori official and compulsory.
The Treaty is the mechanism that makes
the restoration possible.
It is also the mechanism that made
the dispossession possible —
the British used it to claim sovereignty
while Māori understood they were agreeing
to a form of shared governance.
The English version and the Māori version
say different things.
This is not a translation error.
It is the founding ambiguity
of the bicultural nation.

## Apparent
800–1300 CE: Māori arrive from Polynesia.
1642: Abel Tasman sights the islands. Does not land.
1769: James Cook. First extended European contact.
The musket wars: 1807–1842.
Inter-tribal warfare intensified by European firearms.
Population disruption.
1840: Treaty of Waitangi, February 6.
500+ Māori chiefs sign over following months.
The English text: Māori cede sovereignty.
Te Tiriti (Māori text): Māori retain tino rangatiratanga (sovereignty/chieftainship).
British Crown claims sovereignty on basis of English version.
1840s–1870s: land wars.
Confiscation of millions of acres.
Māori population declines from ~100,000 to ~40,000 by 1896.
1877: Wi Parata v Bishop of Wellington. Judge rules Treaty "a simple nullity."
1893: Women's suffrage. First country in world.
1907: Dominion of New Zealand.
1947: legislative independence.
1975: Waitangi Tribunal established.
1985: Tribunal jurisdiction extended to historical claims.
Land settlements begin.
1987: te reo Māori official language.
2011: Whanganui River settlement begins.
2017: Whanganui River granted legal personhood.
2023: New government scales back Treaty co-governance commitments.
The argument continues.
Waitangi Day continues.
