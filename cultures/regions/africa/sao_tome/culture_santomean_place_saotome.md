## Location: São Tomé and Príncipe

## Shown
- The *roças* roads.
  The network of paths connecting the plantation estates.
  Originally built for ox-carts carrying cocoa.
  Now: overgrown in places, maintained in others.
  The estates themselves:
  some in ruins, moss taking the walls;
  some converted to hotels, ecolodges, research stations;
  some still producing cocoa under small-farm collectives.
  The physical inheritance of the plantation economy —
  the most beautiful ruins in the Gulf of Guinea.
  The beauty and the extraction
  on the same walls.
- The São Tomé city waterfront.
  The colonial Portuguese quarter.
  The *forte de São Sebastião* —
  the 16th century fort
  now housing the national museum.
  The slave trade documented inside
  the fort that regulated it.
  The same architectural logic as Namibia's Alte Feste —
  the archive of the atrocity
  inside the structure that administered it.
  The island keeps making this arrangement.
  It has no other buildings old enough.
- The *Tchiloli* performance space.
  Not a fixed venue — any public space.
  The performance: the medieval Portuguese play
  *A Tragédia do Marquês de Mântua*,
  performed in Forro Creole,
  with elaborate costumes, music,
  African percussion alongside European instruments.
  The play is Portuguese.
  The performance is Santomean.
  The transformation of colonial cultural material
  into something the colonised people
  have made entirely their own
  is one of the more defiant cultural acts
  in the African tradition.
  The *Tchiloli* is performed as festival and ceremony.
  It is not museum piece.
  It is alive.
- The cocoa cooperatives.
  Inland from the city.
  The small farms.
  The fermentation tables.
  The drying racks.
  The single-origin chocolate
  now in European specialty shops.
  The island reclaiming the mastery
  its ancestors built under coercion
  and now rebuilding as choice.
  The quality is genuine.
  The volume is a fraction of what it was.
  The distinction is everything.
- The Batepa Memorial.
  A modest monument.
  Not on the tourist circuit.
  The *forros* know where it is.
  February 3 is commemorated annually.
  The massacre is Santomean internal memory.
  The Portuguese government has not formally acknowledged it.
  The island marks it anyway.
  The marking is enough for now.

## Withheld
- Whether you are *forro*, *angolar*, *serviçal* descendant.
  The distinctions between Santomean communities
  are invisible to outsiders
  and present in every local interaction.
  Who your grandparents were on this island —
  which group, which *roça*, which history —
  shapes your position in the room.
  The room does not name this.
  Everyone in the room knows it.
