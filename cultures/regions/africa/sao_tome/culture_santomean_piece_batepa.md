# Piece: The Roças, the Forros, and the Batepa Massacre
## The indigenous Creole people who refused plantation labour were killed for it — and their refusal is the root of modern Santomean identity.

## Owner
- **Place:** [São Tomé City](culture_santomean_place_saotome.md)

## Place
Roça Agostinho Neto — formerly Roça Rio do Ouro.
The most imposing of the *roças*.
The slave quarters separated by nationality:
one for Angolans, one for Cape Verdeans.
Designed to prevent organisation
by making communication across languages harder.
The architecture of control
still standing.
Now: tourists walk through.
Descendants of those workers
live in the surrounding communities.
Both groups know where they are.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Batepa Massacre of February 1953
and São Tomé's independence story begins
with the 1974 Portuguese Carnation Revolution
and follows the standard model:
MLSTP, independence 1975, Marxist state,
economic liberalisation 1990.
With it: something that precedes it
and gives independence its specific charge.
The *forros* — the Creole descendants
of freed slaves and early settlers
who had lived on the island
since the 16th century —
had always refused to work on the *roças*.
This was not laziness.
This was the specific refusal
of a people who knew
that plantation labour
was slavery by another name,
and whose historical memory —
the memory of people whose ancestors
had been enslaved on this island —
told them the plantation
was the place their dignity ended.
By the early 1950s,
the Portuguese colonial authority
needed labour.
The *forros* refused.
The colonial government,
with backing from the Portuguese landowners,
decided to force them.
February 1953: colonial authorities
and Portuguese plantation owners
organised the arrest and killing
of *forros* who resisted.
Estimates: hundreds to over a thousand dead.
The Portuguese called it
a suppression of an uprising.
The Santomeans call it a massacre.
The massacre radicalised
the nascent independence movement.
The MLSTP was founded
the year after Batepa.
The independence movement's energy —
the specific anger —
derives from 1953.
Post-independence: the irony.
The MLSTP nationalised the *roças*.
The plantation system
the *forros* had refused to work
was now state-owned.
The managers fled.
The production collapsed.
The *forros'* descendants
now farmed land
that their ancestors had refused —
under a government
that inherited the same land structure
while claiming to have transformed it.
The transformation was real: independence is real.
The land structure underneath it
continued in a different form.
Remove the piece:
a standard Portuguese decolonisation story.
With it: a country whose founding identity
is a specific refusal —
the *forros* who said
*we are not plantation workers* —
and whose ongoing rebuilding of the cocoa industry
is, at its roots,
the reclaiming of that refusal
as economic agency.

## Apparent
1470: Portuguese discovery.
Island uninhabited.
1485–1490s: first Portuguese settlement.
Enslaved Africans brought.
Jewish children exiled from Portugal also sent.
1500s: major sugar production.
Africa's largest sugar producer.
Mid-1500s: sugar declines as Brazil rises.
Island becomes primarily a slave-trading depot.
*Forros* emerge as a distinct Creole community —
freed slaves and their descendants,
who develop their own culture, language, identity.
*Forros* refuse plantation labour across centuries.
19th century: cocoa introduced.
Cocoa boom: 1880s–1908.
World's largest cocoa producer by 1905.
90% of cultivated land: *roças*.
Labour: *serviçais* — contract workers from Angola,
Mozambique, Cape Verde.
The contract: essentially indenture.
Workers not permitted to leave easily.
British boycott of São Tomé cocoa in 1908:
Cadbury, responding to labour abuses reports.
*Forros*: refuse to work the *roças* throughout.
February 1953: Batepa Massacre.
Colonial authority and planters attack *forro* communities.
Death toll: disputed. Hundreds to over 1,000.
1954: MLSTP founded by Santomean exiles in Gabon.
1974: Carnation Revolution in Portugal.
July 12, 1975: independence.
Manuel Pinto da Costa (MLSTP) first president.
Nationalisation of *roças*.
4,000 Portuguese leave. Expertise gap.
Production collapses.
1990: multiparty elections.
Miguel Trovoada first elected president.
Land reform: *roças* land redistributed to workers.
Small farms replace large estates.
Ethical cocoa production builds from 2000s.
The *Tchiloli* tradition: UNESCO consideration.
The *forros* community: still distinct.
Still the oldest Santomean identity.
Still the keepers of the refusal.
