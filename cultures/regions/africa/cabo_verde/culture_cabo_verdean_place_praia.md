## Shown
- The Plateau. *Platô.*
  The old colonial centre on the flat-topped hill.
  The coloured buildings — ochre, blue, faded pink —
  the Cathedral of Our Lady of Grace,
  the Presidential Palace,
  the slow pace of the square
  where old men sit in the shade
  and nothing moves quickly.
  The Atlantic visible from every edge.
  The ocean that carried the enslaved,
  the emigrants, the returning,
  visible from the colonial architecture
  that was built on the proceeds of that trade.
- The market of Sucupira.
  The largest market in Cabo Verde.
  Clothes, food, electronics, everything.
  The traders from Senegal, Guinea-Bissau, China.
  The noise and the colour.
  The Kriolu negotiation.
  The market as the living proof
  that Cabo Verde is at a crossroads —
  Atlantic, African, Lusophone —
  and has always been.
- The harbour below the Plateau.
  The ferries to the other islands.
  The fishing boats.
  The container ships.
  The constant departure.
  Someone is always leaving from this harbour.
  Someone is always returning.
  The harbour does not distinguish.
  It processes both.
- The *Achada Grande* neighbourhoods.
  The new city spreading inland.
  The remittance houses — built by emigrants
  who send money for construction
  and return for holidays
  and eventually, some of them, to stay.
  The houses with gates and satellite dishes
  next to the unpaved streets.
  The country being built from the outside in.
