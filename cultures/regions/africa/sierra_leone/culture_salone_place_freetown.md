# Place: Freetown
## Location: Sierra Leone

## Owner

## Shown
- The Cotton Tree.
  The ancient tree at the centre of the city.
  The freed slaves who arrived in 1792
  are said to have gathered under it
  to give thanks.
  The tree is hundreds of years old.
  It has been dying slowly for decades.
  It collapsed partially in 2023.
  The stump remains.
  Freetown debated what to do.
  The debate is the city's relationship
  to its history: sacred, fragile, unresolved.
- The Fourah Bay College.
  On the hillside.
  Founded 1827. First university in sub-Saharan Africa.
  Where the Krio elite was educated.
  Where the educated West African middle class
  of the 19th century came.
  Now: teaching 4,000 students on a hillside
  with unreliable power and intermittent water.
  Still producing graduates.
  Still the most historically significant university
  between Cairo and the Cape.
- The War Memorial.
  Built for the first World War.
  The Sierra Leonean soldiers who fought for Britain
  remembered in stone.
  Next to it: informal vendors.
  Surrounding it: the city going about its business.
  The memorial is present.
  The city does not stop for it.
  Both are the relationship to history.
- Lumley Beach.
  Where Freetownians go.
  The ocean.
  The food stalls.
  The music.
  The city at leisure, which is the city
  doing what it has always done:
  being expressive, social, present.
  The war is in the past.
  The beach is in the present.
  The beach is more numerous.
- Aberdeen Bridge.
  The bridge between the Aberdeen peninsula
  and the mainland.
  Where fishing communities live.
  Where the formal city and the informal city
  meet most visibly.
  The fish market.
  The smell of the sea.
  The boats.
  The economy that runs underneath
  the economy in the reports.
