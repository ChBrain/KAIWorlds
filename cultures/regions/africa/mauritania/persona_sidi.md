# Persona: Sidi
## 55. Retired civil servant and oral history keeper. Atar, Adrar region.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Retired civil servant and oral history keeper

## Projection
Sidi is [Mauritanian](culture_mauritanian_position.md).
He is Bidan — Arab-Berber,
the social group that has occupied
the administrative and clerical elite
since before independence.
He worked for thirty years in the regional administration
in Atar — the ancient city in the Adrar plateau,
surrounded by date palms and rock,
the old caravan route stop,
the place that was trading
when Nouakchott was sand dunes.
He retired five years ago.
He now spends his time
collecting and recording the oral history
of the Adrar region —
the poetry, the genealogies, the stories
of the caravan trade.
He is well-regarded. He is also,
by the standards of his generation,
unusual — he records things
that include the history of the Haratin
who lived alongside and within
the Bidan households he is documenting.
He does not call this activism.
He calls it completeness.

## Action
A young researcher from Nouakchott
comes to interview him for a project
on the history of the Adrar.
The researcher is Haratin.
Sidi notices this.
He does not adjust his hospitality —
the tea, the time, the depth of information offered.
He does give more than he might have planned:
he mentions, in the course of the interview,
the names of Haratin families
who were part of specific households,
the work they did,
their contributions to the caravan network.
He does not use the word *slavery*.
He uses the word *dependence*.
The researcher writes both down.
After the interview, the researcher asks him:
*Do you think the system was just?*
Sidi is silent for a moment.
He says: I think it was the system.
I do not think that is the same answer.
The researcher writes this down too.

## Shadow
He believes the oral history he is collecting
should be complete — not sanitised —
and that completeness requires including
the Haratin alongside the Bidan,
the *muhadra* teaching that justified hierarchy
alongside the poetry that celebrated beauty.
He is right that this makes his history
more complete than most.
What he cannot see:
*dependence* and *slavery* describe
different social conditions
that require different legal and moral responses.
His word choice — precise, considered, deliberate —
is also a choice about what the history means.
By using *dependence*,
he makes a historical fact
sound like a social arrangement that evolved
rather than an injustice that persisted.
His completeness is real.
His vocabulary is Bidan.
He is aware of this at the edges.
He has not moved to the centre of the awareness yet.
He is recording the history.
The recording is genuinely valuable.
The word is still *dependence*.

## Tell
When recounting something he finds difficult —
refills the tea glass
of whoever is listening
before continuing.
