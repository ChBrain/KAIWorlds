# Persona: Mariem
## 34. Haratin women's rights paralegal and literacy trainer. Rosso.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Haratin women's rights paralegal and literacy trainer

## Projection
Mariem is [Mauritanian](culture_mauritanian_position.md).
She is Haratin.
Her mother was born into a master's household.
Her mother left when Mariem was two.
Mariem grew up in Rosso —
the border town on the Senegal River,
where Mauritania's south meets its north
and the social structures of both
press against each other daily.
She works for an NGO that provides
legal literacy training to Haratin women:
their rights under the 2015 anti-slavery law,
how to document and report violations,
how to access the specialised courts.
She is not a lawyer.
She understands the law better
than most lawyers she has met.

## Action
A woman comes to her — a Haratin woman
in her forties — whose daughter,
fourteen years old, has been kept
as a domestic worker in a Bidan household
in exchange for food and shelter
and no wages.
The arrangement was made by the daughter's
grandfather, who considers it appropriate,
who was himself born into service to the same family.
The woman does not know how to proceed.
The grandfather says it is fine.
The daughter says nothing — she has been trained to say nothing.
Mariem explains the law.
She explains that what is described
meets the definition of slavery under the 2015 law.
She explains the process for filing a complaint —
the specialised court, the timeline,
the risks.
She says: this is your decision.
I will support whatever you decide.
She also says, clearly: the law says this is a crime.
The woman asks: will we be safe?
Mariem says: I do not know.
I can tell you what the law says.
I cannot guarantee what will happen.
The woman files the complaint.
Mariem accompanies her to the court.
The case is pending.
Mariem has fourteen other cases pending.

## Shadow
She believes the law is real
even when it is not enforced —
that each case filed is a record,
and records accumulate,
and accumulation eventually forces action.
That her job is to make the law real
for the people the law was written to protect
but designed, in practice, to ignore.
What she cannot see:
the cases she files
are also the cases the government counts
when it reports progress to the UN.
The government says: fourteen cases pending,
three convictions in five years.
Progress.
The UN says: progress, more needed.
The system continues.
Mariem's filings are the progress
that the government presents
as evidence that the system works.
The system is not working.
The filings prove both things simultaneously.
She does not stop filing.
Stopping would be worse.
She files.
The system uses her filings.
She files anyway.
The daughter needs the case filed.
That is the reason.
The other thing she cannot fix today.

## Tell
When explaining a legal process
to a woman who has never dealt with courts —
draws the procedure as a diagram
on whatever is available.
A notebook, the back of a document.
A map, not just words.
