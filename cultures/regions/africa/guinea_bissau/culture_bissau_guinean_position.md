# Position: Bissau-Guinean
## The country that won its freedom first and kept none of it — where the coup is the political system.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[Amílcar Cabral and the Coups](culture_bissau_guinean_piece_cabral.md).
The PAIGC — the liberation movement
that Amílcar Cabral built —
was the most sophisticated anti-colonial organisation
in African history.
Cabral was an agronomist, a poet, a strategist.
He insisted the liberation was cultural as well as military:
*return to the source* —
the colonised must reclaim identity,
not simply remove the coloniser.
The movement won.
Guinea-Bissau independent September 1974.
Cabral was assassinated January 1973 —
eleven months before independence —
by a faction within the movement,
reportedly manipulated by Portuguese intelligence.
He did not see what his movement built.
What his movement built:
nine coups or successful coup attempts since 1974.
The most per capita of any country in the world.
The army as the permanent political actor.
No elected president has completed a full term.
The formal economy: cashew nuts.
Guinea-Bissau is the sixth-largest cashew exporter in the world.
The farmers grow them.
The Indian trading companies buy them at low prices.
The revenue does not reach the state.
The state does not reach the farmers.
The informal economy: cocaine transit.
Guinea-Bissau is a documented narco-state.
Colombian cocaine passing through the Bijagós islands
to Europe.
The military and political elite involved.
The position carries all of this.
Cabral's vision and the coups.
The cashew and the cocaine.
The Atlantic islands and the poverty.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows which faction your family supported,
which coup they survived,
which military commander their chief is aligned with.

## Orders
*Kaliandadi* — endurance, getting through.
The Creole word for the specific competence
of navigating a state that does not function
while maintaining the life that has to be lived.
The market functions.
The family functions.
The school sometimes functions.
The hospital sometimes functions.
The government changes.
Life continues in the space
that the government does not reach —
which, in Guinea-Bissau, is most of the space.

## Loses
Cabral.
Not as sentiment — as the specific loss
of the leader who had the theory
for what comes after liberation.
The leaders who followed —
Luís Cabral, Nino Vieira, the generals —
did not have his theory.
They had the army.
The army is what they used.
The position is literate enough
in its own history
to know that a different beginning
might have produced a different country.
The different beginning was removed
by an assassin's bullet
eleven months before it could take power.

## Drives
On the loss —
the women.
Guinean women dominate the informal economy,
the market trade, the household management,
the actual running of daily life.
The coups happen at the top.
The women hold the bottom.
The bottom is what keeps the country alive.
The Bijagós archipelago:
the matrilineal island society
where women initiate marriage
and hold the sacred ceremonies.
A different operating logic
within the same national border.
The position contains multitudes
and has learned to hold them
because the state cannot hold anything consistently.
