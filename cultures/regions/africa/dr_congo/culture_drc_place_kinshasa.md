## Shown
- The Congo River from the Corniche.
  Brazzaville visible on the other bank —
  two capitals facing each other across water,
  the only place on Earth
  where two national capitals are this close
  across an international border.
  Kinshasa has fifteen million people.
  Brazzaville has two million.
  The river between them
  was the reason the Europeans stopped here —
  the Congo's navigability ends at Kinshasa
  and the rapids begin.
  Everything that came in and went out
  passed through this point.
  It still does.
- The Marché de la Liberté.
  The informal economy made visible.
  The *article 15* economy —
  the one that runs when the formal one fails.
  The women who carry goods on their heads.
  The *cambistes* — the street money-changers
  who know the real exchange rate
  before the banks do.
  The traders who have been trading
  through Mobutu, through the civil wars,
  through every economic collapse.
  The market does not close for coups.
- The Académie des Beaux-Arts.
  Founded 1943.
  The place where Congolese contemporary art —
  Chéri Samba, Chéri Cherin, Monsengo Shula —
  developed a visual language
  for the country's contradictions.
  The paintings that fill European galleries.
  Painted here, first.
  With house paint when canvas was unavailable.
  Technically inventive because the resources forced it.
- The Cité de la Musique. The studios.
  Soukous, ndombolo, gospel, rumba.
  The musicians who kept recording
  during the wars.
  Franco Luambo's TPOK Jazz.
  Papa Wemba.
  Fally Ipupa.
  The music that is the country's most successful export —
  more reliable than cobalt in what it returns to its makers.
  The studios in Matonge, the Kinshasa neighbourhood
  that became the name for Congolese diaspora communities
  in Brussels and Paris.
  Matonge Bruxelles. Matonge Paris.
  The city reproduced abroad.
- The Palais de la Nation.
  Where Lumumba governed.
  Where Mobutu governed — gold-plated faucets,
  a zoo in the grounds.
  Where Kabila governed. Where Tshisekedi governs.
  The building carries the full weight
  of what that office has been.
  It is still the address of power.
  Power in the DRC is still worth having —
  the country's resources ensure this.
  The building is the proof.
