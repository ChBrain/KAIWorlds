# Piece: The Déby Dynasty
## The soldier who governed for 30 years dies at the front — and his son inherits the state while France attends the funeral.

## Owner
- **Place:** [N'Djamena](culture_chadian_place_ndjamena.md)

## Place
N'Djamena. The presidential palace.
April 20, 2021.
The announcement: Idriss Déby Itno
has died of wounds sustained in battle
against the FACT rebels in northern Chad.
He had won re-election the day before.
Six terms. 30 years.
He went to the frontline himself —
as he had always done,
as he had done his entire career —
and the rebels, who had advanced 400 kilometres
in ten days during the election,
killed him.
By that evening, a transitional military council
had been announced,
headed by his son,
General Mahamat Idriss Déby,
37 years old.
Emmanuel Macron attended the funeral.
He called Idriss Déby
"a courageous friend of France."
He pledged support for the transition.
The constitutional successor —
the National Assembly speaker —
was not consulted.
France called it "exceptional circumstances."

## Load Bearing
Remove the Déby dynasty
and Chad has the governance crisis
of a heavily armed,
multi-ethnic, landlocked country
at the centre of Africa's conflict belt —
without the specific flavour
of neo-patrimonialism
that Idriss Déby perfected
over three decades.
With it: Chad has the *gendarme de l'Afrique* —
the model in which a reliable strongman
deploys his army as a regional security force
in exchange for Western backing
that keeps him in power.
Déby's army fought in Mali for France in 2013.
In the Central African Republic.
In Sudan.
The Chadian army was the frontline force
that France was reluctant to send its own soldiers for.
In exchange: French troops in N'Djamena.
Political cover.
Military equipment.
Financial backing.
When Déby died, France backed his son —
not because Mahamat Déby
demonstrated governing competence,
but because the arrangement continued to be useful.
The piece's most precise dimension:
the dynasty is not primarily about the family.
It is about the function the family serves
for external powers.
Idriss Déby served that function.
France ensured the son could too.
The son won elections in May 2024.
He was the expected winner.
He ran against his own former Prime Minister.
He won with 61%.
The dynasty is now legitimate —
by the standard of an election
that international observers assessed
with limited scrutiny.
Remove the piece:
a country with governance challenges.
With it: the proof that the international system
of democracy promotion
has a specific hierarchy of values —
and stability, for certain patrons,
ranks above democracy.

## Apparent
1990: Idriss Déby seizes power. Zaghawa-backed coup.
1996: first multiparty elections. Déby wins.
Elections of 2001, 2006, 2011, 2016, 2021:
Déby wins each, amid allegations of fraud and opposition boycotts.
2005: term limits removed by referendum.
Oil production begins. Revenue builds.
2006: rebels advance to within kilometres of N'Djamena.
French forces help hold the city.
2013: Chadian troops fight in Mali for Operation Serval.
Déby positions Chad as regional security pivot.
April 11, 2021: Déby wins sixth term.
April 20, 2021: Déby killed at the front.
April 20, 2021: Mahamat Déby installed.
Constitution suspended.
National Assembly dissolved.
April 23, 2021: state funeral.
Macron present.
18-month transition promised.
October 2022: Mahamat extends transition.
Dissolves transitional council. Named transitional president.
Protests. Dozens killed.
2023: national dialogue. New constitution by referendum.
May 2024: Mahamat Déby elected president.
61% of the vote.
Opposition: contested.
International community: accepts result.
The dynasty: now civilian.
The arrangement: continues.
