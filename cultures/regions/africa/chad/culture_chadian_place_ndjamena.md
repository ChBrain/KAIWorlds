# Place: N'Djamena
## Location: Chad

## Owner
- *

## Shown
- The Chari River.
  The border with Cameroon.
  The river that makes N'Djamena possible
  in a country that is largely desert.
  The water that makes the city
  an agricultural and commercial hub
  for the southern Sahel.
  The diplomatic quarter on the riverbank —
  embassies, UN compounds, international NGOs.
  The humanitarian machinery
  that runs on the assumption
  that Chad is permanently in emergency.
  The assumption is correct.
  The machinery is also the economy
  of a significant portion of the city.
- The Marché Central.
  Where north meets south.
  The traders from the Saharan north —
  Tuareg, Arab, Toubou.
  The Sara farmers from the south.
  The cross-border commerce
  with Cameroon, Nigeria, Sudan, CAR.
  N'Djamena is the crossroads
  that geography made it —
  regardless of who governs.
- The Palais de la Démocratie.
  The National Assembly building.
  Which was dissolved in April 2021
  hours after Idriss Déby died.
  Which reconvened when the transition
  required a legislature for legitimacy.
  The building whose constitutional function —
  to provide the acting president
  when the president dies —
  was bypassed by the military
  while France applauded.
  The building still stands.
  The constitutional function it holds
  was suspended once.
  Everyone in N'Djamena knows
  it can be suspended again.
- The Walia Hotel.
  Where international visitors and NGO workers stay.
  Where negotiations happen.
  Where the international system
  that maintains Chad's stability
  conducts its business.
  The hotel that has been here
  through coups, through rebel advances,
  through the Déby funeral.
  Still operating.
  The restaurant still open at dinner.
  The negotiators still meeting in the lobby.
- The Déby family compound.
  Not public. Not photographed.
  The family that now governs
  lives in the city they govern.
  The compound is present
  in the city's political geography
  the way the palace of any dynasty is present.
  Everybody knows where it is.
  No one discusses it.

## Withheld
- Whether the oil revenue from the south
  goes back to the south.
  The Sara people of Chad's oil-producing regions
  have seen the oil money flow north
  for two decades.
  The infrastructure of southern Chad
  does not reflect the revenue produced there.
  This is known by everyone from the south.
  It is not discussed in rooms
  that contain northerners
  and ambitions.
