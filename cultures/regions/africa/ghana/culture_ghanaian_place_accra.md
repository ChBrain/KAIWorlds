# Place: Accra
## Location: Ghana

## Shown
- Independence Square.
  The flagpole. The arch.
  The eternal flame.
  The Black Star Gate.
  Built for the 1957 moment.
  Used now for state funerals, military parades,
  and the occasional concert.
  Still the centre of Ghana's self-understanding.
  The square where everything important
  has been declared.
- Makola Market. Central Accra.
  West Africa's commercial soul
  in compressed form.
  Fabrics. Electronics. Foodstuffs. Money changers.
  The market women — *kayayei* and established traders —
  who run the informal economy
  that runs underneath the formal economy.
  The price of the cedi set here as much as anywhere.
  The political temperature felt here
  before it shows in polling.
  Makola is the country's most accurate thermometer.
- Jamestown. The colonial harbour.
  The lighthouse. The fishing boats.
  The *ga manche* — the traditional chief of the Ga people —
  whose authority predates the colonial state.
  The wrestling arena where Ga youth compete.
  The old colonial architecture repurposed.
  The neighbourhood that has been there
  since before Accra was Accra.
  Still there. Still Ga. Still itself.
- Osu. The restaurant and nightlife district.
  Oxford Street, Accra.
  The city's middle class at leisure.
  The tech startups. The creative economy.
  The Afrobeats that travels from here
  to London to Lagos to New York.
  *Accra to the world.*
  The ambition is not metaphor.
  The music actually travels.
  The coding bootcamps actually produce engineers
  who work in Berlin and Toronto.
  Osu is Ghana's productive projection.
- The courts. Tema motorway.
  The Supreme Court that decided the 2012 election petition.
  The High Court that handles the appeals.
  The system that worked in 2020
  when the loser went to court
  and the court ruled
  and the loser accepted.
  The building is unremarkable.
  What happens inside it is not.

  In Ghana's two-party system,
  the NDC draws on the Volta and Northern regions.
  The NPP draws on Ashanti and Eastern.

  In public political discourse,
  it is officially irrelevant.
  The official irrelevance is real —
  governments actually govern for all regions.
