# Piece: The Independence and the Transfers of Power
## The first — and what was built in the generations since.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Accra](culture_ghanaian_place_accra.md)

## Place
Accra. The polo ground.
Now Independence Square.
The flagpole where the Union Jack came down
and the red, gold and green went up
with the Black Star at its centre.
March 6, 1957, midnight.
Nkrumah in white.
The crowd tens of thousands.
The moment that changed the argument
about whether Africa could govern itself.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 1957 independence
and every subsequent African independence
loses its first and most resonant reference point.
Nkrumah understood this — explicitly, in his speeches —
that Ghana was not just for Ghanaians.
That the moment was continental.
That the Black Star was a star for Africa.
With it: Ghana carries the symbolic weight
of a country that was first,
and then had to build the substance
that justifies the symbol.
The military coups: 1966, 1972, 1979, 1981.
The last coup — Jerry Rawlings — produced
the most consequential thing:
a constitution, elections in 1992,
and a political culture that has since
transferred power peacefully five times.
The load-bearing element is not just 1957.
It is 1957 plus 1992 plus the pattern since —
the accumulated practice of accepting electoral outcomes.
Ghana's piece is not one moment.
It is the long arc from independence
through instability
to the democratic habits
that make it, by West African standards,
an unusual thing: a country that works.
Remove the piece:
a West African state with significant resources.
With it: a country that is the argument
that African democracy can develop
into durable practice —
and the ongoing question of whether
the practice can survive
the pressures that test it.

## Apparent
March 6, 1957: independence.
Kwame Nkrumah, first Prime Minister.
1960: Ghana becomes a republic.
Nkrumah becomes executive president.
1964: single-party state declared.
1966: Nkrumah deposed in military coup
while visiting Hanoi.
CIA later confirmed involvement.
The pan-African project interrupted.
1969–72: democratic government.
1972: Acheampong coup.
1978: Akuffo replaces Acheampong.
1979: Jerry Rawlings — first coup.
Acheampong and Akuffo executed.
Rawlings returns power.
Elections: Hilla Limann wins.
1981: Rawlings — second coup.
He calls it a revolution.
1983: structural adjustment begins.
The IMF era.
Cocoa prices collapse, recovery begins.
1992: new constitution.
Multi-party elections.
Rawlings wins. His party wins.
2000: Rawlings term-limited.
His vice president, Atta Mills, loses.
John Kufuor wins.
Rawlings accepts.
This is the first peaceful democratic transfer of power.
2008: Atta Mills wins a narrow election.
The NDC–NPP alternation begins.
2016: John Mahama loses.
Akufo-Addo wins.
Mahama accepts immediately.
2020: disputed election. Electoral Commission declares Akufo-Addo winner.
Mahama contests in court.
Court rules for Akufo-Addo.
Mahama accepts.
2024: Mahama wins. Akufo-Addo's party loses.
Transfer of power.
Five peaceful transfers since 1992.
The pattern: the most valuable thing Ghana has built.
