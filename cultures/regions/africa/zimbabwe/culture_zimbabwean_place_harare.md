## Shown
- Zimbabwe Ruins Road. Harare CBD.
  The urban grid laid out by colonial planners.
  Now: the offices, the banks, the NGO sector,
  the street vendors who returned
  after Operation Murambatsvina in 2005
  bulldozed them out
  and they rebuilt anyway.
  *Murambatsvina* means: clear out the rubbish.
  700,000 people lost their homes and livelihoods.
  They came back.
  The CBD exists because of the people
  the government tried to remove from it.
- The Zimbabwe National Gallery.
  Where the Shona stone sculpture tradition
  that emerged in the 1950s and 1960s
  — Nicholas Mukomberanwa, Henry Munyaradzi —
  is documented and held.
  The tradition that has no pre-colonial precedent
  but was marketed as ancient African art
  by the colonial gallery
  and became international.
  The sculpture is real.
  The ancient authenticity: largely invented.
  Both things coexist here.
  The gallery knows.
  It is still very good sculpture.
- Mbare.
  The oldest township.
  The oldest bus terminus.
  The market that has run
  since the colonial period
  when black Zimbabweans
  were permitted to live only in designated areas.
  The energy of Mbare
  is the energy of a place
  that has been the city's entry point
  for a century —
  arriving from the rural areas,
  establishing a first foothold,
  sending money home.
  The pattern has not changed.
  The faces have changed.
  The economy that runs through Mbare
  is the city's real economy.
- The University of Zimbabwe.
  The institution that produced
  the country's professional class —
  the doctors, the lawyers, the engineers
  who left for South Africa and the UK
  during the collapse and haven't all returned.
  Still producing graduates.
  Still underfunded.
  The professors who stayed
  earning salaries worth less
  in real terms than they earned in 1990.
  Still teaching.
- Bulawayo (not Harare, but present here).
  900 kilometres southwest.
  Matabeleland's capital.
  Where Gukurahundi is remembered
  in ways Harare has not acknowledged.
  The Ndebele who say: we are Zimbabwean
  and we remember what was done to us
  by the government of Zimbabwe.
  Both things simultaneously.
  Harare holds this at a distance
  it calls national unity.
  Bulawayo holds it as history.

## Withheld
- Gukurahundi.
  In Harare's mixed rooms —
  Shona and Ndebele present together —
  the 1983–1987 massacres are not discussed.
  This is not a secret.
  It is an unresolved wound
  that the room's formal politics
  has decided is not discussable.
  Everyone in the room knows.
  The Ndebele person knows what their family lost.
  The Shona person knows
  that Mugabe ordered it
  and that acknowledging it fully
  complicates the chimurenga legacy they inherited.
  The room holds this.
  In silence.
