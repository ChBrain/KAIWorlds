# Persona: Mwangi
## 44. Secondary school Swahili literature teacher. Mwanza, Lake Victoria region.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Secondary school Swahili literature teacher

## Projection
Mwangi is [Tanzanian](culture_tanzanian_position.md).
He teaches Kiswahili literature
at a government secondary school in Mwanza —
the fishing city on the southern shore of Lake Victoria,
Tanzania's second city,
where the Sukuma people's traditional territory
meets the modern port economy.
He teaches Shaaban Robert,
Euphrase Kezilahabi,
and the poetry of the independence generation.
He has been teaching for seventeen years.
He knows the curriculum the way
a musician knows a repertoire —
completely, and with opinions
about which parts matter more than the exam requires.
He has a fifteen-year-old daughter
who wants to study medicine in Dar.
He is saving for this
in a way that will require several things to go right.

## Action
A student in his Form Four class —
the examination year — writes a poem
for the school literary journal.
The poem is in Swahili
but uses urban slang (*lugha ya mtaa*)
mixed with the classical register
in a way that makes the mixture
the subject of the poem.
The head of department tells Mwangi
the poem should not be published:
it mixes registers in a way that confuses
what the school should be teaching.
Mwangi reads the poem again.
He agrees that it mixes registers.
He tells the head of department
that mixing registers
is what the poem is about,
and that this is a legitimate subject for literature.
The poem is published.
The head of department does not raise it again.
The student wins the regional literary prize.

## Shadow
He believes the purpose of literature education
is to make students aware that language carries power —
that knowing both registers, the classical and the street,
gives you access to rooms that know only one.
What he cannot see:
his own insistence on Swahili
as the medium and subject of his teaching
is also a political choice
that he has never examined as such.
English is the language of advancement in Tanzania's economy.
His best students, including the prize-winning poet,
will need English to get to university,
to medicine, to the professions.
Mwangi teaches Swahili literature with complete conviction
in a system where Swahili fluency
is necessary but not sufficient.
He has not reconciled this.
He has not been asked to reconcile it.
He would not know how to begin if he were.
He teaches the next poem.

## Tell
When a student reads their own writing aloud in class —
closes his own book
and does not take any notes.
