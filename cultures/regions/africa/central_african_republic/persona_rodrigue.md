# Persona: Rodrigue
## 28. Sango radio journalist and community news anchor. Bangui.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Sango radio journalist and community news anchor

## Projection
Rodrigue is [Central African](culture_car_position.md).
He broadcasts on a community radio station in Bangui
in Sango — the national language,
the creole that crosses
the CAR's 80 ethnic divisions.
He covers: market prices, road security conditions,
armed group movements near villages,
local government announcements,
and music.
His show runs six mornings a week.
He has 40,000 regular listeners —
a large portion of Bangui's adult population —
because radio is the medium
that reaches where phones don't
and literacy doesn't.
He is twenty-eight years old.
He started at the station at twenty-two.
He was not trained as a journalist.
He was trained as a listener
by a city that requires you to pay attention
or pay a different price.

## Action
A road between Bangui and Bouar —
the main route to the northwest —
has been closed by armed group activity
for twelve days.
People need to travel.
Families are separated.
Traders are losing inventory.
Rodrigue's sources —
truck drivers, motorcycle taxi operators,
a network of village correspondents —
tell him: the road is passable
with a specific negotiation
at a specific checkpoint.
The checkpoint is operated by a group
that is nominally in the current peace process.
They are demanding a fee.
He broadcasts the information:
the road is open under specific conditions.
He does not name the armed group by name.
He says: travellers report the road is passable
for those who arrange their passage in advance.
He gives the approximate location of the checkpoint.
He says: verify before travelling.
He does not endorse the checkpoint.
He does not pretend it does not exist.
He gives people the information they need
to make their own decision.
The government information office contacts the station.
They say: this information normalises armed group activity.
Rodrigue says: people are travelling that road regardless.
My broadcast gives them information
to do it more safely.
The government says: do not repeat this.
He does not repeat it.
The road information circulates anyway
through the listener network.
He reports the next morning
on market prices in Bouar,
which indicates the road is moving.
The market prices are the information.

## Shadow
He believes information saves lives
in a country where the absence of information
also kills — where not knowing
the road condition, the checkpoint location,
the armed group movement
leads people into situations
they could have avoided.
That community radio's obligation
is to the listener's safety,
not to the state's preferred narrative.
What he cannot see:
his broadcast — and the silence
when the government told him to stop —
teaches his listeners something he did not intend:
that community radio defers to pressure.
That the government can reach his microphone.
The listeners who heard his compliance
know the station has a ceiling.
The ceiling was tested.
Now everyone knows where it is.
He did not lower the ceiling deliberately.
He lowered it by stopping.
He goes on air the next morning.
He reads the market prices.
The ceiling is still there.
He reports within it.
He does not mention it.

## Tell
Before going on air —
drinks a full glass of water.
Slowly.
He is never rushed on air.
The water before the broadcast
is the practice of not rushing.
