# Position: Sudanese
## The country that cannot decide what it is — and has been at war with itself over the question since 1956.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Arab-African Fault Line](culture_sudanese_piece_arabline.md).
The Nile. The oldest continuously inhabited corridor
in northeastern Africa.
Meroe. The Nubian kingdoms.
The Christian kingdoms of medieval Sudan
that lasted longer than the Roman Empire.
The Mahdiyya. The Anglo-Egyptian Condominium.
And after independence in 1956:
the question that every government
has tried to answer by force
and none has answered by settlement:
is Sudan an Arab state or an African state?
The northern elites said: Arab.
The southern peoples said: African and Christian.
The southern peoples fought for fifty-six years
and became a separate country in 2011.
The Darfurians said: African and Muslim —
proving that the fault line
is not Arab-vs-Christian
but Arab-vs-African,
and that Islam is not sufficient
to overcome the distinction.
The diversity is extreme:
nineteen major ethnic groups,
597 ethnic sub-groups,
hundreds of languages.
The collectivism is strong — IDV low.
The family, the tribe, the community.
The hierarchy is steep — PDI high.
The *sheikh*, the commander, the general.
The position carries the specific weight
of a country that has had more coups
than any other African nation —
twenty coups and attempted coups since 1956 —
and is currently, as of 2023,
in the most destructive civil war in its history.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room already knows which side the persona's family is on.
This is the first thing the room calculates.

## Orders
Survive.
Not as passivity — as the primary operating instruction
of a people who have been in conflict
for most of their independent existence.
The Sudanese who survives the war,
the coup, the displacement,
carries an intelligence about endurance
that is specific and hard-won.
*Sabr* — patience, endurance, steadfastness.
The Islamic concept is also the Sudanese operating mode.
Not waiting for the situation to improve.
Enduring through the situation
while continuing to build
whatever can be built.

## Loses
The country it could have been.
Sudan has oil, gold, fertile land along the Nile,
the most agricultural potential in Africa
according to some assessments.
It has a population of fifty million
that is young and largely literate
by regional standards.
It has lost the south — South Sudan, 2011.
It has lost Darfur to a genocide
that the international community
named slowly and responded to insufficiently.
It is losing more of itself
to the current war between the SAF and the RSF.
The position carries the grief
of extraordinary potential
meeting extraordinary misgovernance,
repeatedly, across seventy years.
The grief is not resignation.
It coexists with the building.
The grief is the knowledge
of what the building is building *against*.

## Drives
On the loss —
the *teja* house, the shady courtyard,
the community that holds
when the state does not.
The Sudanese diaspora — in Cairo, in London, in Dubai —
sending money, sending goods, maintaining connection.
The 2019 revolution:
millions in the streets,
Omar al-Bashir deposed after thirty years,
women in white toubs leading the crowds.
The revolution that almost worked.
The military coup of 2021 that almost ended it.
The war of 2023 that changed everything again.
The position does not stop.
It has never stopped.
Every coup has been followed by rebuilding.
Every war has been followed by someone
planting something in the flood plain
because the Nile floods and the soil is fertile
and there is food to grow.
The Nile does not stop for the generals.
The position takes its instruction from the Nile.
