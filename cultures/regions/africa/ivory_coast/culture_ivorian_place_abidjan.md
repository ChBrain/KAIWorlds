## Shown
- Le Plateau. The business district.
  The skyscrapers.
  The Ivorian banks.
  The French multinationals.
  The regional offices of every major company
  that does business in Francophone West Africa.
  Abidjan as the commercial capital
  of the region — not just of Côte d'Ivoire.
  The economy that survived the civil wars
  because the business community
  on both sides of the ceasefire line
  could not afford for it not to survive.
  The pragmatism of capital
  as a stabilising force.
  Not a flattering description.
  An accurate one.
- The lagoon. Ebrié.
  The city is built around it.
  Cocody — the residential district for elites — on one side.
  Yopougon — the popular district, the music district — on another.
  Abobo — the northern immigrant neighbourhood — on the edge.
  The lagoon connecting them all
  and reflecting them back.
  In the civil war, the lagoon was a dividing line.
  After the war, it is still a lagoon.
  The boats cross it.
  The city uses it.
  The division is in the political geography, not the water.
- Yopougon.
  Where Coupé-Décalé was born.
  The dance music that emerged during the civil war —
  excess, joy, performance, survival.
  The music of people who had too much to carry
  and decided to dance with it.
  The maquis — the outdoor bars — running all night.
  The neighbourhood that built the culture
  that the elite on the Plateau
  later claimed as national identity.
  Yopougon knows what it contributed.
  Yopougon is not entirely pleased
  about how the claiming happened.
- Abobo. The northern district.
  Where the Dioula and Mandé communities concentrate.
  Where the civil war's northern fighters
  had their urban stronghold.
  Where the question of who is Ivorian
  was answered in the street
  before it was answered in the courts.
  Still there. Still the northern district.
  Still predominantly Muslim.
  Still the answer to the question
  that *ivoirité* tried to close.
- The Grand Marché. Treichville.
  The commercial heart.
  The cloth vendors. The electronics.
  The Dioula traders who connect Abidjan
  to Bamako and Ouagadougou and Accra.
  The market that never stopped during the civil war.
  The market that never stops.
  The commerce that runs underneath
  every political arrangement.

## Withheld
- Whether you are from the north or the south.
  In Abidjan's professional spaces,
  this is not discussed.
  It is inferred from surname, accent, religion,
  neighbourhood.
  It shapes who gives you business.

  has been officially retired.
  It operates in the informal calculations

  The room shows the business.
