# Piece: Ivoirité and the Two Civil Wars
## The concept that answered the question of who belongs — and the wars it caused.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Abidjan](culture_ivorian_place_abidjan.md)

## Place
Yamoussoukro. The capital.
Houphouët-Boigny's birthplace.
The city he made the political capital in 1983
by fiat, because it was his village.
The Basilica of Our Lady of Peace:
the largest Catholic church in the world,
built in a country 40% Muslim,
by a man who was Catholic in a country
that was at least nominally accepting of this.
Finished 1990, three years before his death.
The Vatican blessed it.
The Pope visited.
The basilica sits mostly empty
in a capital that mostly empty —
business still in Abidjan,
government buildings in Yamoussoukro.
The ambition that outlived its author.
The emptiness as metaphor
for a certain kind of Ivorian power:
large, visible, not fully inhabited.

## Load Bearing
Remove *ivoirité* and the civil wars
and Côte d'Ivoire is a prosperous West African state
navigating a succession crisis
after its founding father's death.
With it: Côte d'Ivoire is the country
that demonstrated how a word
— a concept invented by a politician
to exclude a rival from an election —
can dissolve thirty years
of deliberately constructed national cohesion
in a decade.
Houphouët-Boigny spent thirty-three years
building a country where immigrants
had equal rights, where the north
and south built together,
where the cocoa miracle required
five million immigrant workers
from Burkina Faso and Mali
and they were welcomed because they were needed.
Bédié inherited this
and invented a concept —
who is a *real* Ivorian? —
that made origin the measure of belonging.
The concept excluded Ouattara from running.
It also told five million Burkinabè
that they were not Ivorian,
even if they had been born there.
It told northern Ivorians
that their Muslim identity was suspect.
It told the south that the north
was the problem.
The first civil war was the answer.
The second civil war was the same question
asked again in 2010
when Gbagbo refused to accept
Ouattara's electoral victory.
3,000 dead. 3,000 more.
The concept was retired.
The question is still there.
Remove the piece: a succession crisis.
With it: the proof that
the social cohesion a country takes
thirty years to build
can be dismantled by a political strategy
in five.

## Apparent
1960: independence. Houphouët-Boigny.
The miracle begins.
Cocoa. Coffee. Open borders.
The slogan: *the land belongs to those who work it.*
1970s: world's largest cocoa producer.
GDP growth rates rivalling Asian tigers.
The French stay. More arrive.
The *techniciens français* run schools, hospitals, factories.
Abidjan: *la Paris de l'Afrique de l'Ouest.*
1979: cocoa price crash begins.
The miracle starts to slow.
1993: Houphouët-Boigny dies.
Bédié succeeds.
1994: Bédié invents *ivoirité*.
The citizenship code restricts voting rights.
Ouattara excluded — his parents allegedly Burkinabè.
1995: elections. Ouattara excluded.
Bédié wins.
1999: military coup. Guéï replaces Bédié.
2000: elections. Ouattara excluded again.
Gbagbo wins.
His supporters attack the north.
The first signs of war.
September 2002: military mutiny becomes civil war.
The country splits: south for Gbagbo,
north for the Forces Nouvelles.
A ceasefire line — a *zone de confiance* —
cuts the country in two.
French troops hold the line.
2007: Ouagadougou Accords. Ceasefire.
Disarmament begins.
November 2010: election.
Ouattara wins.
Gbagbo refuses to leave.
The second civil war.
April 2011: French forces help capture Gbagbo.
Ouattara takes office.
Gbagbo goes to the ICC.
2021: ICC acquits Gbagbo.
He returns to Côte d'Ivoire.
The question: still there.
The wars: over.
The word *ivoirité*: officially retired.
The wound: present.
