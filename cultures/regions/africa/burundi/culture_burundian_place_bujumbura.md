## Shown
- Lake Tanganyika. The water begins at the city's edge.
  The deepest lake in Africa, second in the world.
  The fishermen at dawn.
  The hippos further along the shore.
  The swimmers — Bujumbura has a beach culture,
  the anomaly of a lakeside leisure
  inside one of the poorest countries in the world.
  The lake does not care about the politics.
  The lake has been here longer than the country.
- The Central Market. *Marché central.*
  The density of commerce.
  The women selling cassava, beans, sorghum.
  The money changers.
  The mobile phone sellers.
  The informal economy that functions
  while the formal economy staggers.
  The market is where the hill networks
  materialise in the city.
  You know who you can trust here
  because you know which hill they come from.
- The Rohero neighbourhood. The embassies. The NGOs.
  The international presence that arrives after crises
  and departs before resolutions.
  The restaurants that serve the international community
  and the Burundians who work for them.
  The SUVs with UN plates.
  The gap between this neighbourhood
  and the hillside neighbourhoods of Bwiza, Kamenge
  is the gap between Burundi's international face
  and its daily life.
- Independence Square. *Place de l'Indépendance.*
  Where the 2015 protesters gathered.
  Where the police dispersed them.
  Where the coup was announced
  and then reversed
  in the same week.
  The square has seen both the hope and the failure
  of the most recent attempt to change the cycle.
  It is an ordinary square now.
  The ordinary squares of Burundi
  hold more history than their maintenance budgets suggest.
