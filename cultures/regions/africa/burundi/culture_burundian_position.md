# Position: Burundian
## The country that rehearsed Rwanda's genocide — and didn't become Rwanda, but couldn't become anything else either.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Arusha Agreement and the Cycle](culture_burundian_piece_arusha.md).
The hill. *Umusozi.*
Burundi is a country of hills — green, steep, densely farmed.
The hill is the unit of social life,
the unit of identity,
the unit of the violence that recurs.
The Hutu-Tutsi division — the same colonial construction
that produced the Rwandan genocide —
runs through Burundi's entire post-independence history.
But Burundi's version has its own logic:
1972, the *ikiza* — the calamity —
when the Tutsi-dominated army
killed between 100,000 and 300,000 educated Hutus.
Not a genocide recognised by the world.
Mostly forgotten outside Burundi.
The position carries it.
1993: the first freely elected Hutu president, Melchior Ndadaye,
assassinated after four months by Tutsi soldiers.
The civil war that followed: 300,000 dead over twelve years.
The Arusha Agreement of 2000 — negotiated by Mandela —
that produced a power-sharing constitution,
ethnic quotas in government and military,
a decade of relative peace.
2015: President Nkurunziza announces a third term.
The constitution says two. The court says three.
The streets say no. The army tries a coup.
The coup fails. Nkurunziza stays.
The killing resumes, quieter, in the hills.
The collectivism: dense.
The hill. The family. The community.
The Burundian endures together.
The hierarchy: the authority of the hill elder,
the authority of the church —
Burundi is 80% Christian,
the Catholic Church a social architecture —
the authority of the political patron
who provides and expects loyalty.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows which hill, which group, which side.
The room also knows how to be hospitable
while knowing all of this.
Both things operate simultaneously.

## Orders
Endure.
Not as passivity — as the specific Burundian competence
of maintaining life on a hill
in a country that has been at war with itself
for most of its post-independence history,
that is one of the poorest in the world,
that is landlocked, densely populated,
that exports coffee and tea
and people.
The endurance is not resignation.
It is the accumulated knowledge
of how to keep the family fed,
the children in school,
the hill productive,
when the state alternates
between inadequate and actively dangerous.

## Loses
The generation that almost made it.
The Arusha decade — 2005 to 2015 —
was the closest Burundi came
to the future Ndadaye died trying to build.
The economy grew.
The schools filled.
The power sharing held — imperfectly, but held.
The position had begun to believe
that the cycle could be broken.
2015 broke it.
200,000 fled.
The gains reversed.
The position carries the specific grief
of the generation that built something
and watched it dismantled
not by an outside power
but by the man the system had elected.

## Drives
On the loss —
the church, the hill, the savings group.
The Burundian informal economy
built on trust networks, on *tontines* —
rotating credit associations —
on the knowledge of who is reliable
that runs through the hill community.
The diaspora — smaller than some,
but the remittances matter.
The coffee: Burundian single-origin
now prized by specialty roasters.
The hill farmers who grew it
during the war years
and the peace years
and the war years again
do not know this.
But they kept the trees.
The trees are still producing.
The position continues from what remains.
