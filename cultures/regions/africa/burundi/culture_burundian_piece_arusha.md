# Piece: The Arusha Agreement and the Cycle
## The accord that stopped one war — and the third term that started the next.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Bujumbura](culture_burundian_place_bujumbura.md)

## Place
Bujumbura. The shore of Lake Tanganyika.
The longest freshwater lake in the world by depth.
The blue water at the bottom of the hills.
The city that looks west across the water
toward Congo
and knows exactly what that means.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Arusha Agreement
and Burundi is a Great Lakes country
moving from one ethnic civil war to the next
without the architecture of managed coexistence.
With it: Burundi is the country
that built ethnic quotas into its constitution —
60% Hutu, 40% Tutsi in government and army —
as the formal acknowledgment
that the state had been weaponised along ethnic lines
and required structural correction.
The Agreement is load-bearing
because it produced a decade of actual function:
elections held, power transferred,
the army integrated,
the killing reduced to a level
the international community filed under manageable.
The third term is load-bearing as the counter-piece:
August 2015, Nkurunziza declares himself re-elected.
The opposition boycotts.
The street protests are suppressed.
The coup attempt fails.
The killing continues in the hills and the neighbourhoods —
not the mass organised violence of 1972 or 1993,
but the targeted, the disappeared,
the bodies in the morning.
The UN Human Rights Council documents it.
Burundi withdraws from the International Criminal Court.
Nkurunziza dies of cardiac arrest in 2020 —
officially. The timing: days after his successor is inaugurated.
The position does not know what to do with this information.
It files it alongside the other things
it does not know what to do with.
Remove both pieces: a small landlocked African country
navigating poverty and ethnic tension.
With them: the specific Burundian question —
whether an agreement built to stop a cycle
can hold against a leader
who decides his tenure supersedes the agreement.
The answer arrived in 2015.
The position is still living inside it.

## Apparent
1962: independence from Belgian trusteeship.
1965: coup. Tutsi army consolidates power.
1972: the *ikiza*. 100,000–300,000 Hutus killed.
1988: Ntega and Marangara massacres.
1993: Ndadaye elected — first Hutu president.
October 1993: Ndadaye assassinated.
Civil war begins. 300,000 dead over twelve years.
1994: the Rwandan genocide. Regional destabilisation.
1996: Buyoya coup.
2000: the Arusha Peace and Reconciliation Agreement.
Nelson Mandela as chief mediator.
Ethnic power-sharing mandated by constitution.
2005: elections under new constitution.
Pierre Nkurunziza elected.
2010: Nkurunziza re-elected.
2015: Nkurunziza announces third term.
Constitutional court rules it legal — one judge flees the country.
April 2015: coup attempt by General Godefroid Niyombare.
Fails within days.
200,000 flee. UN estimates 1,200 killed 2015–2017.
Burundi withdraws from ICC.
UN commission denied entry.
2018: referendum extends presidential terms.
May 2020: Evariste Ndayishimiye elected.
June 2020: Nkurunziza dies. Cardiac arrest, officially.
Ndayishimiye opens Burundi cautiously to international engagement.
The cycle is paused.
It has been paused before.
