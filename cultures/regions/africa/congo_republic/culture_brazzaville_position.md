# Position: Congolese (Brazzaville)
## The small Congo — which has spent most of its independence governed by one man.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Sassou Nguesso Dynasty](culture_brazzaville_piece_sassou.md).
Brazzaville — named for the French explorer
who signed a treaty with the Teke king in 1880
and gave France the right bank of the Congo.
The oldest city in Central Africa by colonial founding.
The capital of French Equatorial Africa.
The city where de Gaulle rallied Free France in 1940.
The Congo River between Brazzaville and Kinshasa —
the two capitals facing each other.
Population: six million.
The Bakongo in the south — 48% of the population.
The M'Bochi in the north — 12%.
The oil fields offshore in the Atlantic.
Oil: 80% of exports.
The collectivism: high — the clan, the family, the region.
The hierarchy: steep in formal structures,
negotiated in daily life.
The north-south axis
that has run through Congolese politics
since independence —
the M'Bochi north that produced Sassou Nguesso,
the Bakongo south that produced the opposition.

## Orders
*Mbote* — greeting, goodwill, mutual acknowledgement.
The position navigates through relationship.
The patron-client network —
who knows whom,
who owes whom,
who can be called —
is the operative infrastructure
of Congolese Brazzaville life.
Formal institutions exist.
Relationships are more reliable.

## Loses
The democratic transition.
1990: the Berlin Wall falls.
Sassou Nguesso, a Marxist-Leninist,
introduces multiparty politics.
1992: free election. Pascal Lissouba wins.
Sassou Nguesso loses.
He accepts — briefly.
1993–1994: first civil war.
1997: Sassou Nguesso returns with Angolan troops.
Lissouba flees.
8,000–10,000 dead.
800,000 displaced.
The democracy lasted five years.
The position carries
the specific shape of a loss
that was not a defeat in battle
but a reversal of what had been won —
the moment that told everyone in Brazzaville
that the transition was not irreversible.

## Drives
On the loss —
the river.
The markets.
The music — *ndombolo* crossed from Kinshasa.
The Congolese religious movements —
the Kimbanguists, the evangelicals —
that provide continuity the state does not.
The informal economy that has survived
civil war, oil booms, and oil busts.
The diaspora in France
sending remittances.
The position builds in the space
Sassou Nguesso does not occupy.
That space has always existed.
It is smaller than it should be.
It persists.
