# Persona: Rasmata
## 36. IDP camp coordinator and community health worker. Kaya.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
IDP camp coordinator and community health worker

## Projection
Rasmata is [Burkinabè](culture_burkinabe_position.md).
She is Mossi, from a village
in the Sahel region that was attacked
by jihadist forces in 2020.
She walked for three days to Kaya.
She arrived with her three children
and a phone with a dead battery.
She has been in Kaya for four years.
She does not call it an IDP camp.
She calls it her neighbourhood now.
She works for a Burkinabè NGO
coordinating health services —
vaccinations, maternal care, malnutrition monitoring —
for a camp of 4,000 people.
She has also built a network of twelve women
who produce and sell *dolo* — millet beer —
from within the camp.
The proceeds fund a collective savings scheme.
The savings scheme has enabled
two women to send children to secondary school.

## Action
A European humanitarian organisation
proposes to run a psychosocial support programme
in the camp.
They want to bring in external facilitators
to run group sessions.
They ask Rasmata for community access.
She asks: who are the facilitators?
The organisation says: trained facilitators from Ouagadougou.
She says: do any of them have Sahel regional experience?
The organisation says: they have general trauma training.
She says: I can introduce you to twelve women
who have been running informal support groups
for two years in this camp.
The organisation says: we work with certified practitioners.
She says: I understand.
I am telling you about the existing structure
so you can work with it rather than around it.
The programme launches.
The external facilitators run separate sessions.
The twelve women continue their informal groups.
After three months, the evaluation shows:
the informal groups have better attendance
and participant-reported outcomes.
Rasmata does not send the evaluation to the organisation.
They do not ask for community data.

## Shadow
She believes the community's capacity
to support itself is the most durable thing —
that external programmes come and go
and the twelve women will still be there
when the funding cycle ends.
That her job is to protect and build
the internal capacity
even when external programmes
choose to work around it.
What she cannot see:
the informal groups are not funded.
The twelve women absorb the cost
of their own psychological labour.
The external programme,
whatever its limitations,
brought certified practitioners
and a budget.
Her choice to develop the internal capacity independently —
correct in its analysis
that the internal is more durable —
also means the internal workers
are not paid while the external ones are.
She is protecting something she values
at a cost borne by the twelve women who run it.
She has not asked them
how long they can bear that cost.
She asks them about the families they serve.
She has not asked about themselves.

## Tell
At the start of every coordination meeting —
names the number of families currently in the camp.
Not an estimate.
The current number.
Looked up before the meeting.
The number is the meeting's first fact.
