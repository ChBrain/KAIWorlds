## Shown
- Avenue Habib Bourguiba. The main boulevard.
  French colonial architecture, cafés, the Palmarium hotel,
  the Municipal Theatre.
  Where the protesters marched in January 2011.
  Where the crowds surged when Ben Ali fled.
  The avenue named for the man who built the country.
  The man who also imprisoned his opponents,
  who banned the hijab, who ruled for thirty-one years.
  The avenue holds both.
  Tunisians walk it knowing both.
- The Zitouna Mosque. In the medina.
  The oldest university in the world —
  founded 737 AD, predating Bologna by three centuries.
  Where Islamic scholarship was produced
  for twelve centuries.
  Where the tradition of Tunisian intellectual life
  that Bourguiba built the secular state on top of
  runs underneath the secular state.
  Both are present on the same streets.
  The secular and the religious in Tunisia
  are not in complete opposition.
  They are in permanent negotiation.
- The UGTT headquarters. Rue Chaâbane.
  The union that has been organising Tunisian workers
  since 1944, before independence.
  The union that called general strikes against French rule.
  That called general strikes against Ben Ali.
  That convened the National Dialogue Quartet in 2013.
  That won the Nobel Peace Prize.
  The most powerful civil society organisation
  in the Arab world.
  Still here.
  Still arguing.
- The Bardo National Museum.
  The finest collection of Roman mosaics in the world.
  Built in a Husseinid palace.
  Site of the 2015 terrorist attack — 22 dead, mostly tourists.
  The country responded: the museum stayed open.
  Soldiers at the entrance, yes.
  But open.
  Tunisians' relationship to terrorism has been:
  we know what this is, we have been here before,
  we are not going to close.
- Sidi Bouzid. Not Tunis — but present in Tunis.
  The market town five hours south
  where Bouazizi burned.
  Where the revolution started.
  Where, in 2011, everything changed.
  Sidi Bouzid remains one of Tunisia's most deprived regions.
  The revolution changed the government.
  It did not change Sidi Bouzid.
  This gap — between the democratic achievement
  and the economic conditions
  that produced the revolution —
  is the unresolved thing at the centre of the country.

## Withheld
- The fatigue.
  Tunisians have been politically engaged
  at intensity since 2011.
  Fourteen years of constitutions,
  coalitions, crises, dialogues,
  elections, coups-by-decree.
  The civil society infrastructure is intact.
  The people inside it are tired.
  The fatigue does not show in the argument.
  The argument continues.
  The fatigue is underneath,
  in the bones of people
  who have been paying attention for a very long time.
