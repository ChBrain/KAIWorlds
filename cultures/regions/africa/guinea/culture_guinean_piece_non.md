# Piece: The Non of 1958
## The most principled act of decolonisation in Francophone Africa — and its terrible aftermath.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Conakry](culture_guinean_piece_non.md)

## Place
Conakry. The Place du 8 Novembre.
Now: the Place des Martyrs.
The square where Touré spoke.
Where de Gaulle was received.
Where the crowd heard the speech
that changed West African history.
The square renamed after the stadium massacre of 2009.
The massacre of those demanding democracy
on the anniversary of the Non.
The same place. Different names.
Different bodies. The same demand:
freedom.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Non
and Guinea joins the French Community
like every other West African territory.
It receives French support.
It builds institutions slowly.
It becomes, probably,
a country like Senegal or Côte d'Ivoire —
complex, partially free,
bound to France in ways that benefit France
more than Guinea.
With it: Guinea receives nothing from France —
no civil servants, no medicine, no records —
and must build from nothing.
The premise of the Non is correct:
self-governance with difficulty
is preferable to comfortable subordination.
The outcome is devastating:
Touré uses the chaos of the first years
to consolidate absolute power.
The Cold War brings Soviet support
that Touré accepts and uses
to entrench one-party rule.
Camp Boiro fills with political prisoners.
The best and most educated Guineans leave.
The country that chose freedom
becomes one of Francophone Africa's
most repressive states.
The Non is load-bearing
in both directions simultaneously:
it is the founding act of principle
that every subsequent Guinean political actor
must account for —
and it is the precedent
for how a principled act
can be hijacked by the person who performed it.
Remove the piece:
a French Community member-state, constrained but functional.
With it: a country that chose correctly in 1958
and suffered for it —
and carries both the honour and the grief
of that specific combination.

## Apparent
August 25, 1958: de Gaulle visits Conakry.
He advocates for yes.
Sékou Touré responds publicly:
*We prefer poverty in freedom to riches in slavery.*
September 28, 1958: the referendum.
Guinea votes 95% for independence.
Every other French West African territory votes yes to the Community.
October 2, 1958: independence.
The French leave.
They take what they can, destroy what they can.
The telephones: ripped out.
The hospital medicines: removed.
The school records: destroyed.
The French community of civil servants: withdrawn.
Two high schools. No university.
Touré faces the void.
He accepts Soviet aid.
He accepts Chinese aid.
He accepts Ghanaian solidarity —
Nkrumah offers a loan of £10 million.
The Cold War arrives in Conakry.
1960: the Guinea-Ghana union. Symbolic.
Never effectively operative.
The 1960s: single party, nationalisation.
The economy does not develop.
The Guinean diaspora grows —
in Senegal, in Côte d'Ivoire, in France.
The *intellectuels* leave.
Those who stay and dissent:
Camp Boiro.
1971: the *complot* paranoia.
Mass arrests.
Thousands detained.
Hundreds die of torture and starvation.
1977: the market women revolt.
Women protest economic conditions.
They attack the offices of the PDG.
Touré is forced to soften.
The first crack.
March 26, 1984: Touré dies during heart surgery in Cleveland.
Seven days later: military coup.
Lansana Conté.
The PDG dissolved.
Political prisoners released.
200,000 exiles invited to return.
2008: Conté dies.
Military coup: Moussa Dadis Camara.
September 28, 2009: security forces attack
a pro-democracy rally at a Conakry stadium.
157 dead. Hundreds wounded. Rapes documented.
The date: the 51st anniversary of the Non.
The demand of those killed: democracy.
The irony: unbearable.
2010: first democratic elections since 1958.
The process: slow, credible, contested.
2024: democratic transfer of power confirmed.
The Non was right.
The cost was enormous.
Both are Guinea.
