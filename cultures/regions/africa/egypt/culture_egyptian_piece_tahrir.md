# Piece: Tahrir and What Came After
## Eighteen days that changed everything — and what happened to the change.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Cairo](culture_egyptian_place_cairo.md)

## Place
Tahrir Square. Downtown Cairo.
*Tahrir* means liberation.
The name was given in 1953 after the revolution
that ended the monarchy.
By 2011 most Egyptians had stopped thinking about the name.
Then for eighteen days they thought about nothing else.

## Load Bearing
Remove Tahrir 2011 and the Egyptian position
loses its most recent test of the limit.
The depth was always there.
The endurance was always there.
The patience was always there.
What 2011 added — and what cannot be removed —
is the knowledge that the limit exists,
that the patience is not infinite,
that under sufficient pressure
the Egyptian street will fill
and the impossible will happen.
Mubarak fell.
The knowledge that Mubarak fell
cannot be unfiled.
The subsequent suppression cannot erase the knowledge.
What came after — the Muslim Brotherhood's year in power,
the military coup of 2013, the Sisi government,
the renovation of Tahrir to cover the memory —
all of it runs on top of the knowledge
that eighteen days happened
and produced the impossible.
Remove the piece: a very old country
with very high patience and a steep hierarchy.
With it: a very old country with very high patience,
a steep hierarchy, and the specific knowledge
that the patience has a breaking point
and that breaking point was reached within living memory.
The obelisk of Ramses II now stands in Tahrir.
The obelisk does not cancel the eighteen days.
It marks the state's awareness that the eighteen days occurred.

## Apparent
Tahrir Square. January 25, 2011.
The first day. Smaller than expected.
January 28: the Day of Rage.
The police deploy full force.
The protesters hold.
That night: police vehicles burning.
The curfew ignored.
February 1: one million people in Tahrir.
The largest protest in Egyptian history.
February 11: Hosni Mubarak resigns
after thirty years in power.
The square erupts.
Women and men cleaning the square the next morning.
Removing the rubble. Relaying the cobblestones.
The Egyptian instinct: after the struggle, the maintenance.
2012: Mohamed Morsi elected.
The Muslim Brotherhood in the presidency.
2013: June 30 protests. Thirty million Egyptians in the streets.
July 3: General Abdel Fattah al-Sisi removes Morsi.
The same crowds that filled Tahrir for 2011
fill it again for 2013.
The military returns. Different name, same institution.
August 14, 2013: Rabaa massacre.
The sit-in of Morsi supporters dispersed.
Hundreds killed.
Human Rights Watch: one of the worst mass killings
of demonstrators in modern history.
2014: Sisi elected president.
2019–2020: Tahrir Square renovated.
Ancient obelisk erected at the centre.
Ram-headed sphinxes from Karnak placed around it.
The 2011 revolution replaced, architecturally,
with Pharaonic imagery.
The square is now under permanent security presence.
Mass gatherings require government authorisation.
The name Tahrir remains.
The liberation: pending.
