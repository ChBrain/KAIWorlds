## Shown
- The Rova of Antananarivo.
  The royal palace complex on the highest hill.
  Burned in 1995 — the fire's origin still debated.
  The ruins visible from most of the city.
  The rebuilding in progress.
  The Merina kingdom's authority
  was expressed from this hill.
  The French colonial administration
  operated from this hill.
  The independent republic operates from this hill.
  The hill remains.
  The institution on it changes.
  The Malagasy relationship to the ancestors
  who were crowned here
  does not change.
- The Avenue de l'Indépendance.
  The main artery of the lower town.
  Colonial French architecture.
  The busiest market in the city —
  the Zoma, moved to a covered structure
  but still the city's commercial heartbeat.
  Rice. Vanilla. Zebu meat.
  The *lamba* — the woven garment,
  used for daily clothing and for wrapping the dead.
  The same cloth serves the living and the ancestors.
  The market doesn't separate them.
- The highland plateau.
  The terraced rice paddies
  that descend from Antananarivo
  into the surrounding valleys.
  The Merina rice culture —
  wet rice cultivation
  brought from Maritime Southeast Asia
  two thousand years ago.
  The paddies are maintained.
  The rice is the staple.
  The agricultural knowledge is in the landscape.
  The landscape looks Austronesian.
  It is in the middle of the African continent.
  This is the everyday surrealism of Madagascar.
- Tsimbazaza Zoo and Botanical Garden.
  The lemurs.
  The baobabs.
  The 90% endemism of the island's species.
  The biodiversity that is
  one of the strongest arguments
  for Madagascar's global importance.
  The deforestation — 90% of original forest gone.
  The conservation work
  running simultaneously with the deforestation.
  Both things happening at once.
  The Malagasy position
  navigates between them.
- The highland cemeteries.
  The family tombs —
  more substantial than many family homes.
  Built to last longer than the living.
  The site of the *famadihana*.
  The place where the ancestors are present.
  The most architecturally significant
  thing a family builds
  is the tomb.
  The priorities are clear.
  The ancestors are permanent.
  The living are temporary.
