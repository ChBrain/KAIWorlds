# Persona: Emilia
## 32. Bubi community health worker and environmental rights documenter. Bioko island, southern villages.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Bubi community health worker and environmental rights documenter

## Projection
Emilia is [Equatoguinean](culture_equatoguinean_position.md).
She is Bubi —
indigenous to Bioko,
the community that preceded
the state that now governs the island.
She works as a community health worker
in the southern villages of Bioko,
funded by a Catholic mission health programme.
She also, carefully,
documents environmental damage
from oil infrastructure on the island:
soil contamination near pipelines,
fish stock changes, water quality.
She sends the documentation
to an NGO in Gabon
that compiles the regional reports.
Her name does not appear on the reports.
She uses an identifier.
She has been doing this for five years.
She has not been arrested.
She is careful.

## Action
A government contractor
arrives in the village
to assess land for a new pipeline route.
The proposed route crosses
the traditional land of two Bubi clans —
land that is not formally titled
but has been in continuous use
for agriculture and ceremony.
The contractor presents:
compensation will be paid.
The amount: stated.
The clans have not been consulted
before the route was decided.
The compensation amount
is below market equivalent
for the agricultural use disrupted.
Emilia documents the arrival,
the proposed route,
the stated compensation figure.
She contacts the Gabon NGO.
The NGO connects her
with a Cameroon-based legal organisation
that has experience
with pipeline land rights in Central Africa.
The legal organisation prepares
a written objection
on behalf of the clans —
using the NGO as the named party,
not the clans directly,
to reduce the risk of retaliation.
The objection is filed with
the government's environmental office.
The route is not changed.
The compensation is increased by 15%.
The clans accept.
She documents the outcome.
The route proceeds.
The 15% is real.
The land is disrupted.
She sends the file to Gabon.

## Shadow
She believes the documentation —
the ongoing record of environmental decisions
on Bioko,
their effects on Bubi communities,
the gap between the state's stated commitments
and what it does —
is the foundation of accountability
that will matter after the current regime.
That keeping the record
is the work available in this context.
What she cannot see:
she has been doing this for five years
at significant personal risk.
The record she is building
serves a future accountability
she will not live to see
if the regime lasts.
The immediate cost is hers.
The future benefit —
if it arrives — will be diffuse.
She is subsidising
the community's future with her present.
She is not calculating it this way.
She is calculating:
this is what can be done.
I will do it.
That calculation is correct.
It is also absorbing
a cost no one can properly account for.
She prepares the next report.
She goes to the next village clinic.
The oil platform is visible from the shore.

## Tell
When explaining an environmental change
to a village elder —
asks first what the elder remembers
about that forest, that water, that shoreline.
What it looked like before.
The before is not nostalgia.
It is the baseline.
Without the baseline
the damage is not measurable.
The elder's memory is the measurement.
