## Shown
- Johannesburg. The city that gold built.
  The largest city in Africa not on a river or coast —
  built entirely because someone found gold in 1886.
  Sandton: the financial district.
  The glass towers where the continent's largest stock exchange operates.
  Soweto, twenty minutes south:
  where Mandela and Tutu grew up on the same street.
  Orlando Towers. The cooling towers of the old power station,
  now painted with murals.
  Vilakazi Street: the only street in the world
  to have housed two Nobel Peace Prize laureates.
  The two facts — Sandton and Soweto —
  separated by a highway and thirty years of democracy
  that have not closed the distance.
- Constitution Hill, Johannesburg.
  The old Fort Prison, Number Four.
  Mahatma Gandhi was held here. Mandela was held here.
  The Constitutional Court built above the old cells.
  The cells visible through glass floors.
  The court above them: the highest court in the land.
  The architecture says: we remember what this was.
  We built something else on top of it.
  We have not hidden what is underneath.
- Robben Island. Visible from Cape Town.
  The ferry takes thirty minutes.
  Former prisoners serve as guides.
  Mandela's cell. The quarry where prisoners broke limestone
  and Mandela's eyes were permanently damaged by the glare.
  The island where the leaders of the liberation
  taught each other, debated, wrote.
  The island that produced the government of 1994.
- Table Mountain. Above Cape Town.
  The flat-topped mountain visible from the sea.
  The cable car. The fynbos.
  Below it: the Bo-Kaap with its coloured houses,
  the Cape Malay community,
  the District Six Museum where the forced removals are documented.
  The mountain does not change.
  The city below it changes constantly.
  The mountain witnesses both.
- The load-shedding schedule.
  Not a monument. A daily condition.
  Which hours the electricity will be off.
  Every South African knows the schedule.
  Every South African has adapted to it.
  The generators. The inverters. The candles.
  The adaptation is the current condition of ubuntu
  applied to the state's failure:
  *we will manage what you will not fix*.

## Withheld
- The conversation about land.
  It is not withheld from South Africans — it is everywhere,
  in parliament, in the newspapers, on the radio.
  But it is withheld from comfortable rooms.
  The question of who the land belongs to
  — who paid for it with labour and dispossession
  and who holds the title deed —
  is the question the TRC did not answer.
  It is still open.
  Every South African knows it is still open.
  Most rooms conduct their business
  without naming it directly.
