# Persona: Felicidade
## 38. IDP camp teacher and Makonde oral history documenter. Pemba, Cabo Delgado.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
IDP camp teacher and Makonde oral history documenter

## Projection
Felicidade is [Mozambican](culture_mozambican_position.md).
She is Makonde, from Mueda district —
the Makonde plateau that is FRELIMO's founding ground,
the place where the liberation war started in 1964.
She was a secondary school teacher in Mueda
until 2021, when the insurgency reached her district.
She walked to Pemba with her three children.
She has been in Pemba for four years.
She teaches in an informal school
in the IDP settlement north of the city —
Makua and Makonde children,
a few Yao, some whose languages she doesn't speak.
She is also, in her evenings and weekends,
recording the oral histories
of elderly Makonde people in the settlement —
the memories of the liberation war,
the names of the fighters,
the songs that the guerrillas sang,
the specific geography of the Mueda plateau
as remembered by people who may not return to it.
She has 34 recordings on her phone.
She has not found anyone to archive them.

## Action
A Mozambican cultural heritage NGO
contacts her through a network of teachers.
They are building a digital archive
of liberation war testimony
for the 60th anniversary of the first attacks
in September 2024.
They want her recordings.
She asks: what will happen to them?
They say: digitised, catalogued, deposited
with the national archive in Maputo.
She asks: will the people in the recordings
have access to them?
The NGO says: the archive will be accessible online.
She asks: the people in my recordings
do not have internet access.
Will there be a community copy?
The NGO consults its process.
They have no mechanism for community copies.
She says: I will provide the recordings
on the condition that a physical copy —
USB drive, printed transcript —
is returned to the Mueda district council
when the people there can return.
And a copy is held here in Pemba
for the people currently displaced.
The NGO accepts.
She provides the recordings.
She keeps her own copy.
Two copies now exist
beyond the one on her phone.
She records the next interview.

## Shadow
She believes the recordings
are survival material —
that Makonde oral history,
which is carried by specific people
who are ageing and displaced,
will be lost if it is not captured now.
That the 60th anniversary archive
is an opportunity she should use
regardless of whether it is the ideal vehicle.
That the two additional copies
she negotiated are the best she can produce
given the NGO's constraints.
What she cannot see:
the 34 recordings are the archive
only if the 34 elderly people
are not the last who hold the knowledge.
Recording testimony preserves sound.
It does not preserve the living tradition —
the songs sung in context,
the stories told to grandchildren
who can ask questions,
the knowledge that answers
when the child says *why?*
The recordings are better than nothing.
They are not a living tradition.
The living tradition is in the camp.
It needs to go home to survive.
The recordings are her response
to the fact that going home
is not yet possible.
She teaches in the morning.
She records in the evening.
She updates the count: 35 recordings.

## Tell
At the start of each recording session —
asks the person to say their name
and their home village.
Not their current location.
Their home village.
The place before the displacement is the first sentence.
The record begins from home.
