# Piece: The Herero and Nama Genocide and the SWAPO Liberation
## The first genocide of the 20th century — and the liberation narrative that was built over it without resolving it.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Windhoek](culture_namibian_place_windhoek.md)

## Place
Windhoek. The Alte Feste —
the old German fort built in 1890.
Now a national museum.
Its collection: the German colonial period.
Its location: the capital of independent Namibia.
The genocide is documented inside
a building that the German colonial military built
to control the people it later exterminated.
The irony is present.
Nobody discusses it.
Everyone moves through the museum anyway.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 1904–1908 genocide
and Namibia has the South African occupation,
the SWAPO liberation struggle from 1966,
independence in 1990 —
the land dispossession is still there
because South African settlers
were also deeply embedded.
With it: Namibia has an additional layer —
the *first genocide of the 20th century*,
conducted by General Lothar von Trotha
with the Vernichtungsbefehl,
the extermination order:
*Every Herero, with or without weapons,
with or without cattle,
will be shot.*
80% of the Herero population killed.
Half the Nama population killed.
The survivors driven into the Omaheke Desert.
Their cattle taken.
Their land taken.
German settlers moved onto it.
When the South Africans took over in 1915
and then the Boer settlers arrived,
the land that had been Herero and Nama
remained occupied by white farmers.
SWAPO's liberation narrative begins in 1960 —
with the Ovambo resistance
to South African apartheid rule.
This narrative is real and accurate.
It does not include the Herero and Nama genocide
as a central element —
because SWAPO's constituency is Ovambo,
and the Ovambo were not the genocide's primary victims.
After independence, land reform
was designed around SWAPO's political needs:
the willing seller, willing buyer policy
meant that Herero and Nama ancestral land
remained with the white farmers whose families
had occupied it since the genocide.
2021: Germany acknowledges the genocide.
Offers €1.1 billion over 30 years.
Calls it development aid, not reparations.
Denies legal liability.
The Herero and Nama communities:
not adequately consulted in the negotiations.
They rejected the settlement.
The German and Namibian governments
proceeded anyway.
Remove the piece:
a post-colonial state with land inequality.
With it: a country where the land inequality
has a specific address —
the genocide of 1904–1908 —
and where the post-independence government
has not resolved it,
partly because resolving it fully
would require acknowledging
that the liberation narrative
was built over a different people's unresolved wound.

## Apparent
1884: Germany claims South West Africa.
First settlers arrive.
1890s: land appropriation begins. Herero and Nama increasingly dispossessed.
1904: Herero uprising.
Herero kill 123 German settlers and soldiers.
General Lothar von Trotha arrives.
August 1904: Battle of Waterberg.
Herero defeated.
Extermination order issued.
Herero driven into Omaheke Desert.
Survivors die of thirst, starvation, or are shot.
1905: Nama uprising. Same treatment follows.
1904–1908: 40,000–80,000 Herero killed. 10,000 Nama killed.
Concentration camps established.
Prisoners used as forced labour.
German scientists take skulls for racial science.
1908: Herero and Nama effectively destroyed as political entities.
Their land: occupied by German settlers.
1915: South Africa occupies the territory in WWI.
Apartheid policies applied from the 1940s.
Bantustans established.
SWAPO founded: 1960.
1966: armed struggle begins.
Sam Nujoma leads from exile.
1988: UN Resolution 435 implemented.
South Africa agrees to withdraw.
March 21, 1990: independence.
Nujoma first president.
Post-independence land reform: slow.
Willing-seller/willing-buyer: most Herero and Nama land stays with white farmers.
2006: first German "regret" statement.
Not an apology.
2021: €1.1 billion settlement announced.
Herero and Nama communities reject it.
Consultations called inadequate.
German and Namibian governments proceed.
The land: still in question.
Sam Nujoma died February 8, 2025. Aged 95.
Netumbo Nandi-Ndaitwah elected president November 2024.
The genocide's legacy is present.
The settlement is contested.
The reckoning is incomplete.
