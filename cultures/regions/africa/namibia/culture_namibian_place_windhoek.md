## Shown
- Independence Memorial Museum.
  Built by North Korean construction workers.
  Designed in socialist realist style.
  A six-metre statue of Sam Nujoma
  outside the entrance.
  Inside: the liberation struggle from 1960.
  The pre-1960 history — the genocide,
  the German colonial period —
  in the Alte Feste museum nearby,
  separately administered.
  Two museums. Two histories.
  They do not share a building.
  The independence museum begins in 1960.
  The genocide museum is in the old fort.
  The architectural arrangement
  is also the political arrangement.
- The Alte Feste.
  The 1890 German fort.
  Now: the State Museum of Namibia's Historical Gallery.
  The genocide documented inside.
  The skulls that were taken to Germany
  for racial science experiments
  were returned in 2011 — partly.
  A ceremony was held.
  More repatriations followed.
  The fort contains the documentation
  of what was done here.
  It is attended mostly by tourists
  and researchers.
  Windhoek's daily life moves around it.
- The Katutura township.
  Established 1959.
  *Katutura* means "the place where people do not want to live."
  The apartheid government forcibly relocated Windhoek's black residents here.
  Now: a functional, densely populated neighbourhood.
  The name remained.
  The city grew around it.
  Katutura's markets, shebeens, music, and food
  are where Windhoek's actual cultural life happens —
  not the German quarter,
  not the post-independence government district.
  The name that was a forced dislocation
  is now a neighbourhood that claimed itself.
- The German quarter. Klein-Windhoek.
  The Lutheran church. The Turnhalle building —
  where the independence negotiations happened.
  The cafes with German names and German cakes.
  The 0.4% German community
  visible in the built environment far beyond their numbers.
  Lüderitz and Swakopmund even more so.
  The colonial architecture
  is the country's most visible layer
  and represents its smallest community.
  Namibia moves through it
  without particularly celebrating or mourning it.
  It is there.
- The Namib dunes at Sossusvlei.
  400 kilometres south.
  Not technically Windhoek —
  but present in every Namibian's sense of the country.
  The oldest desert.
  The red dunes at dawn.
  The silence.
  The emptiness that is also abundance —
  the desert ecosystems,
  the oryx, the gemsbok.
  The land that was taken from the Herero and Nama
  includes land like this.
  The beauty and the dispossession
  occupy the same ground.
