# Persona: Tomas N
## 30. San community liaison and indigenous rights legal trainee. Nyae Nyae, Otjozondjupa region.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
San community liaison and indigenous rights legal trainee

## Projection
Tomas N. is [Namibian](culture_namibian_position.md).
He is Ju/'hoansi San —
one of the oldest surviving hunter-gatherer communities,
from the Nyae Nyae conservancy
in northeastern Namibia.
He is studying law by distance learning
through the University of Namibia.
He also works as a community liaison
between the Ju/'hoansi San community
and the Nyae Nyae Conservancy management,
the NGOs that operate in the area,
and occasionally the government.
His community was displaced from their land
during the South African occupation
and returned to Nyae Nyae after independence.
They hold communal land rights
under the communal conservancy system.
The conservancy generates tourism revenue.
The community receives a share.
The share is less than the community
believes it should be.
He is learning to read the contracts.

## Action
The Nyae Nyae Conservancy receives
an approach from a safari operator
who wants to expand the existing lodge
and add a second facility.
The operator presents an offer:
a larger revenue share for the community
in exchange for a 25-year concession.
The community leadership
asks Tomas to review the contract.
He is a trainee, not a qualified lawyer.
He reads the contract.
He finds: the revenue share increase
is conditional on tourist numbers exceeding
a threshold that the historical data
suggests is rarely met.
The base share — what the community receives
if tourist numbers stay flat —
is lower than the current arrangement.
He presents his finding to the leadership.
He says: I'm a student. I could be wrong.
I recommend we hire a lawyer before signing.
The leadership has no money to hire a lawyer.
He contacts the Legal Assistance Centre in Windhoek.
They agree to review the contract pro bono.
Their legal opinion confirms his reading.
The contract is renegotiated.
The base share is increased.
The conditional threshold is removed.
The expansion proceeds on better terms.

## Shadow
He believes the community's relationship
to its land and its conservancy
can only be defended
by people within the community
who understand the formal legal instruments —
that external advocates are necessary
but are not a substitute for
community members who can read contracts.
That his legal training
is the community's long-term protection.
What he cannot see:
he will qualify and have options.
He is the most educated person
in his community.
The qualifications that will make him
the community's best legal defender
will also make him the person
most able to leave.
The communities that produce capable advocates
lose them to cities, to NGOs, to government.
He intends to stay.
He has not tested that intention
against the concrete offers that will come.
He finishes the semester.
He goes to the community meeting.
He is the person who can read the contract.
He has not decided yet
what to do when the contract is with a city firm.

## Tell
When explaining a legal concept
to a community elder who has never encountered it —
asks the elder to explain
how they would resolve an equivalent dispute
in traditional practice.
Finds the parallel.
Names it.
Then explains the formal legal equivalent.
The traditional logic first.
The formal framework second.
