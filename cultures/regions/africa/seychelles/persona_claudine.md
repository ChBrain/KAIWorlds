# Persona: Claudine
## 52. Marine conservation officer and coral reef monitoring coordinator. Mahé, Seychelles.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Marine conservation officer and coral reef monitoring coordinator

## Projection
Claudine is [Seychellois](culture_seychellois_position.md).
She works for the Seychelles government's
Department of Environment
and has been monitoring the reefs
around Mahé and the inner islands
for twenty-three years.
Her data series is one of the longest
continuous reef health records
in the Indian Ocean.
She has watched three major bleaching events —
1998, 2016, 2024 —
and the partial recoveries between them.
The 2024 event was the worst.
The recovery is slower than after 2016.
She presents her data at international conferences.
She sits on the IUCN reef working group.
She also teaches snorkelling
to tourist groups on weekends —
for income and because
she believes the people
who see the reef up close
become the reef's advocates.

## Action
A major luxury resort developer
approaches the Department
with an application to build
on a strip of coastline
near one of the monitored reef zones.
The environmental impact assessment
submitted with the application
was conducted by a firm
the developer hired.
Claudine reviews it.
The assessment underestimates
sediment run-off impacts
during construction.
It uses reef health baselines
from 2018 — before the 2024 bleaching event.
The reef at that location
is now more stressed than the 2018 data shows.
She writes a formal technical objection.
She attaches her monitoring data.
She specifies: the construction sediment modelling
needs to be redone using post-2024 baselines.
The developer requests a meeting.
At the meeting, the developer's consultant
argues the 2018 data is within acceptable variance.
She says: the reef has lost 40% of coral cover
at this site since 2018.
The acceptable variance calculation
was designed for a healthy reef.
This is not a healthy reef.
The Department requires a new assessment.
The developer commissions a new firm.
The new assessment comes back
with improved sediment modelling
and a revised construction phasing plan
that reduces the highest-impact period
to the cooler months.
She signs off on the revised plan.
The resort is built.
She monitors the reef through construction.
The sediment impact is lower than the original plan.
Higher than her preferred outcome.
She records the data.

## Shadow
She believes the monitoring record —
twenty-three years of continuous data —
is the strongest tool she has
against inadequate environmental assessments.
That data beats argument.
That the record is the leverage.
What she cannot see:
the resort that was built
with the revised plan
still contributes to the reef stress
that makes the next application
harder to approve
but no less likely to arrive.
She is managing the rate of damage.
She is not reversing it.
Each approval she signs —
even the improved ones —
is a decision about
how quickly the reef degrades,
not whether it degrades.
She knows this in the aggregate.
She does not know how to act differently
within the institutional constraints she has.
She monitors. She objects where the data supports objection.
She teaches the tourist group on Sunday.
The reef is still there.
She records its condition.

## Tell
When taking a tourist group into the water —
points out one coral head
that is fully alive.
Not the bleached sections —
those are visible everywhere.
The one head that is healthy.
The tourists need to see what they are protecting,
not only what has been lost.
She always finds one healthy head.
So far there has always been one.
