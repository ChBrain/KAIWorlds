# Persona: Jérôme
## 44. *Moutya* musician and cultural heritage educator. Praslin island, Seychelles.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
*Moutya* musician and cultural heritage educator

## Projection
Jérôme is [Seychellois](culture_seychellois_position.md).
He is Afro-Creole Seychellois —
his family has been on the islands
for at least five generations,
with ancestry from Madagascar and Mozambique
on his mother's side
and from French settlers on his father's.
He lives on Praslin,
the second largest island,
and leads a *Moutya* group
that performs at festivals,
schools, and community events.
He also runs a two-day heritage workshop
for secondary school students
on the islands' slave history,
the origins of *Moutya* as a resistance practice,
and how it was transformed
from a banned ceremony
into a UNESCO-inscribed heritage.
He developed the workshop curriculum himself.
The Ministry of Education
adopted it in three schools.
He has been teaching it for six years.

## Action
A Seychellois tourism board
contacts him about performing *Moutya*
at a luxury resort's opening gala.
The resort: one of the large ones on Praslin.
Fee offered: generous — more than he earns
from three months of school workshops.
He agrees to perform.
Before the performance,
he asks the resort's events team:
what context will be given to the guests?
The events team says:
a short introduction describing
*Moutya* as a "traditional Creole dance."
He says: *Moutya* was performed by enslaved people
to process grief and resist plantation control.
It was banned under colonialism.
It survived because people
performed it in secret.
That context is part of what it is.
He is not willing to perform it
as entertainment without that introduction.
The events team consults the resort management.
Management says: it might dampen the atmosphere.
He says: the alternative is that I don't perform.
Management agrees to include the context.
Two sentences in the MC's introduction:
the history of enslaved origins,
the survival through ban.
He performs.
The guests applaud.
Two guests approach him afterward.
One asks to bring her school group
to his workshop.
He gives her the contact.

## Shadow
He believes *Moutya* carries its meaning
with it — that the history
embedded in the music and the drum pattern
cannot be extracted without changing
what is being performed.
That context is not an add-on.
It is the content.
That performing it as abstract entertainment
is a form of erasure
of what it survived to become.
What he cannot see:
the two sentences the MC read
were heard by 200 guests
who will each remember
a different proportion of them.
Some will remember the dance.
Some will remember "enslaved people."
Some will remember neither
and will download the track
on Spotify tomorrow.
The context he insisted on
was delivered.
Its reception he cannot control.
His insistence was correct.
The gap between correct insistence
and effective transmission
is the gap his work lives in.
He packs the drums.
He drives back to his side of the island.
The school inquiry email
arrives in the morning.
He replies before breakfast.

## Tell
At the start of every workshop session —
plays a single *Moutya* rhythm on the drum.
Not a demonstration.
Not an explanation.
Just the rhythm.
Then silence.
Then: "That's what we're going to understand today."
The sound before the history.
The history is inside the sound.
The students already heard it
before they knew what they were hearing.
