# Piece: The Copper, the Copperbelt, and the 1991 Transition
## The miners who built a democracy — and the resource whose price they could not control.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Lusaka / Copperbelt](culture_zambian_place_lusaka.md)

## Place
Lusaka. The capital.
Kitwe. The Copperbelt.
The mine shaft headframes — the distinctive towers
that mark the copper beneath.
October 31, 1991: Frederick Chiluba
defeats Kenneth Kaunda 73% to 27%.
The first peaceful transfer of presidential power
in Zambia's history.
Kaunda concedes.
The transition holds.
The mine workers made it possible.

## Load Bearing
Remove the copper
and Zambia is a landlocked southern African country
with 72 ethnic groups and subsistence agriculture —
a governance challenge, no particular geopolitical significance.
With it: copper makes Zambia significant,
makes it wealthy at independence,
makes Kaunda's socialist humanism
economically viable for a decade,
and then destroys it when the price falls in 1973.
The dependency was always the risk.
The nationalisations of 1969 were popular and logical —
why should a foreign company
keep the profit from Zambia's copper?
They also, when combined with the copper price collapse,
created a state enterprise that could not adapt,
a currency that inflated,
food riots in 1986 when the IMF forced price liberalisation.
The Copperbelt's trade unions were the ones
who had built the labour organisation
that Kaunda's party relied on.
When the economy failed, the unions turned.
Frederick Chiluba was the union president.
He led the Movement for Multiparty Democracy.
Kaunda agreed to elections.
He lost.
He accepted.
This is the piece's most precise element:
Zambia was the first sub-Saharan African country
where a sitting president accepted defeat
in a competitive election and left.
Remove this element: the copper dependency
produces the debt, the IMF pressure, the authoritarianism,
and the country joins the queue of single-party African states
that transition messily or not at all.
With it: the specific combination
of a strong labour tradition,
a president willing to accept a vote,
and a civil society that kept the pressure constitutional —
produces Africa's 1991 democracy landmark.
Not perfect: Chiluba then tried to extend his own term.
Failed. The constitution held.
Mwanawasa. Banda. Sata.
Each transfer accepted.
2021: Hichilema wins. His sixth attempt.
The incumbent accepts.
The democracy is not a Zambian accident.
It is a Zambian achievement.
Built from the mines.

## Apparent
1964: independence. Kaunda first president.
Copper = 96% of exports.
1969: Kaunda nationalises mines.
Zambia Consolidated Copper Mines (ZCCM) formed.
1972: one-party state. UNIP only legal party.
1973: oil shock. Copper prices collapse.
Zambia's economy collapses with it.
1976: Zambia becomes a heavily indebted country.
1986: food price riots. IMF forces subsidy removal.
1990: attempted coup fails.
Riot police kill food rioters.
International pressure and domestic unrest intensify.
1991: one-party restriction lifted.
MMD forms. Chiluba leads.
October 31, 1991: elections.
Chiluba wins. Kaunda concedes.
Africa's first peaceful presidential transfer.
1995: ZCCM privatised. Mines sold to foreign companies.
Revenue declines because the Development Agreements
allowed major tax breaks.
2002: Chiluba attempts third-term amendment. Fails.
Mwanawasa elected.
Chiluba later prosecuted for corruption.
2008: Mwanawasa dies. Banda wins.
2011: Sata wins. PF defeats MMD.
2014: Sata dies. Lungu wins by-election.
2016: Lungu re-elected.
2021: Hichilema wins with 57%.
His sixth attempt.
Lungu concedes.
The democracy holds.
The copper price: still determines the budget.
Still rising and falling beyond Zambia's control.
Still the dependency.
Still the source of the organising tradition
that gave the country what it has.
