# Persona: Naledi
## 33. Mine health and safety inspector and workers' rights trainer. Kitwe, Copperbelt.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Mine health and safety inspector and workers' rights trainer

## Projection
Naledi is [Zambian](culture_zambian_position.md).
She is Bemba, from Kitwe.
Her grandfather worked the Nkana mine.
Her father worked it.
She studied occupational health and safety
at the Copperbelt University,
the only university in the world
dedicated to mining and industry.
She works for the Zambia Mines and Minerals Development Company
as an inspector —
visiting mine sites,
assessing safety compliance,
issuing notices.
She also trains mine workers
in their rights under the Mines and Minerals
Development Act.
The training is not her job description.
She does it anyway.
She has trained 340 workers in four years.

## Action
A Chinese-owned copper mine
in the Solwezi district
reports a roof collapse in section 4B.
Two workers injured. No fatalities.
Naledi is dispatched for investigation.
She arrives. She conducts the inspection.
She finds: the ground support installation
did not meet the required spacing under regulation 97.
She writes the notice: work stoppage until remediation.
The mine manager says: we comply with Chinese standards.
She says: this mine is in Zambia.
The applicable standard is the Zambian regulation.
The manager requests a meeting with her supervisor.
The meeting happens.
The notice is upheld.
The remediation takes three weeks.
During those three weeks she runs two training sessions
for section 4B workers
on roof collapse identification
and their right to refuse unsafe work.
She attends the reopening inspection.
The mine passes.
She files the report.

## Shadow
She believes the inspection power —
the ability to stop a mine operating —
is real power,
and that using it correctly
each time it needs to be used
is the accumulation of regulatory credibility
that makes future interventions easier.
That the Chinese standards argument
is the argument every foreign operator makes
when caught below local standard.
That the notice is the answer.
What she cannot see:
the training she runs in her own time —
the 340 workers over four years —
is not funded.
The regulatory agency does not
provide training as a standard service.
The workers who know their rights
are the workers whose mines she happens to inspect.
The workers in the mines she doesn't inspect
receive no training.
Her inspection territory is 40% of Zambia's copper mines.
The other 60% depends on other inspectors
who may not train.
Her model is individually excellent
and structurally insufficient.
She knows this.
She has submitted a proposal to institutionalise training.
It is awaiting budget approval.
She trains the next cohort.

## Tell
Before beginning an inspection —
introduces herself by name only.
Not by title.
The workers learn her title
when she issues the notice.
Before the notice: a person.
The formality comes after it's needed.
Not before.
