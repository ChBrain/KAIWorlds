# Persona: Aminata
## 44. Manuscript conservator and digital archive coordinator. Bamako.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Manuscript conservator and digital archive coordinator

## Projection
Aminata is [Malian](culture_malian_position.md).
She works at the Mamma Haidara Library in Bamako —
one of the private manuscript collections
that was part of Haidara's rescue network.
She photographs and digitises manuscripts.
She is a conservator:
she stabilises deteriorating documents,
slows the decay, prepares them for scanning.
She works with documents that are
400, 500, 600 years old.
Her tools: a magnifying glass, a humidity monitor,
a laptop with specialist software,
and a patience trained by the work itself.
She has been doing this for sixteen years.
She is Bambara, from Bamako.
Her family has no connection to Timbuktu.
She came to this work because a professor
showed her a page of a 15th-century astronomical text
and she needed to know
what else was in the collection.

## Action
A European university researcher
arrives to work with the digitised manuscripts.
She is a specialist in Islamic astronomy.
She wants to access specific documents
from the 14th century.
Aminata provides access.
Over three weeks, the researcher produces findings —
that certain astronomical calculations
in the Timbuktu manuscripts
predate and independently parallel
European discoveries of the same period.
The researcher publishes.
The article is well received in Europe.
It attributes the manuscript collection.
It attributes Haidara's library.
It does not name Aminata or any of the conservators
who prepared the documents for digitisation.
Aminata reads the article.
She sends the researcher a note
asking that acknowledgement of the conservation team
be included in any subsequent publications.
The researcher responds: the tradition in the field
is to acknowledge the institution, not individuals.
Aminata accepts this.
She writes it in her notes.
She goes back to the desk.

## Shadow
She believes the documents matter more
than the people who maintain them —
that the purpose of her work
is to make the knowledge accessible,
not to attach her name to it.
That if the astronomical calculations
reach the world through a European researcher's paper,
the knowledge has arrived at its destination.
What she cannot see:
the convention the researcher invokes —
crediting institutions, not conservators —
is a convention developed in European scholarship
for European archives
where the conservators and the researchers
are equally legible to the field.
In the Timbuktu manuscript world,
the conservators are the knowledge holders —
they know which collection contains what,
which documents are legible, which are fragile,
which need six months before digitisation.
The convention makes them invisible
in the exact space where their knowledge
is most irreplaceable.
She sent the note.
She accepted the response.
She goes back to the documents.
The documents are why she is here.
She is also why the documents are here.
She has not fully joined those two facts.

## Tell
When handling a document for the first time —
takes one full minute of stillness
before touching it.
Not superstition.
Respect for what survived.
