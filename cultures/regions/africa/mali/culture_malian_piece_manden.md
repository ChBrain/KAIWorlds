# Piece: The Manden Charter and the Manuscripts of Timbuktu
## The constitutional memory of 1235 — and the 2012 moment when it was nearly burned.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Bamako / Timbuktu](culture_malian_place_bamako.md)

## Place
Timbuktu. 900 kilometres north of Bamako.
The city that was, in the 14th century,
one of the world's great centres of learning.
The university of Sankore.
25,000 students from across the Islamic world.
700,000 manuscripts in private libraries —
on mathematics, astronomy, medicine, law, philosophy.
The salt trade and the gold trade passing through.
The city that became legend in Europe
precisely because it was unreachably distant.
*Timbuktu* as a word for the end of the world.
For Malians: the beginning of something
the world still has not fully recovered.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Manden Charter
and the Mali Empire is a remarkable political achievement
without formal codification.
With it: the empire is the source
of a political philosophy —
social equality, prohibition of enslavement,
the rights of persons —
that predates the Magna Carta by nineteen years
and was not in Latin.
Remove the manuscripts of Timbuktu
and Mali is a country with a great oral tradition
and no physical record of its intellectual history.
With them: the world's most complete archive
of pre-colonial African scholarship —
proof that the continent had universities
and universities had students
and students produced knowledge
before any European ever arrived.
The load-bearing moment is 2012:
jihadist groups affiliated with Al-Qaeda
control Timbuktu.
They destroy shrines.
They threaten the manuscripts.
Abdel Kader Haidara — a Timbuktu librarian —
organises a clandestine rescue operation.
Over two years, 377,000 manuscripts
are transported south to Bamako
in trunks, in sacks, in boxes, in secret.
The most important rescue of cultural heritage
in the 21st century, largely unreported.
Remove the 2012 rescue: manuscripts destroyed.
With it: the manuscripts survive —
in Bamako, in university libraries worldwide —
and the question they pose to every colonial narrative
about African civilisation
continues to require an answer.
The piece is load-bearing not only for Mali
but for the argument about Africa's intellectual history.

## Apparent
1235: Sundiata Keita defeats Sumanguru Kanté at Kirina.
The Mali Empire founded.
The Manden Charter declared —
an oral proclamation, preserved by griots.
Social norms, prohibition of enslavement, freedom of speech.
UNESCO inscribes it as Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2009.
1324–25: Mansa Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca.
12,000 attendants. 80 camels carrying gold.
He distributes so much gold in Cairo
that Egyptian gold prices collapse.
His passage through the Mediterranean world
puts Timbuktu on European maps.
14th–15th centuries: Timbuktu as university city.
Sankore, Djinguereber, Sidi Yahia mosques as learning centres.
The manuscript tradition: private libraries
passing knowledge through generations.
1591: Moroccan invasion. The Songhai Empire falls.
Timbuktu's scholars exiled.
The manuscripts go underground — to private homes.
1960: independence. Republic of Mali.
The manuscripts largely unknown internationally.
2000s: international awareness grows.
UNESCO supports preservation.
2012: Tuareg rebellion in the north.
MNLA (secular) and then Ansar Dine and AQIM (jihadist) take Timbuktu.
The Ahmed Baba Institute — the official manuscript library — is at risk.
January 2013: French military intervention begins.
The jihadists, before withdrawing, burn approximately 4,000 manuscripts
at the Ahmed Baba Institute.
But Haidara's private rescue:
the 377,000 manuscripts transported south since 2012
are safe.
The rescue is completed.
The manuscripts survive.
The crisis continues — in different forms.
The manuscripts exist.
The argument they make exists.
