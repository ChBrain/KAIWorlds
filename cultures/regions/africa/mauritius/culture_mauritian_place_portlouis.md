## Shown
- The Aapravasi Ghat.
  The 14 stone steps on the harbour.
  The partial ruins of the immigration depot.
  The UNESCO plaque.
  The foundation of the nation
  made visible in a 1,640 square metre site.
  Most visitors walk past it on the way
  to the waterfront market.
  It is small. It carried hundreds of thousands.
- The Caudan Waterfront.
  The modern harbour development.
  Luxury retail, restaurants, the casino.
  Where Port Louis presents itself
  to the world economy.
  The financial centre behind it —
  the Global Business Companies,
  the offshore trusts,
  the Africa-routing jurisdiction
  that makes Mauritius more money
  than sugar ever did.
  The Aapravasi Ghat is 200 metres away.
  The relationship between the two things
  is not discussed at the waterfront.
- The Central Market.
  The indoor market in the city centre.
  Spices — turmeric, cinnamon, saffron, chilli.
  Vegetables. Fish from the lagoon.
  Dholl puri wrapped at speed.
  The communal food culture:
  Indian, Chinese, Creole, French —
  all in the same building,
  all recognisably distinct,
  all modified by each other.
  The culinary evidence of what the island is.
- The Champ de Mars Racecourse.
  The second oldest horse racing track in the Southern Hemisphere.
  1812.
  Every community bets.
  Racing is the one institution
  the communal divisions
  have not fragmented.
  Everyone is at the track on race day.
  The *noubann*/*zotbann* distinction
  blurs when the horses run.
- The Port Louis harbour.
  The container ships.
  The tuna canneries.
  The transshipment hub.
  Mauritius as the logistics node
  for the Indian Ocean.
  The island without resources
  that made itself useful
  to everyone with resources nearby.
