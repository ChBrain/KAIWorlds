## Shown
- The seven hills. Kampala was built on seven hills
  like Rome, which the British noted with satisfaction.
  Each hill has its own character.
  Mengo: the Kabaka's palace.
  Old Kampala: the mosque.
  Rubaga: the Catholic cathedral.
  Namirembe: the Anglican cathedral.
  The hills make the city legible —
  faith and power distributed across the landscape,
  each on its own elevation.
- Owino Market. The largest market in East Africa.
  Everything. More than everything.
  The economy that runs beside the formal economy.
  The price of everything negotiated.
  The exchange rate known to every stall.
  The city's real financial centre
  is not the bank towers on Kampala Road.
  It is here.
- The Kasubi Tombs. UNESCO World Heritage site.
  The burial ground of four kabakas.
  The thatched building burned in 2010 — arson,
  cause disputed.
  Rebuilt.
  Still the spiritual centre of Buganda.
  Still receiving mourners and petitioners.
  The fire did not reduce its significance.
  It increased it.
- Kololo Airstrip. The open ground
  where political rallies happen.
  Where Museveni addresses crowds.
  Where opposition parties, when permitted,
  also address crowds.
  The same ground, different purposes, alternating.
  The ground itself is neutral.
  What happens on it is not.
- Makerere University. Founded 1922.
  The intellectual centre of East Africa
  for sixty years.
  Where Nyerere studied.
  Where Ngugi wa Thiong'o taught.
  Where the generation that built post-colonial East Africa
  was educated.
  Currently underfunded. Currently contested.
  Still the most important university
  in the region.
  Still producing the people
  who will argue about the continent's future.

## Withheld
- The fear.
  Kampala is a warm city.
  The *boda-boda* drivers are friendly.
  The restaurants are good.
  The people are hospitable in a way
  that takes visitors by surprise.
  Underneath: the country has had
  three governments since independence
  that used state violence against citizens at scale.
  People remember.
  The warmth and the memory coexist
  without one cancelling the other.
  The room shows the warmth.
  The memory is in the bones.
