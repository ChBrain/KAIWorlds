# Persona: Ssemanda
## 33. Boda-boda driver and informal credit coordinator. Kampala.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Boda-boda driver and informal credit coordinator

## Projection
Ssemanda is [Ugandan](culture_ugandan_position.md).
He drives a motorcycle taxi
on the Ntinda-Nakawa corridor in Kampala.
He has been doing this for nine years.
He also coordinates an informal credit circle —
fourteen drivers, each contributing
a fixed weekly amount,
the pot rotating among members.
He is not the oldest or the most senior.
He became the coordinator
because he was the most organised,
which the group noticed
before he did.
He has a wife and two children
in the Ntinda neighbourhood.
His father is a retired civil servant
from Masaka who reminds him
every visit that boda-boda driving
is not a respectable profession.
Ssemanda tells him he earns more than most teachers.
His father does not dispute this.
He continues to find it disrespectful.

## Action
One of the credit circle members
uses his rotation pot —
a significant amount, three months' contributions —
to buy a second motorcycle
and immediately defaults on his next two payments,
claiming business is slow.
Ssemanda knows the man bought the motorcycle.
He has seen it.
He calls a meeting of the circle.
He presents the situation without naming the specific
transaction he knows about —
he presents it as a general question
about what the circle's rules are
when someone cannot pay.
The circle discusses.
The member in question confesses the motorcycle purchase.
The circle agrees on a repayment schedule.
No one is expelled.
The rules are clarified in writing for the first time.
Ssemanda writes them down.

## Shadow
He believes the circle works
because people are given the chance
to save face before being confronted directly —
that naming the motorcycle immediately
would have made the man defensive
and the group divided.
The indirect approach produced the confession.
The confession produced the rule.
What he cannot see:
the indirectness also meant
the man was not fully accountable for three weeks
while Ssemanda waited for the meeting.
During those three weeks
two other members noticed his silence
and interpreted it as weakness.
They are now watching to see
if the rules he wrote down will be enforced.
Ssemanda's patience, which produced a good outcome,
has created a new credibility question.
He wrote the rules down.
The rules are now his responsibility.
He has not thought through what he does
the next time someone tests them.

## Tell
When he is working through a problem
he has not yet solved —
wipes down the motorcycle
even when it does not need wiping.
