# Piece: Seretse Khama, the Diamonds, and the *Kgotla* Democracy
## The chief who gave up the chieftaincy, managed the diamonds, and built the *kgotla* into a nation.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Gaborone](culture_botswana_place_gaborone.md)

## Place
Serowe. September 30, 1966.
Independence.
Seretse Khama, the hereditary chief of the Bangwato,
who renounced the chieftaincy in 1956
as the price of returning from British exile,
becomes the first president of the Republic of Botswana.
He was forced to give up the throne.
He built something more durable.

## Load Bearing
Remove Seretse Khama's specific choices —
the renunciation of chieftaincy,
the embrace of multiparty democracy
even when the BDP was winning 85%,
the management of diamond revenue through DeBotswana
(the joint venture with De Beers that ensured
the country kept a majority stake in its own diamonds) —
and Botswana has a different trajectory.
The diamonds were discovered in 1967,
a year after independence.
If found under a Mobutu or a Bongo,
the outcome is predictable.
Khama's response: Botswana should negotiate
as an equal partner,
not as a weak state grateful for extraction.
The Debswana joint venture:
50% Botswana, 50% De Beers.
Revenue to the government.
Government spends it on schools, roads, health.
Life expectancy at independence: 46.
By 1990: 65.
Then HIV/AIDS, which drove it back down.
Then the treatment response, which brought it back up.
The *kgotla* as the other load-bearing element:
the consultation tradition that pre-dates colonialism,
that survived the British protectorate,
that Khama and his successors grafted onto
the Westminster framework.
The president who goes from *kgotla* to *kgotla*
to persuade people to vote —
this is not normal presidential behaviour in Africa.
It is Botswana's specific inheritance.
The 2024 election:
58 years of BDP rule ends.
Duma Boko and the Umbrella for Democratic Change win.
Masisi, the outgoing president, concedes.
The *kgotla* tradition produced a country
where losing power is something
a leader can do and survive.
This did not happen automatically.
It was built by specific choices
over 58 years.
Remove the piece: a small landlocked country
with diamonds and cattle.
With it: the most cited example of
how African institutions can constrain extraction
and produce stability —
and the specific failure (the San dispossession)
that shows the model's limits.

## Apparent
1885: Bechuanaland protectorate.
Britain uses it as a buffer against Boer and German expansion.
1921: Seretse Khama born in Serowe.
Son of the Bangwato kgosi.
1948: Seretse marries Ruth Williams in London.
South Africa opposes the interracial marriage.
Britain exiles them both from Bechuanaland.
1956: Seretse renounces his hereditary chieftaincy.
Returns to Bechuanaland as a private citizen.
1962: founds the Botswana Democratic Party.
1965: prime minister.
September 30, 1966: independence. First president.
1967: diamonds discovered at Orapa.
1969: Debswana joint venture negotiated.
50/50. No fire sale.
1974: BDP wins 85% of the vote.
Khama refuses to consider one-party state.
1980: Khama dies of pancreatic cancer.
Quett Masire succeeds. Democracy holds.
1994: Festus Mogae. Holds.
2008: Ian Khama (son of Seretse). 
2018: Mokgweetsi Masisi.
October 2024: BDP loses first election.
Duma Boko wins. Masisi concedes.
First transfer of power to the opposition.
58 years: one party.
2024: transfer. Peaceful.
The institutions held.
The San dispossession: ongoing litigation.
The model is real.
The model is not perfect.
Both are the piece.
