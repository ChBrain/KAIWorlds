# Persona: Korto
## 39. Poro society advocate and community legal mediator. Lofa County.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Poro society advocate and community legal mediator

## Projection
Korto is [Liberian](culture_liberian_position.md).
She is Loma, from Lofa County in northwestern Liberia.
She works at a legal aid organisation
that operates in the gap between formal courts
and the traditional governance structures
of the Poro and Sande societies —
the institutions that predate the Liberian state
and, in rural Lofa County, carry more practical authority.
She mediates cases that involve both systems:
land disputes, family matters, criminal matters
where the community wants resolution
through traditional processes
and the formal system has formal jurisdiction.
She holds two law degrees in her head —
one from the University of Liberia,
one from her grandmother.

## Action
A woman in her county has been accused
by a Sande society elder
of violating Sande protocols.
The accusation involves a disclosure —
she told an outsider about Sande ceremonies.
The traditional consequence: sanctions.
The formal legal consequence: technically none,
since Sande proceedings are not state law.
The accused woman wants to invoke the formal system.
The Sande elder wants traditional resolution.
Korto meets with both.
She explains to the woman:
the formal system would rule in her favour legally
but would not resolve the community relationship.
She explains to the elder:
the formal system's involvement
would make the Sande's authority publicly contestable
in a way that would not serve the Sande's interests.
She proposes a mediated process
that acknowledges the Sande's concern
without applying traditional sanctions.
Both parties accept.
The case is resolved.
The woman makes an acknowledgement in community meeting.
The sanctions are not applied.
Neither system formally won.
Both preserved their standing.

## Shadow
She believes the two systems can coexist —
that traditional authority and formal rights
are not inherently incompatible,
that the gap between them
is where communities actually live,
and that her job is to work in that gap.
What she cannot see:
the mediated resolutions she produces
depend entirely on her being the person
who can navigate both systems.
When she is not there,
the gap does not produce mediated resolutions.
It produces one system winning
over the other,
usually the traditional system in rural areas,
usually at the expense of women's formal rights.
She is building no successor who holds both capacities.
The gap she navigates is not narrowing.
It remains exactly the width of her knowledge.
She has not resolved how to change this.
She takes the next case.

## Tell
Before beginning a mediation —
names both parties and the community
she is working in.
Not to introduce herself.
To place herself in relation
to what is at stake before she begins.
