# Piece: The Post-Election Violence of 2007
## The crack in the harambee — and what it revealed about what the country was actually built on.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Nairobi](culture_kenyan_place_nairobi.md)

## Place
Nairobi. Kibera. Eldoret. The Rift Valley.
The places where the killing happened.
Also: every government office that processed the disputed results.
Also: Kofi Annan's hotel room
where the power-sharing agreement was signed
on February 28, 2008.
The country that almost broke
and chose, just barely, not to.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 2007–08 post-election violence
and Kenya is the most stable, developed country in East Africa,
the regional hub of finance and business and NGOs,
the country that Kenyans are proud to be from.
With it: all of that, and the knowledge
that in 61 days, approximately 1,500 people were killed
and 600,000 displaced —
not by an invading army but by Kenyans,
organised along ethnic lines
that the political system had spent forty years deepening.
The piece is load-bearing because it is the moment
the gap between the aspiration and the architecture
became visible in the worst possible way.
*Harambee* survived 2007–08.
So did the ethnic arithmetic.
Both are the country now.
Remove the piece: a stable East African hub.
With it: a stable East African hub
that knows exactly what its stability depends on
and what can happen when it fails.
Every election since 2008
has been negotiated with this knowledge in the room.
The knowledge changes how negotiations proceed.
It has not yet changed the architecture that made them necessary.

## Apparent
December 27, 2007: the presidential election.
Mwai Kibaki (Kikuyu) vs. Raila Odinga (Luo).
December 30: the Electoral Commission declares Kibaki the winner.
Odinga and his Orange Democratic Movement
allege massive rigging.
The announcement triggers violence in Kisumu, Eldoret, Nairobi.
January 1–15, 2008: Kikuyu and Luo communities attacked.
Kalenjin militia active in the Rift Valley.
The church at Kiambaa, Eldoret:
thirty to thirty-five people, mostly Kikuyu,
burned alive when the building is set alight.
This image travels the world.
The government deploys security forces.
The violence continues for weeks.
By mid-January: 1,000 dead, 300,000 displaced.
The African Union sends Kofi Annan.
Four weeks of mediation.
February 28, 2008: the National Accord.
A power-sharing government.
Kibaki as President, Odinga as Prime Minister.
The killing stops.
2008–2013: the grand coalition government.
2010: a new constitution, widely supported.
2013: elections held under the new framework.
Held peacefully, contested legally, resolved without violence.
The lesson partially learned.
2017: elections again disputed.
Court nullifies the result — unprecedented in Africa.
A re-run held. Violence limited.
The lesson learned, then tested, then partially held.
The architecture: unreformed.
The knowledge: present in every room.
