## Shown

  The waterfront promenade.
  The Gulf of Guinea.
  The oil platforms visible offshore at night —
  lights on the water where the revenue comes from.
  Libreville's architecture says *middle income*:
  glass towers, paved roads, restaurants.
  The city that looks like the per capita GDP figure.
  The townships behind the waterfront

  Both are Libreville.
  The Bord de Mer is the city's face.
  The face is not the whole.
- Camp Charles de Gaulle.
  The French military base outside Libreville.
  Pre-positioned. Staffed. Operational.

  the French restored him.

  the French did not intervene.

  Oligui was manageable.

  The base is still there.

- The Musée National des Arts et Traditions du Gabon.
  The pre-Bongo Gabon.
  The Fang masks — some of the most important
  African sculptural objects in the world.
  The ones in this museum
  are not the ones in European museums.
  Many of Gabon's most significant objects
  left during colonialism.
  The museum holds what remained.
  It holds it carefully.
  It is well-funded by regional standards.
  The Bongo regime invested in culture
  as a legitimacy project.
  The investment was real.
  The legitimacy was purchased.
  The masks are still extraordinary.
- The Forêt du Mondah.
  The forest reserve north of Libreville.
  Gorillas. Chimpanzees. Mandrills.
  The forest that exists because
  80% of Gabon is forested
  and the country has not cleared it
  the way its neighbours have.
  This is a consequence of low population density
  and oil wealth — when you have oil revenue,
  you don't need to cut all the trees.
  The forest survived the dynasty by accident.
  It is now Gabon's most durable asset —
  its carbon credits, its conservation finance,
  its international credibility.
  The trees outlasted the Bongos.
