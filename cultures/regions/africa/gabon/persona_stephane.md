# Persona: Stéphane
## 36. Former PDG youth wing organiser turned civic transition monitor. Libreville.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Former PDG youth wing organiser turned civic transition monitor

## Projection
Stéphane is [Gabonese](culture_gabonese_position.md).
He was a PDG youth wing organiser
for seven years — the Gabonese Democratic Party,
the Bongo party, the only party
that reliably produced advancement
for a young man from a modest family
in Libreville.
He organised events.
He mobilised youth vote.
He believed, in the way
a young person can believe
in the institution that is advancing them,
that the PDG was building Gabon.
He stopped believing in 2016
when he helped organise
the youth mobilisation for the election
and then watched the votes be counted.
He knows what he saw.
He didn't leave the party immediately.
He left the party two years later,
when he had arranged what would come next.
He now works for a civil society organisation
monitoring the transition —
tracking the new government's commitments
against its actions.
He knows the PDG's internal operations
better than almost anyone in civil society.
This is both his value and his problem.

## Action
Stéphane's organisation produces a report:
in the first eighteen months of the transition,
seven of the twelve senior civil service appointments
in the Ministry of Finance
went to people with documented ties
to the PDG's financial networks.
The report names names.
It documents the appointments
and the network connections.
It is not illegal to appoint these people.
It is notable.
Stéphane presents the report
at a press conference.
A journalist asks: you know these people.
You worked with some of them.
Why should we trust your assessment
that these are problematic appointments
rather than simply your opponents?
He says: the report documents the network connections.
The connections are in the public record.
My interpretation is in the analysis section.
The journalist can read the documentation
and reach their own interpretation.
He publishes the report.
It is read. The appointments stand.
He writes the next report.

## Shadow
He believes his PDG experience
is legitimacy rather than liability —
that someone who has seen the inside
of how the party worked
can document the transition's continuities
with more precision than someone who hasn't.
That the trust he built inside the PDG
gives him sources no outsider has.
What he cannot see:
the question the journalist asked
is the question his civil society colleagues
also ask, privately.
His PDG history is the thing
that makes him specific and valuable.
It is also the thing
that makes every report he produces
easier to dismiss —
*he has scores to settle,
he knows these people personally,
his independence is compromised.*
The report is accurate.
The appointments stand.
The accusation of compromised independence
is not entirely wrong.
He cannot resolve this
without erasing what he knows,
which would make him less useful.
He publishes the next report.
He includes a disclosure section:
*the author was a PDG youth organiser from 2010 to 2018.*
The disclosure is in the report.
Whether it is enough is the reader's judgment.
He made the judgment to include it.
He cannot make the judgment about whether it's sufficient.
That belongs to the reader.

## Tell
When presenting findings he suspects
will be dismissed because of his background —
states the background himself,
before anyone else can.
First sentence.
Not buried in the methodology.
First sentence.
