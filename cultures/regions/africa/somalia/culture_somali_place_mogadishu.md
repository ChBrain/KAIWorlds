## Location: Somalia / Somaliland

## Shown
- Mogadishu. Lido Beach.
  The corniche rebuilt since 2012.
  Young people on the beach on Fridays.
  The same beach where the bodies were in the 1990s.
  The same beach where Al-Shabaab bombs have gone off.
  And also: the beach.
  The city's refusal to stop being a city
  is the most Somali thing about it.
- The Mogadishu Cathedral.
  Built by the Italians in 1928.
  Damaged in the civil war.
  Used as a goat pen for years.
  Slowly being restored.
  There are almost no Catholics in Mogadishu.
  The restoration is historical, not devotional.
  The building is there.
  The city maintains it.
  This is the city's relationship to its own past:
  complicated, present, not erased.
- Villa Somalia. The presidential compound.
  Behind high walls. Security perimeter.
  The federal government operates inside it
  and controls the perimeter around it.
  The country extends considerably beyond the perimeter.
  The government's authority and the country's extent
  are not the same thing.
  Both are real.
- Hargeisa. Capital of Somaliland.
  The bombed-out MiG fighter jet
  on a plinth in the centre of the city —
  one of the planes that bombed Hargeisa in 1988.
  The government of Somaliland placed it there
  as a monument to the bombing.
  The plane that destroyed the city
  is now the city's most recognisable landmark.
  This is how Somaliland holds its history:
  in full view, transformed, not forgotten.
  The city rebuilt around it.
  The city functions.
  It has functioning courts, police, elections.
  It has no international recognition.
  It functions anyway.
- The Hamarweyne market, Mogadishu.
  The old market near the harbour.
  Running since the medieval period.
  Stopped during the worst years.
  Running again.
  The market is older than the state.
  The market will outlast the current government.
  The market is the continuity.
