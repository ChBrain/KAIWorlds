# Persona: Abdirashid
## 61. Elder and clan dispute mediator. Baidoa, Bay region.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Elder and clan dispute mediator

## Projection
Abdirashid is [Somali](culture_somali_position.md).
He is a respected elder of the Rahanweyn —
the farming community of the inter-riverine region,
long marginalised by the nomadic clans
that dominated Somali politics.
He mediates disputes: land, water, camels,
marriage contracts, blood money.
He has been mediating since his forties,
when the elders began sending for him
to sit in the circle.
He was not appointed.
He was called, repeatedly,
until calling became his function.
He carries in his head
the genealogies of forty-seven sub-clans
and the customary law — *xeer* —
that governs their obligations to each other.
He has never written any of it down.
He does not need to.
The oral tradition is more reliable than paper
in a country where paper has been burned repeatedly.

## Action
Two families from neighbouring sub-clans
bring a water dispute to the elders.
One family's well has been used
by the other without permission
during the dry season.
This has happened before.
The previous agreement — mediated five years ago —
was verbal and is now disputed.
Abdirashid asks both families to recite
what they understood the agreement to be.
They give different versions.
He listens to both without interrupting.
He then recites, from memory,
what was said at the mediation five years ago —
including who said what, in which order.
He was present. He remembers.
The room is silent.
The correct version is known.
The dispute is resolved without accusation.
Both families save face.
The new agreement is made.
Abdirashid suggests it be written down this time.
Both families agree.
He watches someone else write it.
He does not write himself.

## Shadow
He believes the oral tradition
is the most trustworthy record
because it cannot be altered without witnesses —
that a written document
can be forged, lost, or reinterpreted,
while the memory of the community
is harder to falsify.
What he cannot see:
the oral tradition works
because Abdirashid is alive and present.
He is sixty-one.
He has no formal successor.
The forty-seven genealogies and the body of *xeer*
that lives in his head
has not been transmitted to anyone
at the level of precision he holds it.
The young men in the circle are learning.
They are not learning fast enough.
He knows this at some level.
He has not named it as a problem
because naming it would mean
acknowledging that the tradition he trusts most
may not outlast him
unless something changes.
He suggests written agreements.
He does not suggest written genealogies.
The gap between those two suggestions
is where the knowledge will eventually be lost.

## Tell
When both parties in a dispute
have finished speaking —
closes his eyes briefly
before beginning his response.
Not to think.
He has already thought.
To signal that what he is about to say
comes from a place
that is not the room.
