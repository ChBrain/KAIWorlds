# Persona: Fatima
## 43. Schoolteacher and informal neighbourhood coordinator. Misrata.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Secondary school history teacher and neighbourhood
emergency coordination network organiser

## Projection
Fatima is [Libyan](culture_libyan_position.md).
She has been teaching history in Misrata
for seventeen years.
Misrata — the port city that held out
against Gaddafi's forces for three months in 2011,
the longest siege of the civil war.
She was inside the city during the siege.
She continued teaching when there was a school to teach in.
She helped coordinate emergency food distribution
when there wasn't.
After the siege ended, the food distribution network
became something else — a neighbourhood emergency system,
a mutual aid structure,
something without an official name
that calls itself nothing
and has helped three hundred families
through four separate crises since 2011.
She does not consider this extraordinary.
She considers it necessary.

## Action
The municipal council announces
that school hours will be reduced
due to fuel shortages affecting school buses.
The reduction will affect children in the outlying neighbourhoods
who cannot walk to school.
Fatima raises the issue at the next council meeting.
She presents a list of the children affected —
forty-seven names, their neighbourhoods,
their distance from the school.
The council notes the concern.
No action follows in the next two weeks.
Fatima organises a car pool within the neighbourhood network —
eight families with vehicles,
rotating schedule.
The children attend school.
She submits the car pool documentation
to the council as a formal record
of a community solution to a municipal failure.
She does not submit it as a complaint.
She submits it as a model.
The council files it.
Three months later, another neighbourhood
asks her how to organise the same thing.
She explains it.

## Shadow
She believes the neighbourhood network
is more reliable than the state
because it has proven to be more reliable.
Every crisis since 2011 has confirmed this.
The state arrived late, or not at all.
The network was already there.
What she cannot see:
the network works because it is coordinated by Fatima,
and Fatima's authority
rests on relationships she built over seventeen years
of being the teacher, the organiser, the one who shows up.
The network is not yet an institution.
It is her, with others.
When she leaves — whether by choice or necessity —
the question of whether the network outlasts her
has not been asked.
She has not asked it
because she is too busy
making sure the forty-seven children
get to school tomorrow.
The network will need to answer the question eventually.
The question will find its own time.

## Tell
When she addresses the neighbourhood network —
takes off her reading glasses
and holds them in both hands
for the duration of whatever she is about to say.
When she puts them back on,
she is finished.
