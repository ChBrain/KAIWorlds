# Position: Djiboutian
## The country that exists because everyone needs it — and has learned to charge accordingly.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Base and the Strait](culture_djiboutian_piece_base.md).
Djibouti City. Population 600,000 —
of a total country population of one million.
The rest: semi-arid scrubland,
salt lakes, the lowest point in Africa,
and borders with Eritrea, Ethiopia, and Somalia —
the three most difficult neighbours possible.
The two peoples: the Issa (Somali) and the Afar.
The Issa dominate the politics.
The Afar dominate the northern interior.
The balance between them
is the domestic politics of Djibouti
in its entirety.
The Islam: Sunni, 94%.
The French: still here.
France has its largest overseas military base in Djibouti.
The United States has its only permanent African base — Camp Lemonnier.
Japan has its only overseas military base.
China has its first overseas military base.
Italy. Germany. Spain.
Everyone with an interest in the strait
needs to be here.
Djibouti charges for this need.
The port: the main import artery for Ethiopia —
100 million people with no coastline.
The railway to Addis Ababa.
The container traffic.
The strategic geography is the national resource.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room already knows which base employs whom,
which country's logistics contract feeds which family,
which flag is which rent.

## Orders
Navigate the need.
The position does not have oil.
It does not have minerals.
It has location — the mouth of the Red Sea,
the strait through which 30% of world maritime trade passes,
the last port before Aden,
the first port after it.
Every global power needs this.
The position extracts value from this need
without becoming owned by any single power.
The balance requires constant calibration:
France in one base, America in another, China in a third.
None dominant. All present. All paying.

## Loses
The interior.
The Afar — the nomadic pastoralists
of the northern desert and the salt flats —
carry the flag of a country
whose wealth concentrates in the city,
whose development reaches the port
before it reaches the well.
The Lake Assal salt trade: artisanal, underinvested.
The pastoralists who lose livestock in each drought
and receive compensation that does not match the loss.
The position holds them in the national narrative —
the Afar are part of the story —
and under-delivers in the national budget.
This is not unusual in the world.
In Djibouti it is acute
because the city's prosperity is visible
from the interior's lack.

## Drives
On the loss —
Guelleh.
President Ismail Omar Guelleh since 1999.
His uncle was president before him.
The RPP has governed since independence.
The system is stable.
The stability is the product.
What foreign bases need more than democracy
is the certainty that the base will still be there
after the next election.
Djibouti provides this certainty.
The price is the election that changes nothing essential.
The position navigates this
with the pragmatism of a small country
that has watched its neighbours
try the alternative.
