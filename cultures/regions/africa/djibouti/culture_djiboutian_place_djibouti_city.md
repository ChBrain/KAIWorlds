## Shown
- The Port of Djibouti.
  The cranes. The containers.
  The ships waiting at anchor in the gulf.
  Ethiopian trucks queued at the terminal gates.
  The reason the country exists as it does.
  The geography made visible as commerce.
- The European Quarter. *Plateau du Serpent.*
  The French colonial architecture.
  The French Foreign Legion base at the edge of it.
  The cafés that serve espresso
  and the restaurants that serve steak-frites
  in 35-degree heat.
  France did not leave when it granted independence.
  It renegotiated.
  The terms: a base and a relationship.
  The relationship is visible in the quarter.
- The African Quarter. *Arhiba. Balbala.*
  The majority of the population.
  The khat market in the afternoon —
  the daily import from Ethiopia,
  the leaves chewed by the majority of adult men,
  the economy that runs on this ritual.
  The activity of the morning contrasted
  with the stillness of the khat afternoon.
  The city has two tempos.
  The port runs on one.
  The rest of the city runs on both.
- Camp Lemonnier.
  The American base.
  The drones that fly from here
  over Somalia, over Yemen.
  The flag visible from the road.
  The soldiers in the supermarkets.
  The rent that is paid quarterly
  into the Djiboutian treasury.
  The geopolitics of the Horn of Africa
  made mundane by the visibility of the base
  next to the apartment blocks.
