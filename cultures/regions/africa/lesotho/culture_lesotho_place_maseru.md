## Shown
- Thaba Bosiu.
  The Mountain of the Night.
  40 kilometres from Maseru.
  The mesa where Moshoeshoe I governed.
  The graves of the kings.
  The paths up the mountain
  that the Sotho defenders used
  to push back Boer attackers.
  The national monument.
  Visited by school groups, pilgrims,
  and tourists who come to the highlands.
  The mountain where the country was decided.
  Still standing exactly as it was.
  The most load-bearing place in Lesotho.
- Maseru CBD.
  The border city — directly on the South African border.
  The Maseru bridge crosses into South Africa.
  Lesotho's economy moves through this bridge.
  The goods, the workers,
  the remittances that sustain rural families.
  Maseru is the country's most visible point
  of the dependency it manages.
  The city built at the crossing.
- The textile factories.
  Maseru's periphery.
  Lesotho is one of Africa's largest textile exporters.
  The AGOA agreement with the US
  gives Lesotho preferential access
  to the American market.
  The factories employ primarily women.
  The women sustain rural Lesotho
  through the wages they send home.
  The manufacturing economy
  and the highland agricultural economy
  and the remittances from South Africa
  are the three legs of Lesotho's survival.
  All three run simultaneously.
- Maletsunyane Falls.
  The highest single-drop waterfall in Africa.
  192 metres.
  Four hours from Maseru into the highlands.
  The Lesotho that tourists come for.
  The Basotho pony treks.
  The highland villages.
  The wool and mohair —
  the highland sheep and Angora goats
  that produce export income.
  The country that is entirely mountains
  has found a way to make the mountains work.
- The Parliament building.
  Where the governments come and go.
  The political instability concentrated here.
  The prime ministers who serve months
  before no-confidence votes.
  The monarchy watches from above.
  The parliament changes.
  The king remains.
  The building has housed many governments.
  The mountain has housed one nation.
