# Piece: The Gacaca
## The grass where the community sat — and the decision it forced.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Kigali](culture_rwandan_place_kigali.md)

## Place
Every hill in Rwanda.
Not a single institution — a distributed one.
12,000 community courts across the country.
Held on the grass, in the open,
in the places where people already gathered.
The word means the grass.
The court was where the community sat.

## Load Bearing
Remove the gacaca and Rwanda has two options after 1994:
a formal court system that would take decades
to process 1.2 million cases,
or an amnesty that acknowledged nothing
and named no one.
Both options leave the génocidaires
and their neighbours in the same unresolved space.
The gacaca was a third thing — a specifically Rwandan thing,
adapted from a pre-colonial community dispute mechanism
and applied to crimes that no community dispute mechanism
had ever been designed for.
The load it bears:
every Rwandan who lives on the same hill as someone
who killed people on that hill
has a gacaca verdict between them.
The verdict is not always believed.
The reconciliation is not always felt.
But the speaking happened.
The name was said. The act was named.
The community heard it.
The community then had to continue to exist.
That continuation — imperfect, constrained, sometimes coerced —
is the gacaca's load-bearing function.
Remove the piece: 1.2 million unprocessed cases,
a country held together by unspoken knowledge
of what each person did or did not do.
With it: a country where the speaking happened,
where the official record was made,
where the survivor — even the one who does not forgive —
knows that the act is on record.
The record is not justice in full.
It is the foundation under which
the building of the country can begin.

## Apparent
April 6, 1994. The plane is shot down.
The génocide begins the next morning.
100 days. Approximately 800,000 killed.
Primarily Tutsi. Also moderate Hutu.
The pace: approximately 8,000 people killed per day.
The world watches. The UN withdraws forces.
The RPF advances from the north.
July 4, 1994: the RPF takes Kigali.
The genocide ends.
The prisons: designed for 45,000 people,
holding 130,000.
The court system destroyed.
Most judges dead or fled.
The ICTR established in Arusha, Tanzania.
Far from Rwanda. Expensive. Slow.
2001: the government proposes gacaca.
2005: the courts begin operating.
12,000 community courts.
1.2 million cases heard.
The process: the community gathers.
The person accused of genocide crimes
faces the community.
Survivors speak. Witnesses speak.
The accused may confess.
Confession brings reduced sentence —
community service, return to the hill.
Non-confession: longer sentence.
Some confessions are true.
Some are performances for the sentence reduction.
The community often knows the difference.
The community attends regardless.
Attendance is not optional.
2012: the gacaca courts close.
The cases adjudicated.
The verdicts recorded.
The génocidaires on the same hills
as the survivors.
The Tuesday market continuing.

## Notes
The gacaca cannot be fully separated
from the Kagame government's control of the narrative.
RPF crimes during and after the genocide
were not subject to gacaca jurisdiction.
Critics note that the community courts
were also sites of political consolidation —
Hutu cooperation with the process
produced lighter sentences,
cementing a particular loyalty
to the post-genocide order.
The gacaca was both a genuine attempt
at communal justice
and an instrument of the ruling party.
Both are true.
The position holds both
as it holds everything else:
what happened, and what was built from it,
are the same country.
