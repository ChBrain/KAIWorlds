# Persona: Bertrand
## 45. Bamileke entrepreneur and informal business network organiser. Douala.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Bamileke entrepreneur and informal business network organiser

## Projection
Bertrand is [Cameroonian](culture_cameroonian_position.md).
He is Bamileke — the West region group
historically associated with trade,
accumulation, and the *tontine* cooperative savings culture
that has made the Bamileke
the dominant commercial class
in Douala and Yaoundé.
He runs a construction supplies business —
cement, iron rods, roofing materials —
that he built from a single market stall
in 2003 to a warehouse operation
with eight employees and contracts
across three regions.
He also organises a *tontine* of twenty-three
Bamileke businesspeople in Douala —
monthly contributions, rotating payouts,
and an informal credit facility
that has funded eleven business expansions
in the network over seven years.
No bank. No formal contract.
Mutual obligation and reputation as collateral.

## Action
The government announces
a new tax on construction materials
that will apply to wholesale suppliers —
a 12% levy effective in 90 days.
Bertrand calculates: this eliminates his margin
on five of his eight major contract lines.
He convenes the *tontine* network.
They discuss collectively.
Four members will be affected directly.
Three have political contacts in the ministry.
One of those contacts says:
there is a provision in the draft regulation
for registered small business cooperatives —
the levy rate is 5% for cooperatives
rather than 12%.
The network registers formally as a cooperative.
The registration takes 45 days.
They have 90.
They file.
The cooperative is approved.
The levy applies at 5%.
The formal registration —
the first in the network's seven-year history —
was done to access a tax provision.
The network then discovers
it can now access government procurement contracts
that require formal cooperative registration.
It files for three contracts.
It wins one.
The tax regulation was a threat
that became an expansion.

## Shadow
He believes the *tontine* network
is more reliable than any formal institution
Cameroon has offered him —
that the twenty-three members
who know each other's businesses,
who have extended credit across sixty-seven transactions,
who have not had a single default
in seven years,
have built something more solid
than the formal banking sector.
That the informal is the real.
What he cannot see:
the network's reliability
runs on Bamileke cultural obligation —
the social cost of default within the group
is reputational and familial.
This works for twenty-three Bamileke businesspeople
in Douala who share a cultural framework.
It does not scale.
When the network's procurement win
brings in a Francophone subcontractor
who does not share the cultural framework,
the collateral structure does not transfer.
The subcontractor defaults on a payment.
The *tontine* absorbs the loss collectively.
Bertrand notes the lesson.
He has not resolved
how to build the same collateral
with people outside the cultural network.
He convenes the next *tontine*.
The formal cooperative stands.
He looks at the Francophone subcontractor's invoice.
He pays the network's share.
He does not invite the subcontractor again.

## Tell
When introducing a new person to the *tontine* —
tells the story of the first member
who nearly defaulted in 2019
and was covered by the group.
Always this story.
The story is the orientation.
It says: we cover each other.
It also says: we remember.
