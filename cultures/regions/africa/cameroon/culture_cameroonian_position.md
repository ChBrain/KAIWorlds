# Position: Cameroonian
## Africa in miniature — and the federation that was promised and dismantled.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Anglophone Crisis](culture_cameroonian_piece_anglophone.md).
Cameroon: the country the Germans called *Kamerun*,
the British and French divided after 1919,
and whose two halves reunified in 1961
with a federal promise
that was dismantled eleven years later.
*Africa in miniature* — the colonial tourist phrase
that stuck because it is accurate:
savanna, forest, desert, mountains, coast
within one country.
280 languages. The Bamileke in the west.
The Fulani in the north. The Beti in the centre.
The Ewondo, Bassa, Duala.
French in the east and north and centre — 80% of the population.
English in the northwest and southwest — 20%.
The collectivism: high.
The clan and the chieftaincy and the *tontine*.
The hierarchy: steep.
Biya has been president since 1982.
Paul Biya. Forty-three years.
Ninety-one years old.
Still governing, or being governed around.

## Orders
*On va gérer* — we'll manage it.
The Cameroonian position navigates
a country of extraordinary cultural richness
under an increasingly absent president
and an ongoing civil war
in the northwest and southwest.
Management is the mode.
Not resolution. Management.
The country has been managing
the Anglophone question since 1961.

## Loses
The federation.
The 1961 promise of a two-state federal republic —
East Cameroon, West Cameroon —
with the Anglophone system preserved:
common law, British education,
political autonomy.
The 1972 constitution: unitary state.
One star on the flag instead of two.
The Anglophone regions absorbed
into a Francophone-majority state
that treated their institutions as obstacles.
Francophone judges in common-law courts.
French curricula in English schools.
Exclusion from senior government positions.
The position carries the knowledge
that something was agreed and broken —
not by conquest but by constitutional amendment,
which made it harder to name.

## Drives
On the loss —
the Cameroonian cultural abundance.
The Lions Indomptables — the national football team,
the only African team to reach a World Cup quarter-final.
The music: makossa, bikutsi, afrobeat fusion.
The food — the ndolé, the eru, the koki —
that crosses regional and linguistic lines.
The Cameroonians who hold both languages —
who move between Douala and London,
between Yaoundé and Montreal,
without losing either side.
The position builds in the spaces
that the crisis does not occupy.
The crisis occupies the northwest and southwest.
The rest of the country is building.
The building is real.
The crisis is also real.
