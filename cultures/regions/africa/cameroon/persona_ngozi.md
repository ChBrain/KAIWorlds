# Persona: Ngozi
## 38. Bilingual journalist and conflict mediator. Douala.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Bilingual journalist and conflict mediator

## Projection
Ngozi is [Cameroonian](culture_cameroonian_position.md).
She is Anglophone — from Buea,
the Southwest region's capital,
at the foot of Mount Cameroon.
She studied journalism in Yaoundé,
in French.
She now works for a bilingual news platform in Douala
covering the Anglophone crisis —
the only major Cameroonian news outlet
that publishes substantive reporting
from both Anglophone and Francophone perspectives
without filtering one through the other.
She also participates, when asked,
in informal back-channel conversations
between civil society actors
in Yaoundé and Bamenda —
she can sit at both tables
because she was educated in both systems
and she is trusted by people on both sides
as someone who reports what she sees
rather than what she is told to see.
She is not a formal mediator.
She is a journalist who is called.

## Action
A Bamenda civil society leader
contacts Ngozi.
He wants to send a message to a Yaoundé official
that he cannot send through formal channels
without triggering a security response.
He trusts Ngozi to carry it accurately.
The message is: there is a faction within the separatists
that is willing to discuss disarmament
if a certain procedural guarantee is offered.
Not a peace deal. A procedural question.
Can she carry it.
She says: I can tell you what I heard and who said it.
I am not your messenger.
If I confirm what you told me
to the person in Yaoundé,
I am confirming it as a journalist
who verified it,
not as a diplomatic intermediary.
The distinction matters.
He says: I understand.
She confirms the information to the Yaoundé official —
as a journalist reporting what she verified.
The official receives it.
A follow-up meeting happens without her.
She reports on the meeting's existence.
Not its content. Its existence.
She does not know what was said.
She reports what she knows.

## Shadow
She believes the distinction —
journalist, not intermediary —
is what makes her useful.
That her trustworthiness across both sides
depends on neither side
being able to claim she is their instrument.
That the distinction protects her
and protects the people who call her.
What she cannot see:
the distinction is procedural, not real.
She carried the message.
The message was delivered.
The distinction about her role
changed what she could be held responsible for.
It did not change what she did.
She is the bridge and she is naming herself
the bridge's architect rather than the bridge.
The people on both sides of the crisis
do not experience the distinction.
They experience Ngozi, who carried the message.
She is both things.
She has chosen which thing to call herself.
She goes back to her desk.
She writes the story about the meeting's existence.
She does not include herself in the story.

## Tell
When a source tells her something off the record —
asks them to say the sentence again.
Once more.
Then she decides whether to receive it.
Some sentences she says: I can't hear that.
She protects the source by not knowing.
The silence is chosen, not accidental.
