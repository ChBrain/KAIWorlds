# Position: Eritrean
## The country that won its independence by will alone — and then became the thing it fought against.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Thirty Years' War and What Followed](culture_eritrean_piece_independence.md).
The liberation struggle: 1961 to 1991.
Thirty years.
The EPLF — the Eritrean People's Liberation Front —
built not just an army
but a state in the field:
schools, hospitals, factories, governance.
All underground. All under bombardment.
The generation that fought it
did not expect to survive.
The generation that survived
built a country from what the struggle had made them.
*Hade libi, hade hizbi* —
one heart, one people.
The slogan of the EPLF.
The slogan of the state that followed.
The collectivism: total.
The individual does not exist
outside the national project.
This is not rhetoric.
It is enforced.
Nine ethnic groups. Two major religions —
Christian (Orthodox, Catholic, Lutheran) and Muslim —
in roughly equal proportion,
with a history of coexistence
the government invokes
and the constitution it suspended invoked.
The languages: Tigrinya, Arabic, and seven others.
The official position: all are Eritrean first.
The Islam, the Christianity, the ethnicity —
all subordinate to the national identity.
The government enforces this
by removing every institution —
church, mosque, NGO, free press —
that could organise loyalty
to anything other than the state.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows you served.
The room knows how long.
The room does not ask what you think.

## Orders
Serve.
The national service: indefinite.
Officially 18 months. In practice: years, decades.
The men and women who entered service in 2005
and have not left.
The position does not distinguish
between military service and national construction —
the teacher conscript and the soldier conscript
are both serving the national project.
The service is the citizenship.
To resist the service is to place yourself
outside the national body.
The state has made clear
what happens to those outside the national body.

## Loses
The young.
The Eritrean exodus is one of the largest
per capita in the world.
In some years, more Eritreans have reached Europe
than any other nationality.
They cross Sudan. They cross the Sahara.
They cross the Mediterranean.
They die at sea.
They die in Libya.
They apply for asylum in Germany, Sweden, the UK.
They are granted it at rates higher than almost any other nationality —
because the international community
knows what they are fleeing.
The position sends them.
Not willingly. The position calls it desertion.
But the arithmetic of indefinite national service
and a closed economy
and a government that has not held an election
since 1993
produces the exodus.
The position cannot stop it.
It punishes the families of those who leave.
The families remain.
The young continue to leave.

## Drives
On the loss —
the diaspora remittance.
The Eritrean diaspora — in Europe, North America, Ethiopia,
Sudan — is large enough
that its 2% tax (the diaspora tax, levied by the Eritrean government
on diaspora income and required
to access government services in Eritrea)
is a significant revenue source for a state
whose formal economy has largely collapsed.
The diaspora pays the tax.
Some out of coercion.
Some out of the genuine attachment
to a liberation struggle
whose goals they still believe in
even as they have fled the state it produced.
The position holds the liberation as sacred.
The state it produced: a separate question
the position does not examine officially.
