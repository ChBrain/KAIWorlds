# Piece: The Secret War
## 1964–1973. The most heavily bombed country per capita in history — and the bombs that are still in the ground.

## Owner
- **Place:** Vientiane

## Place
Vientiane. The COPE Visitor Centre.
Prosthetics. Wheelchairs. The photographs.
The people who stepped on something
that was dropped fifty years ago.
The centre is beside the National Rehabilitation Centre.
The patients are current.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Secret War and Laos is a small landlocked country
that was drawn into the Vietnam War
and sustained significant damage.
With it: Laos is the most heavily bombed country
per capita in the history of aerial warfare —
270 million cluster bomblets dropped
between 1964 and 1973,
more than all the bombs dropped in World War II combined.
Called the Secret War because the United States
was officially neutral in Laos while conducting
580,000 bombing missions.
The secret is now known.
The bombs are not gone.
Approximately 80 million unexploded cluster bomblets
remain in the ground.
They kill and maim approximately 300 people per year.
Still. In 2024.
Remove the piece: a country affected by war.
With it: a country still being killed by a war
that ended before most of its current population was born,
conducted by a government that called it secret,
in service of a strategy that did not prevent
the outcome it was designed to prevent.
The *bor pen nyang* is not only philosophy.
It is also the position of a country
that cannot afford to be at war with its past
when its past is still in the ground
and people are still stepping on it
and the clearance will take another century at current rates.
The letting go is partly chosen.
It is partly the only thing available.

## Apparent
1953: independence from France.
1954: the Geneva Accords. Laos declared neutral.
1960s: the civil war between the Royal Lao Government,
the Pathet Lao, and the neutralist forces.
1964: the United States begins bombing the Ho Chi Minh Trail
through eastern Laos. Classified.
580,000 bombing missions. Nine planes per day, every day, for nine years.
270 million cluster bomblets.
1973: the ceasefire. The bombing stops.
1975: the Pathet Lao takes power. The Lao PDR established.
The Hmong — who had fought for the CIA — flee or are killed.
The re-education camps.
The exodus: 10% of the population leaves.
Today: 30% of the land mass is contaminated with UXO.
The clearance rate: at current pace, 100+ years to clear.
The United States has contributed to clearance funding.
The contribution is not proportionate to the contamination.
The Lao government does not press the point loudly.
The *bor pen nyang* is the foreign policy too.
