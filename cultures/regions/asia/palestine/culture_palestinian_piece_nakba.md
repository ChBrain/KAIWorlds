# Piece: The Nakba and the Key
## The catastrophe of 1948 — and the object that has carried the claim across three generations.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Ramallah / Gaza / The Camps](culture_palestinian_place_ramallah.md)

## Place
No single place.
The West Bank. Gaza. Beirut. Amman. Damascus.
Ramallah as the administrative centre
of the Palestinian Authority —
the government that governs
parts of the West Bank
and negotiates for a state
that does not yet exist.
Gaza as the territory
under Hamas governance and Israeli blockade
and now — since October 2023 —
under conditions the UN has described
as catastrophic.
The camps in Lebanon and Jordan:
Shatila. Balata. Jenin. Dheisheh.
Some established in 1948.
Still there.
Still called camps.
Now cities, in practice.
The geography of Palestinian life:
multiple, dispersed, none fully home.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Nakba
and Palestinian identity exists
as the identity of an Arab people
in historic Palestine
navigating the Ottoman period,
the British Mandate,
the emergence of Zionist settlement —
a contested but unresolved political situation
without the catastrophic rupture of 1948.
With it: the event that defines
every Palestinian conversation about identity,
rights, and the future.
The Nakba — Arabic for catastrophe:
the 1948 Arab-Israeli war
and its consequences for Palestinian Arabs.
700,000–750,000 Palestinians
fled or were expelled from their homes.
400–600 villages destroyed or depopulated.
The new state of Israel established
over the areas cleared.
The right of return: UN Resolution 194, December 1948.
*Refugees wishing to return to their homes
and live at peace with their neighbours
should be permitted to do so.*
The resolution: passed.
The return: not permitted.
The resolution: cited in every subsequent negotiation.
The key is the load-bearing object:
not a symbol chosen for effect.
The actual keys to actual houses
that actual families took with them
because they expected to return.
The houses demolished, sold, occupied.
The keys: preserved.
The *tatreez* embroidery is the second piece:
the cross-stitch patterns of Palestinian villages,
each village with its own specific pattern.
A woman from Beit Nuba
wears a different pattern
than a woman from Lifta.
Both villages destroyed in 1948.
Both patterns still worn
by the descendants in the camps.
The village absent from the map.
The pattern present in the cloth.
Remove the Nakba and the key and the embroidery:
a political conflict over a territory.
With them: a people who carry their history
in their pockets and on their bodies.

## Apparent
Late 1800s: Zionist immigration to Ottoman Palestine begins.
1917: Balfour Declaration.
British support for "a national home for the Jewish people" in Palestine.
"Nothing shall be done which may prejudice
the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities."
The phrase: not honoured.
1920–1948: British Mandate.
Jewish immigration accelerates.
Arab-Jewish violence accelerates.
1947: UN Partition Plan. Resolution 181.
Jews: 33% of population, receive 56% of territory.
Arabs: 67% of population, receive 44%.
Arab states reject.
1948: Israel declares independence. May 14.
Arab states invade. May 15.
The war.
The Nakba.
700,000–750,000 Palestinians displaced.
July 1948: Lydda and Ramle.
50,000–70,000 Palestinians expelled on foot.
The Lydda Death March.
1948–1949: armistice lines.
1967: Six-Day War. Israel occupies West Bank and Gaza.
1987: First Intifada.
1993: Oslo Accords.
Palestinian Authority established.
The two-state solution: framework agreed.
Implementation: stalled.
2006: Hamas wins Palestinian legislative elections.
2007: Hamas takes control of Gaza.
Gaza blockade begins.
2008, 2012, 2014, 2021: Gaza wars.
October 7, 2023: Hamas attacks Israel.
1,200 killed, 251 taken hostage.
October 8, 2023: Israeli military operations in Gaza.
By 2024: over 40,000 Palestinians killed.
2.3 million displaced within Gaza.
ICJ orders Israel to take measures
to prevent genocide.
The key: still in the drawer.
The embroidery: still worn.
The resolution: still cited.
