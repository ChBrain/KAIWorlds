## Shown
- Rudaki Avenue. The main boulevard. Named for the poet. The statues: Ismoil Somoni, Rudaki, the national heroes. A country constructing its identity in bronze along its central street.
- The National Museum of Tajikistan. The Bactrian gold, the Buddhist artefacts, the Zoroastrian fire altar. The deep history that predates Islam, that predates the Silk Road, displayed as the foundation of the national identity. The Islamic period displayed alongside, not instead of.
- The Rogun Dam construction site. Not visible from Dushanbe — it is 110 kilometres east in the Vakhsh Valley. But present in every conversation about the country's future. The dam that will be the tallest in the world when complete. The argument Tajikistan is making: we have clean electricity and the region needs it. The argument is in progress.
- The Green Bazaar. The produce, the dried fruit, the spices. The Pamiri vendors with their distinct features. The Uzbek traders. The Afghans across the Panj River, 80 kilometres south, in a world defined by the same river and a different government.
- The Russian Orthodox church. Still open. The Russian-speaking community it serves: shrinking. Each year fewer.

## Withheld
- The men who are not here. Dushanbe shows the boulevard and the bazaar and the statues of poets. It withholds the villages whose male population is in Russia — the specific absence that is present in every household, in every woman managing alone, in every child who knows their father from video calls and Eid visits. The room shows the capital. It withholds the economy the capital rests on, which is the labour of the people who are not in it.
