# Piece: The End of the Shah Dynasty
## 2008. The world's only Hindu kingdom becomes a federal democratic republic — and the question of what that means is still open.

## Owner
- **Place:** Kathmandu

## Place
Kathmandu. Narayanhiti Palace.
The royal palace where, on June 1, 2001,
Crown Prince Dipendra shot most of the royal family
and then himself.
Ten members of the royal family died.
The massacre was officially declared a suicide.
The country never fully believed the official account.
The palace is now a museum.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Shah dynasty's end
and Nepal is a Hindu monarchy
managing a Maoist insurgency
in a landlocked country between India and China.
With it: Nepal is the country that abolished
the world's last Hindu kingdom in 2008,
declared a federal democratic republic,
wrote a new constitution in 2015
after seven years of political negotiation,
and is still deciding whether the new form
has changed the old substance.
The 2001 palace massacre is load-bearing
not because it caused the republic —
the Maoist insurgency (1996–2006, 17,000 dead)
and the people's movement caused the republic —
but because it destroyed the legitimacy
of the institution the monarchy required.
A crown prince who killed his family
and shot himself was king for three days
while on life support.
The throne passed to his uncle,
who had been widely disliked for decades.
The institution that had held Nepal together
for 240 years lost its sacred aura
in one night.
The republic followed.
The constitution followed.
The migration follows.
Remove the piece: a transition in forms of government.
With it: a country that lost its sacred centre
in a single horrifying night
and has been building a replacement
from political material
that has not yet produced
what the sacred centre provided —
not legitimacy, but the feeling of continuity,
of the country knowing what it is.
The mountain is still there.
The country is still deciding what the mountain means
now that the king does not crown it.

## Apparent
1769: Prithvi Narayan Shah unifies Nepal. The Shah dynasty begins.
Nepal never colonised — it negotiated with the British,
ceded some territory, maintained sovereignty.
1950s: Nepal opens to the outside world.
Everest climbed 1953. The Sherpa and the outsider.
1960: King Mahendra suspends parliament, assumes direct rule.
The Panchayat system — partyless democracy.
1990: Jana Andolan — the People's Movement. Democracy restored.
1996: the Maoists begin their insurgency. The People's War.
June 1, 2001: the palace massacre.
2001–2005: the insurgency intensifies.
2005: King Gyanendra assumes direct rule.
2006: the second Jana Andolan. The king capitulates.
The Comprehensive Peace Agreement with the Maoists.
2007: the interim parliament. The Maoists join the government.
2008: the Constituent Assembly elected. The monarchy abolished.
Gyanendra leaves the palace.
2015: the constitution promulgated. Federal democratic republic.
Also 2015: the earthquake. 9,000 dead. The country rebuilds.
2015–2024: twelve governments in nine years.
The same faces rotating through the coalition.
The young people continue leaving.
The remittances continue arriving.
Everest continues being climbed.
The question of what Nepal is
continues not being answered.
