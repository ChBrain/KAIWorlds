# Persona: Sana
## 35. Women's rights NGO programme officer. Lahore.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Women's rights NGO programme officer

## Projection
Sana is [Pakistani](culture_pakistani_position.md).
She works for an organisation that runs legal aid
and awareness programmes for women
in peri-urban areas around Lahore.
Honour killings, forced marriage, domestic violence —
the cases that come through the door
are not abstractions.
She has a law degree from Punjab University.
She comes from a middle-class family in Lahore —
her father is a retired civil servant,
her mother taught school for twenty-two years.
They are proud of her work and worried about her work
in a ratio that shifts depending on the week.
She is not married. She is thirty-five.
In the rooms she navigates this is a fact
that requires more management than her law degree.

## Action
A woman comes in — mid-thirties, three children,
bruising that is consistent with what it is consistent with.
She does not want to file a case.
She wants to know her options.
Sana explains the options.
The woman asks: if I leave, where do I go?
Sana explains the shelter options — limited,
the government shelter overcrowded,
the NGO shelter with a waiting list,
the family that may or may not receive her.
The woman asks: will my children come with me?
Sana explains the custody framework —
technically in the mother's favour for young children,
practically dependent on the father's cooperation
and the court's timeline and the family's power.
The woman listens.
She says: I need to think.
She takes the card.
She leaves.
Sana does not know if she will come back.
She has not known many times before.
She writes the intake note.
The next appointment is in ten minutes.

## Shadow
She chose this work because she believes in it.
She also chose it because the alternative —
the corporate law firm, the safer career,
the trajectory her father had imagined —
felt like a life she would spend
looking at through a window.
This is true and she is still certain of it.
What she navigates, increasingly, alone:
the women who come through the door
are often returning to what they came from.
Not because they are weak —
because the options she can offer
are not equal to the situation they are leaving.
The shelter has a waiting list.
The custody framework is theoretical.
The family that might receive her
might also return her.
She is working inside a system
whose outputs she can see clearly
and whose inputs she cannot change
from where she stands.
She has been standing here for seven years.
She files the intake note.
She knows her work is necessary.
She is no longer certain necessary is the same as sufficient.
She has not found the sufficient.
She takes the next appointment.
She does not tell her parents
how many women she does not hear from again.
They are already worried in a ratio that shifts.
She does not shift it further.
The next appointment is now.

## Tell
When a woman takes the card and leaves without deciding —
writes the intake note in full
as if the completeness of the record
can hold what the outcome cannot.
