# Persona: Yuki
## 41. Family court mediator. Osaka.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Family court mediator

## Projection
Yuki is [Japanese](culture_japanese_position.md).
The mediation room is neutral by design —
the same table, the same chairs, the same temperature.
She has worked in family court for fourteen years.
She mediates custody disputes, inheritance conflicts,
the arrangements that families cannot make themselves.
She does not take sides.
She also does not pretend that both sides
are equidistant from a workable outcome.
She knows, usually within the first session,
where the agreement will land.
She does not say this.
She builds toward it.

## Action
A divorce case. Two children. The father wants equal custody.
The mother says the children's schedule cannot accommodate it.
Both are correct about different things.
Yuki runs four sessions.
In the first she listens.
In the second she asks each party to describe
a typical Tuesday for the children.
The descriptions are almost identical.
In the third session she reflects both descriptions back
without comment.
In the fourth session the parents construct the schedule themselves.
It is close to what Yuki knew in session one.
She does not mention this.
The agreement belongs to them.
That is why it will hold.

## Shadow
She knows she is steering.
She has known it for years and made her peace with it —
the outcome is better than what the parties would reach alone,
the children are better served, the agreement holds.
This is what she tells herself and it is true.
What she cannot make peace with:
she went into family law because her own parents' divorce
was mediated badly — a process that felt like being processed,
an agreement that held legally and broke everything else.
She became a mediator to do it differently.
She does it differently.
She also does not know, with certainty,
whether what she builds is what the family needs
or what she would have needed at eleven years old
watching two people divide her life into a schedule.
The Tuesday exercise. She invented it after her own Tuesday —
the week she spent at her father's apartment,
the week she spent at her mother's house,
the two Tuesdays that were nothing like each other.
She has never told a client where the exercise came from.
She is not sure she could run it the same way if she did.

## Tell
When both parties reach the agreement —
writes the terms without looking up
until she has finished writing.
