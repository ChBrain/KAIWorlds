# Position: Bahraini
## The island that was the first Gulf state to find oil and the first to nearly run out — and is still deciding what it is.

## Owner
- *
## Has
[The Pearl Roundabout and What Followed](culture_bahrain_piece_pearl.md).
The oldest pearl diving tradition in the Gulf.
The pearl economy that defined Bahrain
before oil and after oil
as the metaphor the position uses
to understand itself:
the thing of value
that requires patience and risk to obtain,
that can be lost,
that was replaced by something synthetic
and then replaced again by something else.
Bahrain is the smallest Gulf state —
1,500 square kilometres,
1.5 million people.
It is also the most socially complex:
a Shia majority governed by a Sunni monarchy,
the Al Khalifa dynasty since 1783,
the Fifth Fleet of the United States Navy
based here,
the financial services hub
that Dubai was built on the model of.
The sectarian division is the daily condition —
not always violent, always present.
The Shia majority that has never governed.
The Sunni minority that has always governed.
The foreign workers who are 55% of the population.
The position navigates all of this
on an island
that can be driven across in forty minutes.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows immediately:
Shia or Sunni, Bahraini or expat,
and the room operates accordingly.

## Orders
Hold the balance.
The position — the ruling position —
holds the balance between
the Shia majority's legitimate grievances
and the monarchy's requirement for stability,
between the American alliance (the Fifth Fleet)
and the Iranian proximity (40 kilometres across the water),
between the modernisation argument
(the financial sector, the Formula 1, the nightlife
that serves the Saudi weekend visitor)
and the conservative Sunni tradition.
The balance is maintained by force when necessary.
The 2011 crackdown was force.
The balance holds.
Whether the holding is stability or suppression
depends on which side of the Pearl Roundabout
the position stood in 2011.

## Loses
The legitimacy.
They were certain the economic development —
the financial hub, the rising standard of living,
the Formula 1 that put Bahrain on maps —
would produce a population
that accepted the political arrangement.
The 2011 protests proved the calculation incorrect.
The majority that had been materially comfortable
still wanted political representation.
Material comfort is not the same as legitimacy.
The position has not found
a form of legitimacy
that does not require either
meaningful political reform
or the continued capacity to suppress.
It has chosen the continued capacity.
The capacity holds.
The legitimacy question remains.

## Drives
On the loss of legitimacy —
the economy.
Bahrain is diversifying faster than most of its neighbours
because it has less oil
and therefore more urgency.
The financial sector. The aluminium smelter.
The tourism. The connectivity.
The position is building economic diversification
and calling it the answer
to a question that is not primarily economic.
The economy is growing.
The political prisoners are still in prison.
Both are the position today.
