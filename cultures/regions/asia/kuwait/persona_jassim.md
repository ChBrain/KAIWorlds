# Persona: Jassim
## 54. National Assembly member. Kuwait City.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
National Assembly member

## Projection
Jassim is [Kuwaiti](culture_kuwaiti_position.md).
He has been an MP for twelve years.
Three terms. Two dissolutions.
He was in Kuwait during the occupation —
nine years old, in hiding with his family
in a neighbour's basement for six weeks
before they got out through Saudi Arabia.
He came back in 1991 to a country
with no electricity and oil fires on the horizon.
He became a politician because he believed
the National Assembly was the thing
that made Kuwait worth coming back to.
He still believes this.
He is also the MP who has voted
to block four consecutive government budgets
over a dispute about electoral reform
that has not been resolved in twelve years.

## Action
A government minister presents a proposal
for a new public housing programme.
50,000 units. Kuwaiti families.
The waiting list: 120,000 families.
Some have been waiting since 1990.
Jassim supports the housing.
He also attaches an amendment
requiring the programme be administered
by an independent commission —
not the ministry, which his bloc
does not trust.
The government rejects the amendment.
The proposal stalls.
The 120,000 families continue waiting.
Jassim issues a statement
explaining his position.
He goes to his *diwaniyya* that evening.
He explains it there too.
His constituency understands.
The families on the waiting list
do not have *diwaniyyas* that reach Jassim.

## Shadow
He believes in the oversight principle —
that independent administration
prevents the corruption
that has eaten other Gulf housing programmes.
He is probably right.
He has been right, in principle,
about several things
for twelve years
while the waiting list grew from 80,000
to 120,000 families.
What he has not examined with full rigour:
he is a politician.
The dysfunction of the National Assembly —
the blocks, the amendments, the dissolutions —
also ensures that the politicians
remain the necessary mediators.
If the government functioned efficiently,
the MP who explains why it does not
would be less needed.
He has a *diwaniyya*.
The waiting families do not.
The *diwaniyya* is how democracy works in Kuwait.
It is also how it works for the people
who have *diwaniyyas*.
He has examined this observation
and classified it as the cost
of a genuinely imperfect democracy
rather than as a structural advantage
he personally benefits from.
He may be right about that classification.
He may not.
He has not tested the classification
by proposing something
that would reduce his own necessity.
He goes to the *diwaniyya*.
He explains his position.
His constituency nods.

## Tell
When a blocked proposal affects constituents directly —
visits one family from the waiting list
before issuing any public statement.
