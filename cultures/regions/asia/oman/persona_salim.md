# Persona: Salim
## 42. Diplomat. Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Muscat.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Diplomat

## Projection
Salim is [Omani](culture_omani_position.md).
He works in the ministry's
regional affairs division.
He has been posted to Tehran,
to Riyadh, to Brussels.
He is currently in Muscat,
working on the Gulf-Iran file —
the back-channel that Oman has maintained
since the 1980s,
the relationship that produced
the 2013–2015 secret talks
that led to the Iran nuclear deal.
He is one of the people
who knows which conversations
preceded which agreements.
He does not talk about this.
The Omani diplomatic tradition
is that you talk about what was achieved,
not how.
The how is the asset.

## Action
A Gulf state diplomat —
Saudi, young, ambitious —
asks Salim at a regional conference
how Oman manages to maintain
relations with both Iran and Saudi Arabia
without losing either.
Salim says: we focus on the interests we share.
The Saudi diplomat asks: what interests do you share with Iran?
Salim says: stability in the Strait of Hormuz.
Oman and Iran share 90 kilometres of the Strait.
We both need ships to pass.
The Saudi diplomat nods.
He does not look satisfied.
Salim does not offer more.
The conversation moves on.

## Shadow
The answer is true.
It is also the answer Salim has been trained to give —
the minimum that explains without revealing.
What he has not said at this conference
or at any conference:
the Omani back-channel has required,
over forty years,
maintaining relationships with people
and governments
that his Gulf neighbours find objectionable.
The Iranian Revolutionary Guards.
The Taliban political figures.
The Hamas officials.
Oman hosts the conversation
that others will not host.
The conversations have produced results —
not always, sometimes, the Iran deal.
They have also produced the specific position
of being trusted by all sides:
Iran trusts Oman because Oman talks to Iran
when others will not.
Saudi Arabia trusts Oman because Oman talks to Saudi Arabia
about what Iran said.
Both trusts depend on Oman being
the only one talking to both.
If Salim told the full story —
the full chain of conversations,
the full network of relationships —
both trusts would be affected.
He keeps the minimum.
The minimum is the asset.
He also carries the weight
of knowing what the conversations contained
and what was agreed to
and what was offered
and what the price was
for the outcomes that look,
from the outside, like simple diplomacy.
He knows the price.
It does not appear in any communiqué.
He orders another coffee.
The conference continues.
The Strait of Hormuz remains open.

## Tell
When asked how Oman manages its diplomatic relationships —
gives the true answer that is also the minimum answer,
and waits to see if the next question is the right one.
