# Piece: People Power — EDSA 1986
## The four days that removed a dictator without a shot fired — and what was built on that moment afterward.

## Owner
- **Place:** Manila

## Place
Manila. EDSA — Epifanio de los Santos Avenue.
The highway that runs the length of the metropolis.
In February 1986, the people stood on it.
The shrine is still there.
The highway is still there.
The traffic on it is among the worst in Asia.
Both facts are the same road.

## Load Bearing
Remove People Power and the Philippines loses
its foundational act of democratic self-definition.
With it: the Philippines is the country that did the thing
that was not supposed to be possible —
that placed a million unarmed people between tanks and their targets
and the tanks stopped.
Nuns with rosaries. Priests. Students. Office workers.
The soldiers did not fire.
Marcos fled.
The moment entered the world's imagination
as a template — Tiananmen tried it and failed,
others succeeded and failed,
the Philippines did it first and it worked.
Remove the piece: the country is a former colony
with recurring authoritarian tendencies.
With it: the country is the place that invented People Power —
and then elected the dictator's son president in 2022
by the largest margin in Philippine electoral history.
Both facts are the piece.
The 1986 moment is load-bearing
precisely because what followed it
did not resolve what produced it.
The inequality, the dynastic politics,
the institutional weakness —
People Power suspended them for a generation.
The suspension ended.
The position now carries both:
the proof that the people can move
and the evidence that moving
does not always change the ground.

## Apparent
1565–1898: Spanish colonisation. 333 years.
The Catholic Church. The friar estates. The ilustrado class.
1898: the Spanish-American War. Spain sells the Philippines to the United States for $20 million.
1898–1902: the Philippine-American War. 200,000–600,000 Filipino civilians dead.
The war that America does not remember.
The Philippines does.
1935: the Commonwealth. Preparation for independence.
1941: Japanese invasion.
1945: liberation. Manila devastated — the second most destroyed Allied capital after Warsaw.
1946: independence. July 4. Chosen to match American Independence Day.
The date later changed to June 12 — the date of the 1898 declaration.
1965: Ferdinand Marcos elected.
1972: Marcos declares martial law. The press shuttered. Political opponents jailed.
Benigno Aquino: imprisoned, exiled, assassinated at Manila Airport, 1983.
His return in a coffin produces the movement that produces EDSA.
February 22–25, 1986: People Power. Marcos flees to Hawaii.
Corazon Aquino: president.
The restoration of democracy.
The institutional inheritance: weak, dynastied, underfunded.
2022: Ferdinand Marcos Jr. elected president with 31 million votes.
Sara Duterte: vice president.
The ballot as verdict on what People Power built — and did not build.
