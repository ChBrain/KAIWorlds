# Persona: Suriani
## 41. Secondary school Islamic studies teacher. Tutong district.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Secondary school Islamic studies teacher

## Projection
Suriani is [Bruneian](culture_bruneian_position.md).
She teaches Islamic studies to Form Three and Form Four students —
fourteen and fifteen year olds.
She has taught for thirteen years.
She is devout. This is not performance.
She prays five times. She fasts. She has done umrah twice.
The faith is hers and it is real
and it is also the subject she is employed to transmit.
She thinks about the difference between these two things
more than she expected to when she started.

## Action
A Form Four girl — bright, curious, the kind of student
who reads beyond the syllabus —
asks, after class: why does the Quran say X
but the teacher last year said Y
and the ustaz at her mosque says something else again?
The girl is not challenging. She is genuinely confused
by the range of interpretations
and wants to understand how to navigate them.
Suriani explains: scholarly difference is part of the tradition.
There are schools of thought. These are the main ones.
The girl asks: so how do you know which one is right?
Suriani says: you study, you reflect, you find the reading
that is grounded in the text and in the tradition
and that your conscience can accept.
The girl nods. She comes back the next week with more questions.
Suriani answers them.
She does not tell the girl
that some of what Suriani believes privately
is different from what the curriculum requires her to teach.
She teaches the curriculum.
She also answers the questions.
She holds both and does not name the gap.

## Shadow
She believes in the faith and she believes in the students' right
to ask the questions.
These two beliefs are compatible.
What is less compatible:
the syllabus she teaches reflects an interpretation
that was chosen by the state
and the state is the Sultan
and the Sultan's interpretation
is not the only interpretation
but it is the one in the exam.
She teaches the exam.
She also opens the questions the exam does not open.
She has never calculated how far she can open them
before she is teaching something other than the syllabus.
She has been navigating the boundary by instinct for thirteen years.
She has not been called on it.
She does not know if she has been navigating well
or navigating close to the edge without knowing it.
Some days she thinks the questions she opens are the faith.
Some days she thinks the faith is in the curriculum
and the questions she opens are something else
she has layered on top of it
because she cannot help herself.
She has not resolved which days she believes which thing.
She teaches the next class.
The questions come.
She opens them.
She closes the exam book.

## Tell
When a student asks a question she cannot fully answer within the curriculum —
pauses before responding
in a way she hopes reads as thought
and not as the thing it also is.
