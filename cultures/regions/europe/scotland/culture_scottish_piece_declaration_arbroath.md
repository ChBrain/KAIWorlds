# Piece: The Declaration of Arbroath 1320
## The letter that said: even if our king surrenders, we do not.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Edinburgh](culture_scottish_place_edinburgh.md)

## Place
[Edinburgh](culture_scottish_place_edinburgh.md).
Not behind glass in a museum display.
In the legal architecture.
In the specific insistence
that sovereignty does not reside in the monarch
but in the community of the realm.
In 1320, when most of Europe
was building the opposite argument.

## Load Bearing
Without the Declaration of Arbroath
the Scottish position loses its most specific claim:
that the consent of the people
precedes the legitimacy of the king.
*It is not glory, it is not riches, neither is it honour,
but it is liberty alone that we fight and contend for.*
Written to the Pope.
Not a declaration of independence in the modern sense.
A declaration of the conditions
under which independence is the only possible outcome.
Remove this piece and the position becomes
a set of institutional differences without an argument.
With it: the institutional differences
are the expression of something older
than the Act of Union.

## Apparent
The document. Held at the National Records of Scotland.
Seven hundred years old.
Still cited.
Still placed in the argument
every time the question returns.
The thistle. The language - Scots, Gaelic, English -
three languages in the same country,
none of them dominant.
The separate legal system, still running.
The separate education system,
the one that gave Scotland
a university system older than England's Oxford.
The Saltire. The St Andrew's cross.
Older than the Union Jack.
Still flying when the Union Jack is not.

## Yearbook
1320: The Declaration. Scotland not yet England's.
1603: Union of the Crowns. James VI of Scotland becomes James I of England. One king. Two kingdoms.
1707: Acts of Union. The parliaments merge. The legal system does not.
1745: The last Jacobite rising. Culloden. The suppression of the Highlands.
1999: The Scottish Parliament re-established at Holyrood after a gap of 292 years.
2014: Independence referendum. 55% No. The question does not close.
2016: Scotland votes 62% Remain in the EU referendum.
      The UK votes Leave. Scotland's vote is overridden.
      The question reopens.
