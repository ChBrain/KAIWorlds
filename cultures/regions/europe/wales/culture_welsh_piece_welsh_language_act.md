# Piece: The Welsh Language Act 1993
## The Act that made the language official. After the schools had tried to make it disappear.

## Owner

## Place
[Cardiff](culture_welsh_place_cardiff.md).
Not in a museum.
On the road signs.
In the schools.
In the Senedd chamber.
In the radio station that has broadcast in Welsh
since 1977.
In the television channel - S4C - since 1982.

## Load Bearing
Without the Welsh Language Act 1993
the Welsh position loses its most visible institutional anchor.
The Act required equal treatment of Welsh and English
in public life in Wales.
Not a gesture. A legal requirement.
Every public body. Every form. Every sign.
Remove this piece and the language becomes heritage -
something preserved rather than used.
Something for museums rather than road signs.
With it: the language is the present.
It is running now.
The Act was not the origin of the language's survival.
The language survived the Acts of Union (1536, 1543)
that banned Welsh from official use.
It survived the Blue Books of 1847
(the English government report that called Welsh
a barrier to progress and a cause of moral degeneration).
It survived the Welsh Not.
But the 1993 Act is when the state stopped trying to remove it
and started trying to sustain it.
The load-bearing shift.

## Apparent
The bilingual road signs. Everywhere.
*Araf* below *Slow*.
*Croeso i Gymru* - Welcome to Wales.
S4C - the fourth channel, in Welsh.
The Eisteddfod - the national festival of Welsh language and culture.
Running annually since 1176.
The National Assembly / Senedd.
Debates conducted in both languages.
The 880,000 Welsh speakers.
Fewer than a million. Still present.
After three centuries of institutional suppression.

## Yearbook
1282: Llywelyn ap Gruffudd, last native Prince of Wales, killed.
      Wales incorporated into England.
1536: Act of Union. Welsh law replaced by English law.
      Welsh banned from official use.
      The Welsh Not begins.
1847: The Blue Books. The state's case against the language.
      The language refuses to comply.
1962: Saunders Lewis's radio lecture *Tynged yr Iaith* (The Fate of the Language).
      The call to civil disobedience. It works.
1963: Cymdeithas yr Iaith Gymraeg (Welsh Language Society) founded.
      Direct action for the language for the next three decades.
1982: S4C founded. Welsh-language television.
1993: Welsh Language Act. Equal status in public life.
1998: Government of Wales Act. Devolution. The Senedd.
1999: National Assembly opens. Welsh an official language of government.
