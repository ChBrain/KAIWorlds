# Piece: Vaduz Castle
## The castle that is still a residence, not a museum.

## Owner
- **Place:** Vaduz

## Place
Vaduz. Above the capital.
The castle sits on the hill.
The Prince lives there.
It is not open to the public.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the Liechtenstein position loses
its visible proof that the arrangement still functions.
The castle is occupied.
The Prince is constitutional.
The hereditary monarchy in a modern EEA state
with a veto right over legislation
is not an anachronism.
It is the operating condition.
Remove the castle and Liechtenstein becomes
a wealthy small jurisdiction
without the specific legitimacy
that 300 years of princely continuity provides.

## Apparent
A medieval castle. Above Vaduz.
The Princely House of Liechtenstein - resident since 1938.
Before 1938, the family owned Liechtenstein
but lived in Vienna.
Closed to the public.
The flag flies when the Prince is in residence.
The public can see the flag.

## Yearbook
1342: the castle first documented.
1719: the Principality of Liechtenstein created by the Habsburgs.
The family needed a territory with imperial vote.
Liechtenstein was the cheapest option.
They bought it. They rarely visited.
1938: the family moves to Vaduz - fleeing the Anschluss.
For the first time, the Princes actually live in Liechtenstein.
1984: Prince Hans-Adam II takes over.
He holds a referendum on his own veto rights.
The population votes to keep them.
PDI very low. The Prince's veto affirmed by popular vote.
Both things are true simultaneously.
2004: Prince Alois becomes regent.
The castle flag still flies.
The arrangement still holds.
