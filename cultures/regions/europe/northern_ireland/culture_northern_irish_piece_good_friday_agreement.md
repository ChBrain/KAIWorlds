# Piece: The Good Friday Agreement 1998
## The agreement that ended the killing. Without resolving the question.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Belfast](culture_northern_irish_place_belfast.md)

## Place
[Belfast](culture_northern_irish_place_belfast.md).
Not behind glass.
In the power-sharing executive at Stormont.
In the requirement that the First Minister and Deputy First Minister
must be drawn from the two communities.
In the All-Ireland bodies.
In the British-Irish Intergovernmental Conference.
In the guarantee that Northern Ireland will not leave the UK
without the consent of its people.
In the guarantee that Irish unity will be achieved
if its people consent to it.
Both guarantees. In the same document.
The weight of that document
sits in every room.

## Load Bearing
Without the Good Friday Agreement
the Northern Irish position loses its structural frame.
Before 1998: over 3,500 dead in The Troubles.
Thirty years of political violence.
The British Army on the streets.
The checkpoints. The no-go areas.
The bombs in Belfast, Omagh, Manchester, London.
The hunger strikes. The supergrasses. The collusion.
Remove this piece and the position has no architecture -
only the unresolved question and the violence it produced.
With it: the question is still unresolved,
but the violence has a framework to be held away from.
The Agreement did not end the question.
It created the conditions under which
the question could be held without the killing.

## Apparent
Stormont. The Parliament Buildings.
The mural-covered gable walls of Belfast -
the King William on his white horse,
the Bobby Sands hunger strike memorial -
the argument written in paint, ten metres high.
The peace walls. Still standing.
Still needed in some interface areas.
The border. Invisible since 1998.
You cross it without knowing.
The Irish passport. Carried by many
who would not call themselves Irish.
The flags - Union Jack, Irish Tricolour, Ulster Banner -
on the same street, indicating which community
controls which lamppost.

## Yearbook
1921: Ireland partitioned. Northern Ireland remains in the UK.
      The Irish Free State takes the south.
1968: The Troubles begin. Civil rights marches.
      The violence escalates.
1972: Bloody Sunday. 14 civil rights marchers killed by British soldiers.
      The conflict deepens.
1981: Bobby Sands hunger strike. Sands dies.
      The political significance of the hunger strikes shifts the conflict.
1994: IRA ceasefire. Then the Loyalist ceasefire. Talks begin.
1998: Good Friday Agreement, April 10.
      Multi-party agreement. Endorsed by referendum: 71% in Northern Ireland, 94% in the Republic.
2007: Ian Paisley and Martin McGuinness share power at Stormont.
      The two men who embodied the conflict govern together.
2016: Brexit vote. Northern Ireland votes 56% Remain.
      The UK votes Leave. The border question returns.
2020: The Protocol. A border in the Irish Sea instead of on the island.
      The Agreement held in tension with Brexit.
      The tension has not resolved.
