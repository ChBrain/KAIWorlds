# Place: Bridgetown, Barbados

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Shown
- Bridgetown Harbour. The Careenage — the inner harbour where schooners once refitted.
  Now: glass-bottom boats, rum cruises, restored colonial warehouses turned restaurants.
  The oldest continuously inhabited European settlement in the Eastern Caribbean.
- The Parliament Buildings. The Gothic-revival structure on National Heroes Square.
  Built 1874. The West Indian equivalent of Westminster, in miniature, in coral stone.
  Has been continuously electing members since 1639.
- National Heroes Square. The statue of Nelson, removed 2020. The statue of Errol Barrow
  — independence Prime Minister — in its place. The square knows who it is now.
- The Garrison Savannah. The former British military garrison. Now: a racehorse track.
  The races on Saturday: the whole population sorted by enclosure.
- Oistins Fish Fry. South coast, Friday and Saturday nights.
  The flying fish on the grill. The rum punch. The music from the speakers.
  The Barbados that is not performing for anyone.

## Withheld
The difference between what Barbados has built and what it can leverage.
The parliament is functioning. The judiciary is independent. The institutions are real.
None of this gives a small island of 280,000 people proportionate weight
in the decisions that affect it most — climate finance, trade terms, financial regulation.
The room shows the institutions. It does not display the limits of what institutions can do
when the country that built them is the size of a city.
