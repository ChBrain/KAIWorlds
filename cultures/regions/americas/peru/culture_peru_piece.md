# Piece: The Ayllu and What Survived
## The oldest collective institution in the Americas, and the period that tried hardest to destroy it.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** Lima

## Place
The Andes. The altiplano.
Everywhere and nowhere in particular —
the *ayllu* is not a building.
It is a form of organisation
that has outlasted every empire
that has tried to replace it.
Also: Ayacucho. Huanta. Putis. Accomarca.
The villages that appear in the CVR.
The names that the Truth and Reconciliation Commission
spent two years collecting.
Also: Lima, Callao, San Juan de Lurigancho —
the shantytowns where the *ayllu* migrated
and became the *asociación de vecinos*,
the *comité de vaso de leche*,
the *junta de regantes* —
different name, same structure.

## Load Bearing
Remove the *ayllu* and IDV 16 is inexplicable.
How does a country with PDI 64 —
a clear hierarchy, the *patrón* expected and accepted —
also produce IDV 16,
one of the most collectivist scores in the Americas?
The answer is: two collectivisms coexisting.
The vertical collectivism of the Spanish colonial order
— hierarchy, deference, the patron —
and the horizontal collectivism of the Andean *ayllu*
— reciprocity, *ayni* (mutual help), communal land tenure.
Both are real. Neither has disappeared.
The *ayllu* predates the Inca.
It survived the Inca.
It survived the Conquest.
It survived the Republic's land reforms.
It survived Sendero Luminoso and the army both,
which treated it alternately as enemy infrastructure
and as human shield.
The CVR's finding: 75% of the 69,000 killed were
indigenous, rural, and Quechua-speaking.
The *ayllu* was the primary target
of a conflict it had not started.
It survived.
It is still the operating unit
of rural Andean Peru.
In Lima's shantytowns,
it is the operating unit under different names.
Remove it and Peru is a Spanish colonial legacy with an indigenous population.
With it, Peru is the society where the deepest institution
is older than everything imposed upon it,
and has outlasted everything imposed upon it,
and IDV 16 is not a statistic
but a measurement of that institution's persistence.

## Apparent
Pre-Inca: the *ayllu* as the basic unit of Andean society.
Extended kinship group. Communal land. *Ayni*: reciprocity.
*Minka*: collective labour for community benefit.
*Mit'a*: rotational labour service — later appropriated by the Inca state, then by the Spanish.
1438–1533: the Inca empire. Uses and absorbs the *ayllu* structure.
1532: Francisco Pizarro arrives. Atahualpa captured, ransomed, killed.
The Inca empire falls. The *ayllu* does not.
1542: the Viceroyalty of Peru. The Spanish colonial system.
*Reducciones*: indigenous people forcibly concentrated into towns.
The *ayllu* continues inside the *reducción*.
1821: Independence. The Republic of Peru.
The *ayllu* is legally invisible. It continues.
1969: Velasco Agrarian Reform. Land redistribution.
The *comunidades campesinas* legally recognised.
Approximately 6,000 still exist.
1980: the Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso) begins.
Maoist insurgency rooted in Ayacucho.
The army's counter-insurgency response.
Andean communities trapped between both.
1983–1985: the worst years. Accomarca. Putis.
Mass graves.
The *rondas campesinas* — community self-defence patrols —
organised from within the *ayllu* structure
to resist both Sendero and the army.
2001–2003: the CVR. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission.
69,280 killed. 75% indigenous.
The Commission names the dead.
The state partially acknowledges.
No senior military officer is convicted.
The *ayllu* continues.
Today: Lima is the city where rural Peru arrives.
San Juan de Lurigancho: largest district in Peru.
The water committee. The neighbourhood association.
The communal kitchen. The *vaso de leche*.
The *ayllu* in urban form,
under names the urban government can process.
The oldest institution in the Americas
is also the newest arrival in the capital.
