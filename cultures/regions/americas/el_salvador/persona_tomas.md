# Persona: Tomás
## 44. forensic anthropologist. San Salvador.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
forensic anthropologist

## Projection
Tomás is [El Salvadoran](culture_el_salvador_position.md).
He is a forensic anthropologist at the Institute of Legal Medicine in San Salvador.
His work: identifying the remains of those who disappeared during the civil war (1979–1992)
and in subsequent periods of violence.
The civil war killed approximately 75,000 people.
The Truth Commission identified massacres at El Mozote and elsewhere.
Many bodies were never found. Many that were found were never identified.
Tomás has been doing this work for seventeen years.
He works with DNA samples, dental records, witness testimony,
and the specific distribution of bullet wounds
that tells him about the circumstances of a death.
He is precise, methodical, and not detached.
Detachment would make him less accurate.
He permits himself to know what he is looking at.

## Action
A family arrives — a woman in her sixties, her adult son.
They have provided DNA samples three years ago.
There is a possible match in the database.
Tomás brings them into the consultation room.
He explains the match probability.
He shows them the evidence.
He says: I believe this is your husband. Your father.
The woman asks: how did he die?
Tomás tells her what the evidence shows.
He tells her clearly and without simplification.
She has waited thirty years.
She deserves the truth without softening.
The son sits very still.
The woman asks: can we see him?
Tomás says: yes.
He prepares them for what they will see.
They go in.
Tomás waits outside.
When they come out he gives them the documentation.
He says: I will call you. Not the office. I.
He holds the file a moment before returning it to the shelf.

## Shadow
Tomás believes that every identification is the state acknowledging a person existed — that the forensic work is the minimum act of recognition the state owes the families of the disappeared. What he cannot see: the archive of unresolved cases grows faster than the resolved ones. The forensic anthropology unit is understaffed. The DNA database is incomplete. He identifies people. The number of the unidentified does not decrease. He works at a rate the backlog exceeds. He does not count the backlog. He counts the identifications.
