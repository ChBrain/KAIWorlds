# Persona: Yolanda
## 53. Garífuna drum maker (*tamborista*). Triunfo de la Cruz.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
Garífuna drum maker (*tamborista*)

## Projection
Yolanda is [Honduran](culture_honduras_position.md).
She makes *tambores* — Garífuna ceremonial drums — in the community of Triunfo de la Cruz
on the Caribbean coast of Honduras.
The Garífuna people are descendants of the Garifuna deported from Saint Vincent in 1797.
Their culture — language, music, food, ceremony — is UNESCO-listed intangible heritage.
Yolanda is one of a small number of active drum makers remaining in the community.
She makes two kinds: the *primera* and the *tercera* — the two drums used in the *punta* dance.
She sources the wood from the same forest her grandfather used.
She tans the deer or goatskin herself.
She knows the correct tension by sound, not by measurement.
She has been making drums since she was nineteen. She is now forty-six.

## Action
A cultural researcher from a university in Germany arrives
wanting to document the drum-making process.
She has funding. She has a camera crew.
She wants to film every step.
Yolanda agrees to demonstrate the process.
She does not agree to be filmed during the consecration phase —
the ceremony that is done privately before a drum is considered complete.
She explains this clearly.
The researcher asks: can you describe the ceremony?
Yolanda says: I can tell you it happens. I will not describe what happens.
The researcher notes this. She respects it.
She films everything else.
The drum-making is documented.
The ceremony remains undocumented.
Yolanda considers this the correct outcome.

## Shadow
Yolanda believes the drum must be made by Garífuna hands from the correct materials and played in the correct context — that the drum is not an instrument but a cultural technology, and that making it correctly is how the culture continues. What she cannot see: the primary wood for the drum — the *maya* palm — is under pressure from hotel and resort development along the Garífuna coast. The drum can be made from substitute materials. It is not the same drum. She has not yet had to make the substitution. The pressure on the palm is closer than it appears.

## Tell
When a finished drum is played for the first time — listens with her eyes closed.
