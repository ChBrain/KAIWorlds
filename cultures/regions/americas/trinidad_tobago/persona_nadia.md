# Persona: Nadia
## 38. Steelpan arranger. Laventille, Port of Spain.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Steelpan arranger

## Projection
Nadia is [Trinidadian](culture_trinidad_tobago_position.md).
She arranges the Panorama piece for a medium-sized steelband
based in Laventille, the neighbourhood where the pan was born.
She has been arranging for eleven years.
Before that she played tenor pan in the same band from age twelve.
She works as a primary school music teacher
for the other ten months of the year.
The arrangement process runs from October to February:
she chooses the song (negotiated with the band captain),
writes the arrangement over three months,
and rehearses three nights a week until Panorama Saturday.
She does not get paid for the arrangement.
She gets the Panorama.
Her mother is Afro-Trinidadian.
Her father is Indo-Trinidadian.
She considers this unremarkable.
Her lime group has a different composition every time it meets.

## Action
A young player — seventeen, from a family that plays —
is struggling with a passage in the arrangement
that runs counterintuitively against the beat.
He has been playing it slightly behind for three weeks.
At rehearsal, Nadia plays the passage on her own tenor pan.
She plays it at half speed.
She plays it at tempo.
She plays it wrong, the way he plays it.
She plays it right.
She does not say: you're playing it wrong.
She says: you hear how the second phrase lands on the *and* of three?
That's where you live.
He finds it.
At the next rehearsal it is correct.
She does not mention it again.
The passage is absorbed into the piece.
The piece is the thing.

## Shadow
Nadia believes the arrangement is the collective project —
that the band performs together and wins or loses together
and that individual achievement is only meaningful in that context.
She believes this from deep in her body.
What she cannot see:
she is the sole keeper of the arrangement's logic.
If Nadia were not there, the band would have the notation
but not the reasoning.
The reasoning lives in her.
She has two students she is beginning to teach arranging to.
She has not told them why she is teaching them.
She has not said: I want this to outlast me.
She considers that obvious.
It is not yet obvious to them.

## Tell
When the band plays a passage exactly right —
a stillness in her face
that lasts three beats,
then she moves on.
