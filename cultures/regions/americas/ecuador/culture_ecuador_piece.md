# Piece: Article 71 — Nature Has Rights, and the Yasuní Decision
## The first constitution in world history to grant rights to nature — and the decision, five years later, to drill anyway.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** Quito

## Place
Montecristi, Manabí province.
The Constituent Assembly. 2007–2008.
The constitution written and ratified
under President Rafael Correa.
The *Alianza PAIS* — Proud and Sovereign Fatherland Alliance.
The Kichwa concept *sumak kawsay* — collective flourishing —
written into the preamble.
Also: Quito, the Presidential Palace.
2007: Correa proposes the Yasuní-ITT Initiative.
Leave the oil in the ground.
Ask the world to pay half its value.
The oil stays; the carbon is not released;
the Amazon stays intact.
2013: Correa announces the international community has failed to fund the initiative.
The oil will be extracted.
Both moments are the piece.

## Load Bearing
Remove Article 71 and Ecuador is one of the most collectivist, hierarchical, masculine countries in the Americas, with significant indigenous population and Amazonian territory.
With it: Ecuador is the country that put the rights of nature into fundamental law
six months before Bolivia,
that built a legal framework for the non-human world
that has been used in court cases around the world since,
and that then drilled the oil anyway
because the alternative required the world
to pay for Ecuador's restraint
and the world did not.
This is the piece's load:
not just the right granted
but the condition under which the right was maintained or not.
The Yasuní decision is not hypocrisy.
It is the specific form that idealism takes
when it meets economic reality
in a country that is IDV 8 (the group includes the river, the jaguar, the oil under the ground)
and that also has a foreign debt
and a population that needs hospitals and roads.
The position has been carrying both since 2013.
Neither has resolved the other.

## Apparent
Pre-Inca: the Quitu, Cara, Cañari peoples.
1463: the Inca expand into the territory.
1534: the Spanish found Quito.
Independence: 1822 from Spain (as part of Gran Colombia).
1830: Ecuador becomes an independent republic.
1830–2008: cycles of constitutions (twenty in total — the most of any country in Latin America).
1979: return to democracy after military rule.
1990s: the *levantamientos* — the indigenous uprisings.
CONAIE (the Confederation of Indigenous Nationalities) formed 1986.
Becomes the most powerful indigenous organisation in the Americas.
1990: the *levantamiento* of June. Roads blocked. Quito supplied.
The government negotiates.
2000: indigenous-led protest ousts President Mahuad.
The dollarisation crisis. The economy collapses.
2006: Rafael Correa elected.
2007: the Constituent Assembly convened.
2008: the new constitution ratified. Montecristi constitution.
Article 71: *Pachamama* has rights.
The *sumak kawsay* as the national development goal.
2007: the Yasuní-ITT Initiative announced.
2013: the Initiative abandoned. The oil will be extracted.
The Yasuní drilling begins.
2023: a referendum on Yasuní — the first use of a people's referendum in Ecuador on this question.
The result: 59% vote to stop the drilling.
The government is constitutionally bound to comply.
The oil companies begin the withdrawal process.
The referendum invoked Article 71.
The constitution was used to enforce its own promise.
Fifteen years after the constitution was written.

## Note
*The 2023 referendum outcome — 59% to stop drilling — is a significant development: the constitution's rights-of-nature provisions were enforced by citizen initiative, not the state. The piece is still active.*
