# Piece: The 2016 Peace Accord
## The agreement that ended fifty-two years of armed conflict — and the uncertainty of what comes after.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 2016 accord and Colombia is defined by the FARC conflict —
the longest-running armed conflict in the Western Hemisphere,
eight million displaced, 220,000 dead.
With it: Colombia is the country that chose, in 2016,
to negotiate a path out of fifty-two years of war,
that held a referendum on the accord (the yes vote lost by 0.4%),
that had the Congress ratify it anyway,
and that has been living in the uncertain space of post-conflict since.
The position has held conflict as its frame for a generation.
The frame has been removed. The country is learning what is underneath.
The IDV 13 — the extreme collectivism built partly through shared danger — 
now must function without the shared danger.
The warmth that held people together under pressure
must hold them together in peace.
This is harder than it sounds.

## Apparent
1948: *El Bogotazo*. Jorge Eliécer Gaitán assassinated. 200,000 killed in *La Violencia*.
1964: the FARC founded. The ELN founded. Armed conflict begins.
The paramilitaries: AUC. The *narcos*: Escobar, the Cali cartel.
The 1990s: the worst decade. Escobar dies 1993. The paramilitaries expand.
2002: Uribe elected. The *seguridad democrática* policy. The FARC pushed back.
2010: Santos elected. Negotiations begin.
2016: the Peace Accord signed in Cartagena. September 26.
The Nobel Peace Prize awarded to Santos.
The referendum: October 2, 2016. No: 50.2%. Yes: 49.8%.
Congress ratifies the accord. Implementation begins.
The FARC demobilises. The dissident FARC groups continue operating.
2022: Petro elected — the first left-wing president in Colombian history.
Total peace negotiations resumed with all armed groups.
The accord is real. The conflict is partially continuing. Both are true.
