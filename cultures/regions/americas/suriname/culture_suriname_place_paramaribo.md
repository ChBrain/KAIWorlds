# Place: Paramaribo, Suriname

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Shown
- Paramaribo. The capital on the Suriname River, twenty kilometres from the Atlantic.
  The Dutch colonial wooden architecture — the largest concentration in the world.
  UNESCO World Heritage since 2002. The houses painted in faded pastels.
  Maintained by the families who live in them, imperfectly, authentically.
- The inner city: the Saint Peter and Paul Cathedral (wood, 1885),
  the Central Market, the Fort Zeelandia (where political prisoners were held in 1982).
  History at walking distance, not all of it comfortable.
- Lelydorp. The agricultural district outside the city.
  The Javanese community — *tempeh* being made in kitchens,
  *gamelan* audible on festival days.
- The interior: the Maroon villages along the rivers.
  The Saramaka, Ndyuka, Matawai — descendants of escaped enslaved people
  who built autonomous communities in the jungle and have maintained them for 300 years.
  Suriname's most distinctive cultural achievement. Not discussed enough in Paramaribo.
- The Suriname River at dusk. The ferries. The dugout canoes.
  The pelicans. The city and the river having the same conversation
  they have been having since 1667.

## Withheld
The December 1982 massacres. Fifteen prominent critics of Dési Bouterse's military government
were killed at Fort Zeelandia. Journalists, lawyers, professors, soldiers.
Bouterse was convicted in absentia by a military court in 2019 — 20 years imprisonment.
He was in office as President at the time of conviction (2010–2020).
He did not serve the sentence while President.
In 2020 he lost the election. In 2023 the Court of Justice upheld the conviction.
He was arrested in 2023.
The room shows a UNESCO city of wooden colonial architecture.
It does not display what happened at Fort Zeelandia in the week before Christmas 1982.
