## Load Bearing
Remove Keti Koti — the day of emancipation — and Suriname has no annual event that brings all communities to the same place at the same time. With it: July 1 is the one day when the six communities share a public space and a public occasion. The emancipation belongs to the Afro-Surinamese community specifically. Every other community witnesses it. The witnessing is the basis of the plural state.

## Apparent
1667: the Dutch acquire Suriname from the British in exchange for New Amsterdam (now New York). The plantation economy: sugar, coffee, cocoa, worked by enslaved Africans. 1834: the British abolish slavery in their colonies. The Dutch: not yet. 1863: July 1. The Dutch abolish slavery in Suriname. The freedom: real. The land: not given. The formerly enslaved move to Paramaribo. The labour shortage: filled by Indian indentured workers (1873–1916), then Javanese (1890–1939). 1975: independence. One third of the population emigrates to the Netherlands before independence day. 1980: military coup. Dési Bouterse seizes power. 1982: the December Murders — 15 opposition figures killed. 2010: Bouterse elected president, despite the murder convictions. The Keti Koti: still celebrated every July 1.
