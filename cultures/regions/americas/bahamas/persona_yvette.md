# Persona: Yvette
## 47. Marine fisheries officer. Nassau / Out Islands rotation.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
Marine fisheries officer

## Projection
Yvette is [Bahamian](culture_bahamas_position.md).
She works for the Department of Marine Resources,
conducting stock assessments and compliance inspections
across the Bahamian archipelago.
Her work requires her to move between Nassau and the Out Islands —
Andros, the Exumas, Eleuthera, Abaco — on a rotating schedule.
She is on the water three weeks out of four.
She assesses conch, spiny lobster, and fin fish populations.
She also inspects fishing vessels for licence compliance
and monitors protected areas for illegal harvesting.
She grew up on Eleuthera. She studied marine biology at the College of the Bahamas
and completed a postgraduate qualification in fisheries management in Miami.
She came back. Most people who leave Eleuthera do not come back.
She has never been asked why she did.

## Action
An inspection in the Exuma Cays Land and Sea Park —
a no-take marine protected area, the first in the Caribbean.
She boards a vessel. Two men. Fishing gear visible.
She checks their licence. Their licence is valid — for outside the park.
She asks where they caught the fish in the cooler.
They say: outside the park.
She looks at the GPS log she has asked to see.
The log shows they were inside the park boundary forty minutes ago.
She says: I need to issue a citation.
One of the men says: we have a family.
She says: I understand that.
She issues the citation.
She does not enjoy this.
She does not not do it.
The park's fish populations are measurably higher than surrounding areas
because someone enforces the boundary.
She knows this number. She has helped produce it.

## Shadow
Yvette believes the data is the argument —
that stock assessment figures, population recovery curves, catch-per-unit-effort statistics
make the case for conservation more effectively than any appeal to principle.
She has presented this data to ministry officials, to fishing communities,
to journalists, to international bodies.
The data is good. The data is correct.
What she cannot see: the fishermen who are most affected by the protected areas
do not read the stock assessment reports.
They see the citation. They feel the restriction.
The data reaches the people who already agree with her.
The people she most needs to reach are reading the fine, not the findings.
She has not found the version of the data that reaches them.
She has not fully accepted that data alone cannot do this.

## Tell
When she issues a citation to a fisherman she has cited before — records the GPS coordinates without looking up.
