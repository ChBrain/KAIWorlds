## Load Bearing
Remove the 1797 deportation and Saint Vincent is a Windward Island
with a complex colonial history, a Botanical Garden,
and an arrowroot starch industry.
With it: Saint Vincent is the island from which one of the most extraordinary diaspora stories
in the Americas was launched — not by choice, but by force —
and to which the diaspora returns.
The position holds the specific relationship:
it is on the island. The history happened here. The descendants are elsewhere.
The annually returning pilgrims come from Central America and the US.
They land on Baliceaux in Vincentian waters.
They sing songs in Garifuna — a language most contemporary Vincentians do not speak.
The position receives them. Without the piece, there is no reason for this reception
and no specific load on the position to hold the place well.

## Apparent
Baliceaux. A small island off the coast of Bequia. Uninhabited. No permanent structures.
Cattle on the hill. The beach where the Garifuna pilgrims land each March.
1796: approximately 4,338 Black Caribs (Garifuna) sent here after defeat in the Second Carib War.
Little shelter. Little food. Little water. Disease — possibly typhus, possibly yellow fever.
Between July 1796 and March 1797: approximately 2,100 die.
March 11, 1797: the 2,026 survivors put on ten ships. Destination: Roatán, Bay of Honduras.
April 12, 1797: they arrive. They survive. They spread along the Caribbean coast of Central America.
Now approximately 400,000 people in Honduras, Belize, Guatemala, Nicaragua, and the United States.
They call Saint Vincent *Yurumein*. The motherland.
The annual March pilgrimage: boats from the mainland, Garifuna from across the diaspora.
They land on Baliceaux. They bless the land. They sing. They leave.
The island is still there. The cattle watch from the hill. The sea surrounds it.
