# Piece: September 11, 1973, and the Laboratory
## The coup that turned Chile into an experiment, and the society that eventually rejected the experiment.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** Santiago

## Place
La Moneda. Santiago.
September 11, 1973.
Salvador Allende, elected president,
dead in the bombed palace.
Augusto Pinochet, general, in power.
The stadium converted into a detention centre.
The Caravan of Death moving through provinces.
Villa Grimaldi. Londres 38. Tejas Verdes.
Three thousand killed. Tens of thousands tortured.
Two hundred thousand in exile.
The junta in power until 1990.
And alongside all of this:
the Chicago Boys.
The laboratory.

## Load Bearing
Remove the coup and its aftermath
and Chile is an unusual but unremarkable Latin American democracy.
With it, Chile is the country
that held the first full-scale neoliberal experiment —
Milton Friedman's students writing policy
for a population that, by every cultural measure,
did not want what neoliberalism values.
MAS 28: Chile is one of the most feminine societies in the Americas.
Quality of life. Caring for others. Modesty. Not winning at all costs.
The Chicago Boys' programme said: the market is the measure of all things.
The society said, eventually: no.
The *estallido social* of October 2019:
one million people in the Plaza Italia.
Not a political party. Not a single demand.
A society that had absorbed thirty years of high GDP
and high inequality
and finally, on a Tuesday in October,
occupied the streets to say: *No estamos todos*.
We are not all here. The prosperity did not reach us all.
The piece is load-bearing because
every tension in the Chilean position —
the rule-following and the distrust,
the reserve and the fury,
the care for others and the economic system that said efficiency first —
runs through September 11, 1973,
and what was built on top of it.

## Apparent
Pedro de Valdivia founds Santiago, 1541.
The Mapuche: the people who were never fully conquered.
The Spanish colony, 1540–1818.
Independence: 1818. O'Higgins.
The nineteenth century: immigration. Germans in the south.
British engineers. The nitrate boom. The copper boom.
1970: Salvador Allende elected. The first democratically elected Marxist head of government in the world.
The UP coalition: Popular Unity.
Land reform. Nationalisation of copper mines.
Copper: the salary of Chile. *El sueldo de Chile.*
1972: shortages. Truck owners' strike. CIA involvement confirmed in later documents.
September 11, 1973: coup.
La Moneda bombed. Allende dead.
The constitution in force since 1980, written under Pinochet,
remains the basis of Chilean law.
1975: the economic shock. Prices freed overnight.
Public spending slashed. State enterprises privatised.
The private pension system: AFPs. Each worker saves for themselves.
GDP rises. Inequality rises with it.
1988: plebiscite. Pinochet votes himself out.
1990: Patricio Aylwin, civilian president. The transition.
2006: first Bachelet presidency. A woman whose father was tortured by the regime she now heads.
The copper mines still produce. The AFPs still operate.
October 2019: a subway fare increase. Then the streets.
*Evadir, no pagar. Otra forma de luchar.*
Fare-dodging: the small refusal that became the large one.
One million on October 25. The largest demonstration in Chilean history.
2020: the constitutional convention voted. 78% in favour of a new constitution.
2022: the new constitutional draft rejected. 62% against.
The country that voted to change
then voted against the specific change.
This is also Chile.
The copper mines still produce.
The Andes are still there on clear days.
