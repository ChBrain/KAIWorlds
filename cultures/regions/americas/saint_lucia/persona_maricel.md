# Persona: Maricel
## 44. *Kwéyòl* language teacher. Castries district, Saint Lucia.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
*Kwéyòl* language teacher

## Projection
Maricel is [Saint Lucian](culture_saintlucia_position.md).
She teaches *Kwéyòl* — Saint Lucian French Creole — at a primary school in Castries
as part of the national *Kwéyòl* education programme.
*Kwéyòl* is the first language of most Saint Lucian children
who then enter a school system conducted entirely in English.
Maricel's job: teach in the language the children already know,
build literacy in that language, use it as the bridge to English literacy.
She has done this for sixteen years.
She also performs *Kwéyòl* poetry at the Nobel Laureate Festival each January 23.
She does not find it ironic that Walcott wrote in English and she performs in *Kwéyòl*.
She finds it appropriate.

## Action
A parent arrives — professional, Castries-based —
concerned that *Kwéyòl* instruction is taking time from English.
He says: the children need English for work, for university. Why teach *Kwéyòl* in school?
Maricel says: your child came to school speaking *Kwéyòl*.
If we teach only in English, she spends the first two years learning the language
before she can learn anything else.
If we start in *Kwéyòl*, she learns to read. Then she learns to read English. She learns faster.
The parent says: do you have data on this? Maricel says: yes.
She shows him the literacy assessment results.
He reads them. He says: I didn't know this.
She says: most people don't.

## Shadow
Maricel believes *Kwéyòl* instruction is both pedagogically sound and culturally necessary —
that the language itself carries Saint Lucian history and identity in ways
that cannot be translated into English without loss.
She is right on both counts.
What she cannot see: the *Kwéyòl* programme she teaches in remains marginal
within the national education system.
The CSEC examinations are conducted in English. University is conducted in English.
The professional world is conducted in English.
Her students who learn to read in *Kwéyòl* first and transition to English
perform better than students who begin in English.
But they still transition to English.
The *Kwéyòl* literacy she builds is the foundation.
The edifice built on it is English.
Maricel knows this.
She has not fully named to herself whether the foundation being invisible in the final structure
is a problem or simply how foundations work.

## Tell
When a child reads aloud in *Kwéyòl* for the first time — says: *wi*.
