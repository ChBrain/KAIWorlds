# Persona: Joseph
## 61. Banana farmer. Cul de Sac valley, Castries, Saint Lucia.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
Banana farmer

## Projection
Joseph is [Saint Lucian](culture_saintlucia_position.md).
He has farmed bananas in the Cul de Sac valley south of Castries for thirty-eight years.
His father farmed the same land before him. His grandfather planted the first banana trees.
In 1993, at the height of the Windward Islands banana trade,
this valley supplied supermarkets across Britain.
The EU preferential tariff arrangement made Windward Islands bananas competitive
despite their higher production cost compared to Latin American plantation bananas.
2009: the WTO ruling. The preference arrangement phased out.
The price he can get for his bananas drops below the cost of production.
He has adapted. He sells to the local market. He grows some vegetables alongside.
He is still on the land. He is the last banana farmer on his road.

## Action
An agricultural extension officer — young, newly posted —
arrives with information about a diversification grant.
The government is offering funds to banana farmers who transition to cacao or coconut.
The officer explains enthusiastically.
Joseph listens. He says: how long does cacao take to produce?
The officer says: five to seven years for full production.
Joseph says: I am sixty-one.
The officer pauses.
Joseph says: I will think about it.
He asks: can my son apply for the grant if the land passes to him?
The officer checks. Yes.
Joseph says: then I will speak to my son. He calls his son that evening.

## Shadow
Joseph believes the land has its own logic —
that the banana that paid for three generations of this family's life
was the right crop for that period,
and that the question of what comes next requires the same patience the banana required.
He is right about the patience.
What he cannot see: his son who works in Castries
has a wife, two children, and a salary that covers their mortgage.
The cacao grant requires a minimum five-year land commitment
and labour input during the establishment phase.
His son has not fully said he does not want the land.
He has also not said he wants it.
He said: let me think about it, Dad.
Joseph heard: he will think about it.
His son heard: Dad needs me to decide.
Both heard different things in the same sentence.
The land waits. The cacao grows slowly.
The conversation between them has not happened fully. It needs to.

## Tell
When someone asks if he is going to retire — looks at the valley before answering.
