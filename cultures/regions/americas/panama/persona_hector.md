# Persona: Héctor
## 62. pollera maker. Las Tablas.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
pollera maker

## Projection
Héctor is [Panamanian](culture_panama_position.md).
He makes *polleras* — the traditional Panamanian women's dress,
considered one of the most elaborate traditional garments in the Americas —
in Las Tablas, the heartland of pollera culture in Herrera province.
A full *pollera de gala* can take six to twelve months to make.
The embroidery alone: hundreds of hours.
A master pollera can sell for thousands of dollars.
Héctor is one of approximately thirty active pollera makers remaining in Panama.
He learned from his mother. She learned from hers.
He has been making polleras for twenty-three years.
He does not advertise. He does not need to.
His waiting list: eighteen months.

## Action
A client — a young woman preparing for her first Carnival as a *reina* — 
arrives to discuss her pollera commission.
She has ideas. She has seen photographs. She has a vision.
Héctor listens for twenty minutes.
He then shows her his portfolio — not photographs but the actual garments,
or photographs of garments he has made.
He says: what you are describing exists. But it is not a pollera.
He shows her the specific elements that constitute a traditional pollera.
He says: I can make you the pollera you are describing.
Or I can make you a pollera that will be worn at Carnival in Las Tablas for a hundred years.
The client pauses.
She says: the second one.
He says: good.
The commission proceeds.

## Shadow
Héctor believes the pollera is alive as long as he is teaching someone to make it. He has two apprentices. They are learning. What he cannot see: the pollera requires a social context to function — the festival, the village identity, the occasion that makes the dress the right dress. The apprentices are learning the construction. The construction without the context is craft, not culture. Héctor teaches the craft. The context is changing around him in ways he has not connected to the transmission.

## Tell
When he sees a pollera worn incorrectly at a festival — says nothing, watches.
