# Piece: December 1, 1948 — The Barracks Become Schools
## The day Costa Rica abolished its army and turned the military budget into an education budget.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** San José

## Place
San José. The Bellavista Fortress.
December 1, 1948.
José Figueres Ferrer — *Don Pepe* —
takes a sledgehammer to the wall of the fortress
in front of cameras.
He hands the building to the Ministry of Education.
He signs the new constitution.
Article 12: *Se proscribe el Ejército como institución permanente.*
The army is proscribed as a permanent institution.
No other country in the hemisphere will do this.
Costa Rica has not had an army in seventy-six years.

## Load Bearing
Remove the abolition and Costa Rica's PDI 35 is unexplained —
why would a Central American country in 1948
produce an egalitarian cultural profile?
Remove it and MAS 21 is anomalous —
why is this the most feminine culture in Latin America?
The abolition is the piece that answers:
a country that constitutionally decided
that the budget for weapons
was the budget for teachers
would, over three generations, produce
a population with different values
than its neighbours.
Less hierarchy because the military — the ultimate hierarchy — was gone.
More care for others because the state spent on health and education.
More feminine because the dominant metric shifted
from military strength to social welfare.
The *pura vida* is not a national slogan that was invented.
It is the cultural residue of a constitutional decision
that changed what was worth spending money on.
Remove the abolition:
Costa Rica is a small Central American country
with some pleasant geography.
With it:
Costa Rica is the country that has ranked first in the Happy Planet Index,
that has among the highest life expectancy in the Americas,
that generates over 99% of its electricity from renewable sources,
that abolished the army when its neighbours were building theirs up,
and that has spent seventy-six years proving
that the decision was not naive.

## Apparent
Pre-colonial: the Huetar, Bribri, Chorotega peoples. No large centralised empire.
The Spanish colony, 1502–1821. A peripheral outpost. Few indigenous people. Few enslaved people. A relatively egalitarian colonial society because there was not much to extract.
1821: Independence. Costa Rica part of the Central American Federation, then independent.
1869: Free and compulsory primary education. Before most of Europe.
1871: Abolition of capital punishment.
The coffee economy: the *cafetaleros*. The coffee export that funded education and infrastructure in the nineteenth century.
1940s: Calderón Guardia president. Social reforms: social security, labour rights. The alliance between the Communist Party and the Catholic Church that produced them. Strange but real.
1948: The disputed election. Calderón refuses to leave. Figueres leads the uprising. Forty-four days of civil war. Two thousand dead.
Victory. And then: the decision that no one expected.
Figueres abolishes the army.
He also gives women and Afro-Costa Ricans the vote — 1949 constitution.
1949 to present: no army.
The military budget: now education and health.
Costa Rica's literacy rate: 97.9%.
Public university system: four public universities.
Healthcare system: routinely ranked among the best in Latin America.
2022: Costa Rica ranked 12th in the World Happiness Report.
1st in the Happy Planet Index multiple times.
The barracks is the Museo Nacional.
Don Pepe's sledgehammer swing is on the museum's wall.
The bullet holes are also there.
Both are the piece.
