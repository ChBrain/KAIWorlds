# Piece: The Three Abolitions
## The country that abolished slavery in 1905, 1961, 1981, and 2007 — and is still abolishing it.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Nouakchott](culture_mauritanian_place_nouakchott.md)

## Place
Nouakchott. The capital.
Built on sand dunes after independence.
The fastest-growing city in Africa for decades —
not from industry or commerce
but from drought, desertification,
and the displacement of nomadic populations
who walked to the city
when the desert became unlivable.
Including Haratin fleeing their masters
in the interior.
The city became the place where the contradiction
was most visible.
Where you could not avoid it.

## Load Bearing
Remove the slavery question
and Mauritania is a Saharan state
navigating post-colonial development,
ethnic diversity, and the resource pressures
of climate and drought.
With it: Mauritania is the country
where the most fundamental question
of human status —
who is a person, who is property —
has been answered with declarations
that the legal system, the religious establishment,
and the social structure
have collectively refused to enforce.
The Three Abolitions are not three failures.
They are one continuous act
of performative compliance
with external standards
that has never been backed
by internal will to change.
1905: French colonial abolition.
Not enforced. The French found it too disruptive.
1961: constitutional abolition at independence.
Not enforced. The new government needed the old system.
1981: presidential decree.
Last country in the world.
Triggered by a public slave auction
that generated international scandal.
A law passed to manage the scandal.
No criminal penalties. Not enforced.
2007: criminalization. Under international pressure.
Two convictions in twenty years of the law.
The system continues.
Remove the piece:
a Saharan state with ethnic complexity.
With it: the most concentrated demonstration
of how a state can maintain a social order
through legal declaration as substitute for change —
how law can be the instrument
not of transformation
but of transformation's performance.
And alongside it: the counter-piece,
which is also load-bearing:
Biram Ould Dah Abeid,
who burned the books that justified slavery,
who ran for president,
who was jailed for activism,
who came second in the election,
who is still there.
The system is durable.
The resistance is also durable.
Both are the country.

## Apparent
Mauritanian society pre-colonial:
Bidan as Arab-Berber warrior and clerical class.
Haratin as agricultural labour and domestic service.
Slavery descent-based: born to a slave mother,
you are a slave.
Islam used to justify it:
Maliki jurisprudence in the *muhadra* —
the religious schools where boys memorise
texts that contain, among other things,
the conditions under which slavery is licit.
1905: French colonial administration declares abolition.
Instructs local administrators to enforce it.
Local administrators do not enforce it.
French accept this.
1960: independence.
1961: the Universal Declaration of Human Rights
incorporated into the constitution.
Slavery implicitly abolished.
Not enforced.
1974: El Hor — *Free Man* —
the first Haratin emancipation movement.
The government suppresses it.
1981: a woman is sold publicly in a marketplace.
The scandal reaches the international press.
The government issues a decree abolishing slavery.
No criminal penalty. No enforcement mechanism.
2007: international pressure from the UN,
NGOs, aid donors.
A law criminalising slavery passed.
One prosecution. One conviction.
Ahmed Ould El Hassine, 2011.
Convicted of owning two boys, Said and Yarg.
Released on bail four months later.
2015: the law strengthened.
Specialised courts created.
Two convictions under the new law.
2021: the government reports progress to the UN.
The UN Special Rapporteur welcomes the progress
and urges more action.
The estimate of enslaved Mauritanians:
90,000 to 600,000, depending on the methodology.
The government's estimate: zero.
The government says slavery is totally finished.
The government has said this since 1905.
