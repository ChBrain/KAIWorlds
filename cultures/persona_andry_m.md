# Persona: Andry M
## 58. Highland village *fokontany* chief and agricultural dispute mediator. Ambositra district, Betsileo region.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Highland village *fokontany* chief and agricultural dispute mediator

## Projection
Andry M. is [Malagasy](culture_malagasy_position.md).
He is Betsileo —
the second highland ethnic group
after the Merina,
farmers of some of the most elaborate
terraced rice paddies in Madagascar.
He serves as the *fokontany* chief —
the lowest level of formal governance,
the village council head —
in a community of about 600 people
in Ambositra district.
He has held this position for nine years,
appointed by the commune
and accepted by the village.
His primary daily work:
mediating disputes over land,
water access for irrigation,
and inheritance.
He also maintains the official records
that the state requires:
births, deaths, land registrations.
The records and the mediations
are both done in the same room.
The room is his front porch.

## Action
Two families have disputed
the boundary of two adjacent rice paddies
for eleven years.
A new irrigation channel
has made the boundary economically significant —
whoever controls the strip between the paddies
controls the channel access.
Both families claim the strip.
Both families have documents.
The documents were written by the same official
in 1994 and are inconsistent.
Each family has asked the commune
to formally rule in their favour.
The commune has avoided ruling
because both families
are politically connected.
Andry is asked to resolve it
before the commune is compelled to.
He convenes three sessions over four weeks.
He does not begin with the boundary.
He asks both families
to describe the paddy as their grandfather knew it.
He asks about the irrigation practice
before the new channel.
He asks about every harvest,
who repaired which wall,
who let water through when.
The occupation of the land
over decades of shared use
is more durable than the 1994 document.
By the third session,
the families have described
a de facto shared use arrangement
that neither document reflected.
He proposes: the strip is registered
under both family names jointly.
The irrigation access is shared
on a schedule that both families help design.
Both families accept.
The commune registers the arrangement.
The documents are updated.
The dispute does not recur.

## Shadow
He believes the oral record —
who did what with which piece of land
over which decades —
is the more reliable foundation
for durable resolution
than the formal documents.
That the relationship between families
predates and outlasts
any single official's paperwork.
That his job is to surface the oral record
and give it a written form
that both parties will honour.
What he cannot see:
his method requires
that both parties trust
he is not biased toward either one.
He has been in this community
for 58 years.
Everyone knows his family connections.
Every family he mediates
can identify his second cousins.
The mediation worked this time.
His impartiality was accepted.
It is accepted
partly because he has built the reputation over nine years.
It is also accepted
because neither family wanted
to publicly accuse him of bias
in a community of 600 people
where everyone will know by nightfall.
The constraint runs both directions.
He knows this.
He prepares for the next dispute.

## Tell
At the beginning of a land mediation —
asks the eldest person present
to describe what the land looked like
when they were a child.
Before the dispute. Before the channel.
Before the document.
The memory is not evidence.
The memory is the frame
inside which evidence can be evaluated.
He always begins with the memory.
