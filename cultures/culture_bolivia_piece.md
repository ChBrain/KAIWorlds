# Piece: The 2009 Constitution — The Earth Has Rights
## The first constitution in the world to grant legal rights to Mother Earth, written by an indigenous-majority country that had been waiting 184 years to write it.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** La Paz / Sucre

## Place
Sucre. The Gran Mariscal de Ayacucho Assembly.
2006–2009.
Two hundred and fifty-five delegates, elected by the people.
The majority: indigenous.
The first time in Bolivian history.
February 7, 2009: the new constitution ratified.
The country renamed: *Estado Plurinacional de Bolivia* —
the Plurinational State of Bolivia.
Thirty-six indigenous nations recognised.
*Pachamama* — Mother Earth — granted constitutional rights.
The concept: *Vivir Bien* — Living Well, not Living More.
The Aymara concept *suma qamaña* and the Quechua *sumak kawsay*
written into the preamble of the fundamental law.
The earth is not a resource. It is a subject.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 2009 constitution
and Bolivia is a high-PDI, extremely collectivist Andean country
with significant indigenous population and high UAI.
Unremarkable.
With it: Bolivia is the country that made the indigenous cosmology
the legal framework of the state —
that said *Pachamama* is not a metaphor,
she is a rights-holder,
and the state is accountable to her.
This is not symbolic.
The Law of Mother Earth (2010) and the Framework Law of Mother Earth (2012)
operationalise the constitutional provision:
seven rights of Mother Earth, including the right to regenerate,
the right not to be structurally altered,
the right to clean water.
Remove the constitution:
the Quechua and Aymara cosmology continues,
as it has for thousands of years,
underground, uncredited, as peasant superstition.
With the constitution:
the cosmology is the law.
IDV 10 — the most collectivist score in the entire Andean bloc —
is the *ayllu* and the *aynuqa* and the *Pachamama*
made into Hofstede coordinates.
The position predates the measurement by several millennia.

## Apparent
Pre-Inca: the Tiwanaku civilisation on Lake Titicaca.
800 BCE to 1100 CE. The *aymara* people.
1438–1533: the Inca empire. The *ayllu* structure incorporated.
1532: Spanish arrive. Potosí silver mine — the richest in the world.
The *mita* forced labour system. Millions of indigenous deaths in the mines.
The silver financed the Spanish empire for two centuries.
1825: Independence. August 6. Named for Simón Bolívar.
The republic: governed by the *criollo* and *mestizo* elite.
The indigenous majority: excluded.
The twentieth century: land reforms (1952), nationalisation of mines, political cycles.
1964–1982: military dictatorships, with interruptions.
1985–2005: democratic period. Neoliberal structural adjustment. Privatisation.
2000: the Cochabamba Water War. The city's water supply privatised, prices rise 50%, the population revolt. The government backs down. The water is re-nationalised.
2003: the Gas War. Plan to export Bolivian gas through Chile. Mass protests. The president flees. The gas remains Bolivian.
2005: Evo Morales elected. First indigenous president of Bolivia, of the Americas.
2009: the new constitution. Forty-four percent of delegates indigenous.
2010: the Law of Mother Earth.
2019: Morales ousted in disputed election. The *wiphala* — the indigenous flag — removed from government buildings. Restored after eighteen days of protests.
2020: Luis Arce elected. Morales's Movement for Socialism returns.
The *wiphala* is back.
The constitution is still in force.
The lithium is still being mined.
