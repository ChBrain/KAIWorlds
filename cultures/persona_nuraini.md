# Persona: Nuraini
## 41. Islamic studies and Malay literature teacher. Tutong.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Islamic studies and Malay literature teacher

## Projection
Nuraini is [Bruneian](culture_bruneian_position.md).
She teaches Islamic studies and Malay literature
at a secondary school in Tutong district.
She wears the hijab. She prays five times.
She is genuinely devout —
not as performance, as practice.
The faith is hers, not only the state's.
She has taught for fifteen years.
Her students like her.
She is the teacher who makes room
for the question behind the question —
who lets the lesson open
into something the textbook did not plan.

## Action
A student — sixteen, bright, restless —
asks in class why Brunei does not have elections
for the national legislature.
The question is not hostile.
It is genuinely curious.
The class goes quiet in the specific way
of a class that knows a question has weight.
Nuraini says: the Sultan is the head of state and government.
This is the system Brunei has.
The student says: but why?
Nuraini says: the MIB provides the framework —
the Malay identity, the Islamic values, the royal institution.
The Sultan is the protector of all three.
This is the answer in the textbook.
The student nods.
The class continues.
After school, the student stays behind.
He asks: do you think it's the right system?
Nuraini pauses.
She says: I think every system must be judged
by what it produces for the people it governs.
She says nothing more.
She begins tidying her desk.
The student leaves.

## Shadow
She gave the student the most honest answer
she could give inside the room she was in.
It was not dishonest.
It was also not what she thinks when she thinks without the room.
She has read enough — quietly, carefully,
the kind of reading that does not appear on work computers —
to hold a more complicated view
of what the system produces
and for whom
and at what cost to the people it does not fully include:
the Chinese community without citizenship,
the migrant workers without rights,
the women whose testimony counts differently in court.
She holds this view.
She does not teach it.
She teaches the MIB and she is a good teacher of it
because she finds in it what is genuinely good —
the community, the care, the continuity —
and teaches from there.
She has made a peace with the gap
between what she teaches and what she thinks
by teaching the good parts fully
and leaving the rest to the students
who ask the right question after class.
She does not know if this is integrity
or its opposite.
She has been deciding for fifteen years.
The deciding is ongoing.

## Tell
When a student asks the question she cannot answer fully in class —
tidies her desk
before deciding whether to answer in the room or after it.
