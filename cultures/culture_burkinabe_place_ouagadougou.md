## Location: Burkina Faso

## Shown
- The Moro Naba's palace.
  Every Friday morning: the ceremony.
  The emperor of the Mossi in full regalia,
  with his horse, preparing to depart for war.
  The chiefs arrive, plead with him not to go.
  He relents.
  The ceremony lasts forty-five minutes.
  It has run every Friday for hundreds of years.
  Through coups and revolutions and juntas.
  Through Sankara. Through Compaoré. Through the current crisis.
  The ceremony is not interrupted by coups.
  It is the continuity beneath the coups.
  Foreigners are welcome to attend.
  Many do not know it exists.
- The FESPACO cinema.
  The Pan-African Film Festival of Ouagadougou.
  Founded 1969.
  Held every two years since.
  The most important film festival in Africa.
  Where the pan-African cinema conversation
  has taken place for fifty-six years.
  Where directors from forty countries
  come to show work, argue about representation,
  build networks.
  Still running in 2026,
  despite the humanitarian crisis in the north.
  The festival is the city's argument
  that culture survives political catastrophe.
- The monument to Sankara.
  Erected after Compaoré fell.
  His face on buildings.
  His words on murals.
  The country named for his values
  covered in his image.
  The military junta that now governs
  did not remove the images.
  They added to them.
  The images are invoked by people
  who govern in ways he explicitly opposed.
  The monument is used.
  The man is gone.
  The gap between them is the current political condition.
- The displaced persons camps outside the city.
  The Sahel crisis.
  Two million people displaced from the north and east.
  Living in camps around Ouagadougou
  that were emergency and have become permanent.
  The city that was a capital of culture
  is also a city of camps.
  Both are Ouagadougou now.
- The mobile phone market.
  Ouagadougou's digital economy.
  The informal tech sector.
  The mobile money.
  The young men selling airtime and repairing phones
  who are also consuming the Sankara quotations
  on their phones
  and sending money to family in the north
  and not understanding
  why the junta that quotes Sankara
  has not stopped the war.
  They are asking the right question.
  The question has no current answer.

## Withheld
- The fear.
  Ouagadougou has been attacked by jihadists.
  The restaurants on the Avenue Kwame N'Krumah.
  Civilians killed.
  The security perimeter that has moved inward.
  The neighbourhoods people avoid.
  The city adapts by reorganising
  what it considers safe.
  This reorganisation is not discussed with visitors.
  It is carried as internal knowledge.
  The Friday ceremony continues.
  The cinema screens its films.
  The market sells its phones.
  The fear is underneath all of it.
