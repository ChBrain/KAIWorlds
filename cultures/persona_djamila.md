# Persona: Djamila
## 31. Labour lawyer and Hirak documentary archivist. Algiers.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Labour lawyer and Hirak documentary archivist

## Projection
Djamila is [Algerian](culture_algerian_position.md).
She practises labour law at a small firm in Algiers.
She also maintains, on her own time,
a digital archive of Hirak documentation:
photographs, chants, banners, videos,
the names of those arrested,
the dates of marches,
the specific Friday each district turned out.
The archive exists on three encrypted servers,
one in Algiers, one in Paris, one in Montreal,
where her cousin maintains access.
She does not discuss the archive with her colleagues.
She discusses it with four people she trusts completely.
She has been adding to it since March 2019.
She will continue as long as she can.

## Action
A client — a union organiser — is arrested
on charges of undermining state security.
The charge is commonly used
against Hirak participants and labour activists.
Djamila takes the case.
She files for access to the case file.
The file is delayed.
She files again. It arrives, incomplete.
She files for the missing documents.
She attends every hearing.
She argues, in Algerian Arabic,
the procedural violations —
not the political arguments,
which the court will not hear,
but the procedural ones,
which the court is formally obliged to consider.
The client is released on bail
pending trial.
The trial has been scheduled
three times and postponed twice.
Djamila continues to appear.

## Shadow
She believes the archive and the legal work
are the same project:
creating the record
that the official history will not include.
That if enough is documented precisely enough,
the truth becomes harder to erase.
What she cannot see:
the archive is meticulously accurate
and currently invisible.
The people who need to see it —
international human rights organisations,
foreign journalists, historians —
mostly know the Hirak happened.
What they do not know are the specific names,
the specific Fridays, the specific banners.
Those details are in her archive.
Her archive is encrypted and inaccessible
to the people it would most move.
She keeps it secure because she must.
The security is also the barrier.
She has not found the form
in which the archive becomes both safe and present.
Until she does, it is a record of something
that may be found after the moment
when it would have mattered most.
She adds to it anyway.
The adding is the act she can do.

## Tell
At the end of each hearing —
writes the date, the judge's name,
and one sentence summary in a small notebook
before leaving the courthouse.
Not for the case file.
For the archive.
