# Piece: The Graveyard of Empires
## What Alexander, the British, the Soviets, and the Americans learned — and what it cost the Afghans each time.

## Owner
- **Place:** Kabul

## Place
Kabul. The Arg — the presidential palace.
Occupied by the British, the Soviets,
the republic, the Taliban.
The flag above it has changed six times
in living memory.
The palace continues.

## Load Bearing
Remove the graveyard logic
and Afghanistan is a landlocked country
in a difficult neighbourhood
with significant mineral wealth
and persistent internal conflict.
With it: Afghanistan is the place
that has ended every external project
launched against it —
not by defeating the invader militarily
but by outlasting them,
by being more complex than the invader's model,
by costing more than the invader was willing to pay
and less than the Afghan was willing to accept.
The British learned this in three wars.
The Soviets learned it in ten years and a million dead.
The Americans learned it in twenty years,
$2 trillion, and an August afternoon in 2021
when the government they had built
lasted less than two weeks without them.
Remove the piece: a series of foreign interventions.
With it: the specific logic of Afghan resistance —
the *jirga* that cannot be bombed into compliance,
the tribal structure that absorbs external authority
without being transformed by it,
the patience of a society that has been invaded repeatedly
and has outlasted every invader
while paying the highest price.
The price is load-bearing too:
40 years of near-continuous war
have destroyed the infrastructure,
the institutions, the educated class,
the possibility of the normal life
that the Afghans who fought the wars
believed they were fighting for.
The empires are buried.
The Afghans are also buried, in their millions.
The graveyard contains both.

## Apparent
329 BCE: Alexander the Great enters Bactria. The Afghans resist.
Alexander marries Roxana — a Bactrian. He does not conquer Afghanistan. He passes through it.
1839–1842: the First Anglo-Afghan War. The British retreat from Kabul.
16,000 British and Indian soldiers and camp followers.
One survivor reaches Jalalabad.
1878–1880: the Second Anglo-Afghan War.
1919: the Third Anglo-Afghan War. Afghan independence.
1978: the Saur Revolution. Communist coup.
1979: the Soviet invasion. December 24.
1979–1989: the Soviet-Afghan War. One million Afghans dead.
The CIA funds the mujahideen. Pakistan channels the money.
The stingers arrive. The Soviets leave.
1989–1996: the civil war. The mujahideen factions fight each other.
Kabul destroyed by the factions that had just liberated it.
1996: the Taliban take Kabul.
2001: September 11. The American invasion.
October 7, 2001.
2001–2021: twenty years. $2 trillion. 240,000 dead.
The republic built. The institutions built. The girls in school.
August 15, 2021: the Taliban enter Kabul.
The government dissolves.
The evacuation.
The Taliban govern again.
The girls are out of school above grade six.
The women are out of public life.
The mineral wealth — lithium, copper, rare earths,
estimated $1 trillion — is now administered by the Taliban
with Chinese investment interest.
The graveyard has new visitors inspecting the ground.
