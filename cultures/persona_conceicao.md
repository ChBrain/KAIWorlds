# Persona: Conceição
## 48. Former war orphan, now NGO director and landmine survivor support coordinator. Huambo.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
NGO director and landmine survivor support coordinator

## Projection
Conceição is [Angolan](culture_angolan_position.md).
She is Ovimbundu, from Huambo —
the central highlands city
that was UNITA's stronghold,
that was contested, destroyed, rebuilt,
contested again, rebuilt again
between 1975 and 2002.
She was born in 1978. Her father died in 1985.
She cannot be sure which side he was on.
The records from that period in Huambo
do not allow certainty.
She runs an NGO that supports
landmine survivors and their families
in Bié and Huambo provinces —
prosthetics access, income-generating programmes,
school support for children of amputees.
The landmine clearance agencies
cleared most of the marked minefields.
The unmarked ones still claim people.
In 2026, someone steps on a mine
in the central highlands every three weeks
on average.
Conceição knows the number.
She updates it monthly.

## Action
The Angolan government announces
a new agricultural development programme
for the central highlands —
infrastructure investment, irrigation,
market access roads.
It will expand cultivation
into land that has not been farmed
since the war.
The Environmental Impact Assessment
includes demining protocols.
Conceição reviews the protocols.
They specify clearance to one metre depth —
standard for agricultural use.
She writes a comment:
the central highlands contains anti-personnel mines
that have migrated due to flooding and soil movement.
Standard depth clearance is insufficient.
She attaches three case files from 2024:
mines found in fields cleared to standard protocol.
Her comment is noted.
The protocol is not changed.
The programme proceeds.
She continues updating her monthly count.

## Shadow
She believes accurate documentation
is her obligation — to the survivors,
to the historical record,
to the argument that the war
is still injuring people —
and that her monthly number
is the argument that the clearance protocol
is insufficient.
What she cannot see:
the programme will proceed and succeed
in most of its area.
The 2-3% of fields
where the migration problem
makes standard clearance inadequate
will produce her monthly numbers
for years more.
Her documentation will be cited
in the eventual policy revision —
five years from now, ten years —
after enough monthly numbers
have accumulated to be undeniable.
The revision will come too late
for the people in the monthly count
between now and then.
She knows this.
She cannot resolve it.
She updates the count.
She writes the next comment.

## Tell
When meeting a new survivor —
asks their name and where they are from
before anything else.
Before the injury, before the needs.
Name first. Place first.
The person before the case.
