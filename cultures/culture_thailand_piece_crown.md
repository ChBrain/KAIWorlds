# Piece: The Unbroken Crown
## The only Southeast Asian country never colonised — and what that continuity costs.

## Owner
- **Place:** Bangkok

## Place
Bangkok. The Grand Palace.
The Emerald Buddha inside it.
Changed three times a year by the king —
into seasonal robes of gold, of gilt, of enamel.
The king climbs the altar.
The country watches.

## Load Bearing
Remove the unbroken crown and Thailand is a Southeast Asian nation
with a complex colonial history — like its neighbours.
With it: Thailand is the country that threaded the needle
between British Burma and French Indochina
through a combination of skilled diplomacy,
strategic concession, and the legitimating continuity
of an unbroken monarchy.
The Chakri dynasty has sat the throne since 1782.
The monarchy is not simply a symbol.
It is the load-bearing wall of Thai national identity —
the thing that distinguishes Thailand from every neighbour,
that survived every coup (thirteen successful, nine attempted since 1932),
that has been invoked to end political crises
and to justify political interventions.
The *lèse-majesté* law is the monarchy made legal.
The institution is protected from examination
because examination is the thing it cannot survive
in its current form.
Remove the piece: Thailand is a Buddhist kingdom
with recurring political instability.
With it: Thailand is the country whose political instability
is managed through and around an institution
that cannot be named as part of the problem.
The surface holds because the thing beneath it
is protected from being surfaced.

## Apparent
1238: the Sukhothai Kingdom. The first Thai state.
1350: the Ayutthaya Kingdom. 417 years.
1767: Ayutthaya sacked and burned by Burma.
1782: the Chakri dynasty founded. Bangkok established as capital.
Rama I. The dynasty continues to Rama X, the current king.
1850s–1890s: the great powers close in.
King Mongkut (Rama IV) and King Chulalongkorn (Rama V)
negotiate, concede, modernise — and survive.
Siam cedes territory to Britain and France.
It does not become a colony.
1932: a coup ends absolute monarchy. Constitutional monarchy begins.
The coups since: 1933, 1947, 1948, 1951, 1957, 1958, 1971, 1976, 1977, 1991, 2006, 2014.
The monarchy survives each one.
In some it is a bystander. In some it is not.
2016: King Bhumibol Adulyadej dies after 70 years on the throne.
The longest-reigning monarch in Thai history.
The country mourns for a year.
The grief is real and also mandatory.
Both are true.
2020–2021: youth-led protests call for monarchy reform.
The first time in modern Thai history the institution is named publicly as requiring change.
The protests are dispersed.
The surface is restored.
The names remain on the *lèse-majesté* charge sheets.
