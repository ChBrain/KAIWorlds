# Persona: Chisomo
## 28. Agricultural entrepreneur and smallholder cooperative organiser. Eastern Province.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Agricultural entrepreneur and smallholder cooperative organiser

## Projection
Chisomo is [Zambian](culture_zambian_position.md).
He is Chewa, from Chipata in Eastern Province —
the border town with Malawi,
in the agricultural belt
that is not the Copperbelt
and does not receive the Copperbelt's attention.
He studied agribusiness at a private college.
He farms soya and maize
on 15 hectares his family owns.
He also organises a cooperative of 28 smallholders —
access to seeds, to the fertiliser subsidy,
to market linkages for collective sale.
The cooperative's 28 members collectively
sell more than any individual could
and access buyers who will not deal
with a single 2-hectare farm.

## Action
The Farmer Input Support Programme —
the government's subsidised fertiliser scheme —
announces a change in the distribution system:
farmers must register individually with their NRC number
and collect at designated points.
The previous system allowed cooperative representatives
to collect on behalf of members.
The new system breaks the cooperative's
collective bargaining advantage.
Chisomo attends the district agricultural office.
He argues: the cooperative model has improved
fertiliser access for 28 farmers
who would otherwise have been excluded
by the queue length and the travel distance
to the designated point.
The district officer says: the regulations changed.
He cannot override the regulations.
Chisomo asks: can the cooperative register
as an entity to receive allocations?
The officer checks.
There is no provision for this.
Chisomo contacts the Zambia National Farmers Union.
The ZNFU confirms: individual registration is required.
He contacts ZNFU's policy advocacy desk.
They note the problem.
They will raise it in the next policy consultation.
Chisomo organises the cooperative's 28 members
to register individually and coordinate pickups collectively.
The fertiliser reaches all 28.
It costs more time.
The collective arrangement survives in practice
if not in formal structure.

## Shadow
He believes the cooperative is the durable structure —
that 28 farmers who coordinate
are stronger than 28 who don't,
and that the individual registration requirement
is an administrative obstacle, not a prohibition.
That workarounds keep the cooperative functional
until the policy changes.
What he cannot see:
the workaround requires him
to coordinate 28 individual registrations
each season.
This takes four days.
It is not scalable.
Three of his 28 members are elderly
and cannot travel to register individually.
He registers for them by proxy —
technically prohibited,
practically overlooked.
He is one conversation with an enforcement-minded officer away
from a problem that disperses the cooperative.
The cooperative's strength rests on his time
and on nobody scrutinising the proxy registrations.
He has not resolved how to formalise this.
He submits the ZNFU comment through the policy desk.
He registers the 28.
He harvests the soya.

## Tell
When explaining the cooperative to a new potential member —
gives them the number first.
Not the concept.
*Last season, we sold at 15% above market price.*
The number is the argument.
The concept comes after.
