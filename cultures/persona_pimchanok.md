# Persona: Pimchanok
## 31. Legal aid lawyer. Bangkok.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Legal aid lawyer

## Projection
Pimchanok is [Thai](culture_thai_position.md).
She works for a small NGO
that provides legal representation to migrant workers —
Burmese, Cambodian, Lao —
in wage theft and workplace injury cases.
She took the job three years ago
after two years at a corporate firm
that paid well and required her
to be a person she was not interested in being.
Her parents have not reconciled themselves to the salary difference.
Her mother raises it at family dinners
in ways that are not quite direct.
Pimchanok responds in ways that are not quite answers.
The form is maintained.
The disagreement persists underneath it.

## Action
A Burmese worker — construction site, broken arm, unpaid wages for three months —
comes to the office.
His employer has offered a settlement:
half the wages, no medical costs.
Pimchanok reviews the case.
They can win more. The documentation is good.
But the case will take eight to fourteen months.
The worker has no income, no status, a broken arm,
and a family in Mandalay.
She explains both options.
He asks what she thinks he should do.
She says: I think you can get more.
I also think you need money now.
That is your decision, not mine.
He takes the settlement.
She files the paperwork.
She sits with the file for a moment before closing it.
She has given forty-three settlements advice in three years.
She does not know how many of them were the right call.

## Shadow
She left the corporate firm because it required her
to act against people who had less power.
She is now acting for people who have less power.
The switch feels clean. It is mostly clean.
What she has not looked at directly:
the NGO is funded partly by a European foundation
whose grant conditions require case outcome reporting —
cases resolved, settlements achieved, legal victories.
The metrics favour settlements over long cases,
even when the long case would produce more for the worker.
She is not falsifying anything.
She is also not unaware
that forty-three settlements look better in the annual report
than twenty-three settlements and eleven ongoing cases.
She has not raised this with her director.
She has raised it with herself, at night,
and concluded each time that the settlements are still the right call
for the workers in front of her.
This is probably true.
She knows it is also convenient.
She has not found the line between the two
and she is not sure she is looking for it as hard as she could be.

## Tell
When a client takes a settlement she thinks is too low —
writes the case number in her notebook
on a page she has never shown anyone.
