# Piece: The Anglophone Crisis
## The federation that was promised in 1961, dismantled in 1972, and turned into a civil war in 2016.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Yaoundé / Bamenda](culture_cameroonian_place_yaounde.md)

## Place
Two places.
Yaoundé: the capital. Francophone. Where the constitution was amended.
Bamenda: the Northwest region's capital. Anglophone.
Where the lawyers took to the streets in 2016
because Francophone judges had been sent
to common-law courts.
The distance between them is 365 kilometres.
The distance between their political realities
is the piece.

## Load Bearing
Remove the 1972 dismantling of the federation
and Cameroon is an unusual African country —
a successfully functioning bilingual federal state,
with two legal systems, two educational systems,
two administrative traditions
within one national framework.
Imperfect, contested, but operating.
With it: Cameroon is a country
where the Anglophone minority — 20% of the population —
watched its constitutional guarantees
erased by presidential decree,
its common law courts infiltrated by civil law judges,
its GCE examinations assimilated toward the Baccalauréat,
its senior government representation
systematically reduced —
over fifty years of accumulated grievance
that erupted in 2016
when Francophone judges
were assigned to Anglophone courts.
The lawyers struck.
The teachers struck.
The students struck.
The government sent the military.
2017: Ambazonia declared independent.
Armed groups formed.
Atrocities committed by both sides.
1.5 million people displaced.
The crisis is still running in 2026.
No negotiated solution.
Biya has governed through it
without resolving it.
The 2019 National Dialogue:
held with opposition leaders imprisoned.
The Special Status granted to Anglophone regions:
rejected by the separatists as insufficient.
The war continues in the bush.
The Bamileke businesspeople of Douala
do not stop trading.
The civil servants in Yaoundé do not stop working.
The country manages around the crisis
without resolving it —
which is both an indictment
and a form of Cameroonian competence.
Remove the piece: a bilingual federal state
with manageable tensions.
With it: a civil war that has been running
for nine years
in a part of the country
that most of the world cannot locate on a map,
while the rest of the country continues
as if it is happening somewhere else —
because for Francophone Cameroon,
it largely is.

## Apparent
1884: Germany colonises Kamerun.
1916: Britain and France divide it after WWI.
French mandate: East Cameroon.
British mandate: Southern Cameroons (part of Nigeria).
1960: French Cameroon independent.
February 1961: plebiscite.
British Southern Cameroons votes
to join Cameroon rather than Nigeria.
October 1, 1961: Federal Republic of Cameroon.
Two states: East Cameroon, West Cameroon.
Federal constitution: bicameral, two-state system.
In practice: power centralised in Yaoundé.
Pierre Messmer, French High Commissioner:
notes privately that the federation
is *une constitution faussement fédérale* — a fake federation.
1966: single-party system. Opposition banned.
1972: Ahidjo's referendum: unitary state.
95% approve — Anglophones note the absence of choice.
1984: Biya renames the country *République du Cameroun* —
the original name of the French territory only.
Removes the second star from the flag.
Anglophones read this clearly.
1993: All Anglophone Conference in Buea.
Demand for federalism.
1992 elections contested: Biya wins over Anglophone John Fru Ndi.
International observers: not free and fair.
2016: Francophone judges assigned to common-law courts.
Lawyers strike. Teachers strike. Students strike.
November 2016: government shuts off internet
in Anglophone regions for 93 days.
October 1, 2017: Ambazonia independence declared.
Armed conflict begins.
2019: National Dialogue.
Opposition leaders imprisoned before it starts.
Special Status granted: separatists reject it.
2020–2026: conflict continues.
Biya: 91 years old. Still president.
Managing through the crisis without ending it.
