# Position: Tunisian
## The Arab exception — the country that had a successful revolution, kept it, and then watched it slip.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[Bourguiba's Bet and the Jasmine Revolution](culture_tunisian_piece_jasmine.md).
The oldest continuous statehood in North Africa —
Carthage, Rome's province, Islamic scholarship,
Ottoman province, French Protectorate, independence.
The borders have barely moved in two thousand years.
This produces a specific Tunisian stability of self:
the country knows what it is.
Habib Bourguiba — *al-Mujahid al-Akbar*, the Supreme Fighter —
gave women the right to divorce in 1956,
the year of independence.
Legalised abortion in 1973.
Built one of the best-educated populations in the Arab world.
Did all of this while governing as an autocrat.
Both are the same Bourguiba.
The civil society tradition: deep.
The UGTT — the Tunisian General Labour Union —
founded in 1944, earlier than independence.
The lawyers' order. The human rights league.
The organisations that existed before the state
and outlasted its authoritarian phases.
The collectivism is moderate by Arab standards.
The small country creates high density of connection —
everyone knows someone who knows someone.
The hierarchy is real but negotiable —
PDI lower than Egypt, lower than Algeria.
The position does not accept authority quietly.
It negotiates it. This is the Tunisian difference.

## Orders
Argue.
The Tunisian position debates — in Arabic, in French,
in the cafés of Tunis and the seminar rooms of universities.
The debate is the operating mode.
Not because argument is preferred for its own sake
but because Tunisia's specificity
comes from the sustained pressure of civil society
on power.
The 2013 National Dialogue Quartet —
the UGTT, the lawyers' bar, human rights organisations —
pulled the country back from the edge of civil war
by sitting in the same room and arguing
until an agreement emerged.
They won the Nobel Peace Prize for it.
The argument is the mechanism that works.

## Loses
The democracy it built.
2021: President Kais Saied suspends parliament.
Assumes emergency powers.
2022: a new constitution concentrating power in the presidency.
The transition that seemed stable
was not as stable as it looked.
The position carries the specific grief
of a country that proved democracy was possible
in the Arab world —
the singular achievement,
the one that survived when all the others fell —
and then watched its own democratic structure
be dismantled by a president
elected through that structure.
The Quartet's framework.
The 2014 constitution — one of the best in the Arab world.
Gone, or severely weakened.
The country that demonstrated the exception
is now demonstrating how the exception can end.

## Drives
On the loss —
the civil society organisations are still there.
The UGTT is still there.
The lawyers are still there.
The journalists are still there.
The tradition of organised civilian pressure
that produced the Quartet
and produced the 2014 constitution
pre-dates Saied and will outlast him.
The position holds this
with the specific Tunisian mix
of pride, grief, and continued argument.
The argument does not stop.
The argument is the position.
