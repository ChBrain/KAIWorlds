## Shown
- Djemaa el-Fna. Marrakech.
  The square that UNESCO designated as a Masterpiece
  of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity.
  Storytellers. Snake charmers. Acrobats.
  Henna artists. Food stalls at dusk.
  The smoke of the grills rising.
  And behind the performance:
  a city of 900,000 people
  with traffic and apartment blocks
  and a tech park called Technopark
  and a university.
  The Djemaa is the face Marrakech shows.
  The rest is the city Marrakech is.
  Both are real.
  The square was almost demolished for a parking lot in the 1990s.
  It was saved by UNESCO classification.
  The international apparatus preserved
  something the local government had priced
  at the cost of parking.
  The square knows this.
- The Hassan II Mosque, Casablanca.
  Third largest mosque in the world.
  Built over the Atlantic.
  At night, a laser from its minaret points toward Mecca —
  visible for fifty kilometres.
  Completed 1993. Financed by public subscription —
  every Moroccan contributed, some voluntarily.
  Hassan II said the king should be seen praying.
  The building makes the prayer visible
  from the sea.
- Rue Mohammed V, Rabat.
  The administrative capital.
  The ministries. The parliament.
  The Avenue Mohammed V running through the city centre —
  every Moroccan city has an Avenue Mohammed V.
  The naming is the Makhzen at street level:
  the monarchy organised into the urban grid.
- The Mellah. The Jewish quarter. Every major Moroccan city has one.
  Many are empty now —
  the Jewish community that was 250,000 in 1948
  is 3,000 today.
  The architecture is intact.
  The doors, the balconies, the Star of David
  over some doorframes.
  Morocco's relationship to its Jewish history
  is the most honest in the Arab world —
  the king includes it explicitly in official identity.
  The mellahs are maintained as heritage.
  This does not reverse the departure.
  It remembers it.
- The mountains visible from Marrakech.
  The High Atlas on clear days.
  Behind the Atlas: the Sahara.
  Behind the Sahara: the Western Sahara.
  The territory whose status is unresolved.
  All of it visible in clear weather,
  layered, from the Djemaa.

## Withheld
- The real opinion of the king.
  Moroccans speak freely about politics,
  about the government, about ministers.
  About the king: rarely, and in private,
  and with attention to who is in the room.
  The affection is often genuine.
  The restraint is also strategic.
  The room shows the warmth.
  The calculation about what can be said
  is the room's invisible architecture.
