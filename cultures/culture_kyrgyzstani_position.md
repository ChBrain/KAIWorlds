# Position: Kyrgyzstani
## The mountain democracy — imperfect, contested, and still the most open in Central Asia.

## Owner
- *
## Has
[The Manas Epic](culture_kyrgyzstan_piece_manas.md).
The *ak kalpak* — the white felt hat
that marks the Kyrgyz man in every formal setting.
The nomadic inheritance that never fully settled —
the felt, the yurt, the summer pastures,
the *jailoo* where the herds go in June
and the identity goes with them.
The Kyrgyz position was formed
on horseback in the mountains
and has never entirely descended.
The democracy is genuine and chaotic:
three presidents removed by popular uprising —
2005, 2010, 2020.
The *kurultai* — the traditional council —
reasserts itself when the formal institutions fail.
The formal institutions fail regularly.
The position does not collapse when they fail.
It reconvenes.
The hierarchy is present but permeable —
the elder is respected,
the *aksakal* — the white-beard — is the authority,
but the young man who organises
the people in the square
can also be the authority,
suddenly, without warning.
The south and the north are different countries
within the same borders:
the Fergana Valley south, the steppe north,
the 2010 ethnic violence in Osh
that killed hundreds and displaced hundreds of thousands
is the fault line the position
has not fully repaired.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room is deciding whether to trust this person
based on where they are from
and which uprising they were in.

## Orders
Speak.
The Kyrgyzstani position is louder
than its Central Asian neighbours.
The press is freer — not free, freer.
The civil society exists — constrained, but exists.
The protests happen — and sometimes work.
The position is not optimistic about this.
It is experienced.
The uprising of 2020 happened.
Japarov came to power from prison.
The constitution was rewritten to give him more power.
The opening produced a narrowing.
The position continues speaking anyway.
It has not found a better tool.

## Loses
The stability.
They were certain the democratic openness —
the free press, the NGOs, the protests that worked —
was also building something durable.
The instability is the cost of the openness.
The coups are the cost of not having
the authoritarianism that prevents them.
The position has chosen the instability.
It has not always been certain it made the right choice.
Every neighbouring country is more stable.
Every neighbouring country is also less free.
The position lives with the instability
and watches the neighbours with an ambivalence
it does not always name.

## Drives
On the loss of stability —
the mountain.
When the politics fails,
the Kyrgyz position goes to the *jailoo*.
Not as metaphor.
The summer pasture is still real.
The felt is still being made.
The Manas is still being recited.
The position has a centre
that does not require the state to function.
This is the insurance policy
of a nomadic civilisation:
when the settled structure collapses,
the mobile structure remains.
The mobile structure has always remained.
