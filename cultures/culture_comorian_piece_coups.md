# Piece: The Coups and the Diaspora in Marseille
## Twenty attempts to take power by force — and the community in France that holds the nation together from outside it.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Moroni](culture_comorian_place_moroni.md)

## Place
Moroni. Grande Comore.
The old port. The Friday mosque —
the oldest mosque in the Indian Ocean region.
The *médina* of stone houses with carved wooden doors.
The Indian Ocean visible from every street that ends at the water.
The volcano, Karthala, visible from every street that doesn't.

## Load Bearing
Remove the coup history
and Comoros is a small Islamic archipelago
navigating post-colonial development
without the specific political instability
that has made it the coup capital of the world per capita.
With it: the pattern that explains
why institutional development has stalled,
why each elected government
has been preoccupied with its own survival,
why foreign investors — the ones that arrive —
price in political risk
that a country of 900,000 people
should not have to carry.
The Bob Denard coups are load-bearing specifically:
a French mercenary, working sometimes for France,
sometimes for his own account,
who converted to Islam, became Saïd Mustapha Mahdjoub,
led four separate coups or coup attempts across twenty years,
and was eventually arrested in 1995 by French forces
at the request of the Comorian government.
The French state that sponsored him
arrested him when he became inconvenient.
The position carries this arithmetic:
the coups were not purely indigenous dysfunction.
They were partly the form that external interference took
when the formal mechanisms were unavailable.
The diaspora in Marseille is the counter-piece.
100,000–150,000 Comorians in France,
concentrated in the northern arrondissements,
the 13th, 14th, 15th —
a community that has maintained language,
maintained the *Anda* across the Mediterranean,
maintained Comoros as a project
even when the project in the archipelago
was being disrupted by the next coup.
Remove it: the remittance economy collapses.
The *Anda* becomes impossible for many families.
The social fabric of the islands, already stretched, tears further.

## Apparent
1975: independence from France.
First president, Ahmed Abdallah, overthrown within a month.
Bob Denard's first intervention — on behalf of rival faction.
1978: Denard leads coup. Abdallah restored.
The Presidential Guard, trained and led by Denard and his men.
Denard converts to Islam.
1989: Abdallah assassinated.
Denard suspected. Denard departs under French pressure.
Returns 1995: coup attempt against Djohar.
French forces arrive. Denard surrenders.
Tried in France. Released.
Dies in Paris 2007.
Coup attempts: 1975, 1978, 1995, 1997 (Anjouan secession),
2001, 2008 (Anjouan crisis).
Twenty incidents of attempted or successful unconstitutional transfer of power.
More than any other country in the world relative to years of independence.
2013: new constitution. Rotating presidency between islands.
2019: President Azali amends constitution to reset term limits.
The cycle adjusts its form.
The Marseille diaspora:
established from the 1970s.
The *Anda* performed in Marseille for guests who fly from Moroni.
The satellite dishes on the buildings in the 13th.
The Shikomori in the schools.
The money transfer offices.
The community that is Comoros outside Comoros.
