## Shown
- The CBD. The glass towers of the financial district.
  Upperhill. Westlands. The banks and the NGOs
  and the tech companies and the embassies.
  The regional headquarters of the UN Environment Programme.
  The only UN headquarters in the developing world.
  Nairobi chose this and earned it.
- Kibera. Two kilometres from the glass towers.
  The largest urban informal settlement in Africa.
  Population estimates range from 170,000 to 1,000,000.
  The range is not an error — nobody is certain.
  The settlement that is not on official maps
  but is thoroughly mapped by its residents.
  It has its own economy, its own politics, its own media.
  The distance between the CBD and Kibera
  is the distance Kenya has not yet crossed.
- Uhuru Park. The public park in the centre.
  Where Wangari Maathai planted trees in 1989
  to stop the government building on it.
  She won. The park is still a park.
  The Green Belt Movement still planting.
  Nairobi's lungs, next to the parliament.
- iHub and the Silicon Savannah.
  The tech ecosystem that made Nairobi
  the fintech capital of Africa.
  M-Pesa — mobile money — launched here in 2007,
  the same year the election was disputed.
  Two things happening in the same city
  in the same year.
  One of them changed the world.
  One of them nearly destroyed the country.
  Both are Nairobi.
- The International Criminal Court witness protection offices.
  Discreet. Present.
  The ICC indictments of Kenyan political leaders
  after 2007–08 were eventually dropped.
  The process was watched.
  The watching changed how political leaders calculated.
  The offices are still there.
