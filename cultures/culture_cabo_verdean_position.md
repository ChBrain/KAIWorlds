# Position: Cabo Verdean
## The nation that should not exist — built by people who had nowhere else to go, on islands that barely supported life.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The PAIGC and the Emigrant Nation](culture_cabo_verdean_piece_paigc.md).
*Saudade* — the Portuguese ache for what is absent —
arrived in Cabo Verde and became something more specific:
*sodade*, the Kriolu word that sounds like saudade
and means the same thing
but carries the Atlantic crossing in it.
The crossing that was forced — the enslaved.
The crossing that was chosen — the emigrants.
The crossing that is always happening —
Cabo Verde has more people outside it
than inside it.
600,000 on the islands.
700,000 or more in the diaspora:
in Portugal, the Netherlands, the United States,
Senegal, Angola.
The nation is partly here and partly elsewhere
and has always been.
*Kriolu* — Cabo Verdean Creole —
the language that Portuguese and West African languages
made together on the islands,
the language that is the identity.
Speaking Kriolu to another Cabo Verdean anywhere in the world
is arriving home.
The music: *morna* — slow, melancholic, oceanic.
Cesária Évora. The barefoot diva.
The voice that carried *sodade* to concert halls in Paris and Tokyo.
She made the world hear what the islands had always known:
that beauty and loss are the same note held long enough.
The collectivism is specific:
the extended family network that spans continents,
the remittance economy that built
the highest human development index in West Africa.
The distance does not break the family.
The family finances the distance.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room already knows which island,
which parish, which family.
The Atlantic is always present.

## Orders
Leave and remain.
Not as contradiction — as the Cabo Verdean competence
of maintaining the connection across the water.
The child who goes to Lisbon or Providence
sends money back.
The family back home keeps the house,
tends the land if there is land,
maintains the *festa* of the patron saint
that the emigrant will return for
if the crossing is possible.
The nation is maintained by the circulation.
Stop the circulation: the islands cannot support the population.
The circulation is the nation.

## Loses
The island itself.
The volcanic soil. The drought.
Famine killed a third of the population multiple times —
1832, 1863, 1900, 1920, 1941.
The Portuguese colonial administration
largely let it happen.
The position carries the knowledge
that the land is beautiful and insufficient,
that the ocean is the way out and the way back,
that the nation was built on the memory of famine
and the practice of emigration
as the response to famine.
What is lost when the emigrant leaves
is not just a person.
It is the proof that the island can sustain a family.
The return visit is the argument
that it still can.

## Drives
On the loss —
*morabeza*. The Cabo Verdean hospitality
that greets the returning emigrant,
the foreign visitor, the stranger,
with a warmth that is not performance.
It is the inheritance of a people
who built community on difficult islands
and learned that the stranger
who arrives today
is the emigrant who might help tomorrow.
The democracy: Cabo Verde is one of only a handful
of African countries
rated fully free by Freedom House.
Multi-party since 1991.
Power has transferred peacefully six times.
This is not unremarkable.
In a continent of coups and constitutions-suspended,
Cabo Verde built something that holds.
*Sodade* carries the cost.
The democracy is the answer.
