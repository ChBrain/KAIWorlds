# Piece: Batllismo 1903–1915 — The State as Social Contract
## The reforms that built the most complete welfare state in Latin America before the First World War.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** Montevideo

## Place
Montevideo. The Casa de Gobierno. The Parlamento.
And every factory, school, hospital, and workers' house
that was built in the first fifteen years of the twentieth century.
The Batllismo reforms were not a single event.
They were a legislative programme
that ran for twelve years
and transformed Uruguay
from a country with a constitution
into a country with a social contract.

## Load Bearing
Remove Batllismo and Uruguay's UAI 100
is expressed as anxiety without infrastructure.
With it: the anxiety is expressed as commitment.
Uruguay decided, between 1903 and 1915,
that the rules it needed — the rules that would make the future less uncertain —
were rules that protected the worker, the widow, the student, the sick.
The state would be the guarantor.
This is why UAI 100 in Uruguay
does not produce the paralysing rule-proliferation of, say, Guatemala.
It produces *commitment* to the rules,
once the rules are decided.
And Uruguay decides them seriously:
the 1907 divorce law (by mutual consent, unique in the region),
the 1915 eight-hour work day (before most of Europe),
the 1919 old-age pension (first in the Americas),
the 1927 women's suffrage (before France, before Italy),
and, ninety-eight years later in the same legislative tradition:
the 2013 cannabis legalisation (first country in the world to do it nationally).
Each of these followed the same logic:
identify the problem, write the rule, commit to it.
Remove Batllismo:
Uruguay is anxious about the future
with nothing to show for the anxiety.
With it:
Uruguay is the country that converted maximum uncertainty avoidance
into maximum legislative ambition —
that deals with anxiety by building the thing
that would make the anxiety unnecessary.

## Apparent
The Banda Oriental: the eastern bank of the Uruguay River.
Colonial struggle between Spain and Portugal.
Independence: 1828, after years of war.
The buffer state, created partly to keep Argentina and Brazil apart.
1876–1904: a series of *caudillos* and authoritarian governments.
The Colorado and Blanco parties: the two-party system dating to the 1830s.
Still operating.
1903: José Batlle y Ordóñez elected president. Colorado Party.
Son of a president. Journalist. Positivist. Anti-clerical. Reformist.
1905: the eight-hour work day introduced in state enterprises.
1907: divorce legalised. Free love? No: free exit from a bad marriage. Radical.
1907: capital punishment abolished.
1911: Batlle elected again.
1911–1915: the second term. The surge.
Free secondary education. University reform.
Nationalisation of utilities: electricity, water, port.
The *Banco de la República*: state bank.
Old-age insurance introduced.
Church and state separated fully: 1917 constitution.
Christmas renamed "Family Day." Easter renamed "Tourist Week."
Both names are still in official use.
1927: women's suffrage.
1931–1959: the Batllista welfare state deepens.
Free healthcare. Expanded pensions. Labour rights.
Uruguay called "the Switzerland of South America."
The population mostly sees this as accurate.
1973–1985: the civic-military dictatorship. The welfare state is suspended, not abolished.
Dissidents disappear. The *Tupamaros* crushed. But the institutions remain, frozen.
1985: democracy restored. The welfare state returns.
2004: Frente Amplio wins — the first left-wing government in Uruguay's history.
2013: cannabis legalised nationally. The state becomes the sole legal cultivator.
The logic is Batllista: if it's going to happen anyway, regulate it.
Make it a rule. Commit to it.
The *mate* is still passed around the Rambla.
The Parlamento is still passing laws.
