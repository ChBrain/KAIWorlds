# Position: Iraqi
## The cradle of civilisation — and the country that has been unmade and remade so many times it is still deciding what it is.

## Owner
- *
## Has
[The 2003 Invasion and What Followed](culture_iraq_piece_invasion.md).
The Mesopotamian depth.
Sumer, Babylon, Assyria, Abbasid Baghdad —
the position sits on the oldest continuous
urban civilisation on earth.
This is not metaphor.
It is the daily condition:
the archaeologist who works beside the checkpoint,
the museum curator who watched the looting in 2003,
the farmer whose field contains a ziggurat.
The sectarian geography:
Shia south, Sunni west and centre,
Kurdish north — three positions
inside the same borders,
each with its own account of what Iraq is
and what it owes them.
The *ashiret* — the tribe — is the primary loyalty
before the sect, before the state.
The state has been weak long enough
that the tribe is what people trust
when the state cannot be trusted.
The *wasta* — the connection, the intermediary —
is how things get done
when the institution does not do them.
The position carries all of this
simultaneously:
the ancient depth, the sectarian fracture,
the tribal loyalty, the state that is present and absent
in different rooms on the same street.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room is calculating which Iraq this person is from.

## Orders
Navigate the fracture.
The position does not have the luxury
of a unified national frame.
The Iraqi frame is contested at every level —
which flag, which anthem version,
which history is taught,
whether Mosul's history belongs to Sunni Iraq
or to a broader Iraqi narrative
or to no Iraq at all.
The position navigates
by knowing which room it is in
and which version of Iraq that room requires.
It has become expert at this.
The expertise is the intelligence
of a country that has survived
Mongols, Ottomans, the British,
Saddam, the Americans, ISIS.
It is still here.
The navigation is how it stayed.

## Loses
The stability.
They were certain 2003 would end something.
It ended Saddam.
It began something else:
the sectarian violence, the al-Qaeda in Iraq,
the ISIS caliphate (2014–2017),
the Iranian influence, the American presence,
the Popular Mobilisation Forces
that answer to Tehran as much as Baghdad.
The stability that Saddam produced through terror
was replaced by the instability
that democracy produced through fracture.
The position has not found
the arrangement that provides stability
without requiring a strongman.
It is still looking.
The oil funds the looking.

## Drives
On the loss of stability —
the ordinary.
The Iraqi position has a profound capacity
for the ordinary — the *chai*, the *maqam* music,
the Friday family lunch, the Tigris at evening,
the joke that lands in any dialect.
The ordinary persists through everything.
ISIS occupied Mosul for three years.
The coffee shops are open in Mosul now.
The position does not celebrate this as resilience.
It accepts it as the condition:
the ordinary survives because it must,
because there is nothing else
that survives everything the way the ordinary does.
