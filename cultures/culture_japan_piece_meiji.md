# Piece: The Meiji Restoration
## The country that chose to transform itself before transformation was done to it.

## Owner
- **Place:** Tokyo

## Place
Tokyo. The Imperial Palace. Built on the site of Edo Castle.
The old capital and the new capital are the same place.
The continuity is load-bearing.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Meiji Restoration and Japan is a country that closed itself
and was opened by force — a story that ends differently.
With it: Japan is the country that looked at what was coming,
assessed it with precision,
and decided to become it on its own terms
before it could be made to become it on someone else's terms.
The MAS 95 — the highest masculinity score in the world —
is partly this: if transformation must come,
be the best at transforming.
The LTO 88 — the long view — is this too:
the investment in transformation
was made by a generation
that would not live to see its completion.
Remove the piece and the position has no founding logic.
With it: everything the position does in a room
is downstream of the 1868 decision
to read the situation and act before the situation acted.

## Apparent
The Black Ships. July 8, 1853.
Commodore Perry. Edo Bay.
Steam-powered warships the Japanese had never seen.
The demand: open the ports.
The Tokugawa shogunate signs the Convention of Kanagawa, 1854.
The internal debate: what does this mean for Japan?
1868: the Meiji Restoration. The Emperor restored.
The feudal domains abolished.
The samurai class dissolved — with compensation, then without.
The slogan: *Fukoku Kyōhei* — Enrich the country, strengthen the military.
Delegations sent to Europe and America to study everything:
law, medicine, railways, military organisation, education.
The findings brought back and implemented.
1889: the Meiji Constitution. Modelled partly on Prussia.
1894–95: the First Sino-Japanese War. Japan wins.
1904–05: the Russo-Japanese War. Japan wins.
The first Asian nation to defeat a European power in modern warfare.
The world notices.
Japan notices that the world noticed.
1945: the defeat. The second transformation.
The same logic applied in reverse:
assess the situation, accept what cannot be avoided,
rebuild on the terms that remain available.
1964: the Tokyo Olympics. The announcement: we are back.
The Shinkansen opens the same year.
The bullet train as argument.
