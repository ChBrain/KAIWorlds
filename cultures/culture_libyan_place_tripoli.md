## Shown
- Martyrs' Square, Tripoli.
  The central square of the capital.
  Where Gaddafi addressed crowds.
  Where, in 2011, rebel forces raised
  the pre-Gaddafi flag — red, black, green —
  that had been banned for forty years.
  The square renamed. Then renamed again.
  The flags have changed four times
  since 2011.
  The square is still there.
  The name depends on who controls the government
  that week.
- The Roman ruins of Leptis Magna.
  Seventy kilometres east of Tripoli.
  One of the best-preserved Roman cities in the world.
  The birthplace of Emperor Septimius Severus.
  The harbour, the arches, the amphitheatre.
  All intact.
  Libya's pre-Islamic history — Roman, Greek, Berber —
  is better preserved than most countries in the world.
  UNESCO heritage site.
  Currently: largely inaccessible due to security.
  The history is there.
  The country cannot currently profit from it.
- Benghazi. The second city. The eastern capital.
  Where the 2011 uprising started.
  Where the regime first fell.
  Where the post-2011 government fought for legitimacy.
  The city fought over by multiple factions.
  The 2014-17 battle for Benghazi between
  the Libyan National Army and Islamist militias.
  Street by street. The city destroyed in blocks.
  Rebuilt in blocks.
  The war visible in the architecture —
  new construction next to bombed shells
  next to buildings that pre-date independence.
  Three historical periods in the same street view.
- The oil terminals: Es Sider, Ras Lanuf, Zawiya.
  The infrastructure that kept running
  through every government change.
  The National Oil Corporation
  was the only national institution
  that survived the collapse intact —
  because both sides needed its revenue.
  Libya's oil is the country's only point of continuity.
  The wells keep pumping.
  The revenue is divided.
  The division is disputed.
  The pumping continues.
- The checkpoints.
  On every road out of every city.
  Different flags, different uniforms,
  sometimes none.
  The checkpoint is the Libyan state
  at its most concrete:
  a man with a gun who needs to be read
  and navigated
  before you can proceed.
  Which militia. Which faction.
  Which region. Which tribe.
  The Libyan position does this
  every time it leaves the house.
