# Piece: Kanga Bé — The State That Never Arrived
## The phrase that describes the condition — and Bokassa as its most extreme distillation.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Bangui](culture_car_place_bangui.md)

## Place
Bangui.
The Ngaragba Prison —
where Bokassa's opponents died of suffocation
in overcrowded cells.
Where student protesters were beaten and jailed in 1979.
Where the French decided it was enough
and sent Operation Barracuda
to remove him.
The prison still operates.
Different prisoners.
The same building.

## Load Bearing
Remove the persistent absence of the state —
remove Bokassa, remove the coup cycles,
remove the current civil war —
and the CAR is a landlocked Central African country
with mineral wealth, forest resources,
and the specific challenge of governing
80 ethnic groups across a large territory
with French colonial institutions
that were designed for extraction, not governance.
That challenge is real and would exist regardless.
With it: the CAR is the country
where the state has been
so consistently predatory or absent
that the population developed
*kanga bé* as a philosophy —
not the philosophy of the defeated
but the philosophy of people
who have accurately assessed
the relationship between the citizen
and the Central African state
and chosen endurance as the rational response.
Bokassa is the distillation —
not the cause.
The cause is older than Bokassa:
the pre-colonial tribes' opposition to the state-form,
then the colonial state as plantation,
then independence inherited that plantation
and kept it running for different masters.
Bokassa simply made it maximal:
the coronation ceremony
that cost 30% of the national budget.
The school uniforms made by his wife's company,
required of all students,
enforced by his personal violence —
100 students killed when they protested.
France removing him with paratroopers
because the abuses became too expensive
to defend internationally.
The *Barracuda Syndrome*:
Central Africans so dispossessed
of their own history
that they were not even responsible
for deposing their own tyrant.
Remove the piece: a governance challenge.
With it: the accumulated proof
that the state as offered to Central Africans
has not been theirs,
and the cultural intelligence —
*kanga bé* — that developed in response.

## Apparent
1960: independence. Barthélemy Boganda
— the one leader with genuine national vision —
already dead, March 1959, plane crash.
David Dacko succeeds him.
1965: Jean-Bédel Bokassa coup.
Bokassa is Boganda's great-nephew.
He uses Boganda's name to justify the coup.
He governs by patronage, fear, and hunger for recognition.
1972: President for Life.
1976: Emperor of the Central African Empire.
December 4, 1977: Bokassa I crowned.
Cost: approximately one-third of the national budget.
Funded largely by France.
Present: Valéry Giscard d'Estaing personally received
a gift of diamonds from Bokassa.
April 1979: students protest school uniform decree.
Bokassa's Imperial Guard kills approximately 100.
Bokassa may have participated personally.
September 20, 1979: Operation Barracuda.
French paratroopers remove Bokassa.
Dacko reinstalled.
1981: Kolingba coup.
1993: first multi-party elections. Patassé wins.
1996–97: three army mutinies.
French troops quell them.
2003: Bozizé coup.
2013: Séléka coalition seizes Bangui.
Muslim rebel coalition from the north.
Djotodia declares himself president.
Anti-Balaka (Christian) militias form.
Inter-religious violence across the country.
2014: African Union and French forces restore some order.
2016: Touadéra elected president.
2018: Wagner Group mercenaries deployed.
They fight the rebel coalition.
They also commit documented atrocities.
2021: Touadéra re-elected in disputed vote.
Opposition boycott.
2023: Prigozhin dies. Wagner diminishes.
Russian state intelligence presence continues.
2026: Touadéra still governing.
Armed groups still controlling significant territory outside Bangui.
*Kanga bé.*
Wait it out.
The elephants are still fighting.
The grass is still suffering.
