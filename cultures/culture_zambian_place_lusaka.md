# Place: Lusaka / Copperbelt
## Location: Zambia

## Owner

## Shown
**Lusaka:**
- Cairo Road.
  The commercial spine of Lusaka.
  The banks. The shops.
  The informal traders on every pavement.
  The economy that moves beside the formal economy
  at the same pace.
  Lusaka is Central Africa's fastest-growing city.
  It doubles every decade.
  The migrants arriving from the Copperbelt
  as the mine jobs diminish.
  The city absorbing them
  without adequate infrastructure
  but with the resilience
  of a place that has been absorbing people
  since the Line of Rail brought workers
  to the Copperbelt a century ago.
- The State House.
  Where Kaunda governed for 27 years.
  Where he left — peacefully —
  when Chiluba won.
  Where six presidents have governed since.
  Where Hichilema governs now.
  The building that is the physical evidence
  of the transfers that were accepted.
  Unremarkable architecture.
  Remarkable record.
- The Freedom Statue.
  A bronze figure with broken chains.
  The independence monument.
  Next to it: the national flag, always flying.
  The statue and the flag
  in the context of a city
  where the copper wealth that paid for independence
  is still the economy's backbone
  and still priced in markets
  the country does not control.
  The chains are symbolic.
  The dependency is structural.

**Copperbelt — Kitwe:**
- The mine headframes.
  The towers visible from the road.
  Some operating. Some decommissioned.
  The mineral beneath: still there, running lower.
  The workers who remain:
  fewer than in 1970,
  when the Copperbelt had 100,000 mine workers.
  Now: less than half that.
  The unions still operating.
  The tradition still present.
  The town planning designed for a company town
  now governed by a municipality
  that inherited infrastructure
  designed for a population
  twice what it now has.
- The Nkana Mine.
  Still operating.
  The oldest active copper mine in Zambia.
  Where the mine workers organised in the 1940s.
  Where the labour tradition began.
  The same shaft. Different owners.
  The value still leaves faster
  than the country captures it.

## Offers

  A room between the mine and the market,
  where the democratic tradition is real
  and the copper dependency is also real,
  and where *One Zambia, One Nation*
  has managed to mean something
  without resolving the 72 ethnic groups
  that the motto was invented to hold together.

## Withheld
- The mine's terms.
  The Development Agreements
  signed when ZCCM was privatised in the 1990s
  included tax stabilisation clauses
  that were more favourable to the mining companies
  than to Zambia.
  The details were confidential.
  The public was told the privatisation was necessary.
  The terms were not disclosed until investigative journalists
  obtained them years later.
  The room in Kitwe knows
  that the copper leaves.
  The room does not always know
  where the revenue goes between the shaft
  and the national budget.
  That gap is the withheld.
