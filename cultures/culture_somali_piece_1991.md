# Piece: The Collapse of 1991
## The year the state disappeared — and the clan became the country.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Mogadishu](culture_somali_place_mogadishu.md)

## Place
Mogadishu. Villa Somalia — the presidential palace.
January 1991: Siad Barre flees.
He takes what he can carry.
The palace is looted.
The archives are destroyed.
The state disappears.
What replaces it: clan militias,
warlords, Al-Shabaab,
intermittent governments,
and the clan system —
which has been there the whole time.

## Load Bearing
Remove the collapse of 1991
and Somalia is a difficult post-colonial state
with a strong clan system
and contested political legitimacy.
With it: Somalia is the country
that demonstrated what happens
when an African state collapses entirely —
when there is no government,
no army, no police, no courts, no currency,
no schools, no hospitals for two decades —
and a people still exists.
The Somali people still exist.
The Somali language still exists.
The Somali diaspora still sends money home.
Somaliland still holds elections.
The clan system — the thing that was supposed
to be the obstacle to statehood —
became the infrastructure of survival.
Remove the piece:
a struggling post-colonial state managing clan politics.
With it: the proof that a people
can survive without a state,
and the question of whether the survival
is a foundation for the state
or the permanent alternative to it.
This question has not been answered.
It is the question the country is living inside.

## Apparent
1960: independence. British Somaliland and Italian Somaliland unite.
The five-pointed star on the flag:
the five Somali territories —
British Somaliland, Italian Somaliland,
French Somaliland (Djibouti), Ogaden (Ethiopia), NFD (Kenya).
The greater Somalia project.
1969: Siad Barre seizes power.
Scientific socialism. Clan identity banned publicly.
The clans banned but used: Barre builds his regime
on his own Darod sub-clan while proclaiming unity.
1977–78: the Ogaden War. Somalia invades Ethiopia to reclaim the Ogaden.
The Soviet Union switches sides. Defeat.
The military humiliation accelerates the regime's decline.
The 1980s: resistance movements form on clan lines.
The regime responds with mass killings.
The Isaaq clan in the northwest: 50,000–60,000 killed
in what is now called the Isaaq genocide.
1988: Hargeisa bombed by the Somali Air Force.
The city destroyed.
January 1991: Barre flees. The state collapses.
Mogadishu torn apart by Hawiye sub-clan fighting.
1993: Black Hawk Down. US intervention, withdrawal.
The world watches Somalia and decides not to intervene further.
1991: Somaliland declares independence in the northwest.
Not recognised. Stable.
Puntland: autonomous, not secessionist.
South-central Somalia: warlords, then Al-Shabaab.
2000s–2010s: intermittent federal governments.
The Transitional Federal Government. The Federal Government.
Elections held — indirectly, through clan-based selection.
Al-Shabaab controls significant territory in the south.
The federal government controls Mogadishu and selected towns.
The reconstruction visible in Mogadishu since 2010:
new buildings, new restaurants, a new corniche.
The war continuing outside the city.
Both present in the same country.
