# Place: Lilongwe / Blantyre
## Location: Malawi

## Shown
**Lilongwe:**
- The Parliament building.
  Where the 1993 referendum was ratified.
  Where the 2020 election outcome
  was legislated after the court annulled
  the fraudulent first result.
  The building where Malawian democracy
  does its legal work —
  fragile, functional, tested.
- The Kamuzu Mausoleum.
  The memorial to the 30-year president.
  Open to visitors.
  His portrait on the wall.
  The national narrative:
  founding father and authoritarian
  held in the same building.
  Both true. Both present.
  Malawi did not choose which one.
  It keeps them together.
- Old Town Market.
  The original Lilongwe market
  before the capital was formally established.
  Still trading.
  The tomatoes, the dried fish,
  the chitenje fabric.
  The *zitumbuwa* — deep-fried banana fritters —
  that have been here longer than the capital's name.

**Blantyre:**
- Limbe Market.
  The commercial centre.
  Where the tobacco brokers still operate.
  The price of Malawi's economy
  set here, in a market
  that responds to global prices
  Malawi does not determine.
  The farmers who grew the tobacco
  in Thyolo and Mulanje
  do not control what it is worth.
  They grow it again anyway.
  The dependency persists.
- The CCAP Church in Blantyre.
  The Church of Central Africa Presbyterian.
  Where the Catholic bishops wrote
  the 1992 pastoral letter
  that named Banda's authoritarianism
  and helped trigger the 1993 referendum.
  The church as the institution
  that could say what civil society could not.
  It said it.
  The referendum came the next year.

## Withheld
- What your family said about Banda while he was alive.
  In every Malawian family
  there is the version of Banda that was performed publicly
  and the version whispered at home.
  The performance: *Kamuzu ndi baba* — Kamuzu is father.
  The whisper: what he had done,
  who had disappeared,
  what the Young Pioneers did in your village.
  The mausoleum holds the public version.
  The family holds the whisper.
  The room in Lilongwe contains people
  from both families.
  Neither says which version they had.
