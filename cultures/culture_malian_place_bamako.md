## Location: Mali

## Shown
- The Niger River at Bamako.
  The bridge.
  The pirogues on the water at dawn —
  the fishermen who have been fishing this stretch
  since before Mali was a republic,
  since before colonisation,
  since before the French Sudan,
  since the river began.
  The river is older than every political arrangement
  that has used it.
  The political arrangements come and go.
  The river continues.
  Bamako is built on this certainty.
- The Grand Marché, Bamako.
  The largest market in Mali.
  Cloth, gold, electronics,
  second-hand clothes from Europe and America.
  The *dioulas* — the Mandé traders —
  who have been moving goods
  across West Africa for centuries,
  whose trade routes pre-date colonial borders.
  The market operates on relationships
  older than any government in the building
  at the end of the Avenue de l'Indépendance.
- The Musée National du Mali.
  The collection of pre-colonial sculpture, textiles, masks.
  The Dogon art.
  The Bambara power objects.
  The gold weights.
  The museum that preserves
  what the empire built
  and what the country contains.
  Currently: operating under junta governance.
  The museum remains open.
  Culture as continuity
  through political interruption.
- The Ahmed Baba Institute, Timbuktu.
  Rebuilt after 2012.
  The manuscripts that were not rescued —
  approximately 4,000 — burned by jihadists.
  The rest: 377,000, rescued, catalogued,
  stored in Bamako and internationally.
  The institute reopened.
  Researchers visit.
  The manuscripts are being digitised.
  The knowledge is being made permanent
  in a way the physical manuscripts could not be.
  The burning was intended to destroy memory.
  It accelerated its preservation.
- The Djinguereber Mosque.
  Timbuktu.
  Built 1327.
  Mud and wood.
  The timber joists protruding from the walls
  need replacing every year —
  the community climbs up and does it,
  as they have done for seven centuries.
  Maintenance as ritual.
  The mosque is still standing
  because every generation
  replaced the wood.
  This is the Malian metaphor
  most worth holding.
