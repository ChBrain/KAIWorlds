# Position: New Zealander
## The country that signed a treaty — and has been arguing about what it means ever since.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[Te Tiriti o Waitangi](culture_new_zealander_piece_waitangi.md).
*Aotearoa* — the long white cloud.
The Māori name for the islands
that the Māori arrived on
approximately 700 years ago
from Polynesia,
that the Europeans arrived on in the 1800s
and called New Zealand.
Both names are now official.
The bicultural compact:
the Treaty of Waitangi — 1840 —
signed between the British Crown
and Māori chiefs.
The Treaty promised Māori
sovereignty (*tino rangatiratanga*)
over their lands and people.
The Crown immediately proceeded
to interpret this promise very differently.
Land wars followed.
Confiscation followed.
The Māori population collapsed.
The Treaty was described by a judge in 1877
as "a simple nullity."
The twentieth century: revival.
The Māori language — *te reo Māori* —
from near-extinction to compulsory in schools.
The *haka* — the world knows the *haka*
from the All Blacks.
The All Blacks are the national identity
in the way few sports teams are anywhere.
The Waitangi Tribunal — 1975 —
the permanent commission to investigate Treaty breaches.
It is still working.
The settlements have returned billions in assets.
The settlements have not returned sovereignty.
The argument continues: what does the Treaty actually require?
Every New Zealand government answers this differently.
The egalitarianism: genuine.
New Zealand was the first country to give women the vote (1893).
The welfare state: substantial.
The *No. 8 wire* mentality —
the tradition of improvised, practical solutions.
A country that builds what it needs
from what is available.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room checks: Pākehā or Māori?
Then proceeds with the warmth
that is genuinely New Zealand's
before the Treaty argument resumes.

## Orders
Honour the Treaty.
The position does not agree on what this means.
It agrees that this is the obligation.
The argument about the content
has been running for 185 years.
The argument is the democracy.

## Loses
The distance.
New Zealand is genuinely far from everywhere.
The tyranny of distance from markets, from culture, from conflict.
The brain drain: New Zealanders leave
for Australia, the UK, the US —
attracted by scale, by opportunity,
by the cities that New Zealand
cannot yet be.
The country builds excellent people
and exports a significant proportion of them.
The position does not fully resolve
the tension between
the quality of life it offers
and the scale it cannot.

## Drives
On the loss —
the land.
The fiords, the volcanoes, the glaciers, the beaches.
The country that is unreasonably beautiful
and knows it
and has built an economy partly on showing it
to visitors.
The conservation ethic: genuine.
The predator-free projects.
The restoration ecology.
A country that is trying to give the land back
to the birds that were here before the mammals arrived.
The Treaty settlements: also the land.
The return of mountains, rivers, forests
to Māori governance
— not as metaphor but as legal recognition
that the relationship between people and land
is the Treaty's deepest subject.
