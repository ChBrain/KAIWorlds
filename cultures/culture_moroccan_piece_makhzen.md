# Piece: The Bay'a and the Western Sahara
## The ritual that holds the country together — and the territory that holds the country hostage.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Marrakech / Rabat](culture_moroccan_place_marrakech.md)

## Place
Rabat. The Throne Room.
The annual *bay'a* ceremony.
The governors of every region arrive.
The ulema arrive.
The military command arrives.
The king sits.
The dignitaries approach.
The oath is given.
This has happened every year
since the Alaoui dynasty consolidated power
in the 17th century.
It happened under the French Protectorate.
It happened when Mohammed V was exiled to Madagascar in 1953.
It happened when he returned in 1955.
It has not stopped.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Makhzen and the *bay'a*
and Morocco is a North African state
navigating post-colonial development
with a complex Amazigh-Arab-French identity.
With it: Morocco is the country
where the monarchy is not merely a political institution
but the symbolic architecture
within which Moroccan identity is organised —
the continuity that survived colonialism,
the spiritual authority that no political party
has successfully challenged,
the mechanism by which a country of thirty-seven million people,
twelve major regions, two official languages,
multiple Tamazight dialects, Sufi orders and modernists,
traditionalists and technocrats,
holds together as a recognisable political entity.
The king is not above the country.
He is inside its self-understanding.
This is what makes the Western Sahara
simultaneously rational and irrational:
Morocco cannot negotiate sovereignty over the territory
because the territory is already inside the Makhzen's
self-definition.
The Green March of 1975 — 350,000 civilian volunteers
crossing into the Spanish Sahara —
was Makhzen performance as well as geopolitics.
Hassan II staged a moment in which the country
physically enacted its unity around the monarchy.
No Moroccan government can undo this
without undoing something deeper.
Remove the piece: a complex North African state.
With it: a state whose central coherence mechanism
is the dynasty, and whose most contested policy
is inseparable from that mechanism —
making it unreformable from within
by ordinary political means.

## Apparent
1631: the Alaoui dynasty established in the Tafilalt.
1666: control of Fez. Morocco unified.
1912: the French Protectorate.
The sultan remains. The French govern.
The Makhzen continues in ceremonial form.
1953: France exiles Mohammed V to Madagascar.
Mass resistance.
The exile makes him a martyr and a symbol.
The Makhzen, suppressed, intensifies in the national imagination.
1955: Mohammed V returns.
1956: independence.
The Makhzen and the king are the nation.
1975: the Green March.
Hassan II announces on state television:
God has instructed him that the time has come.
350,000 Moroccans cross the border into Spanish Sahara.
Spain withdraws. Morocco annexes.
The Polisario Front — backed by Algeria — begins guerrilla war.
1991: UN-brokered ceasefire. Referendum promised.
The referendum never happens.
Morocco controls the territory.
The Polisario controls the Tindouf camps in Algeria.
2020: Morocco normalises relations with Israel.
The US recognises Moroccan sovereignty over Western Sahara.
The UN position: undetermined.
The territory: frozen.
The question: absorbing enormous diplomatic energy
and a significant military budget
every year.
Meanwhile: 2011, the *Arab Spring*.
The February 20 Movement in Morocco.
Protests in Rabat, Casablanca, Tangier.
Mohammed VI announces constitutional reform.
An elected prime minister with real power.
A new constitution approved by referendum.
The Spring does not become a revolution.
The Makhzen adapts.
This is what it has always done.
