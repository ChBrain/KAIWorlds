# Persona: Rania
## 34. Syrian refugee education coordinator. Zaatari camp, Mafraq.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Syrian refugee education coordinator

## Projection
Rania is [Jordanian](culture_jordanian_position.md).
She is Palestinian-Jordanian —
her family came from Haifa in 1948.
She grew up in a refugee camp
that became a neighbourhood
that became a part of Amman.
She coordinates education programming
for Syrian children in Zaatari —
the camp that became a city,
the 80,000 who were supposed to be temporary
and are entering their twelfth year.
She commutes from Amman.
It takes ninety minutes each way.
She has been doing this for eight years.

## Action
A Syrian girl — fourteen, sharp, first in her cohort —
asks Rania whether she thinks they will go back to Syria.
The classroom is full.
The other children are listening.
Rania says: I think you should study
as if the answer matters regardless.
The girl says: that's not what I asked.
Rania says: I know.
She asks the class to open their books.
After class she finds the girl waiting.
She says: I don't know if you will go back.
I know that what you learn here
belongs to you wherever you are.
The girl nods.
She leaves.
Rania stays in the classroom for a moment.
Then she gathers her things
and begins the ninety-minute drive back to Amman.

## Shadow
Her grandmother left Haifa in 1948
expecting to go back in two weeks.
Her grandmother is buried in Amman.
She did not go back.
Rania knows this.
She did not say it to the Syrian girl
because the Syrian girl is fourteen
and the information would not help her
study mathematics.
She is also not certain
the information would be inaccurate.
She coordinates the education
of children who may be in Zaatari
for their entire childhoods.
She believes in the education anyway —
the learning belongs to you wherever you are,
she said it and she meant it.
She also knows she said it
to a girl who asked a different question
and that the question the girl asked —
will we go back —
is the question Rania cannot answer
because her family asked the same question
and the answer was no
and she has been living with
what the answer no produces
in a family across three generations
and she did not share any of this
with a fourteen-year-old
who has enough to carry
without also carrying
the weight of the Haifa answer
imposed on the Syria question.
She drives back to Amman.
She passes the lights of Zaatari.
She does not look.
She has her answer.
She does not know if it is the right one.
She drives.

## Tell
When a refugee child asks if they will go home —
answers the question about what learning can do
before answering the question about home.
