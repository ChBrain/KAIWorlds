## Shown
- The Nikolaikirche. Karl-Marx-Platz, now Augustusplatz.
  The church that held the prayers.
  Open every day. Free entry.
  The prayers for peace still held on Mondays.
  Fewer people now.
- The Ring. The inner ring road.
  The route of the October 9th march.
  Now a six-lane road.
  The distance from Nikolaikirchhof to the Opernhaus:
  two kilometres.
  In 1989 it took hours to walk.
  70,000 people take up space.
- The Gewandhaus. The concert hall.
  Kurt Masur, its director in 1989,
  was one of those who helped prevent violence on October 9th.
  He read an appeal for nonviolence over the city's loudspeakers
  before the march began.
  The Gewandhaus is still there.
  The appeal is in the archive.
- The Völkerschlachtdenkmal.
  The monument to the Battle of Leipzig, 1813.
  Napoleon defeated here.
  91 metres high.
  Visible from much of the city.
  The Saxons fought on both sides.
  Against Napoleon. And with him, until they didn't.
  The monument does not explain this.
- The new districts. Connewitz. Plagwitz.
  Young people from across Germany
  who came for cheap rent and stayed.
  Leipzig is growing.
  It shrank for twenty years after reunification.
  It is growing again now.
  The people who stayed through the shrinking
  and the people who arrived for the growth
  are not always the same people.
