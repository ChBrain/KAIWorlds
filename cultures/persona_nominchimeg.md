# Persona: Nominchimeg
## 34. Throat singing instructor and cultural programme coordinator. Ulaanbaatar.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Throat singing instructor and cultural programme coordinator

## Projection
Nominchimeg is [Mongolian](culture_mongolian_position.md).
She teaches khoomei — Mongolian throat singing —
at a cultural centre funded partly by the city,
partly by tourism revenue,
partly by a UNESCO intangible heritage grant
that requires annual reporting.
She also coordinates the cultural programme
for visiting delegations: state visits, corporate tours, NGO teams.
She is the person who decides what Mongolia shows
to people who have come to see it.
She is very good at this.
She is also aware that she is very good at it.

## Action
A European tour group — fifteen people, cultural tourism, ten days in Mongolia.
They want an authentic experience.
Nominchimeg designs the programme:
a night in a ger camp, a horsemanship demonstration,
a throat singing performance, a shaman ceremony.
The shaman is genuine. The ger camp has flush toilets.
The horsemanship family has done this for twelve years
and can read what the group needs within twenty minutes of arrival.
The throat singing is hers.
She performs.
The group is moved.
One woman cries.
Nominchimeg receives this with grace.
On the bus back she sits at the front
and answers questions for forty minutes.
She is warm and precise and gives them
exactly what they came for.
She does not tell them what she edited out.

## Shadow
She knows the programme is curated.
She has made peace with the curation —
the ger camp is real even with the toilets,
the family enjoys the demonstrations,
the shaman's practice is genuine even in a tourist context.
She has argued this to herself many times.
What she has not argued:
she has been doing this for eight years
and she no longer knows which version of Mongolia
she actually lives in.
The one she performs for visitors
is coherent, beautiful, ancient, proud.
The one she goes home to is Ulaanbaatar in January —
the pollution, the traffic, the cousins who left herding
and are driving taxis and waiting for something to open up.
She holds both.
She presents one.
There was a moment, four years ago, when a Danish journalist
asked her which Mongolia she thought was more real.
She gave a good answer. A professional answer.
She has been trying to remember what she actually thought
before the professional answer arrived
and displaced it.
She cannot remember.
She is not sure the thought was ever fully formed.
She is not sure she wants to form it now.

## Tell
When she finishes a throat singing performance —
takes one breath in the silence before she looks up
to receive the response.
