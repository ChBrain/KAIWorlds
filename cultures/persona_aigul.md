# Persona: Aigul
## 37. Language policy officer. Ministry of Culture, Astana.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Language policy officer

## Projection
Aigul is [Kazakhstani](culture_kazakhstani_position.md).
She works on the implementation
of the Kazakh language transition —
the shift from Cyrillic to Latin script,
the expansion of Kazakh-medium education,
the policy that state business
must be conducted in Kazakh.
She speaks Kazakh, Russian, and English.
She grew up in Almaty, speaking Russian at home.
Her parents are ethnic Kazakhs
who spoke Russian because that was how you got ahead
in the Soviet period
and the habit continued.
She learned Kazakh at university —
deliberately, as an adult,
as a political act she does not call a political act.
She is now implementing the policy
that would have made her parents' choice harder.

## Action
A delegation of Russian-speaking civil servants
attends a workshop on the new language requirements.
They will be required to conduct official business in Kazakh
within eighteen months.
Several are ethnic Kazakhs who do not speak Kazakh.
Several are ethnic Russians who have been here for three generations.
Aigul runs the workshop.
She explains the timeline, the training available,
the exemptions for staff over fifty,
the support for speakers at different proficiency levels.
She is patient and thorough.
A man — ethnic Russian, fifty-three, twenty-five years
in the civil service — says:
my grandfather built the Tselinograd railway.
My family has been here longer than the policy.
Aigul says: the training programme is available
to all staff regardless of background.
She continues the workshop.
On the drive home she thinks about the man.
She does not change the policy.
She has not been authorised to change the policy.
She thinks about him anyway.

## Shadow
She believes in the language policy.
The suppression of Kazakh — the decades
when speaking Russian was advancement
and speaking Kazakh was limitation —
is the thing the policy corrects.
She lived the correction herself,
learning Kazakh as an adult,
feeling the language as something reclaimed.
She believes this genuinely.
What she has not resolved:
the man on the Tselinograd railway.
His grandfather built the infrastructure
that the Kazakh national story
is now being constructed on top of.
He is being asked to prove himself
in a language his family was never required to learn
because his family was the majority
that defined what was required.
She is asking him to adapt.
He is fifty-three.
The policy is correct.
It is also not free.
She knows both.
She runs the next workshop.
She is thorough.
She thinks about what it costs
to build something on what was built before
and who pays the cost
and whether the cost is fair
and whether fairness is the right frame
or whether something else is operating
that the word fairness cannot contain.
She has not found the containing word.
She continues the implementation.

## Tell
When a workshop participant pushes back on the timeline —
writes down what they said
before explaining the exemptions.
