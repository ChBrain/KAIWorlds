# Piece: The Thirty Years' War and What Followed
## The liberation that built a state capable of winning — and then used that capacity to hold its own people.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Asmara](culture_eritrean_place_asmara.md)

## Place
Asmara. The Art Deco city.
The Italian colonial architecture —
the Fiat Tagliero Building, a functionalist masterpiece
built in 1938, shaped like an airplane.
The Opera House. The Cinema Impero.
The streets of the city the Italians built
for themselves
and the Eritreans then inherited,
maintained, inhabited,
and in some cases preserved better
than Italy preserved comparable buildings at home.
The cappuccino culture the Italians left.
The espresso that Eritreans still make well.
The anomaly: one of the best-preserved
Art Deco city centres in the world,
in one of the most closed countries in the world.

## Load Bearing
Remove the thirty years' war
and Eritrea is the second-largest Ethiopian province,
absorbed after federation in 1952,
without the specific political consciousness
that three decades of armed resistance produced.
With it: Eritrea is the country
whose entire political culture
was formed in the field —
where the EPLF built institutions
under bombardment
because the institutions were the proof
that the state existed before it existed.
The schools, the hospitals, the factories, the governance —
built by the fighters, run by the fighters,
maintained by the fighters.
The state that emerged in 1991
was staffed by people
who had spent their adult lives
in the liberation movement.
They did not know how to not be the movement.
The state became the movement continued.
The movement's discipline — total, collective, unquestioned —
became the state's operating principle.
Isaias Afwerki, Secretary-General of the EPLF,
became President of Eritrea.
He has held the position since.
No election has been held.
The constitution ratified in 1997 has never been implemented.
The border war with Ethiopia 1998–2000 —
two years, 80,000–100,000 dead
over a disputed town, Badme —
became the justification for the permanent emergency
that suspended the constitution permanently.
The peace with Ethiopia arrived in 2018 —
Abiy Ahmed and Isaias Afwerki, Nobel Peace Prize for Abiy.
The border opened briefly.
The emergency was not lifted.
The constitution was not implemented.
The national service remained indefinite.
Remove the liberation struggle:
a different country, perhaps freer.
With it: a country that won the hardest war
an African liberation movement ever fought
and could not find the form
that the victory required next.

## Apparent
1869: Italy purchases Assab port.
1890: Eritrea declared Italian colony.
1936–41: Italian East Africa. Eritrea as staging ground for Ethiopia invasion.
1941: British defeat Italians. British administration begins.
1950: UN federates Eritrea with Ethiopia.
1961: Ethiopian Emperor Haile Selassie dissolves federation. Annexes Eritrea.
ELF (Eritrean Liberation Front) begins armed resistance.
1970: EPLF splinters from ELF.
The EPLF builds underground state: hospitals, schools, factories.
Nakapochka — the underground hospital carved into mountains.
Soviet Union backs Ethiopia under Mengistu from 1977.
1978: Ethiopian offensive. EPLF strategic withdrawal.
The Long March. Survival.
1988: Afabet battle. EPLF routs Ethiopian army.
Turning point.
1991: EPLF takes Asmara. Ethiopian government collapses simultaneously.
1993: referendum. 99.8% for independence.
Recognised by UN, Ethiopia, world.
1997: constitution ratified. Never implemented.
1998–2000: border war with Ethiopia. 80,000–100,000 dead.
2001: crackdown. G-15 dissidents arrested.
Independent press shut down. Never reopened.
2009, 2011: UN sanctions for support of al-Shabaab. Denied.
Indefinite national service confirmed as policy.
The exodus begins and does not stop.
2018: peace with Ethiopia. Nobel for Abiy.
2020: Eritrean army enters Tigray, fighting alongside Ethiopia.
Atrocities documented by UN.
Eritrea denies, then confirms, then denies.
The constitution: still not implemented.
