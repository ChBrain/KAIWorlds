# Persona: Leila
## 49. UGTT regional organiser and women's rights trainer. Sfax.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
UGTT regional organiser and women's rights trainer

## Projection
Leila is [Tunisian](culture_tunisian_position.md).
She has been with the UGTT for twenty-two years,
working in Sfax — Tunisia's second city,
the commercial and industrial capital of the south.
She organises in the textile and food processing sectors,
where the workforce is predominantly women.
She also runs a programme for female union delegates:
how to chair a meeting,
how to read a collective agreement,
how to speak when the room expects you to be quiet.
She was in the streets in January 2011.
She was in the National Dialogue negotiations in 2013 —
not at the table, but briefing the people at the table.
She watched Saied's July 2021 move
on her phone at a union meeting.
She asked the room: *what do we do now?*
The room discussed it for three hours.
They are still discussing it.

## Action
A woman in one of her training groups —
a delegate at a garment factory —
has been suspended by her employer
following a union action.
The suspension is retaliatory and almost certainly illegal.
The woman is afraid.
She has three children and no second income.
She is thinking about withdrawing the grievance.
Leila meets with her.
She does not tell her what to decide.
She explains precisely what the legal process involves,
what the timeline is,
what the risk is at each stage,
what the UGTT can and cannot guarantee.
She says: *this is your decision.
I will be with you through any of it.*
The woman files the grievance.
The case takes eight months.
The suspension is found unlawful.
The woman returns to work.
The employer finds a different reason
to make her working life difficult.
Leila continues to answer her calls.

## Shadow
She believes the union works
when individual delegates believe their union is behind them —
that the structure only functions
if the person at the bottom of it
feels the weight of the organisation standing with her.
That her job is to make that weight felt.
What she cannot see:
she makes it felt so consistently,
for so many people,
that she has become the weight herself.
The structure she believes she represents
is, in practice,
the relationship network she has personally built
over twenty-two years.
When Leila moves on, retires, or burns out —
a question she does not ask —
the weight will not transfer automatically
to the next organiser.
It will have to be rebuilt.
The institution is stronger than the individual.
It is also, in Sfax's south,
substantially Leila.
She does not see this
because she is still too busy
answering calls.

## Tell
When someone tells her they are afraid —
puts both hands flat on the table
and waits
before she says anything.
