# Piece: The PAIGC and the Emigrant Nation
## The liberation movement that freed two countries — and the diaspora that built one of them.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Praia](culture_cabo_verdean_place_praia.md)

## Place
Praia. Santiago island.
The plateau city — *praia* means beach in Portuguese,
but the capital sits on a plateau above the shore,
above the bay where the ships arrive and depart.
The *Plateau* neighbourhood: the colonial architecture,
the government buildings, the cathedral.
The *Achada* neighbourhoods spreading outward:
the city growing faster than its infrastructure,
the remittance-built houses of emigrants
next to the traditional homes of those who stayed.

## Load Bearing
Remove the PAIGC
and Cabo Verde is a Portuguese colony
that gains independence in the 1970s wave
without its own liberation movement,
without the figure of Amílcar Cabral —
the most sophisticated anti-colonial thinker
Africa produced in the twentieth century —
and without the specific political culture
that Cabral's movement embedded
in the post-independence state.
With it: Cabo Verde is the country
whose independence was won by a movement
that also liberated Guinea-Bissau,
led by a man assassinated eleven months
before independence,
whose political inheritance produced
the unusual combination of:
one-party state 1975–1991,
then peaceful democratic transition,
then consolidated democracy,
then one of the most stable political systems
in Africa.
The PAIGC's discipline —
Cabral's insistence that the movement
must be more than an armed struggle,
that it must build the institutions of the future state —
is the reason the transition held.
Remove it: a different transition, with different results.
With it: the specific Cabo Verdean political culture
that takes governance seriously
because the founding generation
understood the cost of being ungoverned.
The diaspora is the second load-bearing piece.
The emigrant nation:
the remittances that represent 15–20% of GDP,
the political engagement of the diaspora
in elections, in investment, in cultural production.
Cesária Évora was born here.
She recorded her first international album
in Paris, funded in part by the diaspora network.
The voice that made the world hear Cabo Verde
was produced by the same emigrant circuit
that kept the islands alive during the droughts.

## Apparent
1456: Portuguese navigators arrive. Islands uninhabited.
Settlement begins with enslaved Africans
and Portuguese colonists.
Santiago becomes a centre of the Atlantic slave trade.
Creole language and culture emerge from this encounter.
Eighteen-hundreds: repeated famine. Mass emigration begins.
1956: Amílcar Cabral founds the PAIGC
in Bissau, with his half-brother Luís.
The movement fights simultaneously for Guinea-Bissau and Cabo Verde.
The guerrilla war in Guinea-Bissau, 1963–1974.
Cabral assassinated January 1973 — by a faction within the movement,
reportedly with Portuguese intelligence involvement.
September 1974: Guinea-Bissau independent.
July 1975: Cabo Verde independent.
One-party PAIGC rule. Development focus.
Literacy campaigns. Institution building.
1980: coup in Guinea-Bissau splits the movement.
Cabo Verde renames its branch the PAICV.
1991: multi-party elections. PAICV loses. Peaceful transfer.
The opposition MpD takes power.
Power has transferred six times since.
The democracy holds.
2019: Cesária Évora dies. National mourning.
The world sends condolences.
The islands receive them.
*Sodade* plays at the funeral.
