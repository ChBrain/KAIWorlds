# Piece: The 30-Year Rule and the 1994 Opening
## Kamuzu Banda — the man who built a school like Eton, banned trousers for women, and called his people children in politics.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Lilongwe / Blantyre](culture_malawian_place_lilongwe.md)

## Place
Lilongwe. The Parliament building.
June 1993: the referendum.
63% vote for multiparty democracy.
Banda's 30 years ends.
1994: Bakili Muluzi wins.
Banda concedes — gracefully.
Then says, on state radio:
*I wish to congratulate him wholeheartedly.*
He is then charged with murder
for the deaths of four politicians in 1983.
He is acquitted.
He calls Malawians "children in politics."
He dies in 1997.
He is now memorialised.

## Load Bearing
Remove Banda
and Malawi is a small landlocked country
with colonial-era institutions,
tobacco as its main crop,
and a north-south-central political geography
that produces the coalition politics
it has today.
With it: 30 years of Banda.
The specific character of *Kamuzuism*:
everything named after him.
The Kamuzu Academy — modelled on Eton,
where Malawian children were beaten
for speaking Chichewa.
He spoke Chichewa himself, he said,
but the archaic version — his children
simply failed to understand him.
The Malawi Young Pioneers —
a paramilitary youth wing that terrorised opposition.
The Traditional Courts used to convict opponents.
The 1983 Mwanza murders —
four cabinet members whose car went over a cliff
on a road they should not have been travelling.
The bodies exhumed after 1994.
The memorial erected.
And then: after Muluzi's government disappointed,
after the food insecurity increased,
Banda's reputation began to recover.
By 2007 he was being memorialised as a founder.
The mausoleum in Lilongwe.
Kamuzu Day maintained as a public holiday.
The ambivalent rehabilitation
of a man who was genuinely authoritarian
and genuinely built institutions
that outlasted him —
the roads, the schools, the hospitals —
while imprisoning and killing those
who challenged him.
The piece is load-bearing for Malawi
in the same way Mugabe is for Zimbabwe
with different degrees of violence —
the liberation-era nationalist
who governed too long
and whose legacy the country cannot decide about.
Malawi's answer: we hold both.
He was a founder. He was a dictator.
We name a day after him.
We also named the memorial for those he killed.
The country holds both simultaneously.
This is not resolution.
It is Malawian pragmatism.

## Apparent
July 6, 1964: independence. Banda prime minister.
1965: cabinet crisis. Six ministers resign, citing authoritarianism.
Five flee the country. One is later killed.
1966: one-party state. MCP only party.
1967: women banned from wearing trousers or miniskirts.
1972: Jehovah's Witnesses persecuted. Thousands flee to Zambia.
1975: capital moved from Zomba to Lilongwe — Banda's home region.
1983: four cabinet members die in suspicious car crash.
Banda suspected. Never proven.
1989: Kamuzu Academy opens. Latin and Greek taught.
Chichewa forbidden within the walls.
Early 1990s: Catholic bishops issue pastoral letter
criticising the regime.
IMF and World Bank suspend aid.
June 1993: referendum. Multiparty system wins.
May 1994: Muluzi elected. Banda concedes.
1995: Banda charged with murder. Acquitted.
He calls Malawians "children in politics."
He dies November 25, 1997.
2006: Kamuzu mausoleum opens in Lilongwe.
2019: elections. Results contested. Court annuls them.
2020: re-run. Chakwera wins.
Transfer of power.
The courts worked.
Malawian democracy: fragile, functional.
The tobacco: declining.
The lake: still there.
The mausoleum: still open.
