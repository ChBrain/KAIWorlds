## Location: Benin

## Shown
- The Palais des Congrès, Cotonou.
  Where the Conférence Nationale met in 1990.
  A building unremarkable from the outside.
  What happened inside it
  changed Francophone Africa.
  The building still hosts conferences.
  The history is not prominently marked.
  Beninese who know what happened there
  know it without a plaque.
- Dantokpa Market, Cotonou.
  The largest market in West Africa.
  150,000 people daily.
  Everything.
  The *zémidjan* — the motorcycle taxi drivers —
  weaving through.
  The vodun stalls: herbs, animal parts,
  charms, bones, powders.
  The official and the sacred
  on the same street, in the same transaction.
  Benin does not separate them.
  The market does not pretend.
- Porto-Novo. The official capital.
  The colonial administrative capital.
  Where the government buildings are.
  Where the National Assembly meets.
  Where the president officially resides.
  Nobody calls Porto-Novo the real capital.
  Cotonou is the real capital.
  The official capital and the actual capital
  do not coincide.
  Benin accepted this,
  named it,
  and organised around it.
  This is a characteristically Beninese pragmatism.
- Abomey. 140 kilometres north of Cotonou.
  The royal palaces of the Dahomey kingdom.
  UNESCO heritage site.
  The bas-reliefs depicting the kings' deeds —
  wars, tribute, executions.
  The *Agojie* barracks.
  The throne supported by skulls of defeated kings.
  The kingdom's history presented
  without apologetics.
  This is the kingdom that supplied
  an estimated 20% of all people transported
  in the Atlantic slave trade.
  The palaces do not hide this.
  The history is present.
  The return of 26 bronzes from Paris in 2021:
  they arrived at the airport.
  The president received them.
  Ceremonies were held.
  They are now in a museum being built in Abomey.
  The repatriation is ongoing.
- The vodun temples.
  In Ouidah. In Abomey. Everywhere in the south.
  Vodun is not a secret religion in Benin.
  It is the national heritage.
  January 10: National Vodun Day.
  The ceremonies public, the *bokono* accessible,
  the practice embedded in daily life
  alongside Christianity and Islam.
  The coexistence is not tension-free.
  It is functioning.
