# Piece: The Gnassingbé Dynasty
## The 38-year presidency and its inheritance — Africa's longest-running political dynasty.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Lomé](culture_togolese_place_lome.md)

## Place
Lomé. The capital.
On the coast. The lighthouse.
The Ghana border 200 metres from the city centre.
The border market where Ghanaians and Togolese
trade without any formality being necessary.
The border the British and French drew
through the Ewe people in 1884.
The Ewe live on both sides.
The market moves between them.
The state border is less relevant
than the kinship connection.

## Load Bearing
Remove Eyadema and the dynasty
and Togo is a small West African state
with the north-south division
that characterises many of its neighbours —
manageable, present, not determinative.
With it: Togo is the country
where the north-south division
was institutionalised into a governing system
that has lasted 56 years
because the Kabye military elite
was the most reliable base
for a government that needed reliable support.
The 1963 assassination of Olympio —
the first such event in post-colonial Africa —
set the template:
power in Togo is taken, not given,
and once taken, held.
Eyadema held it for 38 years.
Faure inherited it.
The transition in 2005 when Eyadema died:
the military installed Faure
before an election was held.
ECOWAS forced an election.
Faure won it.
He has won every election since.
Remove the piece: a small West African state.
With it: Africa's most persistent political dynasty —
and the specific political culture
it has produced:
one in which formal institutions exist,
elections are held,
and the outcome is known before they happen.
Not because the elections are obviously falsified —
because the system ensures
that changing the outcome
requires more than voting.

## Apparent
April 27, 1960: independence.
Sylvanus Olympio: first president.
January 13, 1963: Olympio assassinated.
Gnassingbé Eyadema believed to be involved.
January 13, 1967: Eyadema seizes power.
He declares January 13 a national holiday.
The holiday commemorates the first coup
in which he participated.
He governs as a nationalist strongman.
Builds Kabye military dominance.
1991: *Conférence Nationale* convened.
It suspends Eyadema's powers.
Transition government.
1993: Eyadema wins elections —
opposition boycotts, claims fraud.
The transition reversed.
1998: elections. Contested. Violence.
2003: constitution amended: term limits removed.
Eyadema can govern indefinitely.
February 5, 2005: Eyadema dies.
The military immediately installs Faure Gnassingbé,
his son.
ECOWAS suspends Togo.
April 2005: Faure *wins* elections.
Reported post-election violence: 80 dead.
2006: Political accord. Government of national unity.
2010, 2015, 2020: Faure wins elections.
2020: constitution amended.
Term limits reset.
Faure can potentially govern until 2038.
56 years of one family.
Counting.
