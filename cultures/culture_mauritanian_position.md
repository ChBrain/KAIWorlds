# Position: Mauritanian
## The country that abolished slavery three times — and is still abolishing it.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Three Abolitions](culture_mauritanian_piece_abolitions.md).
The Sahara. The crossing point.
Mauritania is where the Arab Maghreb meets Sub-Saharan Africa —
literally, geographically, ethnically, linguistically.
The Bidan — *white* Moors, Arab-Berber —
at the apex of the social hierarchy.
The Haratin — Black Moors, descendants of enslaved people,
Arabic-speaking, Muslim —
in the middle.
The Wolof, the Soninke, the Pulaar — Black Africans in the south.
The country is one place
with three different social orders
running simultaneously.
The desert is vast. The population is small.
Nouakchott — the capital — was built after independence
in 1960, on sand dunes, from nothing.
It did not exist as a city before the French left.
Everything is recent.
Everything is also ancient —
the slave-master relationship,
the caste system justified by Maliki jurisprudence,
the tribal hierarchies that predate Islam in the region.
The collectivism is high in all groups.
The hierarchy is steep —
but which hierarchy depends on which group
you are asking.
The position that occupies this file
is primarily the position of the Bidan elite
in which political power has resided
since independence.
Other positions — Haratin, Pulaar, Wolof —
run alongside it in the same country.

## Orders
Maintain.
The Mauritanian state has always had a single operating instruction:
preserve the arrangement that has existed.
The arrangement: Arab-Berber elite,
Haratin labour and service, African south marginalised.
Every abolition of slavery has been:
a legal act performed under external pressure
followed by no enforcement.
Abolition as declaration.
Declaration as management of international opinion.
Management of international opinion
as preservation of the arrangement.
The position navigates this
with the specific intelligence
of a society that has absorbed external pressure
without allowing it to change internal structure.
*Ma fi mushkila* — there is no problem.
The official position on slavery.
Said while the problem continues.

## Loses
The patience of its own people.
The Haratin — potentially 40% of the population —
have been organising since 1974.
El Hor, SOS Esclaves, IRA-Mauritania.
Biram Ould Dah Abeid —
who burned the Maliki jurisprudence books in 2012,
who ran for president and came second,
who was imprisoned twice for his activism,
who is still there.
The International Monetary Fund, the World Bank,
the NGOs, the UN Special Rapporteur —
all noting the persistence of slavery.
The position carries the accumulating weight
of a system that has survived
every pressure applied to it
by redefining the terms,
prosecuting one person every decade,
and denying the scale.
The system is durable.
The pressure is also durable.
One of them will eventually exhaust the other.
The position does not know which one.

## Drives
On the loss —
*tahaddi* — endurance, challenge.
The Mauritanian position's response
to external pressure is never direct confrontation.
It is redefinition, co-optation, delay.
A law criminalising slavery — 2007. 2015.
The convictions: two.
The slavery: ongoing.
This is not failure of mechanism.
It is the mechanism itself —
laws as symbolic acknowledgement
of international norms
that do not disturb internal arrangements.
The position continues on this basis
until it cannot.
The activists pushing the other way
are Mauritanian too.
Both are the country.
