## Location: Liberia

## Shown
- The Executive Mansion.
  Rebuilt after the civil wars.
  The seat of government.
  Where Taylor governed.
  Where Sirleaf governed.
  Where Weah governed.
  Where Boakai governs now.
  Each government a different attempt
  at the same project:
  building a state that serves everyone
  on a foundation designed to serve some.
- Waterside Market.
  The commercial centre.
  The Lebanese traders who have been here
  since the 1920s.
  The Mandingo traders.
  The market women.
  The economy that keeps running
  when governments change.
  Monrovia's real stability
  is commercial, not political.
- The beaches.
  Elwa Beach. Robertsport.
  Where, on April 22, 1980,
  Doe's government executed 13 ministers
  in front of cameras.
  The same beaches where Liberians swim.
  The same beaches where the fishermen go at dawn.
  The history and the present
  on the same sand.
  Liberians have learned to hold both.
  They had no choice.
- The destroyed buildings.
  Still visible in Monrovia.
  Not all have been demolished.
  The holes in walls.
  The buildings abandoned mid-reconstruction.
  A city that rebuilt selectively —
  what was funded was rebuilt,
  what was not is still visible.
  The civil war is not over in the architecture.
  It is over in the politics.
  Both are true.
- The University of Liberia.
  Founded 1862.
  One of the oldest universities in Africa.
  Liberated by Americo-Liberian elites.
  Closed during the civil wars.
  Reopened.
  Currently producing the generation
  that will govern the country
  in twenty years.
  The faculty teaching on salaries
  that require supplemental income
  to survive.
  The students learning anyway.
