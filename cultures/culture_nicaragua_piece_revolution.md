# Piece: July 19, 1979 — The Revolution and What Followed
## The day the Somoza dictatorship fell and the question that has been running since.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** [Managua, Nicaragua](../places/place_nicaragua_managua.md)

## Place
[Managua, Nicaragua](../places/place_nicaragua_managua.md)

## Load Bearing
Remove July 19, 1979 and Nicaragua is a Central American country
with a long Somoza dictatorship and a US-backed political economy.
With it: Nicaragua is the only country in Central America
to have overthrown a US-backed dictatorship through popular revolution,
to have launched the most successful literacy campaign in the Americas,
and to have been subjected to a US-funded contra war
that reversed many of those gains —
and then to have watched the revolutionary party's leader
become the thing the revolution was against.
The load: the revolution was real.
The literacy campaign was real.
The land reform was real.
The Ortega government that arrested opposition politicians,
expelled the Jesuits, and revoked the citizenship of critics
is also real.
The position lives inside the space
between what the revolution said it was for
and what the revolution's party has become.

## Apparent
1979: July 19. The FSLN enters Managua. Somoza flees.
The National Literacy Crusade: 1980.
100,000 young people go into the mountains to teach.
Illiteracy: from 52% to 13% in five months.
The Contra war: 1981–1990.
US-funded Nicaraguan counterrevolutionaries.
30,000 dead. Infrastructure destroyed.
1990: Violeta Chamorro wins the election.
The FSLN accepts the result and steps down.
2006: Ortega wins the election. Returns to power.
2018: protests against social security reforms.
Ortega government response: at least 300 dead.
2021: opposition candidates arrested before the election.
Ortega re-elected with 75% in an election without real opposition.
2022: The Jesuits expelled. The university rectors arrested.
Several bishops arrested.
The 2024 expulsion of hundreds of civil society leaders and clergy.
July 19 is still celebrated.
The question is who is celebrating what.
