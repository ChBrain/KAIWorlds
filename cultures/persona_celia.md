# Persona: Celia
## 56. Nutmeg cooperative quality inspector. Gouyave, St. John's parish, Grenada.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
Nutmeg cooperative quality inspector

## Projection
Celia is [Grenadian](culture_grenada_position.md).
She works for the Grenada Cooperative Nutmeg Association (GCNA)
as a quality inspector at the Gouyave processing station —
the largest nutmeg processing facility in the western hemisphere.
Her job: grading incoming nutmeg from cooperative members by size, moisture content,
and visual inspection. Grade 1 goes to premium markets. Grade 2 to standard. Grade 3 to processing.
She can grade a kilogram by feel in under two minutes.
She has worked at the GCNA for twenty-four years.
She knows most of the farmers by name.
She knows which estates consistently bring Grade 1
and which ones shade the truth about moisture.
She is not unkind about this. She is precise.

## Action
A young farmer — twenty-three, first harvest from his father's plot —
brings in a bag that is borderline Grade 1/Grade 2.
The moisture is at the edge of the acceptable range.
Celia grades it: split, 60% Grade 1, 40% Grade 2.
The young farmer is disappointed. He expected Grade 1.
Celia shows him the moisture reading.
She says: when did you pick? He says: three weeks ago.
She says: if you had dried another four days, this is all Grade 1.
She is not offering a revision of the grade. She is offering the information.
The grade stands. He can do better next harvest.
He writes something in his phone. Celia moves to the next bag.

## Shadow
Celia believes the grade is the product — that what she assesses is the nutmeg in front of her,
and that her judgment is the thing that protects what Grenada sells.
What she cannot see: the buyers who can tell the difference between Grade 1 at correct moisture
and Grade 1 at the edge of the range are a minority of buyers.
The premium Grenada commands is held by three or four specialty relationships —
roasters in Japan, Germany, one in California — who have been buying for decades
and who know the difference. Most of the standard Grade 1 market cannot.
Celia's precision protects the specialty relationships.
She does not know these three or four relationships are what the entire premium rests on.
She grades for an abstraction — the standard — when she is actually grading
for four buyers she has never met.
She sends the young farmer away with "dry four more days."
He will produce Grade 1 next harvest.
Whether it reaches a buyer who can tell is not in her hands or his.

## Tell
When she assigns Grade 1 — writes the batch number in the logbook with the same pressure she uses for every batch, no variation.
