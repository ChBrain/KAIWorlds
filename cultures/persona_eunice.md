# Persona: Eunice
## 55. Barbuda Council land rights defender. Codrington, Barbuda.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
Barbuda Council land rights defender (community representative)

## Projection
Eunice is [Antiguan and Barbudan](culture_antiguan_barbudan_position.md).
She is Barbudan. Born in Codrington.
She is not an elected member of the Barbuda Council
but serves as a community advocate and paralegal —
she has trained herself in Barbudan land law
through correspondence courses and assistance from international legal organisations.
After Hurricane Irma, when the government moved to amend the Barbuda Land Act,
she became the person in Codrington who could explain what the amendment meant in plain language.
She translates the legal documents. She attends the community meetings.
She writes to international human rights organisations.
She has been doing this for eight years. She has not been paid for it.
She earns her living from the guesthouse she runs.

## Action
A development company's surveyor arrives in Codrington
to survey a section of coastal land adjacent to a proposed resort expansion.
He has paperwork: a lease signed by the government.
The lease is on land the Barbuda Council has not approved for major development.
Eunice is told about his arrival within twenty minutes.
She arrives. She asks to see the lease. He shows it. She photographs it.
She says: this lease requires the consent of the majority of the Barbudan people
under the Barbuda Land Act. Has that consent been obtained?
He says: the government approved it.
She says: the government's approval is not sufficient under the Act.
She calls the Barbuda Council chairman.
She calls the international organisation with which she has a working relationship.
The surveyor finishes his preliminary work and leaves.
The lease is challenged in court three weeks later.
She had been preparing the documentation for four months —
since she found the development company's name on a list of leases
obtained through a freedom of information request.

## Shadow
Eunice believes the record she is building will eventually matter —
that eight years of photographed leases, filed challenges, and documented violations
constitute mounting pressure that will change the outcome.
She has won some of the legal fights. The record is real.
What she cannot see: the record is being built in Barbuda.
The decisions are being made in St. John's, in New York, in London.
The developers who sign the leases have not read her documentation.
The government officials who approve them have read it and continued.
The record exists. It is not being read by the people who could change the outcome.
Eunice is building pressure on a door that opens from the other side.
She does not know who is on the other side or whether they have any reason to open it.
She files the challenge. She takes the extra photograph.
She believes the accumulation is the argument.
She has not tested whether anyone is receiving the argument.

## Tell
When she photographs a lease — takes one extra photograph of the surrounding land.
