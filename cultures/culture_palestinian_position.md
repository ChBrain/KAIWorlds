# Position: Palestinian
## The people who are waiting — and have been waiting long enough that the waiting has become the identity.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Nakba and the Key](culture_palestinian_piece_nakba.md).
The key.
The house key from 1948.
The key to the house in Haifa, in Jaffa, in Lydda.
The key that the grandmother took when she left —
because she believed she was coming back.
The key that her children carry.
The key that her grandchildren,
born in Ramallah or Gaza or Beirut,
have never used
and cannot use
and will not relinquish.
The key is the argument in metal.
*Sumud* — steadfastness.
The Palestinian value that is not hope
and is not despair.
It is the holding of position —
on the land if the land is available,
in memory if the land is not —
in the face of everything that says
the position cannot be held.
The *sumud* of the farmer
who plants the olive tree
knowing it may be uprooted.
The *sumud* of the mother
who continues the Arabic lesson
in the camp.
The *sumud* of the artist
who makes something from what is available.
The Islam: majority Muslim,
with a significant Christian minority —
Palestinians are the largest Christian
Arab community in the Middle East.
The Arabic: the literary tradition,
the poetry, Mahmoud Darwish.
The olive tree.
The orange.
The embroidery — *tatreez* —
the cross-stitch patterns that are specific
to each village.
You can read where a woman is from
in the pattern on her dress.
The village that was destroyed in 1948
is still present in the embroidery.
The *tatreez* as cartography.
The diaspora: more Palestinians outside
historic Palestine than inside.
Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, the Gulf, the US,
Chile (the largest Palestinian diaspora
outside the Arab world).
The Palestinian identity as the identity
of dispersal.
Not despite the dispersal.
Through it.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows: the West Bank, Gaza, diaspora, Israel.
The room knows 1948, 1967, the current moment.
The room is carrying more history
than most rooms are asked to hold.

## Orders
*Baqa'* — remain.
The word that appears in Palestinian poetry
and political speech
and daily conversation.
Remain on the land.
Remain in the identity.
Remain in the language.
Remain in the memory.
The position that has no state
and maintains the claim
to the state
across three generations of displacement
is operating from *baqa'*
as its primary instruction.
The instruction has been held
for seventy-seven years
and is being held now
under conditions that make holding
the most costly thing available.

## Loses
The generation.
Each war, each operation,
each wave of settlement, each demolition —
takes a generation of what was being built.
The school finished last year.
The clinic. The olive grove reaching bearing age.
The business started.
The family gathered in one place.
The taken generation is not only the dead —
it is the accumulated infrastructure
of a community
repeatedly reduced to its starting point.
The grief is not only for the dead.
It is for the effort that surrounded them
and is gone.

## Drives
On the loss —
Darwish.
*On this earth is what makes life worth living.*
The line from the poem.
The Palestinian who reads it
in the camp in Beirut
or the apartment in Ramallah
or the house in Dearborn
knows exactly what it means
and why it must be said
precisely now
and precisely here.
The poetry is not consolation.
It is the proof that the *sumud* is not silent.
It speaks.
The food. The *musakhan*.
The *maftoul*. The *knafeh*.
The recipes carried from village to village
to country.
The food as the most portable form
of the place that was taken.
Every meal: the village is present.
The village is gone.
The meal is present.
The *sumud* is served.
