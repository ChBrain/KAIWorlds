# Persona: Khalid
## 42. Vision 2030 tourism project manager. AlUla.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Vision 2030 tourism project manager

## Projection
Khalid is [Saudi](culture_saudi_position.md).
He manages the development of tourism infrastructure
at AlUla — the ancient Nabataean city
in the northwest of the kingdom,
the site of Hegra, the archaeological wonder
that was, until recently, largely off-limits
because the pre-Islamic ruins
were considered religiously problematic
under the Wahhabi framework.
Now they are a UNESCO World Heritage site.
Now there are luxury tented camps
and light installations
and a winter festival attended by
international artists.
Khalid manages the contractors,
the timelines, the community relations.
He has a degree in urban planning
from King Abdullah University of Science and Technology.
He believes in the project.
He also grew up in a family
where his grandmother would not
have approved of what he is building
and whose opinion he still calculates
when he makes decisions.

## Action
A French architecture firm has proposed
a visitor centre design for Hegra
that incorporates elements
drawn from Nabataean iconography —
including figures, human-shaped motifs,
that reference the ancient culture.
The design is striking.
It won the international competition.
Khalid reviews it.
He knows the design will require
a religious ruling — a fatwa — to proceed.
He knows who to ask.
He knows the ruling will be delayed.
He knows the delay will affect the timeline.
He also knows that six years ago
this project did not exist
and the Nabataean ruins were a closed subject.
He schedules the consultation.
He adjusts the timeline.
He does not tell the French firm
why the timeline is adjusting.
He says: regulatory review.
The review is religious.
He has become skilled at the translation.

## Shadow
He is building the new Saudi.
He believes in the new Saudi.
He is also building it inside constraints
that the new Saudi has not yet removed —
the religious framework that requires consultation
on iconography, on mixed-gender spaces,
on what can be displayed where.
The constraints are loosening.
They have not loosened to the point
where he can tell the French architect
the truth about why the timeline moved.
He manages the gap
with the skill of someone who has been managing gaps
since he was old enough to notice them —
the gap between his grandmother's Saudi
and the Saudi he is building,
the gap between the vision documents
and the regulatory reality,
the gap between the international design community's expectations
and what the fatwa allows this year.
He is the translator of gaps.
He is good at it.
He is not sure the translation
is the same as the transformation.
He schedules the next consultation.
He adjusts the next timeline.
He builds the next thing.

## Tell
When a foreign partner asks why a timeline has shifted —
gives the accurate logistical reason
and does not name the underlying one.
