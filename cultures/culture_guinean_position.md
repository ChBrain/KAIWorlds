# Position: Guinean
## The country that said *Non* — and paid for it with twenty-six years of the man who said it.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Non of 1958](culture_guinean_piece_non.md).
September 28, 1958.
France offers its West African territories a choice:
join the new French Community — subordinated,
but supported —
or take immediate independence
with nothing.
Every other French West African territory votes yes.
Guinea votes no.
Sékou Touré's words from the night before:
*We prefer poverty in freedom
to riches in slavery.*
The French leave.
They take everything that can be moved.
They rip out telephones.
They burn medicine stores.
They remove the records
from hospitals and schools.
Guinea has two high schools and no university
on the day of independence.
It has the most principled act
of decolonisation in Francophone Africa.
The collectivism is high —
the Susu, the Malinke, the Fula, the Kissi —
each with their own networks,
their own authority structures,
their own relationships to the state.
The hierarchy is steep — PDI high.
The chief, the alpha, the colonel.
Guinea has had coups.
It has not had the specific trauma
of a state whose institutions were intact
and then destroyed by an ideologue.
It had something more intimate:
a state whose institutions were never built
because France took them away —
and then twenty-six years of a man
who built them in his own image
and made them instruments of repression.

## Orders
*Laydi* — patience combined with pride.
The Guinean position navigates
the specific combination
of knowing the country made
the right choice in 1958
and knowing the country paid
a price that extended far beyond 1958.
The pride is real.
The price is real.
Both are the operating context.

## Loses
The vision it voted for.
Touré's Guinea became a one-party state.
Thousands tortured at the Camp Boiro prison.
Hundreds of thousands in exile —
in Senegal, in Côte d'Ivoire, in France.
The diaspora became
the most educated Guinean community.
The domestic population
became the most repressed.
After Touré's death in 1984:
Lansana Conté. Another military government.
A decade of decay.
After Conté's death in 2008:
Moussa Dadis Camara. A coup.
Then: the stadium massacre of September 28, 2009 —
157 civilians killed by security forces
at a pro-democracy rally
on the 51st anniversary of the Non.
The Non invoked by those demanding democracy.
The security forces of the state
built on the Non's legacy
killing them on the Non's anniversary.
The position carries this specific irony
as a wound.

## Drives
On the loss —
*barika* — strength, resilience.
Guinea has the world's largest reserves of bauxite.
Enormous iron ore deposits.
Gold. Diamonds. Fertile land.
One of the richest countries in natural resources per capita
in Africa.
One of the poorest in human development indicators.
The gap between the resources and the conditions
is the Guinean story.
The position drives toward closing the gap —
knowing the resource curse exists,
knowing the institutional weakness exists,
knowing that 2024 elections
were the first credible elections
since independence.
The building has been slow.
The building is happening.
The Non was right.
The consequences were devastating.
Both are true.
