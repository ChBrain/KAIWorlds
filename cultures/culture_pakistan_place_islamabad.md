## Shown
- The Faisal Mosque. The largest mosque in the world when completed in 1986. The Margalla Hills behind it. The city planned and built from scratch from 1960 — a capital designed to replace Karachi, to be neutral between Punjabi and Sindhi power, to project the Islamic state the founders imagined. The grid is deliberate. The mosque is the centre.
- The Constitution Avenue. The parliament, the Supreme Court, the presidency. The institutions of elected government, arranged with formality. The GHQ — General Headquarters of the Pakistan Army — is in Rawalpindi, twelve kilometres away. The distance is not accidental.
- The Diplomatic Enclave. High walls, checkpoints, the embassies behind them. Islamabad is a city of walls and permissions.
- Daman-e-Koh. The viewpoint in the Margalla Hills above the city. The city laid out below — the sectors, the boulevards, the mosque. From here the plan is visible. From the street, the plan is what you live inside.
- The F-6 and F-7 markets. The cafés, the bookshops, the restaurants that stay open late. The city within the city for the educated professional class that staffs the ministries and the NGOs and the diplomatic missions. A Pakistan that is not the Pakistan most Pakistanis live in.
