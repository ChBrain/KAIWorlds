## Shown
- The cleanliness.
  Kigali is the cleanest city in Africa.
  *Umuganda* — the last Saturday of each month.
  All citizens stop work from 8 AM to 11 AM
  and clean the streets, repair the roads,
  plant the trees, maintain the community.
  It is mandatory.
  It is also genuinely done.
  The two things coexist in Rwanda
  in a way that other countries find difficult to read.
- The Kigali Genocide Memorial.
  Gasabo district. 250,000 people buried here.
  Bones. Photographs. Clothes.
  Preserved exactly as they were left in 1994
  in some rooms — the church at Ntarama,
  recreated in documentation here.
  School groups visit every week.
  Foreign dignitaries visit.
  Rwandans visit.
  The visit is not a performance.
  It is a practice.
  *Kwibuka* — to remember — is the national policy.
  The memorial is the national classroom.
- The Kigali Convention Centre.
  The glass dome. The hotel towers.
  The conference infrastructure
  that has made Kigali the meeting place
  of the African Union,
  of international summits,
  of the organisations that once watched 1994
  and did nothing.
  The dome is visible from the memorial.
  Both are deliberate.
- The cooperative vegetable market, Kimironko.
  Stalls run by women's cooperatives.
  The savings groups — *tontines* —
  that pool money and rotate loans.
  The informal economy that runs
  beneath the formal infrastructure
  and is the daily texture
  of what the rebuilt country feels like
  from the inside.
- The ingando camps.
  Outside the city. Used for various purposes
  since the genocide —
  for returned refugees, for released prisoners,
  for students, for those whose views
  the government considers in need of alignment.
  Civic education. Unity and reconciliation curriculum.
  Some who attend are grateful for the structure.
  Some attend under duress.
  The camp is not shown on the tourist map.
  It is known by every Rwandan.

## Withheld
- What people say at home.
  Kigali is a city where the official conversation
  and the private conversation
  are known to be different
  by everyone who lives there.
  The official conversation: Rwandans are one people,
  Hutu and Tutsi are colonial constructions,
  unity is the national project,
  the genocide ideology is banned.
  The private conversation: people know who they are,
  who their parents were, what happened on their hill.
  The private conversation does not contradict the official one in public.
  It exists in parallel.
  The city shows the official conversation.
  The private one is in the houses.
