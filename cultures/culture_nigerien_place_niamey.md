## Shown
- Place de la Patrie.
  Formerly Place de la Francophonie.
  Renamed after the coup.
  The square where protesters gathered on July 30, 2023.
  Where they marched to the French embassy.
  Where the name on the square was still French
  while the French were being expelled.
  The renaming happened later.
  The square is still there.
  The new name is the public statement
  of what the coup claims to mean.
  Whether it means what it claims
  is the question the square cannot answer.
- The Grand Marché.
  The central market.
  The trade that runs between Niger and Nigeria,
  between Niger and Burkina Faso and Mali.
  The Hausa trader networks
  that have moved goods across this territory
  for centuries,
  before France, after France,
  during ECOWAS, after ECOWAS.
  The market does not stop
  because the political arrangement changes.
  The market stopped briefly when ECOWAS sanctions
  cut off supply lines in late 2023.
  Then alternative routes opened.
  The market adjusted.
  It is running.
- The Grande Mosquée.
  The Islamic infrastructure
  that has been here longer
  than the French colonial presence.
  Niamey is a majority-Muslim city
  in a majority-Muslim country.
  The mosque is not the coup's institution.
  It is the city's institution.
  The *Izala* movements, the Sufi brotherhoods,
  the Friday prayer circuits —
  they all run through this and the hundreds
  of neighbourhood mosques.
  The religious infrastructure
  is the social infrastructure.
  It predates every government.
- The Corniche.
  The road along the Niger River.
  Where Niamey residents walk at dusk.
  The river in the city.
  The water in the Sahel —
  the most precious thing.
  The dusk light over the Niger
  is the same dusk light
  that existed before any of this history
  and will exist after.
  The city sits next to it.
  The city looks at it.
- The uranium mine community centre, Arlit.
  1,200 kilometres north.
  Not technically in Niamey
  but present in Niamey as a story.
  The workers who come south.
  The families who moved there.
  The 20 million tonnes of radioactive waste
  in the sand outside the town.
  The town that was built for the mine
  and will outlast the mine
  and live with the waste.
  Arlit is the piece made physical.
