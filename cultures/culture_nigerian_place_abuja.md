## Location: Nigeria

## Shown
- Abuja. The planned capital. Built from nothing in the 1980s
  on neutral ground between north and south —
  the deliberate choice of a federation
  that could not agree on Lagos.
  Wide boulevards. Government ministries in rows.
  The Eagle Square where presidents are inaugurated
  and where the crowds come when the power changes.
  Empty at 3pm on a Tuesday.
  Full when it needs to be full.
- Lagos. The real capital.
  Twenty-two million people, the estimates say.
  The estimates are probably low.
  The Third Mainland Bridge at 6am:
  the longest bridge in Africa,
  already gridlocked.
  Victoria Island: the glass towers, the restaurants,
  the offices of the banks and the multinationals
  and the tech startups and the Nollywood production houses.
  Surulere: where Wizkid grew up.
  Where Fela Kuti built the Shrine.
  Both neighbourhoods in the same city.
  Both the same city.
- The New Afrika Shrine, Lagos.
  Fela Kuti's venue, rebuilt by his son Femi.
  Open every Sunday.
  The music that named the corruption
  and made the naming danceable.
- The National Assembly, Abuja.
  Where the federation negotiates itself.
  Loudly. Repeatedly. Without resolution
  on the questions that matter most.
  Still sitting. Still arguing.
  The argument is the democracy.
- The Lekki Toll Gate, Lagos.
  October 20, 2020.
  The #EndSARS protests.
  The army. The lights going out.
  What happened next is contested.
  What is not contested: the young generation
  of Nigeria found its voice that night
  and has not fully lost it since.
