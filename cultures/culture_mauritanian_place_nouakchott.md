## Shown
- The Grand Mosque. The centre of the city.
  Built in 1980 with Saudi money.
  The city organised around it.
  The call to prayer the most reliable sound
  in a city that is still being built.
  Nouakchott is not a historic city.
  It has no medina, no old quarter,
  no centuries of stone.
  It was sand dunes in 1960.
  Now: 1.5 million people on the sand dunes.
  The city is the country's newest thing.
  The social arrangement it contains
  is the country's oldest thing.
- The Port of Friendship.
  The Chinese-built deepwater port
  that opened in 1986
  and was expanded in subsequent decades.
  The fish processing industry.
  The trade.
  The ships that come and go.
  Mauritania's access to the Atlantic
  is its most underused asset.
  The fish stocks that European trawlers
  have been depleting for decades
  under agreements the government negotiates
  and the fishermen living by the coast
  receive little benefit from.
  The resource extraction logic
  that runs through Mauritanian history —
  fish, iron ore, oil —
  is as visible from the port
  as the ships.
- Kebbe. The shantytown on the edge of the city.
  Where the internally displaced live —
  the nomads who lost their herds to drought,
  the Haratin who fled their masters
  and arrived in the city with nothing
  and found: sand, heat,
  a city that had no infrastructure for them.
  Kebbe has been growing since the 1970s.
  The droughts keep arriving.
  Kebbe keeps growing.
  A significant portion of Kebbe's residents
  are Haratin.
  Their legal status:
  free citizens, officially.
  Their economic status:
  at the bottom of a city
  that has few good options
  and allocated them the worst.
- The parliament building.
  New, functional, foreign-aided.
  Where the laws are passed.
  The laws that abolish slavery.
  The laws that criminalise it.
  The laws whose enforcement is the question.
  The building is not the question.
  The enforcement is the question.
  The building looks like governance.
  The enforcement looks like what it is.
- The desert, beginning at the edge of town.
  In every direction.
  The Sahara is not background.
  It is the condition.
  The city was built on it.
  The people came to it.
  The economy is held by it.
  The sand moves into the streets in the harmattan.
  The Mauritanians have always lived
  between the sand and the sea
  and the social arrangement
  that decided who served whom.
