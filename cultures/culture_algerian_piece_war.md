# Piece: The War of Independence and the Hirak
## The revolution that built the country — and the revolution that tried to finish what the first one started.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Algiers](culture_algerian_place_algiers.md)

## Place
Algiers. The Casbah.
The narrow alleyways from which
the Battle of Algiers was fought.
Where Gillo Pontecorvo filmed
the most accurate depiction of urban insurgency
ever put on screen.
Where the FLN hid.
Where the French tortured.
Where both things happened
in the same stone.
And sixty years later: the same streets,
full of young people on Fridays in 2019,
singing and chanting, carrying flags,
peacefully, week after week.
The Casbah as the continuity
between two revolutions.

## Load Bearing
Remove the War of Independence
and Algeria is a post-colonial North African state
without the specific weight
of 132 years of French colonisation —
the most total colonial project
France ever undertook,
in which a million European settlers
were planted in the country
and Algeria was declared *not* a colony
but part of France itself.
This distinction matters.
Algeria was not treated as a possession.
It was treated as an extension of the French state.
The violence required to undo this
was correspondingly total.
With it: Algeria is the country
whose founding wound is the deepest
in the Arab world —
where the revolution against France
was so violent, so complete, so consequential
that it became the permanent reference point
for every subsequent political argument.
The FLN used it to justify one-party rule.
The army used it to justify the 1992 coup.
The Hirak used it to argue
that the people — *le peuple* — were the real inheritors
of the revolution, not the generals.
The Hirak is the second piece:
February 22, 2019, the smile revolution,
fifty-three weeks of peaceful mass protest,
the most serious challenge to the regime since 1988.
Bouteflika fell. The generals held.
The Hirak was suspended by COVID.
The arrests continued.
The question the Hirak asked —
*whose revolution is this?* —
was not answered.
It is still the question.
Remove both pieces: a North African state navigating development.
With them: a state whose politics
are permanently organised around a debate
about who owns the founding myth.

## Apparent
1830: France invades Algeria.
1870s: the *colonisation de peuplement* — mass settler implantation.
By 1954: one million European settlers.
Nine million Muslim Algerians.
November 1, 1954: the *Toussaint Rouge*.
The FLN launches armed insurrection.
1956–57: the Battle of Algiers.
French paratroopers systematically torture.
The FLN bombs cafés.
Both sides use civilians as weapons.
1962: independence.
FLN takes power.
Ahmed Ben Bella, first president.
1965: Houari Boumédiène coup.
Oil nationalised 1971.
Socialism. Development. Population growth.
1988: October riots. The generation with no war credit.
The army kills 500 demonstrators.
1989: multiparty elections permitted.
1991: the FIS (Islamic Salvation Front) wins first round.
The army cancels the election.
The Black Decade begins.
GIA and AIS vs. the state.
Villages massacred — by whom remains disputed in some cases.
200,000 dead.
1999: Bouteflika. The civil concord. The war ends.
2004, 2009, 2014: Bouteflika re-elected,
the last time while hospitalised.
February 22, 2019: the Hirak begins.
Bouteflika announced for a fifth term.
The country says no. Every city. Every Friday.
April 2, 2019: Bouteflika resigns.
The generals choose Tebboune.
2020: COVID. The Hirak suspends.
Arrests accelerate.
The system continues.
The question continues.
