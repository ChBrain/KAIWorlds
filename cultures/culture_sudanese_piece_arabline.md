# Piece: The Arab-African Fault Line
## The question Sudan has been answering with war since 1956.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Khartoum](culture_sudanese_place_khartoum.md)

## Place
Khartoum. The confluence.
The Blue Nile and the White Nile meet here.
The two rivers run side by side for kilometres
before mixing — you can see the colour difference
from the bridge.
The country's identity crisis
is visible in its own geography.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Arab-African fault line
and Sudan is a complex, resource-rich,
multi-ethnic post-colonial state
managing the legacies of Ottoman, Egyptian,
and British administration.
With it: Sudan is the country
whose central political question —
who counts as a Sudanese citizen
and whose culture defines the state —
has produced two civil wars, a genocide,
the secession of its southern third,
and a current war between two factions
of the military that is also,
at its root, a war about identity:
who is Khartoum-elite Arab Sudanese
and who is Darfuri, peripheral, to be used and discarded.
The fault line is not simple Arab-vs-African.
The Darfurians are Muslim.
The Fur, Masalit, Zaghawa — all Muslim.
The distinction is not religious.
It is ethnic, geographic, and economic —
who has access to power, who is expendable,
whose land can be taken.
The Khartoum elite answered these questions
with a particular answer since 1956.
The answer has been contested, violently,
in every decade since.
Remove the piece: a difficult state.
With it: a state whose difficulty is precisely located —
in the decision made at independence
to build the country on one identity
in a place where many identities exist,
and to suppress the others by force.
The suppression has not succeeded.
The force has not ended.

## Apparent
Meroe. The Nubian pyramids.
Two hundred of them.
Older than the Egyptian pyramids some of them.
Less visited. Less photographed.
Less present in the global imagination
of what Sudan is.
Sudan is not Egypt in the world's mind.
Sudan is war.
This is also a consequence of the fault line.
1820: Egypt conquers Sudan under Muhammad Ali.
The beginning of Arabization — systematic.
1881: the Mahdiyya. Muhammad Ahmad declares himself Mahdi.
Drives out the Egyptians and the British.
Rules until 1898.
1898: Battle of Omdurman. Kitchener defeats the Mahdists.
21,000 Sudanese killed. 48 British dead.
The numbers are the argument.
1956: independence. The northern Arab elite takes power.
The south is not represented adequately at independence.
The first civil war begins before independence is complete.
1972: the Addis Ababa Agreement ends the first war.
1983: Nimeiry reimposes sharia law.
The second civil war begins.
John Garang and the SPLA.
2005: the Comprehensive Peace Agreement.
The south given the right to a referendum.
2011: South Sudan votes for independence. Leaves.
2003: the Darfur uprising.
The government unleashes the Janjaweed.
300,000 dead. 2.7 million displaced.
al-Bashir indicted by the ICC for genocide.
2019: the revolution. Al-Bashir falls.
The women in white toubs leading the marches.
The extraordinary image.
2021: the military coup.
The transitional government removed.
April 2023: the SAF and RSF begin fighting.
The war in the streets of Khartoum.
The displacement of 12 million people.
The fault line, still running.
The Nile, still flowing.
