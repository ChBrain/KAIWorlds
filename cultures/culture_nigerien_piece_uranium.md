# Piece: The Uranium Paradox and the Second Independence
## The resource that powers France — and what Niger received in return for sixty years of it.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Niamey](culture_nigerien_place_niamey.md)

## Place
Niamey. The Grande Mosquée.
The Niger River in the Sahel city.
Place de la Francophonie — until July 2023,
when it was renamed Place de la Patrie.
The French embassy, partially burned
on July 30, 2023, by protesters
who had come specifically for it.
The renaming and the burning
in the same week.
Both were the piece made visible.

## Load Bearing
Remove the uranium
and France's nuclear energy programme
is significantly disrupted —
Niger provided 10–15% of French uranium imports
for decades.
With it: Niger is the country
where the *Françafrique* contract
was its most naked:
France got the resource that powers its grid.
Niger got security guarantees, aid,
and a consistent ranking at the bottom
of the Human Development Index.
The eight uranium mines at Arlit
extracted 150,000 tonnes of uranium
since 1968.
The town of Arlit was left with
20 million tonnes of radioactive waste
when the Akuta mine closed in 2021.
The workers at Arlit received wages
that a French worker would not
accept for the same exposure.
One-third of lamps in France, by some estimates,
burn on Nigerien uranium.
Niamey had frequent power outages.
Remove the piece:
Niger is a Sahelian country with a uranium deposit
and the governance options available
to a small landlocked state.
With it: Niger is the proof of concept
for the entire *Françafrique* critique —
the most concrete single example
of the arrangement's arithmetic.
The July 26, 2023 coup
expelled the French ambassador,
revoked French military agreements,
terminated Orano's uranium licence,
nationalized the SOMAIR uranium unit,
withdrew from ECOWAS,
and allied with Mali and Burkina Faso
in the Alliance of Sahel States.
These are real changes.
They are also a military junta's changes —
President Bazoum, the democratically elected president,
remains under house arrest.
The coup's leader, General Tchiani,
is not a democrat.
The arithmetic of the uranium contract was wrong.
The solution to a wrong arrangement
is not guaranteed to be right.
Both things are true simultaneously.

## Apparent
1960: independence. Hamani Diori, first president.
1968: uranium mining begins. Orano (then COGEMA) concession.
1974: Seyni Kountché coup. Military rule.
1993: first democratic elections.
Cycles of elected governments and coups — 1996, 1999, 2010.
2011: Mahamadou Issoufou elected.
2021: Mohamed Bazoum elected.
First peaceful democratic transfer in Niger's history.
July 26, 2023: Presidential Guard commander Abdourahamane Tchiani
seizes power. Bazoum detained.
ECOWAS ultimatum: reinstate Bazoum or face military intervention.
Military intervention: not launched.
Sanctions imposed.
Niger expels French ambassador.
France refuses to recognise expulsion.
September 2023: last French soldier leaves Niger.
December 2023: US military accord revoked.
1,100 US troops depart.
June 2024: Orano uranium licence for Imouraren mine revoked.
June 2025: Niger announces SOMAIR nationalisation.
Orano, 90% French state-owned, holds 63% of SOMAIR.
The uranium relationship is being dismantled
one concession at a time.
2026: Bazoum still under house arrest.
Sahel Alliance consolidating.
Elections not yet scheduled.
The second independence is in progress.
Its direction is not yet determined.
