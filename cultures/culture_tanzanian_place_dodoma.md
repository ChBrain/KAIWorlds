## Shown
- Dar es Salaam. The harbour city.
  *Dar es Salaam* — haven of peace in Arabic.
  The name given by the Sultan of Zanzibar in 1866.
  The colonial capital that remained the de facto capital
  even after Dodoma was designated the official capital in 1974.
  Nyerere moved the capital to Dodoma
  as a *ujamaa* gesture — away from the colonial coast,
  toward the geographic centre of the country.
  The government offices followed, slowly.
  The commercial life did not follow.
  It is still in Dar.
  Two capitals, one country — a small Tanzanian irony.
- Stone Town, Zanzibar.
  The old Arab trading city.
  UNESCO heritage site.
  The narrow streets where the spice trade ran
  and the slave trade ran alongside it.
  Both histories in the same walls.
  The Anglican cathedral built on the site
  of the last slave market, 1873.
  The altar where the whipping post stood.
  This is deliberate.
  The cathedral does not hide what it replaced.
- Mount Kilimanjaro.
  Visible on a clear day from Moshi,
  from parts of Arusha,
  sometimes from the Kenyan border.
  The highest peak in Africa.
  The snow that is disappearing.
  The glacier that has retreated significantly
  since the first European climbers.
  Tanzania's most famous landmark
  is also its most visible climate record.
- The Serengeti.
  One and a half million wildebeest
  moving in a circuit that does not recognise borders.
  The Masai Mara on the Kenyan side.
  The Serengeti on the Tanzanian side.
  The animals move according to rain, not nationality.
  The countries have agreed on this
  for fifty years.
- The University of Dar es Salaam.
  Founded 1961.
  Where Walter Rodney wrote *How Europe Underdeveloped Africa*.
  Where the debate about African socialism
  was most vigorously conducted.
  Where the generation that would govern Tanzania
  was educated in Nyerere's philosophy
  and inherited his questions.

## Withheld
- The Zanzibar question.
  Tanzania is a union: Tanganyika and Zanzibar,
  merged in 1964.
  The union is politically contested.
  Zanzibar has its own president,
  its own elections, its own parliament.
  Zanzibari politics are different from mainland politics.
  The Civic United Front has competed
  against the ruling CCM for decades.
  Elections in Zanzibar have been disputed
  more than once, with violence.
  The union holds.
  Whether it holds because it is genuinely desired
  or because the alternative is worse
  is the question the room does not answer directly.
