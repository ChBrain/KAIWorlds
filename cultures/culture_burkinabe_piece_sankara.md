# Piece: Sankara
## The four years — and why the man killed in 1987 governs more of Africa now than when he was alive.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Ouagadougou](culture_burkinabe_place_ouagadougou.md)

## Place
Ouagadougou. The Council of the Entente building.
October 15, 1987.
Sankara arrives at a meeting.
He is shot.
His twelve companions are shot.
Blaise Compaoré — his best friend, his comrade —
takes power.
The building is still there.
The bullet holes are still in some accounts.
The mythology began on that day.

## Load Bearing
Remove Sankara
and Burkina Faso is the Republic of Upper Volta —
a landlocked Sahelian country
with scarce resources, high poverty,
and the French-backed rotating military governments
that characterised Francophone West Africa.
With it: Burkina Faso is the country
that produced the most cited,
most quoted, most replicated
political speech in post-independence Africa.
*We must dare to invent the future.*
*You cannot carry out fundamental change
without a certain amount of madness.*
*Imperialism is a system of exploitation
that occurs not only in the brutal form of those
who come with guns to conquer territory.
It also functions in the form of economic domination,
in cultural domination.*
These are not academic quotations.
They are on protest signs in Paris,
in São Paulo, in Nairobi.
They are in the manifestos of movements
that did not exist when Sankara was alive.
He governed for four years.
He is among the five most quoted political leaders in Africa
by citation count in social media.
The piece is load-bearing not only for Burkina Faso
but for the entire Francophone West African political imagination —
the vision of what development without dependence
might look like
is still, in 2026, substantially Sankara's.
The country named for his values
is currently governed by a military junta
that invokes his name
and embodies none of his governance philosophy.
This is the piece's most painful dimension:
the gap between the mythical authority
and the political reality.

## Apparent
August 4, 1983: Sankara seizes power.
He is thirty-three years old.
He calls it a revolution.
He means it.
August 4, 1984: the country renamed.
The ceremony: the national flag changes.
The name changes.
The cloth changes.
October 2, 1984: Sankara addresses the UN General Assembly.
He calls for debt cancellation.
*Debt is a cleverly managed reconquest of Africa.*
The speech is applauded.
Nothing changes immediately in debt policy.
The speech is still watched on YouTube.
1983–87: mass vaccinations, tree planting, railway building,
school construction, land reform.
No foreign aid accepted.
The economy runs on local production.
Women's rights expanded.
The female officer corps created.
The Faso Dan Fani national cloth mandated.
October 15, 1987: Compaoré's coup.
Sankara killed at thirty-seven.
His body identified years later
by DNA testing.
Compaoré governs for twenty-seven years.
He rules with French support.
He is the anti-Sankara.
2014: popular uprising. Compaoré flees.
A transitional government.
Elections.
2020: civilian government.
2022: first coup. Captain Ibrahim Traoré.
Second coup. Traoré remains.
Traoré invokes Sankara.
He expels the French.
He invites Wagner.
The Sankara family asks: stop using his name.
Traoré continues using it.
The myth belongs to everyone now.
Including those it would have opposed.
