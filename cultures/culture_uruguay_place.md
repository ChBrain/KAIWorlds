# Place: Montevideo
## Location: Uruguay

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Shown
- The Rambla. The coastal promenade running twenty-two kilometres along the Río de la Plata. Where Montevideanos walk, cycle, fish, and sit on Sunday with their *mate* thermos. The river is wide enough here to be almost indistinguishable from the sea. Buenos Aires is on the other side, 250 kilometres across water. Argentina and Uruguay are cousins who share a river and have been arguing about the water rights since 1825.
- The Ciudad Vieja. The old city. The architecture is Art Deco and nineteenth-century European. Small streets. Bookshops. The Mercado del Puerto: the covered market where the *parrillas* cook beef over charcoal and the smoke rises through the iron roof.
- The Teatro Solís. The oldest theatre in Uruguay, 1856. National ballet. Opera. The serious culture of a small country that takes its culture seriously.
- Estadio Centenario. The first FIFA World Cup stadium, 1930. Uruguay hosted and won. The stadium still runs. Football in Uruguay is a national religion administered with the seriousness of a civil service.
- The Parlamento. The legislative palace. The legislature that produced the first old-age pension in the Americas (1919), the eight-hour work day (1915), the legalisation of divorce (1907), women's suffrage (1927), and the legalisation of cannabis (2013). The building looks like a European parliament because the people who built it were thinking about European parliaments.

## Withheld
- The specific grief of Uruguay's size. Uruguay is 3.5 million people between Argentina (45 million) and Brazil (215 million). The country has produced more per capita Nobel Prizes, writers, and football champions than either of its neighbours. It is nonetheless described, internationally, as "that small country between Argentina and Brazil." The room shows a composed face. Inside the composure is a precise sense of what has been built and a frustration that the building is not more widely recognised.
