# Piece: The Americo-Liberian Contradiction and the Civil Wars
## The freed who became colonisers — and the reckoning that destroyed the country.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Monrovia](culture_liberian_place_monrovia.md)

## Place
Monrovia. Named for James Monroe,
fifth president of the United States,
who supported the colonisation project.
The capital of a country named *Liberia* —
land of liberty —
that denied citizenship to most of its people
for 57 years after independence.
The Masonic Lodge: built 1867.
It survived the civil war.
The only substantial building in Monrovia
that did not need reconstruction.
The symbol of the Americo-Liberian elite
outlasting the violence it provoked.
The lodge is still there.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Americo-Liberian founding
and Liberia is a grouping of indigenous communities
in coastal West Africa, never colonised,
with their own governance structures.
With it: Liberia is the country
where the American contradiction —
a republic built on slavery —
was exported to Africa,
where formerly enslaved and free Black Americans
reproduced the structures of oppression they had fled
on a population that had not enslaved them.
The love of liberty that brought them there
did not extend to the people already there.
This is not incidental.
It is the country's founding structure.
It produced 133 years of caste politics.
It produced Samuel Doe's 1980 coup —
the indigenous reckoning.
Doe executed 13 Americo-Liberian officials publicly on the beach.
The reckoning went on to produce
something worse than what it ended.
The civil wars of 1989–97 and 1999–2003
were not primarily about the Americo-Liberian question.
They were about ethnic rivalry among indigenous groups —
Gio and Mano vs. Krahn — and about Charles Taylor's ambition.
But the institutions of Liberia
were too weak to contain any of it
because the Americo-Liberians had spent 133 years
building institutions to serve themselves
and exclude everyone else.
Remove the piece: an indigenous West African community.
With it: a country whose specific tragedy
is that the people who understood oppression best —
who had survived it in America —
reproduced it in Africa.
And then watched what they built destroy itself.

## Apparent
1816: the American Colonization Society founded.
1822: first settlers arrive at Cape Mesurado.
The indigenous Dei and Bassa resist.
The settlers fight back.
1847: independence declared.
The motto: *The love of liberty brought us here.*
Indigenous people: not citizens.
Not voters. Taxed but unrepresented.
1904: indigenous people given birthright citizenship.
Still no political participation.
1944: William Tubman becomes president.
Unification policy: begins incorporating indigenous leaders.
Still Americo-Liberian domination.
1971: Tubman dies. Tolbert succeeds.
Reform promises. Slow change.
1979: rice price protests. Security forces kill 40.
April 12, 1980: Sergeant Samuel Doe's coup.
Tolbert killed. 13 ministers executed on the beach.
End of the first republic.
Doe: indigenous. Krahn.
His regime: more brutal than what it replaced.
Corruption, ethnic favouritism.
1989: Charles Taylor's NPFL invades from Côte d'Ivoire.
The first civil war.
1990: ECOMOG peacekeeping force.
September 1990: Doe captured, tortured, killed on camera.
Circulated internationally.
1997: elections. Taylor wins. 75% of the vote.
People vote for Taylor to stop the war.
*He killed my ma, he killed my pa, but I'll vote for him.*
1999: second civil war begins.
LURD and MODEL rebel factions.
2003: Taylor resigns. Goes to Nigeria.
Peace agreement.
UNMIL peacekeeping mission.
2005: Ellen Johnson Sirleaf elected.
Africa's first female president.
2006: Taylor extradited to The Hague.
2012: Taylor convicted of war crimes. 50 years.
2011: Sirleaf — Nobel Peace Prize.
2014–16: Ebola. 4,800 dead.
2018: George Weah elected.
2024: Joseph Boakai elected. Transfer of power.
The republic rebuilt. Slowly. Continuously. Not finished.
