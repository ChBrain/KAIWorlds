## Shown
- Tashichho Dzong. The fortress-monastery on the Wang Chhu river. Government offices and monastic quarters in the same building. The administrative and spiritual in the same structure. White walls, golden roofs. The flag flying above.
- The Memorial Chorten. The stupa in the centre of the city. Built in memory of the third king. Elderly Bhutanese walk the circumambulation path every morning. The prayer wheels turning. The continuity visible in the daily circuit.
- The Weekend Market. The vegetable sellers, the handicraft stalls, the farmers from the villages. The traditional dress — the *gho* for men, the *kira* for women — required in official buildings and widely worn. Not performed for tourists. Worn.
- The Youth Centre. The young Bhutanese who are online, who watch Korean drama, who listen to music that did not come from the monastery. The culture the *driglam namzha* meets at the edge of the broadband connection.
- The road to India. The only land connection. Everything that arrives in Bhutan from the outside world comes up this road. The dependence is geographic and economic. India is the largest trading partner, the largest aid donor, the country on whose goodwill the hydropower exports depend.

## Withheld
- The brain drain. Thimphu shows the *gho* and the Dzong and the prayer wheels. It withholds the growing number of young Bhutanese leaving for Australia, for Canada, for anywhere the opportunity is larger than the $200-per-day tourist tariff and the careful development pace allow. The happiness index measures those who stayed. It does not measure why the others left or what they found when they arrived elsewhere.
