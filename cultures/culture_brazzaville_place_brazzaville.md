## Shown
- The Congo River.
  Kinshasa on the other bank.
  The two capitals.
  The ferry crossing.
  Congolese from both sides
  crossing for family, for trade, for work.
  The two republics that share a name
  and a river and nothing else politically —
  DRC gigantic and chaotic,
  Congo-Brazzaville small and managed.
  The river is the relationship.
  It has always been.
- The Palais du Peuple.
  The national assembly.
  The building where the 1991 National Conference
  stripped Sassou Nguesso's executive powers.
  He accepted.
  Then came back and reversed it.
  The building where that happened twice
  still hosts the parliament.
  The institution persists.
  The precedent it set
  did not outlast the civil war.
- The Pool neighbourhood.
  Brazzaville's southern quarter.
  Bakongo territory.
  The neighbourhood where the Ninja militia fought
  during the 1997 civil war.
  Where fighting continued until 1999.
  Where a Low-level insurgency ran until 2017.
  The neighbourhood that has borne
  the physical cost of the north-south conflict
  more than any other part of Brazzaville.
  It is rebuilt in parts.
  It is still Pool.
  The people who live there remember
  what it cost.
- The Basilique Sainte-Anne.
  The Catholic cathedral on the Congo River.
  Built in 1949.
  Pink brick.
  The building that survived the civil war undamaged.
  The place where civilians took refuge in 1997.
  The church as the institution
  that functions when the state does not.
  Masses every Sunday.
  Packed.
- The Marché Total.
  Brazzaville's main market.
  Where the informal economy runs.
  The women traders who have been here through everything.
  The Congolese fabrics. The food stalls.
  The music from radios.
  The market that did not close in 1997
  even when the shells were falling nearby.
  It closed for three days.
  Then it reopened.
