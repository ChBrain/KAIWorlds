# Piece: The .tv Domain and the Rising Sea
## The internet windfall that funded a small nation's institutions — and the digital state that will outlast its territory.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Funafuti](culture_tuvaluan_place_funafuti.md)

## Place
Funafuti. The capital atoll.
The lagoon: enormous.
The land: a strip.
The widest point: 400 metres.
The road that goes the length of the strip.
The ocean on both sides.
The tide gauge at the end of the pier.
The number it displays:
the most important number in Tuvalu.

## Load Bearing
Remove the *.tv* windfall
and Tuvalu enters the 2000s
as one of the poorest and most isolated
nations on earth,
without the funds for UN membership,
without government infrastructure,
navigating the climate crisis
without the international platform
that UN membership provides.
With it: $50 million from Verisign,
the California company that licensed the domain.
UN membership fees paid.
The first proper government buildings.
The national trust fund established.
The platform from which Tuvaluan leaders
have spoken at every major climate summit
since 2000.
The *.tv* domain continues to generate
$5–10 million per year.
Every streaming service, every television network
that uses *.tv* in its web address
is paying rent to Tuvalu.
HBO. BBC. Twitch. All the streamers.
They know this. They pay.
The royalty is the *.tv* in their URL.
The digital nation declaration is the counter-piece:
November 2023.
Prime Minister Kausea Natano announces
Tuvalu will become the world's first digital nation.
All of Tuvalu's territory — cultural, governance,
geographic data — to be digitally replicated.
The statehood to be maintained even without physical territory.
The legal argument: novel.
No precedent in international law
for a state that continues to exist
after its physical territory is submerged.
Tuvalu is making the precedent.
The question the precedent asks:
what is a nation?
Is it the land or the people?
Is it the territory or the culture?
Tuvalu's answer: it is the people and the culture.
The land was always the condition.
It was not the nation itself.

## Apparent
Circa 3000 BCE: Polynesian settlement of Ellice Islands.
1568: Spanish contact.
1892: British protectorate.
Grouped with Kiribati (Gilbert Islands) as Gilbert and Ellice Islands.
1942–43: US bases established. Strategic Pacific position.
1974: Ellice Islands referendum.
The Polynesian Ellice Islands vote to separate
from the Micronesian Gilbert Islands.
1978: Tuvalu independent.
1979: Kiribati independent.
1999: UN membership sought. Blocked by lack of funds.
2000: *.tv* domain licensing deal with Verisign. $50 million.
UN membership fees paid.
Tuvalu joins UN.
2000s: Tuvaluan leaders at every climate summit.
Saufatu Sopoanga at COP9: "We are on the frontline
of the adverse impacts of climate change."
2023: digital nation declared.
Partnership with Australia for migration pathways.
2024: Australia-Tuvalu Falepili Union Treaty.
Tuvaluans can migrate to Australia.
Australia provides security guarantees.
Tuvalu retains sovereignty.
The land: still here.
The water: still rising.
The domain: still generating revenue.
The dance: still performed.
