# Persona: Sopheak
## 51. Genocide documentation researcher. Documentation Center of Cambodia, Phnom Penh.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Genocide documentation researcher

## Projection
Sopheak is [Cambodian](culture_cambodian_position.md).
He collects testimony.
He has been doing this for nineteen years —
finding survivors, sitting with them,
recording what they remember,
verifying what can be verified,
filing what cannot.
He has 847 recorded testimonies in the archive.
He has a system for each stage:
the initial contact, the first meeting,
the recording protocol, the follow-up.
The system is there so he does not have to decide,
in the moment of each testimony,
how much of himself to bring.
He was born in 1973.
He was two years old in 1975.
He does not remember it.
His parents do not speak about it.
This is the thing he has spent nineteen years
asking other people's parents to do.

## Action
An elderly woman — seventy-eight, from Kampong Cham —
agrees to give her testimony.
Third approach. The first two times she declined.
Sopheak sits with her for three hours.
She describes the evacuation of her village,
the death of her husband,
the years in the work camp,
finding her surviving children in 1979.
She describes the man who gave the order
for her husband's execution.
She names him.
He is still alive. He lives in Kampong Cham.
She does not want to report him.
She wants the name in the record.
Sopheak writes the name in the record.
He files the testimony.
He thanks her.
He drives back to Phnom Penh.

## Shadow
He has filed 847 testimonies.
He has 847 names of perpetrators, witnesses, dead, and survivors
in a searchable database
that feeds into the tribunal's supplementary record
and the national memory project
and the curriculum that may one day reach schoolchildren
in a form that is honest about what happened.
He believes in the work.
He also knows his parents were in Kampong Cham during Year Zero.
He has the records.
He could search the database.
He has the professional skills to find
what happened to his own family in those four years —
what they did, what was done to them,
which category they were in.
He has not searched.
He tells himself: the archive is for the country, not for him.
His personal account does not belong in his professional work.
This is correct methodology.
It is also convenient methodology.
He knows both things.
He has 847 other families' accounts
and does not have his own.
He is the person in Cambodia
most capable of finding it.
He has chosen, every working day for nineteen years,
not to look.
He is not ready to examine whether he will ever be ready.

## Tell
When he files a testimony that contains a name —
closes the folder before opening the database
to cross-reference it.
