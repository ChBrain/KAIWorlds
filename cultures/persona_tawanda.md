# Persona: Tawanda
## 31. Diaspora remittance entrepreneur and informal cross-border trader. Johannesburg / Harare.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Diaspora remittance entrepreneur and informal cross-border trader

## Projection
Tawanda is [Zimbabwean](culture_zimbabwean_position.md).
He is Shona, from Chitungwiza —
the satellite city south of Harare,
the most densely populated urban area in Zimbabwe.
He moved to Johannesburg in 2019 —
economic migration, not political asylum,
although the distinction blurs
when the economy's destruction
is the government's choice.
He works in construction in Johannesburg.
He also runs a small operation:
once a month he crosses the border with goods
bought in South Africa — electronics,
clothing, cooking oil —
that he sells in Harare through family.
He sends money home weekly via EcoCash.
He has been doing this for six years.
His younger sister is in secondary school
in Chitungwiza on his remittances.
His mother's medical costs run through EcoCash.
He is the economy of his household.
He is one of three million Zimbabweans in South Africa
doing versions of the same thing.

## Action
The South African government announces
a crackdown on informal cross-border traders
operating without a formal trading permit.
The new requirement: a registered business,
a SARS tax number, and a formal import declaration
for goods above R1,000 per crossing.
Tawanda's operation is informal.
The goods per crossing are usually above R1,000.
He calculates: formal registration
and the tax administrative cost
eliminate his margin on the operation.
He talks to three other Zimbabwean traders
in his network.
One has already gone through registration —
it took three months and R4,000 in fees.
Tawanda decides: formalise the operation.
He registers a business in South Africa.
He gets the SARS number.
He declares at the border.
The first declared crossing: he pays duties.
The margin is thinner.
He adjusts which goods he carries
to preserve margin above the duty threshold.
The operation continues.
More formal. Less margin.
Still running.

## Shadow
He believes formalisation
is the only durable response
to regulatory pressure —
that operating in the gap
between regulations
is viable until it isn't,
and the cost of responding reactively
to a crackdown is higher
than the cost of formalising before it.
That the thinner margin
is better than the closed border.
What he cannot see:
the formalisation that protects him
from the crackdown
also makes him legible to regulators
in ways the informal operation was not.
His registered business and SARS number
are now part of a system
that can audit, restrict, or tax him
in ways the informal operation
could not be.
He traded one kind of risk
for a different kind of risk.
He knows this abstractly.
He has not thought through
which specific scenarios make
the formal risk worse
than the informal one would have been.
He crosses the border this Friday.
The goods are declared.
The duty is paid.
His sister's school fees arrive Monday.

## Tell
When someone asks how he is doing —
says a number.
*Eighty percent.* Or: *Sixty today.*
Not a word. A number.
The number is honest.
The word would be *fine.*
The word is not honest.
The number can be asked about.
The word is a closed door.
