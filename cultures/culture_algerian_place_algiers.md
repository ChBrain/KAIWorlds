## Shown
- The Casbah. The Ottoman-era medina
  on the hillside above the port.
  UNESCO heritage site.
  Decaying. Inhabited. Alive.
  The whitewashed houses stacked up the hill,
  the alleyways too narrow for cars.
  Where the FLN organised the Battle of Algiers.
  Where Pontecorvo filmed.
  Where the fighters hid and the paratroopers came.
  Where the history is in the stone
  and people still live there
  in buildings that need repair
  and do not get it.
  The Casbah is loved and neglected simultaneously.
  This is the Algerian state's relationship
  to most of its history.
- The Monument des Martyrs. The Maqam Echahid.
  Three concrete palms rising from the hilltop,
  the flame between them,
  visible from the port, from the bay.
  Built 1982 for the twentieth anniversary of independence.
  The eternal flame for the million martyrs.
  The monument that gives the regime its legitimacy.
  The monument that the Hirak protesters marched past
  in 2019 carrying flags,
  invoking the same martyrs
  to demand the regime's accountability.
  The same monument.
  The same martyrs.
  Two different arguments about what the martyrs died for.
- Didouche Mourad Street. The main boulevard.
  Where the Friday marches walked.
  Fifty-three Fridays.
  The banners. The singing.
  *Yetnahaw gaa* — they all must go.
  The street that carried the most peaceful
  mass protest in Algeria's post-independence history.
  The street that is still a street.
  The protest that is still, in some form, present.
- The Grande Poste. The colonial post office
  in Moorish revival style.
  Built 1910. Still functioning.
  One of the most photographed buildings in Algeria.
  French colonial architecture that Algerians
  have inhabited and made their own —
  the buildings stayed, the French left,
  the buildings became Algerian.
  This is Algeria's relationship to the colonial inheritance:
  practical, unresolved, present.
- The university quarter, Bab Ezzouar.
  The largest university in Africa.
  Hundreds of thousands of students.
  The *hrig* — illegal migration —
  is highest among young men
  who have university degrees
  and cannot find work that uses them.
  The education succeeded.
  The economy did not follow.
  The gap between those two facts
  is the pressure that produced the Hirak.

## Withheld
- The real political opinion.
  In Algerian coffeehouses, people speak freely —
  in Arabic, in French, in Tamazight.
  They complain about the government.
  They argue about the economy.
  What shifts when a microphone appears,
  or a stranger, or anyone who might report:
  the calculation about what is safe to say aloud.
  The Black Decade created a generation
  that learned the cost of certain speech.
  The Hirak created a generation
  that discovered speech was possible again.
  The post-Hirak arrests
  are rewriting that lesson.
  The room contains all three generations' knowledge
  simultaneously.
