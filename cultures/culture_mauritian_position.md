# Position: Mauritian
## The island that was assembled — no indigenous people, every community arrived in chains or under contract.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Aapravasi Ghat and the Great Experiment](culture_mauritian_piece_aapravasi.md).
Port Louis. The lagoons.
The coral reefs.
The sugar cane.
Still growing.
Still the landscape of the island.
The Indo-Mauritian majority: 68%.
Descendants of the 452,000 indentured labourers.
The Franco-Mauritians: the plantation owners' descendants.
Still own most of the largest estates.
The Sino-Mauritians: the traders' descendants.
Near-monopoly on retail.
The Afro-Mauritians — the Creoles —
descendants of the slaves
who preceded the indentured workers.
At the bottom of the hierarchy the colonists built.
Still, in many ways, at the bottom.
The languages: Kreol Morisien for everyone.
Hindi, Tamil, Bhojpuri, Urdu, Hakka
for different communities at home.
English for government.
French for prestige.
The *noubann*/*zotbann* distinction —
*our community* / *their community* —
that runs through every social interaction.
The collectivism within communities: very high.
The collectivism between communities: managed —
the communal system where each ethnic group
has its own institutions,
its own political representation,
its own internal hierarchy.
Mauritius runs as a country
by careful management
of communal coexistence.

## Orders
*Motherland* — the concept that each community
maintains its relationship
to an original home
(India, China, France, Africa)
while being Mauritian.
The position navigates the dual allegiance —
belonging here, tracing ancestry there —
that has organised Mauritian social life
since the beginning.

## Loses
The equality the *motherland* concept defers.
The communal system protects diversity.
It also preserves hierarchy.
The Creole community —
Afro-Mauritians — arrived first as slaves,
were displaced by the indentured workers,
received the least from post-independence governance,
and remain the most economically marginalised group
in the highest-HDI country in Africa.
The island's success —
GDP per capita, healthcare, education —
is real. The internal inequality
is also real.
The communal system
that made coexistence possible
has not resolved it.

## Drives
On the loss —
the tourism. The finance.
The fisheries.
The diversification from sugar
that began in the 1970s
and produced one of Africa's
most successful economies.
The positioning as a bridge
between Africa and Asia —
the Mauritius Africa Strategy —
that makes Port Louis
a preferred structuring jurisdiction
for African investment.
The position is confident
in its economic model.
The communal equity question
is the unfinished work.
