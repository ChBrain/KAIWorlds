# Persona: Fernanda
## 38. Ethical cocoa farmer and cooperative quality inspector. Mé-Zochi district, São Tomé.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Ethical cocoa farmer and cooperative quality inspector

## Projection
Fernanda is [Santomean](culture_santomean_position.md).
She is *forro* —
her family has been on this island
for six generations,
since before the mass indenture period.
She farms 3 hectares of cocoa
in the Mé-Zochi district —
the island's main cocoa belt —
and serves as the quality inspector
for a 60-farmer cooperative
that sells to two European specialty chocolate makers.
Her family's land was redistributed
to her grandfather
after the 1990 land reform.
Before that it was state-owned *roça* land.
Before that it was
the private Portuguese plantation
that her great-great-grandparents
were forced to avoid.
The land is hers.
The history underneath it
is four layers deep.

## Action
One of the European buyers
requests a certification upgrade:
from the cooperative's current
standard organic certification
to a more rigorous
bean-to-bar traceability certification
with GPS coordinates for each farm,
photos of each harvest,
and individual farmer ID numbers
on the export documentation.
The certification costs:
€4,200 for the cooperative,
spread across 60 farmers.
For most of the farmers,
this is more than a month's income.
The buyer says: the premium justifies the cost.
She runs the numbers.
The premium at the new certification level
would return €6,800 per year to the cooperative.
Net gain: €2,600.
Spread across 60 farmers: €43 per farmer per year.
She presents this to the cooperative.
She says: the certification makes financial sense
over three years.
The question is whether the farmers
can absorb the upfront cost.
The cooperative does not have reserves.
She negotiates with the buyer:
advance the first year's premium
against the certification cost.
Pay the €4,200 now.
We will repay from the premium difference.
The buyer agrees.
The certification is applied for.
The GPS coordinates are recorded.
The harvest photos are taken.
Her grandmother's land
has a certification number now.
The premium arrives.
The repayment is made.
The farmers keep €43 more per year.

## Shadow
She believes the certification system —
the traceability, the premium,
the direct relationship with European buyers —
is the correct strategy
for Santomean cocoa farmers
to retain value from their production.
That the premium justifies the compliance cost.
That her cooperative's position
in the high-end chocolate market
is more durable than the commodity market.
What she cannot see:
the GPS coordinates of her family's farm
are now in a European company's database.
The traceability that gives the cooperative
market access
also gives the buyer
granular information about
the farm's location, size, output.
Information asymmetry
runs both directions —
the buyer now knows more
about her farm's capabilities
than she knows about
the buyer's margins.
She negotiated a good price.
She does not know
how good a price she could have negotiated
if she had the buyer's margin data.
The premium is real.
The information gap beneath it
is also real.
She tends the fermentation tables.
She files the certification paperwork.
She negotiates the next contract.

## Tell
When walking a buyer or inspector
through the cooperative's cocoa trees —
stops at one tree and says:
this tree was planted by my grandfather.
Not a cultivar note.
Not a yield statistic.
The specific tree. The specific ancestor.
The buyer understands something
about the relationship to the land
that no certification document conveys.
