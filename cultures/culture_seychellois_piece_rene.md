# Piece: René's Coup and the Creole Nation
## The man who overthrew democracy built Africa's most developed country — and then handed power back peacefully.

## Place
The State House, Victoria.
Where Mancham governed for one year.
Where René governed for 27 years.
Where Ramkalawan now governs.
The same building.
The same island.
The population of a medium-sized town,
running a country.

## Load Bearing
Remove René's 1977 coup
and Seychelles has:
independence in 1976,
James Mancham as president,
the *gran'bla* economic interests intact,
strong ties to France and Britain.
A small island economy dependent on tourism.
Possibly stable. Possibly not.
With it: the specific outcome.
June 4–5, 1977.
Mancham in London for the Commonwealth summit.
René's men seize Mahé overnight.
The radio station, the power grid, the police headquarters.
By morning: done.
Three people killed.
One unarmed opposition figure.
René declares himself president.
Calls himself an "Indian Ocean socialist."
Not Soviet-style. Pragmatic.
Invests tourism revenue in schools, hospitals, housing.
By the 1980s: Seychelles has universal education,
free healthcare, child mortality rates near European levels.
He also:
runs a one-party state.
Bans opposition.
Survives multiple coup attempts —
including Mike Hoare's 1981 mercenary team
who arrived disguised as a holiday rugby club
and were defeated at the airport.
Most escaped in a hijacked Air India plane.
Simultaneously:
builds an offshore financial sector.
Offers immunity from foreign prosecution
to anyone investing $10 million.
The international backlash forces repeal in 1996.
The offshore sector survives anyway.
The world's only socialist tax haven.
Both things simultaneously.
1991: René legalises multiparty politics.
Not from conviction — because the Soviet Union
has collapsed and the IMF requires it.
He keeps winning elections anyway.
2004: he retires voluntarily.
His party eventually loses.
2020: Wavel Ramkalawan.
First opposition president.
Power transfers without violence.
The country René built from a coup
transferred its power
without the coup repeating.
Remove the piece:
a small island democracy
that might have become
whatever Mancham would have made it.
With it: one of the most unexpected
development stories in post-colonial history —
a coup that built something,
and then allowed the building
to survive the coup's own ending.

## Apparent
1742: French claim the islands. Uninhabited.
1770: French settlement. Enslaved Africans brought.
1814: British control. Treaty of Paris.
Slavery abolished 1835.
Indian indentured workers arrive.
1903: Seychelles becomes a separate Crown Colony.
1960s: Mancham and René form rival parties.
Both demand independence. Disagree on terms.
June 29, 1976: independence.
Mancham president. René prime minister.
Both knew what was coming.
Kenyan officials warned Mancham.
Saudi princes warned Mancham.
The CIA warned Mancham.
June 4–5, 1977: coup while Mancham attends
Queen Elizabeth II's Silver Jubilee in London.
Three dead. René president.
Julius Nyerere of Tanzania had trained
and armed René's men.
1979: one-party constitution.
1981: Hoare mercenary coup attempt.
42 South Africans disguised as rugby players.
Gunfight at the airport.
Most escape in hijacked Air India plane.
1982: second coup attempt. Failed.
1986: third coup attempt. Failed.
René endures.
1991: multiparty system restored. IMF conditionality.
1993: René wins first multiparty election.
2004: René retires voluntarily. James Michel succeeds.
2020: Ramkalawan wins. First opposition president.
Peaceful transfer.
2019–2020: Truth, Reconciliation
and National Unity Commission.
Testimony about disappeared opposition figures.
About the official killed in René's office.
About the 1977 coup's planning —
which René denied until his death in February 2019.
The commission: found. Documented.
Recommended reconciliation processes.
The country continues.
The *Moutya* UNESCO inscription: 2021.
The coral bleaching: ongoing.
Ramkalawan: still governing.
The State House: still the same building.
