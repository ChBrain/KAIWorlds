# Piece: Gross National Happiness
## The idea that made Bhutan famous — and what it cost to maintain the conditions it measured.

## Owner
- **Place:** Thimphu

## Place
Thimphu. The clock tower roundabout.
No traffic lights in the capital — a policeman directs traffic by hand.
When lights were installed, they were removed by popular request.
The policeman is still there.
The story is told to every visitor.
It is true.
It is also the story Bhutan tells about itself.

## Load Bearing
Remove GNH and Bhutan is a small Himalayan monarchy
between India and China
with a population of 800,000
and significant hydropower resources.
With it: Bhutan is the country that told the world
that GDP was not the only measure of a society —
that happiness, ecological integrity,
cultural preservation, and good governance
were also measurable and also worth measuring.
The world paid attention.
The world needed the idea.
The idea spread.
Whether Bhutan embodies the idea
is the question the idea did not fully resolve.
The GNH surveys do show high life satisfaction.
They also survey the population that remained.
The Lhotshampa — the Nepali-speaking southerners —
were largely expelled in the early 1990s.
The citizenship laws applied retrospectively.
The National Assembly defined Bhutanese identity
in ways that excluded people
who had lived in Bhutan for generations.
100,000 people in refugee camps in Nepal for twenty years.
Resettled eventually to third countries — the US, Australia, Canada.
They were not in the GNH surveys.
The happiness Bhutan measured
was measured after the population
that would have measured differently
had been removed.
Remove the piece: a thoughtful development philosophy.
With it: a thoughtful development philosophy
and the question of whether the conditions
that allow it to show what it shows
were produced by choices the philosophy
does not include in its own accounting.

## Apparent
1907: Ugyen Wangchuck becomes first hereditary king.
1952: third king, Jigme Dorji Wangchuck. The modernisation begins.
1972: fourth king, Jigme Singye Wangchuck, age 17.
The GNH concept first articulated in the 1970s:
"Gross National Happiness is more important than Gross National Product."
The four pillars: good governance, sustainable development,
cultural preservation, environmental conservation.
1988–1990: citizenship census. The Lhotshampa issue begins.
The Citizenship Act applied. Many southerners cannot prove citizenship.
1990–1991: protests, government crackdown, mass exodus.
100,000 Lhotshampa in camps in Nepal.
1999: television and internet introduced.
2008: transition to constitutional monarchy.
First elections.
The fifth king, Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck.
2008 also: the GNH index formalised with nine domains and 33 indicators.
Bhutan presents the framework at the UN.
The UN adopts March 20 as International Day of Happiness.
The refugees: resettlement to third countries from 2007.
By 2015 most of the 100,000 resettled.
Bhutan and Nepal have not agreed on the status
of the remaining cases.
The GNH surveys do not include the diaspora.
The king is popular.
The forests are 72% of the land.
The carbon is sequestered.
The tourist tariff is $200 per day.
The policeman directs traffic.
The question remains open.
