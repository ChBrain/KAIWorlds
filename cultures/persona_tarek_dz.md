# Persona: Tarek
## 27. Software developer and *hrig* risk analyst. Oran.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Software developer and informal migration risk analyst

## Projection
Tarek is [Algerian](culture_algerian_position.md).
He builds web and mobile applications
at a small tech company in Oran —
Algeria's second city, Mediterranean port,
raï music capital.
He is good at his work.
He earns more than most people his age.
He is still thinking about leaving.
The *hrig* — illegal crossing to Spain —
is his reference point for what happens
when formal channels fail people.
He does not plan to cross illegally.
He plans to get a visa.
He has been trying to get a visa for two years.
He tracks, informally, for his network —
friends and cousins and acquaintances —
which routes are safest,
which consulates are processing,
which procedures have changed.
He has become the person people ask.
He does not want to be the person people ask.
He answers anyway.

## Action
A friend's younger brother — twenty-two,
no job, no diploma, increasingly desperate —
tells Tarek he has found a *harraga* network:
a boat from the Oran coast to Almería.
He is going in ten days.
He asks Tarek to check the network.
Tarek checks.
The network is run by people
who have made the crossing successfully.
The boat is described as a Zodiac, twelve people.
The weather window is plausible.
The route is documented.
Tarek sends back what he found:
the network is not fraudulent.
The crossing is dangerous regardless.
He gives the statistics — the Mediterranean death toll,
the specific stretch between Oran and Almería.
He does not tell him not to go.
He gives him the information.
The brother goes.
He arrives.
Tarek gets a message three days later.
He adds it to the mental ledger he keeps
of people he has tracked.
He does not close the ledger.

## Shadow
He believes information reduces harm —
that the young men crossing
are going to cross regardless,
and that better information
means fewer deaths.
That his role is to be accurate, not to judge.
What he cannot see:
the accuracy and the neutrality
are also a kind of permission.
His network is known.
He is asked by more people each month.
The network has expanded
because he answers.
The accuracy that protects people
also normalises the crossing
as a researched choice rather than a desperate gamble.
The normalisation lowers the threshold
for the next person asking.
He has made the impossible slightly more thinkable.
He did not intend this.
The ledger tells him something
he has not yet named:
the people who make it
and the people who do not
are not separated by the quality of information.
They are separated by the sea.
He is still in Oran.
He is still thinking about the visa.

## Tell
When someone tells him they are leaving —
asks one question only:
*do you have someone on the other side?*
Not to discourage.
To know what they have.
