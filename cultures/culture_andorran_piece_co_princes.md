# Piece: The Co-Princes
## Two heads of state from two different countries, serving simultaneously since 1278.

## Owner
- **Place:** Andorra la Vella

## Place
Andorra la Vella. The Casa de la Vall — the parliament.
Also: the Élysée Palace, Paris — one co-prince's office.
Also: Urgell, Spain — the other co-prince's see.
The government is here. The princes are not.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and Andorra loses its 746-year survival mechanism.
The co-principality was created to resolve
a territorial dispute between France and the Bishop of Urgell.
Rather than either winning, both became co-princes.
The arrangement resolved the conflict by making it permanent.
It has worked for longer than any resolution by winner
in the same region.
Remove the co-princes and the question of sovereignty
— French or Spanish or independent —
reopens immediately.

## Apparent
Two co-princes. Currently: Emmanuel Macron and Joan-Enric Vives Sicília.
Neither resides in Andorra.
Both hold the title by virtue of their other offices.
When Macron loses the French presidency, his successor becomes co-prince.
The Bishop of Urgell serves until death or resignation.
No election determines either.
The Andorran constitution of 1993
formalises their role.

## Yearbook
1278: the Pareatge — the founding agreement.
Two lords claim Andorra. Solution: both rule jointly.
The arrangement survives the French Revolution
(the French co-prince becomes the French state),
Napoleon, the Spanish Civil War, two World Wars,
de Gaulle, Mitterrand, Chirac, Sarkozy, Hollande, Macron.
1993: Andorra adopts a constitution.
The co-principality formalised in modern law.
The arrangement that began in a medieval territorial dispute
is now a constitutional democracy with two heads of state.
EU accession talks: begin 2023.
How to integrate a co-principality into EU law
is a question no one has answered yet.
