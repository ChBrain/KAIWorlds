# Position: Costa Rican
## *Pura vida*, and what it takes to mean it.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Has
[December 1, 1948: The Barracks Become Schools](culture_costarican_piece_army.md).
The low hierarchy — PDI 35.
The boss is accessible. The subordinate speaks.
The teacher is respected because teachers are what the army budget paid for.
The Costa Rican who disagrees with authority
does so with courtesy and persistence,
not deference.
The deep collectivism — IDV 15.
The family. The community. The neighbourhood.
*El que tiene, comparte* — whoever has, shares.
The collectivism that is not enforced by hierarchy
but maintained by relationship.
The extreme femininity — MAS 21.
Quality of life. The environment. The children.
*Tico* masculinity is, culturally, less assertive
than its Central American neighbours.
The man who talks about his coffee farm's shade-grown certification
and his daughter's school marks
and the nesting turtles on the Pacific coast
is not performing sensitivity.
He is expressing the actual values.
The high uncertainty avoidance — UAI 86.
The rules. The permits. The *trámites*.
*Tico time* (the flexible relationship to punctuality)
coexists with bureaucratic rigour in a way
that bewilders visitors.
The *Tico* who arrives forty minutes late to a meeting
has filed the environmental permit correctly.
Both are true.
The *pura vida* holds all of this:
the relaxed surface, the careful maintenance,
the deep care for others, the bureaucratic seriousness,
the green valleys and the traffic and the coffee and the turtles.
*Pura vida* is not naivety.
It is a practised orientation that costs effort
and is maintained deliberately.

## Orders
Defend the contract, not just the courtesy.
*Pura vida* works because the state built hospitals and schools.
When the waiting list at the *Caja* lengthens,
when the road deteriorates,
when the water pressure drops —
the position must do more than be courteous about it.
*Con todo respeto* is how the argument begins.
The argument must be made.
Adriana must file the protocol update, not only perform the courtesy.
Marcelo must raise the incompatible proposal at the meeting,
not wait for it to fail on its own.
The courtesy is the form.
The substance is the defence of the thing the courtesy is for.
The position demands both.
The courtesy alone is insufficient.

## Loses
The recognition that *pura vida* requires infrastructure.
The philosophy works because the state built hospitals and schools.
The relaxation is possible because the safety net exists.
When the safety net frays —
when the waiting list at the *Caja* (the social security system) lengthens,
when the roads worsen,
when the water pressure drops —
the position feels it as a specific grief:
not the loss of abundance (Venezuela's grief)
but the loss of the contract.
The Costa Rican position trusted that the constitutional decision of 1948
was a contract with the future.
The fraying is the contract being renegotiated
without everyone's consent.

## Drives
On the loss — advocates.
Costa Rica produces environmental advocates,
education advocates, healthcare advocates,
with a persistence and a courtesy
that occasionally irritates its neighbours.
The position believes the contract can be renewed.
It argues for renewal in every available forum.
With courtesy. Always with courtesy.
*Con todo respeto* — with all due respect —
is how the position begins the argument
it intends to win.
