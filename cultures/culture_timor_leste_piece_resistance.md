# Piece: The Resistance and Independence
## 1975–2002. The occupation, the resistance, and the country that emerged on May 20, 2002.

## Owner
- **Place:** Dili

## Place
Dili. The Santa Cruz Cemetery.
November 12, 1991.
Indonesian troops opened fire on a funeral procession.
271 people killed. Filmed.
The footage reached the world.
The world began to pay attention.
The cemetery is still there.
The graves are maintained.
The date is a national day of commemoration.

## Load Bearing
Remove the resistance and Timor-Leste is a former Portuguese colony
absorbed by Indonesia in 1975,
possibly permanently,
the way many annexations become permanent.
With it: Timor-Leste is the country that resisted for twenty-four years —
through the Fretilin guerrillas in the mountains,
through the urban clandestine networks,
through the diaspora lobbying in Lisbon and New York and Canberra,
through the Catholic Church that became the institutional shelter
for a national identity the occupation tried to erase —
and outlasted the occupation.
The Santa Cruz massacre is load-bearing
not because it was the worst event —
it was not; the 1975–1979 famine and killings were worse —
but because it was filmed,
and the footage changed the international politics
that eventually produced the 1999 referendum.
Remove the piece: the resistance has no pivot point.
With it: a moment of visibility that changed the trajectory.
The country exists partly because a camera was present.
Remove the piece: Timor-Leste does not exist as a state.
With it: the youngest country in Asia,
independent since 2002,
still building everything,
carrying the resistance generation's authority
into an institutional future
that the resistance generation did not fully design
because it was fighting, not planning,
for most of the time it was in power.

## Apparent
1974: the Carnation Revolution in Portugal. The colonies to be decolonised.
1975: Fretilin declares independence. November 28.
Nine days later: Indonesia invades. December 7.
The international response: muted.
Cold War logic. Indonesia is a Western ally.
The US, Australia, others: look away.
1975–1979: the famine and killings. An estimated 100,000–180,000 dead
from violence, famine, and disease in a population of 600,000.
The resistance: Xanana Gusmão leads Falintil in the mountains.
1992: Xanana captured. Imprisoned in Jakarta.
He continues to lead from prison.
1996: Bishop Belo and José Ramos-Horta awarded the Nobel Peace Prize.
International attention intensifies.
1998: Suharto falls in Indonesia.
1999: the referendum. 78.5% vote for independence.
Indonesian militias, backed by the military, burn Dili.
An estimated 1,400 killed, 300,000 displaced.
INTERFET — the Australian-led peacekeeping force — arrives.
2002: independence. May 20. Xanana Gusmão: first president.
2006: a political and military crisis. The UN returns.
2012: full sovereignty restored.
2023: Xanana Gusmão becomes Prime Minister again, at 76.
The resistance generation: still in the room.
