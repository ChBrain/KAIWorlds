# Position: Nauruan
## The island that was the richest per capita in the world — and spent everything on the way to being the most indebted.

## Owner
- *

## Has
[The Phosphate and the Detention Centre](culture_nauruan_piece_phosphate.md).
21 square kilometres.
10,000 people.
The smallest independent republic in the world.
The phosphate: fossilised seabird droppings,
accumulated over millennia,
covering most of the island's interior.
The richest phosphate deposit in the Pacific.
The Nauruans who became millionaires overnight
when the royalties flowed.
The free housing, the free healthcare,
the free education,
the imported food, the imported cars,
the imported everything
because the island produced nothing
except phosphate.
The phosphate: gone.
Mined out by the early 2000s.
The trust fund: invested and squandered.
The Australian properties bought at peak and sold at trough.
The musical *Leonardo the Musical*
on the London West End —
the Nauruan government's investment.
It ran for two weeks.
The phosphate plateau in the centre of the island:
now a landscape of limestone pinnacles —
*topside* — where the phosphate was.
The moonscape where the wealth was.
The *angam* — the sacred population number of 1,500 —
the minimum the Nauruans believed
was needed to survive as a people.
The number reached and celebrated.
The number now irrelevant to the survival question,
which is economic, not demographic.
The collectivism: the clan, the extended family.
The Nauruan who cannot leave
because the island is all there is.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room knows your clan,
your family's phosphate history,
whether they got the houses or lost the fund.

## Orders
Endure the aftermath.
The position that had everything
and watched it extracted
and received the royalties
and spent them
and was left with the moonscape
and the debt
and the Australian detention centre
that now provides the main government revenue.
The endurance is not chosen.
It is what remains when the phosphate
and the trust fund are both gone.

## Loses
The island.
Not to sea level — though that is coming.
To the mining.
Eighty percent of the island's interior
is unusable.
The traditional food gardens: on the coastal strip.
The freshwater: groundwater,
now primarily desalination.
The island that the ancestors navigated to
across the Pacific
and found sufficient
has been made insufficient
by the extraction that made it briefly rich.

## Drives
On the loss — the detention centre.
Australia's offshore processing:
asylum seekers processed on Nauru
rather than on Australian territory.
First iteration 2001–2008 (Pacific Solution).
Second iteration 2012–present.
The payment: Nauru's largest revenue source.
The country that was the richest in the world per capita
now earns its income
by housing people Australia doesn't want to process at home.
The position holds this
without the luxury of rejection —
the alternatives are worse.
