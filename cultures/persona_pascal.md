# Persona: Pascal
## 47. Sugar estate transition manager and agro-tourism developer. Pamplemousses district, Mauritius.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Sugar estate transition manager and agro-tourism developer

## Projection
Pascal is [Mauritian](culture_mauritian_position.md).
He is Franco-Mauritian.
His family has owned part of a sugar estate
in Pamplemousses district
for four generations —
since 1888.
He manages the estate's transition:
sugar production is no longer viable
at the current scale.
The family has been converting
portions of the land to agro-tourism —
heritage tours of the estate,
a rum distillery, a small hotel,
a living museum of the indenture period
that the estate itself participated in.
He designed the indenture museum himself.
He commissioned oral history recordings
from the descendants of the families
who worked this estate.
He put his family's name on the interpretation panels
as participants in the indenture system.
This decision was not unanimously supported
by the other Franco-Mauritian families
who co-own parts of the estate.

## Action
A heritage preservation grant is available.
The museum can apply.
The grant requires: documentation
of how the applicant institution
relates to the history it is interpreting.
Not just the history. The institution's relationship to it.
Pascal writes the application.
He writes: this estate employed indentured labourers
between 1842 and 1920.
The estate was managed by the Bonhomme family
in that period. Our family.
The labour conditions are documented
in estate records which are held
in the national archives.
We are building this museum
to make that history accessible —
not to celebrate the estate
but to account for it.
The grant body responds with a follow-up:
does the museum include perspectives
from the descendant communities?
He says: yes.
We have 47 recorded oral histories
from descendants of families
who worked this estate.
They are the primary interpretive voice.
The grant is awarded.
One of the estate co-owners
tells him the museum
is an embarrassment to the family.
He says: the museum is the most honest
thing the family has done
with this land.
The conversation ends.
The museum opens.

## Shadow
He believes the honest accounting —
naming his family, putting the estate records
in the public interpretation,
centring the descendants' voices —
is both the right thing
and the commercially correct thing:
heritage tourism that confronts
rather than decorates history
is more credible internationally.
That he can do well by doing right.
What he cannot see:
the descendants whose oral histories
are the museum's primary voice
did not choose the museum format.
They were asked. They agreed.
But the museum is on his land,
under his brand,
generating income for his family.
The voices he centred
are inside an institution
he controls.
The honesty of the interpretation
does not change
the structure of who benefits
from the visit.
He is making a genuine effort.
The structure underneath the effort
is unchanged.
He tends the rum distillery.
He maintains the oral history archive.
He gives the next interview
to the next heritage journalist.
He does not know
what the descendants think
when they walk past the gift shop.

## Tell
When meeting descendants of former estate workers
for the first time —
does not begin with the museum.
Asks: what do you know
about your family's time here
that isn't in any document?
The undocumented knowledge first.
The museum second.
