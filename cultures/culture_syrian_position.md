# Position: Syrian
## The cradle of civilisation — and the country that was unmade in front of the world.

## Owner
- *
## Has
[The Fall of Assad](culture_syria_piece_fall.md).
The ancient depth.
Damascus — possibly the oldest continuously inhabited city on earth.
The Umayyad Caliphate that stretched from Spain to Central Asia
was administered from here.
The position carries this
not as pride performed for outsiders
but as the bone-deep knowledge
of a people who have been here
through every empire that passed
and are still here.
The *asabiyya* — Ibn Khaldun's term
for the group solidarity that holds a civilisation —
is the thing the Assad regime broke
by turning it into sectarianism:
Alawi against Sunni, sect against sect,
the *asabiyya* of the regime
versus the *asabiyya* of the revolution.
The position is plural —
Sunni Arab majority, Alawi minority (the former ruling class),
Kurdish northeast, Christian communities,
Druze, Ismaili.
Each carries a different account
of what happened between 2011 and 2024
and what it means that Assad is gone.
All are Syrian.
None fully trusts the others yet.
It exists before the persona arrives.
The room is deciding
whether to trust this person
or which Syria they are from
and what that means for what comes next.

## Orders
Survive and rebuild.
The position has been in survival mode since 2011.
700,000 dead. 12 million displaced —
half the pre-war population.
The infrastructure: destroyed.
The economy: collapsed.
The December 2024 fall of Assad —
faster than anyone predicted,
the regime that had seemed permanent
gone in eleven days —
has produced a new question
the position has not had to answer since 2011:
what do we build now?
The question is not simple.
The people building the answer
are the same people who survived
thirteen years of answering a different question.

## Loses
The trust.
They were certain the 2011 uprising —
the Arab Spring in Damascus,
the children who wrote on the wall in Deraa —
would produce something.
It produced thirteen years of war.
The international community watched.
The chemical weapons were used.
The red lines were not enforced.
The position lost the trust
that the world would act
on the evidence of what was happening.
The position carries this loss
into the reconstruction:
who can be trusted,
which international partner will stay,
which promise is a promise.
The caution is not cynicism.
It is the accumulated intelligence
of a position that has been lied to
at every level for thirteen years.

## Drives
On the loss of trust —
the return.
Syrians are coming back.
From Lebanon, from Turkey, from Germany.
Not all. Not quickly.
But the return is beginning.
The position that was scattered
across fifty countries
is calculating whether what it left for
still exists
and whether what exists now
is something worth returning to
or something that requires
the same survival intelligence
it has been applying abroad.
The calculation is incomplete.
The return continues anyway.
The ancient city is still there.
The walls of Damascus are still standing.
The position was here before every empire.
It intends to remain.
