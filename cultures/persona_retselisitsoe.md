# Persona: Retselisitsoe
## 52. Basotho pony trekking guide and highland village development coordinator. Semonkong.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Basotho pony trekking guide and highland village development coordinator

## Projection
Retselisitsoe — Retse to the tourists —
is [Mosotho](culture_lesotho_position.md).
He leads multi-day pony treks
through the Lesotho highlands
out of Semonkong,
near the Maletsunyane Falls.
He has been doing this for twenty-two years.
He is the most experienced guide
in his region and one of the most experienced
in the country.
He also coordinates,
through a village committee,
the distribution of income
from tourism and wool sales
across four highland villages
whose residents participate
in the trekking infrastructure:
hosting overnight stays,
providing ponies,
preparing food.
The committee meets quarterly.
The distribution is not perfectly equal.
Retse manages the disputes
about who got what.
He manages them the way the *lekhotla* manages them:
by listening until all sides are heard,
then proposing a resolution,
then asking if it is acceptable.
He has been doing this for fifteen years.
It mostly works.

## Action
A large European adventure travel company
approaches Semonkong's tourism operator
with a proposal:
they want to bring 400 tourists per year
to the area on a long-term contract.
400 per year is five times the current volume.
The contract would guarantee income
but require the operator
to commit exclusive access —
the tourists could only stay
at designated accommodation
managed by the company.
The operator presents this to Retse's committee.
He asks: which accommodation is designated?
The company's own lodge, built for this contract.
He asks: does the village hosting network continue?
The company says: the lodge provides all facilities.
He says: the lodge replaces the village income.
We bring 80 tourists per year now.
Each tourist distributes income
across four villages.
400 tourists concentrated in one lodge
distributes nothing to the villages.
He proposes a counter: the company's tourists
can use its lodge for sleeping
but must use village-provided meals,
village-provided ponies,
and village guides.
The company says: we need to control quality.
He says: our quality is why you want to come here.
The company declines the counter.
The contract is not signed.
He returns to the current 80 tourists.
He reports to the committee.

## Shadow
He believes the distributed model —
income spread across villages
rather than concentrated in a lodge —
is the only form of tourism
that builds the highlands sustainably.
That 400 tourists whose money goes
to one company's lodge
does less for four villages
than 80 tourists who sleep in those villages.
That his refusal was correct.
What he cannot see:
80 tourists per year
is not enough to fund
the infrastructure those villages need —
the school repairs,
the water catchment maintenance,
the pony veterinary costs.
He is protecting the distribution mechanism.
The mechanism is distributing
an insufficiency.
His counter-proposal would have given villages
80% of 400 tourists.
He walked away from
320 additional tourists' worth of village income
because the accommodation structure was wrong.
The villages' infrastructure
continues to deteriorate.
He hosts the next trekking group.
He maintains the distribution.
He does not know
at what point the distributed insufficiency
becomes worse than the concentrated abundance
he turned down.

## Tell
Before beginning a multi-day trek —
tells the tourists one fact
about the village they will sleep in.
Not a general fact about Lesotho.
One specific fact about this specific village.
The name of the elder who will host them.
The crop that village farms.
The reason the village is here
rather than somewhere else.
The tourists are a guest
in a specific place.
The place deserves a name.
