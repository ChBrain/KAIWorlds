# Piece: Bourguiba's Bet and the Jasmine Revolution
## The modernisation that made the revolution possible — and the revolution that democracy couldn't hold.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Tunis](culture_tunisian_place_tunis.md)

## Place
Sidi Bouzid. A market town in the centre of Tunisia.
December 17, 2010.
Mohamed Bouazizi, twenty-six years old,
street vendor, his cart confiscated,
slapped by a municipal official.
He goes to the prefecture to complain.
He is ignored.
He buys paint thinner.
He stands in front of the prefecture.
He sets himself on fire.
He dies January 4, 2011.
The act of a man at the end of his dignity.
From Sidi Bouzid the protests spread.
Twenty-eight days.
Ben Ali flees to Saudi Arabia.
January 14, 2011.
The Jasmine Revolution.
The Arab Spring begins here.
In a market town in the centre of Tunisia
where nobody expected it.

## Load Bearing
Remove Bourguiba's modernisation
and the Jasmine Revolution is still possible
but it does not succeed.
What made Tunisia different from Egypt,
Libya, Syria, Yemen:
the middle class Bourguiba built,
the education system he funded,
the civil society organisations he allowed to exist
even within his autocracy,
the military he deliberately kept weak —
all of these are Bourguiba's legacy.
The protesters who toppled Ben Ali
were, disproportionately,
the educated sons and daughters
of the middle class Bourguiba created.
The Quartet that saved the democratic transition in 2013
consisted of organisations Bourguiba built or tolerated.
The 2014 constitution was written
by people educated in Bourguiba's universities.
Without Bourguiba, the Jasmine Revolution
might have happened.
It would not have had the infrastructure to hold.
With the revolution but without 2021:
the exception demonstrated.
With 2021:
the demonstration that the exception
requires permanent maintenance
and that the maintenance was insufficient.
Remove the piece: a North African state.
With it: the only Arab country
to complete a democratic transition
after the Arab Spring —
and then to partially dismantle it
from within the democratic system,
by a president elected through it.
The piece is load-bearing for both the achievement
and the failure of the achievement.
Both are the country now.

## Apparent
1956: independence. Bourguiba president.
The Personal Status Code: women's right to divorce,
end of polygamy, equal inheritance contested but advanced.
The most progressive family law in the Arab world.
1973: abortion legalised without restriction.
Before France. Before West Germany.
1987: Ben Ali removes Bourguiba
citing health — a bloodless palace coup.
Ben Ali continues the secular state.
Adds the police state.
2010: Tunisia's per capita income $4,100.
Unemployment 13%. Youth unemployment 30%.
The Arab Human Development Report ranks Tunisia high on education,
lower on political freedom and economic opportunity.
December 17, 2010: Bouazizi.
January 14, 2011: Ben Ali flees.
October 2011: first democratic elections.
Ennahda (Islamist) wins plurality.
2013: political assassinations. Crisis.
The Quartet convenes.
The National Dialogue.
The political parties negotiate a roadmap.
2014: the new constitution.
Ranked among the most progressive in the Arab world.
2015: Nobel Peace Prize to the Quartet.
2014–2021: democratic governance, contested, functioning.
July 2021: Saied suspends parliament.
Assumes emergency powers.
2022: new constitution.
Presidential system. Centralised.
The organisations of civil society:
diminished, surveilled, still present.
The argument: continuing.
