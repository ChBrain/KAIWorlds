## Shown
- Gorée Island. A short ferry from the mainland.
  The House of Slaves. The Door of No Return.
  The narrow doorway that opens to the sea.
  Through it, the Atlantic.
  The place where enslaved Africans were held
  before the crossing.
  UNESCO heritage site.
  Pilgrimage destination for the African diaspora —
  the most emotionally charged
  thirty minutes in West Africa.
  Senegal has made this memory
  part of its national offering.
  It does not hide it.
  It presents it as the door
  through which the world must pass
  to understand something about itself.
- The African Renaissance Monument.
  The bronze statue on the hill above Dakar.
  52 metres. Larger than the Statue of Liberty.
  Unveiled 2010.
  A man, a woman, a child.
  The man pointing toward the horizon.
  Controversial at its opening:
  too expensive, too secular,
  built by a North Korean company.
  Now: the skyline of Dakar.
  Present in every photograph of the city.
  Whether Senegalese like it or not —
  and opinions are divided —
  it is there.
  The ambition that insisted on the monument
  is the same ambition that produced
  the Dakar Biennale, the Youssou N'Dour concerts,
  the city that wants to be seen as a capital
  not just of Senegal but of African thought.
- The medina. The old city.
  The markets. The tailors.
  The mosques on every corner.
  The sound of the call to prayer
  and, minutes later, a church bell.
  The two sounds do not compete.
  They coexist with the specific Senegalese ease
  of a country where religious coexistence
  is not policy — it is practice,
  embedded over four hundred years.
- Sandaga Market and Plateau.
  The commercial centre.
  The *Mouride traders* who have their *dahira* networks here
  and in every city on earth where Senegalese live.
  The women who run the cloth stalls.
  The exchange of goods and gossip
  that constitutes the city's
  real information network.
- UCAD — the Université Cheikh Anta Diop.
  The intellectual heart of Francophone Africa.
  Where Diop argued that Egypt was Black.
  Where the generation that would argue about
  Africa's past and future
  has been trained since 1957.
  Currently: over 80,000 students.
  Chronically underfunded.
  Still the most important university
  in West Africa.
