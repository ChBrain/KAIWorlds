# Piece: Amílcar Cabral and the Coups
## The theorist of liberation who was killed before he could govern — and the country that inherited his name without his vision.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Bissau](culture_bissau_guinean_place_bissau.md)

## Place
Bissau. The capital.
The colonial Portuguese architecture, decaying.
The wide avenues that the colonial plan
did not complete.
The presidential palace that has changed hands
by coup more often than by election.
The mango trees the Portuguese planted
that the city grew around
and still shades.

## Load Bearing
Remove Cabral
and Guinea-Bissau is one of several
small West African states
that won independence in the 1970s wave
through armed struggle,
without the specific intellectual inheritance
of the most sophisticated anti-colonial thinker
Africa produced.
With it: a country whose founding myth
is explicit about what liberation requires —
not just political independence
but cultural reclamation,
not just the removal of the coloniser
but the construction of the self the coloniser suppressed.
*Unity and struggle* — *Unidade e Luta* —
the PAIGC motto.
Cabral's theory was that the African peasant,
dismissed by the coloniser as backward,
was in fact the revolutionary subject —
connected to the land, to the collective,
to the pre-colonial logic
that colonialism had tried to erase.
The guerrilla war proved him partly right:
the PAIGC controlled two-thirds of the country
before independence,
building parallel institutions
— schools, hospitals, courts —
in the liberated zones.
Remove this: a different liberation,
with different institutional inheritance.
With it: the irony that the most theoretically sophisticated
liberation movement in Africa
produced the least stable post-independence state.
The coups are the counter-piece:
1980: Nino Vieira overthrows Luís Cabral (Amílcar's half-brother).
1984: coup attempt suppressed.
1994: first multiparty elections.
1998: army rebellion. Civil war.
1999: Vieira overthrown.
2003: coup.
2005: Vieira returns via election.
2009: Vieira assassinated.
2010: coup attempt.
2012: coup.
2022: coup attempt.
Nine successful or attempted unconstitutional transfers of power
in fifty years of independence.
The PAIGC still exists as a political party.
Cabral's portrait is still on the currency.
The coups continue.
The portrait is printed by the government that follows each coup.

## Apparent
1956: Amílcar Cabral founds PAIGC in Bissau.
1963: armed liberation struggle begins.
PAIGC fights simultaneously for Guinea-Bissau and Cabo Verde.
Cabral builds liberated zones with parallel state functions.
January 1973: Cabral assassinated in Conakry.
Reportedly by a PAIGC member, with Portuguese intelligence links.
September 1974: Guinea-Bissau independent.
Luís Cabral, first president.
1980: Nino Vieira coup. Luís Cabral deposed.
Split between Guinea-Bissau and Cabo Verde branches.
PAIGC becomes PAIGC in Guinea-Bissau;
Cabo Verde renames its branch PAICV.
Subsequent coups: 1999, 2003, 2012.
2005–2009: Vieira returns, governs, assassinated.
2008: UN Office on Drugs and Crime documents
cocaine transit through Guinea-Bissau.
The Bijagós islands as narco-transit point.
Military involvement confirmed.
2014: José Mário Vaz elected.
2019: Umaro Sissoco Embaló elected.
2022: Embaló dissolves parliament following coup attempt.
Cabral's portrait on the 1000 CFA franc note.
The PAIGC offices: still operating in Bissau.
The theory: still cited in African political science courses.
The country: still the world's coup capital per capita.
