# Piece: The Mirabal Sisters, November 25, 1960
## The resistance that named what the hierarchy costs, and was murdered for naming it.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas
- **Place:** Santo Domingo

## Place
The Cordillera Septentrional.
Highway 5 between Puerto Plata and Santiago.
November 25, 1960.
A car. A cliff.
Three women. Staged as an accident.
The bodies of Patria, Minerva, and María Teresa Mirabal
were placed back in their car
and pushed over the ravine.
Their driver, Rufino de la Cruz, was also killed.
Dedé, the fourth sister, had not been in the car.
She lived until 2014.
She spent the rest of her life
as the keeper of what happened.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the Dominican PDI 65
is merely inherited hierarchy —
the authoritarian structure without its reckoning.
With the Mirabal sisters,
the position carries the knowledge
that the hierarchy has a price,
that the price has been named,
that the people who named it paid with their lives,
and that November 25 is now on the UN calendar:
the International Day for the Elimination of Violence Against Women,
established 1999, the date chosen in their honour.
The sisters are not background.
They are the position's shadow
made explicit and irreversible.
The Dominican pragmatism about authority —
the willingness to work within hierarchy,
improvise around it, respect *el jefe* as a form of realism —
carries, underneath, the specific knowledge
of what happens when hierarchy is absolute
and the cost falls on women.
The position does not proclaim this.
It holds it.

## Apparent
Patria, Minerva, and María Teresa Mirabal.
Known as *las Mariposas* — the Butterflies — their code names in the resistance.
Daughters of a family in Ojo de Agua, Salcedo province.
Minerva: the political one. Law degree. Trujillo's persistent antagonist.
She refused to dance with him at a state function.
She was imprisoned, released, imprisoned again.
Patria: the religious one. Came to resistance through faith.
María Teresa: the youngest. Followed her sisters.
They organized against Trujillo through the 14th of June Movement,
*El Movimiento 14 de Junio*.
November 25, 1960:
driving back from visiting their imprisoned husbands
in Puerto Plata.
Ambushed by the SIM — the Secret Service.
Strangled. Bodies placed in the car. Pushed off the cliff.
The official report: accident.
Everyone knew it was murder.
Trujillo was assassinated six months later, May 30, 1961.
Whether the Mirabal murders accelerated the conspiracy is disputed.
That they are connected is not.
Dedé, who survived:
ran the family farm, raised her sisters' children,
gave interviews, maintained the museum, bore witness.
She said she spent decades asking why she was not in the car.
She never stopped asking.
She died at 88.
The museum in Salcedo remains.
The date remains.

## Yearbook
1492: Columbus lands. Hispaniola named.
1496: Santo Domingo founded. First permanent European city in the Americas.
1697: Treaty of Ryswick. Spain cedes western third of Hispaniola to France.
1804: Haiti declares independence. Eastern Hispaniola remains under Spain, then briefly independent (1821), then under Haitian rule until:
1844: Dominican independence from Haiti. February 27. *La Trinitaria.*
1916–1924: US military occupation. Trujillo rises through the National Guard trained by US Marines.
1930: Trujillo seizes power. Begins thirty-one year rule.
1937: The Parsley Massacre. Trujillo orders the killing of Haitian migrants and Dominicans of Haitian descent in the border region. Estimated ten thousand dead. The shibboleth: *perejil* — parsley, a word Haitian Creole speakers could not pronounce with Dominican intonation. Pronunciation was the test.
1938: Trujillo offers land to European Jewish refugees fleeing Nazism. Eight hundred settle in Sosúa. The offer was primarily for image. The land was real.
1960: The Mirabal sisters imprisoned. Released. November 25: murdered.
1961: Trujillo assassinated, May 30.
1961–1966: Civil war, US military intervention, transition.
1966: Elections. Joaquín Balaguer — Trujillo's former puppet president — wins. Governs, with interruptions, until 1996.
1978: First peaceful transfer of power.
1999: UN designates November 25 International Day for the Elimination of Violence Against Women. The date chosen in honour of the Mirabals.
2013: Constitutional Tribunal ruling strips citizenship from Dominicans of Haitian descent. Anti-Haitianism, a Trujillo project, outlives him by half a century.
2020: Luis Abinader wins presidency; ends 16-year rule of Dominican Liberation Party.
The Malecón is still lit on weekends.
The Mirabals are still on the calendar.
Both things are true.
