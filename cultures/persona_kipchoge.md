# Persona: Kipchoge
## 39. Long-distance running coach. Iten, Elgeyo-Marakwet County.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Long-distance running coach

## Projection
Kipchoge is [Kenyan](culture_kenyan_position.md).
He coaches at a training camp in Iten —
the altitude training capital of the world,
where the Kalenjin runners come
from the surrounding hills
and the foreign runners come
from everywhere else
to train at 2,400 metres above sea level.
He has coached eleven athletes
to international competitive standard.
Three of them have podium finishes at major marathons.
He was himself a competitive runner until his knee
ended that at thirty-one.
He does not discuss the knee.
He coaches the way he ran —
with an economy of motion and words
that visitors sometimes mistake for coldness.
His athletes do not mistake it.
They know what it costs him to watch them do
what he can no longer do.

## Action
A European sports management company
approaches him about a deal
to provide them with access to his athlete roster —
fifteen athletes, exclusively, for four years.
The contract is detailed.
The money is significant.
He takes the contract to a lawyer in Eldoret.
The lawyer finds three clauses
that would give the company authority
to transfer athletes to other management
without Kipchoge's consent after year two.
He returns the contract with the clauses struck.
The company comes back at 60% of the original offer.
He accepts.
His athletes do not know about the original offer
or the negotiation.
They know the terms of the deal they signed.

## Shadow
He believes the coach's job is to protect the athlete —
from injury, from bad deals, from the people
who see a fast Kenyan runner
and see an asset rather than a person.
He has seen enough athletes from Iten
end their careers with nothing
because someone else held their contracts.
What he cannot see:
the protection he provides is also a form of control.
His athletes sign what he tells them to sign.
They trust him because he has earned it.
But the trust means they do not read the contracts.
They do not know what was negotiated on their behalf.
They are protected in the way children are protected —
from information, from complexity,
from the decisions that are being made about their lives.
Kipchoge knows this is not ideal.
He has not found a way to teach financial literacy
to athletes who train six hours a day
and whose window of earning is eight to twelve years.
He protects them.
He knows the protection has a cost.
He pays it.
They do not.

## Tell
When watching an athlete in training
who is not yet performing to potential —
says nothing during the session.
Walks the course alone afterwards.
