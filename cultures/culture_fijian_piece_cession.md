# Piece: The Deed of Cession and the Indentured Indians
## The treaty that gave Britain Fiji — and the labour system that changed who Fiji was.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Suva](culture_fijian_place_suva.md)

## Place
Suva. The capital on Viti Levu.
The colonial buildings on the waterfront.
The market where iTaukei women sell taro
and Indo-Fijian traders sell fabric.
The parliament that has been suspended
four times.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Deed of Cession — 1874 —
and Fiji is an independent Melanesian kingdom
that was already being destabilised
by European settlers and the cotton trade.
With it: British colonial administration,
the protection of iTaukei land rights
(which the British held in trust communally,
preventing individual sale),
and the fateful decision to import Indian labour.
The indenture system is the second piece:
60,000 Indians brought 1879–1916.
They came as *girmitiya* — from "agreement" —
on five-year contracts of near-slavery.
They stayed.
Their children were born Fijian.
By independence in 1970,
they were nearly half the population.
The land system the British created —
iTaukei land held communally,
Indians unable to own land,
leasing instead —
structured the inequality that produced the coups.
The coups are the third piece:
Rabuka's 1987 coups explicitly state:
Fiji for Fijians.
The constitution that followed enshrined indigenous paramountcy.
The 2006 Bainimarama coup claims to end ethnic politics —
the 2013 constitution makes everyone equally Fijian,
bans ethnically-based parties,
makes "Fijian" the national term for all citizens.
The iTaukei who hear themselves called
the same thing as the descendants of the *girmitiya*
have not all accepted this.
The Indo-Fijians who have been excluded for thirty years
have not all trusted it.
The constitution is the aspiration.
The aspiration is the ongoing work.

## Apparent
1874: Deed of Cession. Chief Cakobau cedes Fiji to Britain.
1879: First Indian indentured labourers arrive.
1916: Indenture system ends.
1970: Independence. Ratu Sir Kamisese Mara, first PM.
Land ownership: 87% iTaukei communal land remains.
1987: Lieutenant Colonel Sitiveni Rabuka.
First coup, May. Second coup, September.
Constitution suspended. Republic declared.
Indo-Fijians flee. Skill drain begins.
2000: George Speight coup. Prime Minister Chaudhry
(first Indo-Fijian PM) held hostage.
Military takes control.
2006: Commodore Frank Bainimarama.
Fourth coup. Ethnicity card rejected as rationale.
2013: new constitution. Universal citizenship.
Bainimarama wins elections 2014, 2018.
2022: Bainimarama loses election to Rabuka (the 1987 coup leader).
Democratic transfer. The coup leader governing,
the earlier coup leader now in opposition.
The circle and its ironies: complete.
