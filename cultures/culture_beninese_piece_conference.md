# Piece: The Conférence Nationale of 1990
## The sovereign national conference that ended a dictatorship — the model Africa exported.

## Owner
- **Place:** [Cotonou / Porto-Novo](culture_beninese_place_cotonou.md)

## Place
Cotonou. The Palais des Congrès.
February 19 to February 28, 1990.
The representatives of every political tendency
in Benin — the banned parties, the religious leaders,
the unions, the civil society —
convened in one hall.
Mathieu Kérékou — the Marxist-Leninist president —
sat in the room.
The conference voted to declare itself sovereign.
To override him.
He accepted.
Ten days.
The country changed.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Conférence Nationale
and Benin is another small West African state
attempting a transition from dictatorship
through the mechanisms available in 1990:
IMF pressure, civil unrest, donor conditionality.
With it: Benin is the country
that produced a template —
a model of transition that was observed,
studied, and replicated across Francophone Africa.
The Congo, Chad, Mali, Togo, Niger, Burkina Faso —
all held *conférences nationales*
in the years that followed.
None were as clean as Benin's.
Some were reversed.
But the idea —
that citizens could convene,
declare themselves sovereign,
and negotiate a transition
without violence —
came from Cotonou.
And the most important element:
Kérékou accepted.
He lost power. He accepted.
He ran in elections.
He won in 1996. He served.
He accepted the term limit in 2006.
The man who accepted the conference's authority in 1990
was also the man who accepted
democratic term limits in 2006.
The model worked
because the man it was applied to
chose not to resist it.
This was not guaranteed.
It was a choice.
Remove the piece: a transition by other means.
With it: the proof of concept for peaceful African democratic transition —
and the question the rest of the continent
has been working on since:
can you make it structural rather than personal?

## Apparent
1972: Mathieu Kérékou seizes power.
1974: Benin declared a Marxist-Leninist state.
The People's Republic of Benin.
Single party. Nationalisation.
1980s: economic decline. IMF pressure.
Structural adjustment.
Strikes. Civil society pressure.
1989: Kérékou abandons Marxism-Leninism.
February 19, 1990: the Conférence Nationale begins.
Kérékou agrees to attend.
The conference declares itself sovereign.
A transitional government is installed.
Nicéphore Soglo becomes Prime Minister.
March 1991: presidential elections.
Soglo wins. Kérékou concedes.
First peaceful transfer of presidential power
in Francophone Africa.
1996: Kérékou wins back the presidency.
Soglo concedes.
Second transfer.
2001: Kérékou re-elected.
2006: term-limited. Cannot run.
Yayi Boni wins.
Third transfer.
2011: Yayi wins again.
2016: Patrice Talon wins.
Yayi concedes.
The pattern: held.
Note: since 2018, Talon has changed election laws
significantly limiting opposition participation.
2021 elections held with major opposition parties excluded.
The democracy is under pressure.
The model is not eternal.
It requires maintenance.
The maintenance is currently insufficient.
The achievement remains real.
The threat to it is also real.
