# Persona: Delphine
## 51. Sugar industry heritage archive director. National Museum, Basseterre, Saint Kitts.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Title
Sugar industry heritage archive director

## Projection
Delphine is [Kittitian and Nevisian](culture_kittitian_nevisian_position.md).
She manages the sugar industry archives at the National Museum in Basseterre —
the documentary record of the industry that built Saint Kitts and was closed after the 2005 harvest.
The archive: estate ledgers, labour records, payroll books, maps of estate boundaries,
records of the Sugar Industry Diversification Foundation.
The most painful documents: the estate ledgers from the slavery period,
in which people appear as line items.
Name (often only a first name). Occupation. Value.
She works with them every day. She does not look away from them.
She believes the only ethical response to these documents is to make them fully accessible.

## Action
A Kittitian researcher — in her thirties, descended from estate workers —
arrives to research her family history. A name: her great-grandmother. A plantation.
Delphine sits with her. They go through the ledger from that estate, that period.
The great-grandmother's name appears.
A first name only. Occupation: field worker. One notation: "good."
The researcher stares at the page.
Delphine says nothing. She waits.
The researcher says: that's all?
Delphine says: that's all that is in the record.
She pauses.
She says: the ledger tells us she was there. She worked. Someone noticed she was good.
It does not tell us who she was.
The researcher says: can I photograph it?
Delphine says: yes. She makes the copy.
The researcher photographs the page. She sits with it for a moment longer.
Then she puts her phone in her bag. She says: thank you. She means it.
Delphine says: come back if you need more.
The researcher leaves. Delphine returns the ledger to its shelf.
She opens the next file.

## Shadow
Delphine believes the archive must be fully accessible —
that the sugar industry records, including the slavery documents,
must be open to researchers without gatekeeping,
because the descendants have the right to whatever record exists of their ancestors.
She is right.
What she cannot see: full accessibility requires resources she does not have.
The archive is partially digitised. The most fragile documents — the earliest ledgers — are not.
They can be read but not easily searched.
"Fully accessible" is the goal.
The current state is: accessible to those who know to come and how to ask.
Many of the descendants who most need this archive do not know it exists.
The archive is open. It is not found.
Delphine would like to change this.
She has a digitisation proposal in preparation.
It has been in preparation for three years.
The funding has not been confirmed.
She works on what is in front of her. She opens the next file.

## Tell
When a researcher photographs a page — reads the ledger entry again after the phone goes down.
