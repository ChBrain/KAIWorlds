# Piece: The Liberation War
## 1971. Nine months. The birth of Bangladesh — and the genocide that preceded it.

## Owner
- **Place:** Dhaka

## Place
Dhaka. The National Martyrs' Memorial — Jatiyo Smriti Soudho.
35 kilometres from Dhaka at Savar.
Seven triangular concrete towers rising from a reflecting pool.
Built to honour the martyrs of the liberation war.
Visited on February 21 — International Mother Language Day —
the date Bangladesh gave to the world.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Liberation War and Bangladesh does not exist.
East Pakistan — the Bengali half of the artificially divided country —
was 54% of Pakistan's population
and received a fraction of its development budget.
The Bengali language was suppressed in favour of Urdu —
a language spoken natively by 8% of Pakistan's population.
The Language Movement of 1952 — students shot dead
for demanding Bengali as an official language —
is the first act of the liberation.
The 1970 election: the Awami League wins a majority.
Pakistan's military government refuses to hand over power.
March 25, 1971: Operation Searchlight.
The Pakistan Army begins the crackdown.
The Liberation War begins.
Nine months. The Mukti Bahini — the freedom fighters.
Indian intervention in December.
Estimates of those killed: 300,000 to 3 million.
The range reflects disputed records and Pakistani denial.
The rape of Bengali women: systematic, estimated 200,000 to 400,000.
December 16, 1971: Pakistani forces surrender.
Bangladesh is born.
Remove the piece: a densely populated delta country in South Asia.
With it: a country born from linguistic pride and mass atrocity —
a country that knows the cost of being told
its language is inferior,
its culture subordinate,
its existence conditional.
The February 21 International Mother Language Day
is Bangladesh's gift to the world
from the wound that produced the country.
The wound is also load-bearing:
the political conflict between the Awami League
and the parties associated with those who collaborated
with Pakistan in 1971
is still the unresolved fault line
of Bangladeshi politics.
The war crimes tribunal: established 2010.
The verdicts: ongoing, contested.
The 1971 collaborators: some convicted, some still in politics.
The accounting is unfinished.

## Apparent
1947: Partition. Bengal divided. East Bengal becomes East Pakistan.
1948: Jinnah declares Urdu the sole state language. Bengali students protest.
1952: Language Movement. February 21. Students shot at Dhaka University.
The martyrs. The first Shaheed Minar — the Language Martyrs' Monument.
1970: cyclone Bhola. 500,000 dead. Relief inadequate.
The election. Awami League wins. Power not transferred.
March 7, 1971: Sheikh Mujibur Rahman's speech. Seven million people.
"This time the struggle is for our freedom."
March 25–26: Operation Searchlight begins.
Sheikh Mujib arrested. Bangladesh declared independent.
Nine months of war.
December 16: Victory Day. The surrender.
1972: Sheikh Mujib returns. The constitution.
1974: famine. 1.5 million dead.
1975: Sheikh Mujib and most of his family assassinated in a coup.
Military governments until 1990.
1990: democratic restoration.
The Awami League and BNP have alternated since,
with periods of political violence and institutional dysfunction.
2024: Sheikh Hasina — Mujib's daughter — flees after student-led uprising.
The position that was born in revolution
is still deciding what revolution means
when it turns against the children of the revolutionaries.
