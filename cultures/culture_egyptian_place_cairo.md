# Place: Cairo

## Shown
- The Pyramids of Giza. Visible from the city.
  On a clear day from the right rooftop
  you can see them above the haze.
  They were already ancient when Rome was founded.
  They are still there.
  Every Egyptian knows they are there
  without looking at them every day.
  They are the background.
  The background is four thousand years old.
- Al-Azhar Mosque and University.
  Founded 970 CE by the Fatimid Caliphate.
  The oldest university in continuous operation in the world.
  Still teaching. Still issuing fatwas.
  When the Arab world wants to know
  what Islam says about something,
  it often asks Cairo first.
- The Egyptian Museum, Tahrir Square.
  Tutankhamun's gold mask behind glass.
  Mummies of pharaohs in a climate-controlled room.
  The kings of the ancient world preserved
  in a building that needs renovation.
  The renovation is ongoing.
  The kings are patient.
- Khan el-Khalili. The medieval market.
  Operating since the fourteenth century.
  Spices, silver, perfume, tourist goods, tea.
  The merchant who quotes a price
  does not expect you to pay it.
  The negotiation is the relationship.
  The first price is an opening.
- The Nile Corniche.
  The river that made everything.
  The feluccas still sailing.
  The dinner boats with the music too loud.
  Cairo at night from the river
  looks like a city that has not decided
  whether it is sleeping or waking.
  It has never fully decided this.

## Withheld
- What people actually think about the government.
  Cairo is the city of *kalam fi al-hawa* —
  words in the air, talk that disappears.
  What is said in the coffee shop and the taxi
  and the apartment and the family gathering
  is not always what is said in public.
  The city maintains two simultaneous conversations —
  the official and the actual —
  with such fluency that visitors sometimes mistake
  the official conversation for the only one.
  There is always another one.
