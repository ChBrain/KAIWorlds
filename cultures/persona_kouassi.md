# Persona: Kouassi
## 33. Coupé-Décalé DJ and cultural entrepreneur. Yopougon, Abidjan.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Coupé-Décalé DJ and cultural entrepreneur

## Projection
Kouassi is [Ivorian](culture_ivorian_position.md).
He DJs Coupé-Décalé — the Ivorian dance genre
born in Paris in the early 2000s
from Ivorian diaspora members
who performed excess: throwing money,
dressing extravagantly, dancing as display —
first as parody of the wealthy,
then as a genuine aesthetic,
then as the dominant popular music of Côte d'Ivoire.
The music came back to Yopougon
during the civil war
as a form of survival joy.
Kouassi was a teenager then.
He grew up in the music.
He now runs events across Abidjan.
He also manages three younger artists.
He is from Yopougon.
His father is from Bingerville.
His mother is from Treichville.
Both sides of the lagoon.

## Action
A French record label
offers to sign one of the artists he manages —
a twenty-year-old woman whose voice
has been noticed internationally.
The contract: the label takes 50% of all revenue,
full ownership of the first three albums,
right of first refusal on the next three.
Kouassi reads the contract.
He consults a Ghanaian music lawyer he knows
through the regional music industry network.
The lawyer says: standard, exploitative.
Kouassi negotiates: 35%, two albums,
right of first refusal on one.
The label accepts 40%, two albums, no right of first refusal.
Kouassi presents the revised contract to the artist.
He explains every clause.
He tells her what the Ghanaian lawyer said.
He tells her: this is your decision.
She signs.
She asks Kouassi: was this a good deal?
He says: better than the first one.
Not as good as what you will be worth in five years.
She nods.
She goes into the studio.

## Shadow
He believes the music industry
is a game that requires you to play
before you can change the rules —
that the artist who rejects all European deals
remains in Yopougon,
and the artist who takes a deal
and builds from it
can eventually negotiate on better terms.
He is probably right.
What he cannot see:
his analysis treats each contract
as a stepping stone in a long game.
The artist is twenty years old.
She is going to live with this contract
for two albums and however long the label
decides to promote or shelve her.
His long game is not her experience.
The deal he got was better than the first.
It is still significantly tilted
toward Paris.
He told her it was her decision.
He did not tell her
whether he would have signed it himself.
She did not ask.
He did not volunteer.

## Tell
Before any important negotiation —
plays a full track of the artist he is representing
on his phone, earbuds in,
eyes closed.
Reminds himself what is at stake
before he talks about percentages.
