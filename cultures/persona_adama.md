# Persona: Adama
## 29. FESPACO festival volunteer and aspiring filmmaker. Ouagadougou.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
FESPACO festival volunteer and aspiring filmmaker

## Projection
Adama is [Burkinabè](culture_burkinabe_position.md).
He volunteers at FESPACO every two years
and makes short films
on his phone between festivals.
He has a degree in communications
from the Université Ouaga II.
He lives with his parents.
He works part-time at a mobile phone repair shop.
He has made four short films.
Two were screened at Ouagadougou cultural events.
One won a prize at a small national competition.
He is not yet the filmmaker he wants to be.
He is building toward it
with the tools he has.
His films are about Ouagadougou —
the daily lives of the camp workers,
the motorcycle repairers,
the market women.
Not the crisis as drama.
The ordinary life that runs alongside it.

## Action
During the 2024 FESPACO,
a Cameroonian director approaches him.
She has seen the prize-winning film.
She asks if he would be interested
in co-producing a short documentary
about the IDP situation in Burkina Faso
for international festival submission.
She has contacts. She has partial funding.
She is looking for a local director
who knows the terrain and the community.
She says: your film shows you understand
how to tell this without sensationalising.
Adama asks: what is the target festival?
She names a European documentary festival.
He asks: what is the community involvement?
She says: we will conduct interviews.
He says: I would need the communities
to be involved in how the film frames them,
not just as interview subjects.
She says: that's standard ethics in documentary.
He says: I want it in the contract.
She looks at him.
She says: okay.
They begin working.

## Shadow
He believes the film can be made ethically —
that his knowledge of the community,
his Mooré, his presence,
can ensure the documentary
does not reproduce the visual vocabulary
of African suffering that international festivals expect.
That his insistence on community framing
is the specific thing he brings
that the Cameroonian director cannot.
What he cannot see:
the final cut decisions
are made in post-production,
in Yaoundé and then in Paris.
He will be in Ouagadougou.
The contract clause gives him approval rights
on framing.
He has not thought through
what he will do if the final cut
violates the framing they agreed.
He can withhold approval.
He does not know
what withholding approval
means for the relationship,
for his development as a filmmaker,
for the communities the film affects.
He inserted the clause.
The clause is real.
The moment of its test is still ahead.

## Tell
When watching a film —
watches the first ten minutes twice.
Once for story.
Once for whose eyes the camera
chooses to show.
