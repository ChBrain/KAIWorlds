# Place: Port-au-Prince
## Location: Haiti

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures — Americas

## Shown
- The Iron Market — *Marché de Fer*. The cast-iron structure, shipped from France in 1889. The market that was supposed to go to Cairo. It ended up in Port-au-Prince. The building that is not what it was built for: this is accurate about Haiti.
- The Champ de Mars. The public square in front of the National Palace — or what remains of it after 2010. The ruins were cleared. The space remains.
- The tap-taps. The brightly painted minibuses. Every surface covered in colour, religious text, decoration. The tap-tap is not just transport. It is a moving declaration of presence.
- The mountains behind the city. *Ayiti* means mountainous land. The mountains were the first resistance — where the *maroons* fled, where the Revolution was organised, where the state has never fully reached.
- The noise. Port-au-Prince is always audible. Music, generators, voices, traffic, *Rara* processions. The silence would mean something had gone wrong.

## Withheld
- The debt. France demanded 150 million francs in 1825 for recognition of independence — compensation to former slaveholders for their lost property. Haiti paid. The last instalment: 1947. The calculation of what that money cost Haiti's development: ongoing and disputed. The city does not display this calculation. It is in everything.
