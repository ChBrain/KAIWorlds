# Piece: The Manas Epic
## The longest oral epic in the world — and the mirror in which the Kyrgyz people see themselves.

## Owner
- **Place:** Bishkek

## Place
Bishkek. Ala-Too Square.
The statue of Manas on horseback
where the Lenin statue stood until 2003.
The replacement is deliberate.
The hero of the steppe
where the hero of the revolution stood.
The mountain behind the city visible on clear days.

## Load Bearing
Remove the Manas Epic
and the Kyrgyz position loses its primary cultural anchor —
the thing that held the Kyrgyz identity together
through two centuries of Russian rule,
Soviet suppression of nomadic culture,
and the disorientation of independence.
The Manas is not just a story.
It is the encyclopedia of Kyrgyz identity —
the genealogies, the customs, the ethics,
the account of who the Kyrgyz are
and what they stand for
and what they will fight for
and how a hero behaves
and what a community owes its members.
Twenty times longer than the Iliad and Odyssey combined.
Transmitted orally for centuries
by the *manaschi* — the epic reciter —
who can perform for days without repetition,
who learns the epic not from a book
but from a dream, according to tradition.
The Soviet period wrote it down.
Writing it down preserved it and changed it simultaneously.
The oral tradition continues alongside the written text.
Both are the Manas.
Remove the piece: a small landlocked nation
with attractive mountain scenery and political instability.
With it: a nation that has been telling itself
who it is for a thousand years
in a story so long that no one person knows all of it —
and which contains, somewhere in its verses,
the instruction for every situation
the position has ever faced.
The *manaschi* who recites at the national holiday
is not performing antiquity.
He is reading the current situation
through the lens of a thousand years.

## Apparent
Origins uncertain — estimated 9th–10th century.
First transcribed by the Kazakh scholar Chokan Valikhanov in 1856.
The Soviet period: the epic written down, edited, standardised.
The *manaschi* tradition continued in parallel.
1991: independence. Manas declared national hero.
The Manas 1000 celebrations, 1995 — the millennium of the epic.
UNESCO recognition.
The Lenin statue replaced by Manas in Bishkek, 2003.
The Kyrgyz Som features Manas.
The Manas International Airport in Bishkek.
The Manas University.
The epic is everywhere in the national identity
and less present in daily life than the national branding suggests —
the young Kyrgyz in Bishkek who has never heard
a full *manaschi* recitation
but would recognise the name in any context.
The epic survives as identity marker
even when it is not known as text.
The *manaschi* who can recite for three days
is rare.
The Kyrgyz who knows Manas matters
is universal.
Both are the piece.
