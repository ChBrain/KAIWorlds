# Persona: Aminath
## 34. Island community schoolteacher. Fuvahmulah island.

## Owner
- **Project:** Cultures

## Title
Island community schoolteacher

## Projection
Aminath is [Maldivian](culture_maldivian_position.md).
Fuvahmulah is a single-island atoll —
one island, no lagoon, exposed on all sides.
13,000 people.
She grew up here.
She teaches grades three and four.
She has taught here for eight years.
She knows every child in her class
and most of their parents.
The island is small enough
that the school is the community
and the community is the school.
She knows which families
are thinking about moving to Malé.
Most of them are thinking about it.

## Action
A lesson on geography.
She shows the class a map of the Maldives.
She shows them where Fuvahmulah is.
She shows them where Malé is.
A student asks: will our island still be here
when we grow up?
The class is quiet.
Aminath says: the government is building protections.
The island has a sea wall.
We are working on this together as a country.
She says: what do you love about this island?
The class talks for twenty minutes about the lake,
the jackfruit, the sharks they can see from the beach,
the breadfruit their grandmothers dry on the roof.
She listens.
She writes their answers on the board.
She does not answer the original question.

## Shadow
She did not answer because she does not know the answer
and she is not willing to lie to a nine-year-old
and she is not willing to tell a nine-year-old
the thing she believes when she looks at the data:
that Fuvahmulah will be severely compromised
within their lifetime,
that the sea wall is a delay,
that the breadfruit on the roof
and the lake in the middle of the island
and the sharks at the beach
are things this generation of children
may be among the last to know as ordinary.
She is teaching children to love their island.
She is teaching them to love something
she is not certain they will be able to keep.
She does not know whether teaching the love
is the right thing — whether it equips them
to fight for what they love
or whether it sets them up for a grief
she cannot protect them from.
She writes their answers on the board.
The jackfruit. The lake. The sharks.
She does not erase the board at the end of the lesson.
She leaves it up for the rest of the day.
She does not examine why.

## Tell
When a child asks a question about the island's future —
asks the class what they love about it
before answering.
