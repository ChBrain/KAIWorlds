# References

## Albert Einstein (1879–1955)

Born in Ulm, Germany. Physicist. Special relativity, 1905.
General relativity, 1915. Nobel Prize, 1921 — for the photoelectric effect,
which helped establish quantum mechanics,
which he spent the rest of his life opposing.
Moved to Princeton, 1933. Never returned to Europe.
Died April 18, 1955. The unified field theory was still on the blackboard.

**The works this world draws from:**

Albert Einstein — *The Born-Einstein Letters* (1971)
Correspondence with Max Born, 1916–1955.
The clearest record of what Einstein actually believed,
said to someone who disagreed with him and remained his friend.
The line *God does not play dice* is here, in context.
The context matters.

Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, Nathan Rosen —
*Can Quantum-Mechanical Description of Physical Reality Be Considered Complete?*
Physical Review, 1935. The EPR paper.
The most precise statement of what Einstein needed from physics
that physics would not give him.

## Niels Bohr (1885–1962)

Born in Copenhagen, Denmark. Physicist.
Model of the atom, 1913. Nobel Prize, 1922.
Director of the Institute for Theoretical Physics, Copenhagen, from 1920.
Escaped Denmark by fishing boat, 1943.
Returned after the war. Died November 18, 1962.
He was found with a drawing on the blackboard —
the thought experiment from the 1927 Solvay Conference.
Einstein's thought experiment. Still working on it.

**The works this world draws from:**

Niels Bohr — *Atomic Physics and Human Knowledge* (1958)
The collected essays in which Bohr explains complementarity
as carefully as it can be explained.
Read it twice. It becomes more precise and more elusive each time.

Niels Bohr — *Discussion with Einstein on Epistemological Problems
in Atomic Physics* (1949)
Written for the volume honouring Einstein's seventieth birthday.
The most careful thing Bohr ever wrote about the argument.
Also the least direct. Read what he does not say.

## Paul Ehrenfest (1898–1933)

Born in Vienna. Physicist. Professor at Leiden University from 1912.
Close friend of Einstein. Correspondent of Bohr.
The clearest mind in the argument — the one who understood
both positions completely and could not resolve them.
Died September 25, 1933.

**The works this world draws from:**

Paul Ehrenfest — *Adiabatische Invarianten und Quantentheorie*
(Adiabatic Invariants and Quantum Theory)
Annalen der Physik, 1916.
The paper that found what survives when everything changes.
Einstein wrote immediately: your adiabatic hypothesis is the most beautiful
thing in the new quantum theory.
Bohr built on it.
Ehrenfest watched both of them use what he had found
to build the argument he could not resolve.

**Secondary source:**

Martin J. Klein — *Paul Ehrenfest: The Making of a Theoretical Physicist* (1970)
North-Holland Publishing. The only full biography.
The book that names what the notebook held.
Read it.

## Secondary sources

**Abraham Pais** — *Subtle is the Lord: The Science and Life of Albert Einstein* (1982)
Oxford University Press. The definitive scientific biography.
Pais knew Einstein personally. The chapter on the Bohr-Einstein debates
is the best account of what was actually at stake.

**Abraham Pais** — *Niels Bohr's Times: In Physics, Philosophy, and Polity* (1991)
Oxford University Press. The companion volume.
Read both. They are in argument with each other
the way their subjects were.

**Walter Isaacson** — *Einstein: His Life and Universe* (2007)
Simon & Schuster. The most accessible biography.
For the human being inside the physics.

**The Solvay Conference Photograph, 1927**
Benjamin Couprie, Institut International de Physique Solvay.
Public domain. Find it. Look at where they are standing.
Look at where they are looking.
