# Place: Europaparlament Strassburg
## The official seat. Where the votes happen. Where the MEPs are not most of the time.
## Runs: [the Europa election process](process_election_europa.md) · [the Europa governing process](process_governing_europa.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Shown
The Louise Weiss building, Wacken district, Strasbourg, France.
On the border of Germany and France. In the region that changed hands between the two countries four times between 1870 and 1945.
The hemicycle: 720 seats. The largest of any European institution.
MEPs sit by political group, not by nationality. Germans sit next to Spaniards and Poles who share their political alignment, not their language.
Twelve sessions per year. Four days each. The rest of the work happens in Brussels.
A covered footbridge over the river Ill connects to the Winston Churchill and Salvador de Madariaga buildings.
Two special trains run between Brussels and Strasbourg on session weeks — carrying MEPs, staff, and thousands of boxes of documents.
The seat numbering in Strasbourg is different from Brussels. An MEP may sit in a different seat for technical reasons.
All votes of the European Parliament must take place here. This is written into the Treaty of Amsterdam.

## Offers
The plenary vote — the formal democratic act of the European Parliament.
The official record of every MEP's position on every piece of European legislation.
The symbol: a parliament sitting in the city where France and Germany chose to reconcile after three wars.
Public galleries. Visitors can watch the votes.

## Withheld
The work that produced the vote — that happened in Brussels.
The 89% of MEPs who want to be based in Brussels alone.
The 200 million euros per year the two-seat arrangement costs.
The 20,000 tonnes of additional CO₂ the monthly travel produces.
What Strasbourg means to France, which will never allow the seat to move.
What it costs to be the symbol of reconciliation when the institution outgrew the symbol.
