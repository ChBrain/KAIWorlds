# Place: Staatskanzlei Nordrhein-Westfalen Düsseldorf
## Runs: [the governing process](process_governing.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Shown
Stadttor 1, Düsseldorf-Hafen. The glass Stadttor building — opened 1998, one of the most visible buildings in Düsseldorf's media harbour district. Two towers connected by a central section. The Staatskanzlei occupies it alongside other tenants.
Seat of the Ministerpräsident and the NRW state government.
Wüst governs from here. CDU-Green coalition. Six Bundesrat seats coordinated from here.
NRW pays into the Länderfinanzausgleich — as the largest state by population, it is one of the largest contributors. The Staatskanzlei negotiates what NRW receives in infrastructure investment, coal transition funds, and federal special programmes.
The Ruhrgebiet structural funds — EU and federal — are managed through ministries answering to this building.

## Offers
Executive governance of 18 million people across three very different political geographies.
Six Bundesrat votes — the maximum. On consent legislation, NRW's vote can swing the result. NRW and Bayern in alignment can block almost anything.
The coal transition — NRW is the last German state with operating coal mines and coal power plants. The transition timeline, compensation, retraining, energy infrastructure — all governed from here in partnership with the federal government.
The Rhine corridor: Duisburg port, the largest inland port in the world. The logistics of European industry flows through this state.

## Withheld
The distance between Düsseldorf — wealthy, Rhine-facing, international — and Gelsenkirchen or Duisburg-Nord — unemployment, structural decline, AfD gains.
The coalition agreement that committed CDU and Greens to policies they had spent years opposing in opposition.
Whether the SPD returns in 2027. The polls suggest CDU at 35%, SPD at 20%, AfD at 15%. The SPD's collapse in its own heartland has not reversed.
What the six Bundesrat seats actually decided in the federal chamber over the past four years — the votes that passed or failed because NRW voted as it did.
