# Position: Mitglied des Europäischen Parlaments — Deutschland

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections
- **Process:** [the Europa election process](process_election_europa.md)
- **Process:** [the Europa governing process](process_governing_europa.md)
- **Place:** [Europaparlament Strasbourg](place_europaparlament_strassburg.md)
- **Place:** [Europaparlament Brussels](place_europaparlament_bruessel.md)

## Has
One of 96 German seats in the European Parliament — the largest national contingent, representing 84 million Germans among 720 MEPs from 27 member states.
Membership of a European political group — not the German party but the cross-national bloc: EPP (CDU/CSU), S&D (SPD), Greens/EFA (Greens), ECR or ESN (AfD), GUE/NGL (Linke), Renew (FDP when present).
Two working locations: Strasbourg for 12 plenary sessions per year, Brussels for committee work and the rest.
The five-year mandate — from the June 2024 election to the June 2029 election.
The committee assignment — where the actual legislative work happens. Agriculture, environment, trade, budgets, foreign affairs: committee membership determines what legislation the position can actually shape.
The constituency: Germany as a whole. Unlike Bundestag members, MEPs have no individual Wahlkreis. They represent the national list result, not a geographic district.

## Orders
Legislate in the EP's domains — agriculture, trade, environment, consumer protection, single market, digital regulation, migration at European level.
Work within the European political group rather than the German party — the EPP contains CDU/CSU alongside Italian, French, and Polish conservatives whose positions on agriculture, rule of law, and environmental policy diverge significantly. The German MEP navigates this.
Attend Strasbourg. The monthly plenary is in Strasbourg because France insisted in the Treaty of Amsterdam and has not relented. The position travels between Brussels (where committees meet) and Strasbourg (where votes happen) on a permanent rotation that most MEPs consider inefficient and that no member state has been able to change.
Explain the European Parliament to German voters who mostly pay it limited attention outside election years.

## Loses
The direct mandate.
Bundestag members represent a constituency. German MEPs represent a national list. The accountability relationship is between the MEP and the party that placed them on the list, not between the MEP and a geographic community. The position is more dependent on party hierarchy and less dependent on voters than any state or federal elected office.
The proximity.
Brussels and Strasbourg are not Berlin. The MEP works in buildings most German voters have never entered, on legislation most German voters cannot name, in committees whose decisions shape daily life through regulations that arrive without a German political moment attached. The position has real power and limited visibility.
The national agenda.
55% of German EP voters in 2024 voted on Bundespolitik rather than Europapolitik. The position is elected partly by people who are expressing an opinion about something else.

## Drives
On the loss of direct mandate —
builds profile through committee work — becoming the person who is recognised in a specific domain: agriculture, digital regulation, trade. The position's European influence is narrow but real within that domain.
Uses the German contingent's size as leverage — 96 of 720 seats. German MEPs in the EPP, S&D, and Greens/EFA groups are often pivotal in cross-group negotiations because of their number.
Maintains the German party link while building European relationships — the EPP German delegation does not vote as a German bloc but as an EPP bloc. The position must serve both.
