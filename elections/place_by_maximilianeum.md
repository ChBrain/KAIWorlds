## Shown
Maximilianstraße 1, München. Stadtteil Haidhausen. Eastern bank of the Isar, above the Maximiliansbrücke.
Built 1857–1874 by Friedrich Bürklein for King Maximilian II — as a gallery, a school of history, a home for gifted students. Not designed as a parliament. Never intended as one.
The Landtag has rented it from the Studienstiftung Maximilianeum since 1949. The state does not own the building it governs from. The foundation does. Stipendiaten still live in it.
203 seats. Majority: 102. Current composition: CSU 85, Freie Wähler 37, Greens 32, AfD 32, SPD 17.
The Plenarsaal was renovated in 2004–2005. Glass ceiling, pale wood, curved seating rows. Much light. The building behind it — the working extensions of 1958, 1964, 1992, 2012 — is deliberately invisible from the front.
The Kreuzgang holds biographical records of 316 members of Bavarian parliaments who suffered under the Nazi dictatorship. Searchable at a terminal since 2008.
The Bundesrat delegation: 6 seats in Berlin. The largest delegation of any state except NRW.

## Offers
Legislation across Bavaria's domains — education, police, agriculture, infrastructure, culture.
Election of the Minister-President.
The six Bundesrat seats — Bavaria's weight in every federal bill.
The Bavarian constitution, adopted by referendum on the same day as the first Landtag election, 1 December 1946.
The public gallery. The Kreuzgang. The view from the Maximilianstraße across the Isar.

## Withheld

What Söder says in CSU faction meetings that does not reach the Plenarsaal.
The distance between the CSU's self-image as Bavaria's sole legitimate representative and the 63% of voters who voted for someone else in 2023.
The building's own ambivalence — a royal monument housing a democracy, owned by a foundation, rented by a state, inhabited by students.
