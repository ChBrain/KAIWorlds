# Piece: Bundesrat Delegation Hamburg

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections
- **Place:** [Bundesrat Berlin](place_bundesrat_berlin.md)

## Place
[Bundesrat Berlin](place_bundesrat_berlin.md).
And simultaneously: [Senatskanzlei Hamburg](place_hh_senatskanzlei.md).

## Load Bearing
Without it Hamburg has no voice in federal legislation.
Without it the port — Europe's third largest, Germany's largest — has no direct federal representation in the upper chamber.
Without it 1.9 million Hamburgers have no seat in the chamber that shapes the trade, infrastructure, and environmental legislation that determines what happens at the port.
Remove it and Hamburg governs locally but cannot shape the federal rules it operates under.

## Apparent
Three seats. The minimum allocation. The same as Bremen and Mecklenburg-Vorpommern.
Occupied by the Erster Bürgermeister and two Senators.
Cast as a bloc. SPD-Green coalition position on every federal bill.
Three votes in a chamber that requires 35.
Hamburg alone is never decisive. Hamburg in coalition with other SPD-led states carries weight.
The delegation changes when the state government changes. Since the SPD has governed Hamburg almost continuously since 1946, the delegation has been predominantly red for eighty years.

## Yearbook
May 2025. The new SPD-Green Senate is confirmed. The Bundesrat delegation is appointed. Tschentscher leads it.
Hamburg's three votes travel to Berlin on every consent bill and objection bill. Port infrastructure, trade policy, maritime law, environmental standards for shipping — all pass through this chamber.
The port handles 50 million tons of goods per year. 41,000 ships pass through the Kiel Canal annually — relevant to SH's delegation too, as the canal runs through their state.
Hamburg's three seats sit next to Schleswig-Holstein's four. Two northern states. Different governments in 2025: SPD-Green in Hamburg, CDU-Green in SH. Their Bundesrat votes on the same bill may differ.
The opposition in the Bürgerschaft — CDU, Linke, AfD — has no seat here. 44% of Hamburg's voters have no representation in the room that shapes federal law.
