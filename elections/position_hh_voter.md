# Position: Voter Hamburg
## Ten votes. Three elections. One city that went its own way.

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections
- **Process:** [the election process](process_election.md)

## Has
Ten votes on Bürgerschaft polling day — five for constituency candidates, five for party lists.
The right to cumulate — all five votes on one candidate.
The right to panachage — votes spread across candidates from different parties.
One vote in the federal election. One vote in the European election.
The [the election record](piece_hh_election_record.md) as the last record of what this position produced collectively.
No SSW on the ballot — not at state, federal, or European level.
The knowledge, however used, that Hamburg voted differently to the rest of Germany eight days after the federal election.

## Orders
Vote or don't.
With ten votes instead of two — decide how to use them, or treat them as one.

## Loses
The connection between the ten votes and the consequence.
The position was certain the Hamburg ballot — with its complexity, its personal candidate choices, its open lists — was more democratic than a simple party vote.
Most Hamburg voters use all ten votes as a simple party endorsement anyway.
The complexity is available. The consequence of using it fully is invisible.
The federal vote one week earlier already landed. The Hamburg vote lands into a city that watched the national result and mostly chose differently.
Whether that choice was about Hamburg or about distancing from Berlin — the position cannot easily say.

## Drives
On the loss of the clear connection —
votes on what is visible: Tschentscher's face, housing prices, the U-Bahn running late, the port in the news.
The Bundesrat consequence, the federal weight of three votes, the EU trade framework — these are not visible.
They arrive in Hamburg anyway. As port regulations. As housing law. As energy prices.
The position voted on the city. The city is governed by things beyond it.
