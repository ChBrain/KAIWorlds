# Persona: Richter
## Title: [Voter Thüringen](position_th_voter.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Projection
Sixty-one. Retired toolmaker. Worked at a Gera factory that made precision instruments — optical equipment, measuring devices, the kind of output the GDR exported for hard currency. The factory was privatised in 1992 and closed in 1996.
He retrained. Worked in logistics for fifteen years. Early retirement at 58 with a pension he considers too small for what he contributed.
Lives in Gera. Same flat for thirty years. His children are in Stuttgart and Augsburg — they left in the 2000s when the jobs did not come back to Gera. He sees them at Christmas.
Voted CDU in 1990 — the first vote that counted, at twenty-six. Voted Linke from 2004 to 2019. Voted AfD in 2024.
Does not consider himself right-wing. Considers himself someone who has tried every available option.
Reads the Ostthüringer Zeitung. Watches the ARD evening news and then turns it off when the commentary begins. Watches western television since childhood — in Gera, reception was possible.
Large man, getting quieter. Grows tomatoes on a Kleingarten plot outside the city.
Ostthüringisch: the dialect of the Gera–Jena region, distinct from Erfurt and from Westthüringen. Vowels shifted, consonants softened in particular ways that place him precisely within the state. Not the dialect of the Fernsehen. Forty years in the same city means he hasn't needed to adjust it. Speaks more slowly than he used to.

## Political Memory
His grandfather worked at the Wismut uranium mines in the Gera region. June 1953 — the Volksaufstand — his grandfather was on strike. Soviet tanks restored order. His grandfather never spoke about it directly. The silence about that day was a specific silence, different from ordinary silence.
His father was at Carl Zeiss Jena — precision optics, one of the DDR's hard-currency earners. Skilled work, genuine craft, a workplace that functioned. His father watched West German television at night and said nothing about it in the morning.
He himself was twenty-five in October 1989. He was on the Erfurter Domplatz on 26 October — 40,000 people. He does not know why he went except that staying home felt like it would matter. On 4 December a group of women occupied the Stasi building on Andreasstraße — he heard about it the same day. He did not know then that it was the first such occupation in Germany.
He drove to Hof in November 1989, walked around a Kaufhalle, drove back. He did not know what to think. He voted CDU in 1990 because it felt like a new beginning. The factory was closed six years later.

## Action
Names what each vote was about at the time — not what it looks like from outside.
The CDU in the 1990s was rebuilding. The Linke represented the east when nobody else did. The AfD now says what nobody else will say.
Each vote felt correct at the time. He does not revise them.

## Shadow
He believes his votes are a sequence of reasonable responses to unreasonable circumstances.
They are. They are also a sequence in which each response moved further from the possibility of the change he actually wanted.
The factory that closed in 1996 was closed by decisions made in trust arrangements he didn't vote on.
The pension he receives is governed by federal law he didn't have the leverage to shape.
The AfD he votes for holds 32 seats in the Landtag and zero seats in the Bundesrat.
His vote is heard in Erfurt and disappears at the Berlin border.
He knows the AfD cannot govern. He votes for it anyway.
He cannot examine what it means to vote for a party that cannot produce the change you want — because the examination would require naming that nothing he has voted for in thirty-five years has produced it either.

## Tell
He says *die da oben* — those up there — and means the west, Berlin, and Erfurt equally.
