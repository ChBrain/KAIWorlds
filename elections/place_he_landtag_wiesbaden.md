## Shown
Schlossplatz 1–3, Wiesbaden. The Hessischer Landtag sits in a complex of buildings including the baroque Stadtschloss. Wiesbaden is the administrative capital; Frankfurt is the economic capital. The tension between the two cities is structural — the government is in the spa town on the Rhine, the money is in the banking tower on the Main.
133 seats currently. CDU 52, AfD 24, SPD 22, Greens 22, FDP 6, Linke 7. Majority: 67.
CDU-SPD coalition holds 74 — seven above majority.
5 Bundesrat seats.
Voting age: 18.
Next election: 2028.

## Offers
Legislation for 6.4 million people — including the regulatory environment for Germany's financial centre.
Election of the Ministerpräsident.
Five Bundesrat seats in Berlin — Hessen votes with CDU-SPD position, centrist on most issues, specific on financial market regulation.
The Hessenschutzgesetz — Hessen's state police has broader data collection powers than most states. The legislative chamber approved this. The coalition's security policy is shaped partly by Frankfurt's specific risk profile as a financial centre.

## Withheld
The AfD's 24 seats — better than in Sachsen at the 2023 election, the first time AfD came second in a western state. What 18.4% means in a state that was historically a CDU fortress is not fully visible from inside the coalition.
The SPD's 22 seats as junior partner in a coalition where CDU holds 52 — the arithmetic of junior partnership when the SPD's own vote share was its worst ever.
Frankfurt in the Landtag: the city returns representatives from CDU, SPD, Greens, and occasionally FDP. The financial centre's workers vote differently from the financial centre's residents. Both send members to this chamber.
