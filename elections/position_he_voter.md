# Position: Voter Hessen

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections
- **Process:** [the election process](process_election.md)

## Has
Two votes — Erststimme and Zweitstimme. Voting age: 18.
One vote in the federal election. One vote in the European election.
The [election record](piece_he_election_record.md) as the accumulated history.
Geography as political identity: northern Hessen (Kassel) is more SPD; the Rhine-Main metropolitan area is mixed; Frankfurt's working-class districts vote SPD, its banking districts vote CDU; the Taunus and Rheingau vote CDU; the Odenwald and Vogelsberg are rural CDU. The AfD's 18.4% in 2023 sits unevenly across this landscape — stronger in rural northern Hessen, weaker in Frankfurt proper.
The ECB voter — the Frankfurt voter who works in a European institution, cannot vote in European Parliament elections as a non-German EU citizen, and is not represented in the Landtag by definition. The position's immediate neighbours may not be eligible to vote.

## Orders
Vote or don't.
In 2023: 66.0% voted — slightly below 2018. The AfD's 18.4% was produced by this electorate. So was the SPD's 15.1% — the worst result in the party's history in Hessen.
The Frankfurt voter in 2023 who chose CDU over SPD for the first time since the 1980s, because Faeser's campaign felt like a federal intervention in a state election. The campaign was shaped in Berlin. The vote was cast in Hessen.

## Loses
The SPD as a plausible alternative.
The "rotes Hessen" identity — Frankfurt labour movement, IG Metall, SPD strongholds in Offenbach and Kassel — has not disappeared but it no longer produces an SPD that can win the state. The SPD at 15.1% is the residue of a tradition that built the postwar city and does not govern it.
The Greens as the ambitious second option. In 2023 the Greens fell nearly five points — from 19.8% in 2018 to 14.8%. The voter who chose Greens in 2018 thinking they might lead a government found by 2023 that the Greens were a declining junior partner. Some went to SPD. Some went home.

## Drives
On the loss of SPD as alternative —
votes in a state where the SPD is now 15.1% and a junior coalition partner.
The position that voted SPD from conviction finds itself in a coalition with the CDU without having chosen it.
The position continues to vote — because the alternative is to become part of the gap between the result and the registered electorate.
The Frankfurt voter who voted CDU for the first time since the 1980s did so partly because Faeser's campaign felt like a federal intervention. The position voted for the state, not against itself.
