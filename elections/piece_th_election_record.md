## Load Bearing
Without it the governing process has no ground.
Without it the Kemmerich moment has no context.
Without it the Brombeer coalition has no explanation.
Without it 1990 has no weight — it was the first free vote in forty-four years.
Remove it and 2024 looks like an accident. With it, it looks like a direction that began before there were elections to record.

## Apparent
A state that has existed since 14 October 1990.
Before that: dissolved by the GDR in 1952. Reconstructed from the districts of Erfurt, Gera, and Suhl.
Eight elections since reunification. No election has produced a comfortable majority since 2009.
The largest party in the chamber since 2024 cannot govern.
The government that governs has no majority.
88 seats. Majority: 45.
Brombeer coalition: CDU + BSW + SPD = 44 seats. One short.

## Yearbook
**2024** — 1 September. AfD **32.8%** — first German state election won by a party classified as confirmed right-wing extremist. CDU 23.6%. BSW 15.8% — first entry into a state parliament. Linke 13.1% — lost over half its voters. SPD 6.1% — worst ever. Greens 3.2% — outside. FDP 1.1% — outside. Turnout 73.6%. AfD holds Sperrminorität: 32 of 88 seats — can block constitutional changes, Landtag dissolution, constitutional court appointments. CDU-BSW-SPD Brombeer coalition: 44 seats. One short of majority. Coalition agreement signed November 2024. Voigt elected Minister-President 12 December with Linke votes — 51 of 88. The CDU has an incompatibility resolution against formal cooperation with the Linke. The arrangement is called a "Pflichtenheft." The secret ballot left open whether AfD votes also came. Linke faction leader: "The AfD must not get a platform."

**2029** — Next regular Landtag election. Due by September 2029.

**2019** — 27 October. Linke **31.0%** — first time in reunified Germany a successor party to the SED won a state election outright. AfD 23.4% — second. CDU 21.8% — third, worst since 1990. SPD 8.2%. Greens 5.2%. FDP 5.0% — barely in. Turnout 64.9%. No viable majority. Ramelow (Linke) leads minority government. February 2020: Kemmerich (FDP) elected Minister-President in third round with AfD votes — the first time since 1945 a German government was formed with the decisive support of a far-right party. National outcry. Kemmerich resigns the next day. Ramelow re-elected in March on third ballot. Minority government continues to 2024.

**2014** — 14 September. CDU 33.5%. Linke **28.2%** — best result to date. SPD 12.4% — worst to date. AfD 10.6% — first entry into Thüringen Landtag. Greens 5.7%. FDP outside. Turnout 52.7% — lowest in state history. Bodo Ramelow (Linke) becomes Minister-President — first and only Linke Minister-President in German history. Red-red-green coalition. CDU in opposition for first time since 1990.

**2009** — 30 August. CDU 31.2%. Linke 27.4%. SPD 18.5%. Greens 6.2%. FDP 7.6%. Turnout 56.2%. CDU-SPD grand coalition. Dieter Althaus (CDU) had already stepped down after a skiing accident in which a woman died — Christine Lieberknecht (CDU) becomes Minister-President. Last election to produce a comfortable two-party majority.

**2004** — 13 June. CDU **43.0%** — absolute majority. Linke/PDS 26.1%. SPD 14.5%. FDP and Greens outside. Turnout 54.0%. Bernhard Vogel (CDU) then Dieter Althaus. CDU governs alone for the second consecutive term.

**1999** — 12 September. CDU **51.0%** — absolute majority of both seats and votes. PDS 21.3%. SPD 18.5%. FDP and Greens outside. Turnout 59.9%. Bernhard Vogel. CDU peak. Governs alone. The high-water mark of CDU dominance in the east.

**1994** — 16 October. CDU 42.6%. PDS 16.6%. SPD 29.6%. FDP outside. Greens outside. Turnout 74.8%. CDU-SPD coalition. Bernhard Vogel (CDU) Minister-President. FDP exits the Landtag — from 9.3% in 1990 to below threshold in four years.

**1990** — 14 October. First free election since 1946. CDU 45.5%. SPD 22.8%. PDS 9.1%. FDP 9.3%. Greens 6.5%. CDU won 44 of 89 mandates — missed absolute majority by one seat. CDU-FDP coalition. Josef Duchac (CDU) Minister-President. Hessen CDU had "adopted" the Thuringia CDU as a reunification partner — many of the first candidates came from the west. The state is two weeks old. Erfurt has been a city since 742 AD and the capital of the GDR district for forty years. It is now the capital of a state that was dissolved in 1952 and has just been reconstituted.

## Political Memory: The Years Without Elections (1952–1989)

*No Landtag elections were held in this period. Thüringen as a state did not exist.*

---

**1920** — Thüringen founded. Seven former duchies and principalities unite into the Land Thüringen. Weimar is the capital — the city of Goethe and Schiller and the new republic. The state is young, plural, contested. The SPD is founded in Eisenach in 1869. Erfurt gives its name to the SPD programme of 1891. Thüringen considers itself the cradle of German social democracy and German culture simultaneously.

**1933** — The NSDAP had its first state government in Thüringen in 1930 — before Hitler's national seizure of power. Wilhelm Frick as Interior Minister. Thüringen was where the Nazis tested what a government could do before they controlled the national one. This is not spoken of often. It sits underneath the Weimar pride.

**1945** — American forces occupy Thüringen in April. The state is handed to the Soviet zone in July per Allied agreement — despite being liberated by the Americans. Hermann Brill (SPD) briefly leads a democratic government under US occupation. Then the Soviets arrive. Brill is removed. The SED takes control.

**1946** — Halbfreie Landtagswahl. SED wins 49.8%. CDU 18.9%. LDPD 28.5%. The result is managed but not yet falsified — the SED does not yet hold an absolute majority of votes. This is the last election in Thüringen with a meaningful result for thirty-eight years.

**1950** — Scheinwahl. Einheitsliste. 99.7% approval. The form of an election with no content. It legitimises nothing and everyone knows it. From this point the Landtag is a procedural body. Real decisions are made in the SED Bezirksleitungen — the district party committees in Erfurt, Gera, and Suhl.

**1952** — 25 July. The Thüringen Landtag votes itself out of existence. The GDR dissolves the state under the law on "further democratisation of state organs" — a law passed by a parliament with no democratic mandate, dissolving a parliament with no democratic mandate. Thüringen becomes three Bezirke: Erfurt, Gera, Suhl. The name survives in the landscape. The institution disappears for thirty-eight years. From this moment, no one in Thüringen elects a state government. SED Bezirkssekretäre govern the three districts. In 1952 the Hardliner Gerhard Müller takes the Erfurt district. He will still be in post in 1989.

**1952** — Border zone established. A five-kilometre deep Sperrgebiet along the western border — Thüringen bordered West Germany along its entire western edge: Bavaria, Hesse, Lower Saxony. Hundreds of border families forcibly relocated in *Aktion Ungeziefer* (Operation Vermin) — removed from their homes without notice, often leaving possessions behind. The village of Böseckendorf in the Eichsfeld escapes relocation by fleeing en masse overnight. The border cuts through villages, farms, family lines.

**1953** — 17 June. Volksaufstand. The workers' uprising. Centred in Ostthüringen — Jena, Gera, the Wismut uranium mining region in the southeast. Workers strike against work quotas. Soviet tanks restore order. The uprising is crushed. It is the one moment between 1946 and 1989 when the population of the territory acts politically as a collective.

**1961** — 13 August. The Wall. The border Thüringen already had — the Sperrgebiet — is now total. The border villages that remained are now militarised. Several are evacuated and demolished. Approximately 6,000 people relocated. From this point the western boundary of Thüringen is one of the most fortified borders in Europe. In the night of 9–10 November 1989, the crossings into Bavaria, Hesse, and Lower Saxony will open. Twenty-eight years of closure.

**1970** — 19 March. The Erfurter Gipfeltreffen. West German Chancellor Willy Brandt meets GDR Prime Minister Willi Stoph at the Hotel Erfurter Hof — the first meeting of the two German governments since division. A crowd storms the Stasi and police cordons on the Bahnhofsvorplatz to get to Brandt. They cheer him from below the hotel windows. He appears. The crowd chants his name. The SED did not plan for this. Brandt does not encourage it and cannot stop it. He stands at the window. For one afternoon Erfurt is the most watched city in Germany.

**1970s–1980s** — Thüringen is developed as a DDR-Hochtechnologiezentrum — a high-technology centre. Carl Zeiss Jena. Mikroelektronik Erfurt — the GDR's attempt to build a competitive microchip industry, employing tens of thousands. The Automobilwerk Eisenach produces the Wartburg. Precision instruments, optics, textiles. The economy functions but the gap between the production numbers and what workers can actually buy is visible to anyone who looks. Western television — receivable across most of Thüringen, which borders West Germany on its entire western edge — shows a different world every night.

**1989** — Autumn. Demonstrations begin in Thüringen as everywhere. The three Bezirkssekretäre — Müller (Erfurt), Ziegenhahn (Gera), Albrecht (Suhl) — are known Hardliners. Unlike Hans Modrow in Dresden, they offer no reform signal. The demonstrations grow despite them, not because of them.

**26 October 1989** — 40,000 people on the Erfurter Domplatz. The largest demonstration in Thüringen since the Volksaufstand of 1953. On 10–11 November, the Oberbürgermeisterin and Bezirkschef Müller resign.

**9 November 1989** — The Wall falls. The border crossings into Bavaria, Hesse, and Lower Saxony open in the night. The Sperrgebiet that has existed since 1952 is dissolved. People from Gera drive to Bayern. Some come back. Some do not.

**4 December 1989** — First occupation of a Stasi district headquarters in Germany — not in Berlin, not in Leipzig, but in Erfurt. A group of women, led by members of *Frauen für Veränderung* (Women for Change), enter the MfS Bezirksverwaltung on Andreasstraße in the early morning. Kerstin Schön calls Almuth Falcke. They go in. Within days, similar occupations happen across the GDR. Erfurt was first. This is not remembered nationally. In Erfurt it is on a plaque.

**14 October 1990** — First free election since 1946. The first vote that counted in forty-four years. CDU 45.5%. The state is reconstituted. Josef Duchac becomes Minister-President. The people who voted CDU that day included many who had spent their working lives in factories that would be privatised in 1991 and closed before 1996. The world the election opened is not the world many of them voted for.
