# Place: Bundesrat Berlin
## Where the states govern federally — without the opposition.
## Runs: [the governing process](process_governing.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Shown
Leipziger Straße 3-4, Berlin.
The former Prussian House of Lords. Built 1899. Acquired by the Bundesrat in 2000 when the institution followed the Bundestag from Bonn to Berlin.
A second seat remains in Bonn — the building the Bundesrat used for fifty years. It is still used. Two seats for one institution, like the Parliament across the city.
The plenary chamber: a horseshoe of seats. Sixteen state delegations. Each state occupies a fixed position. The seats are labelled by state, not by person — the person changes when the government changes.
69 seats total. No opposition benches. The opposition of each state has no chair here.
The President of the Bundesrat chairs the sessions — the role rotates annually through the sixteen Minister-Presidents in alphabetical order.
The chamber is smaller than the Bundestag across the Spree. The decisions made here are not smaller.

## Offers
Federal legislative power without a federal election.
Consent or refusal on approximately half of all federal legislation.
Objection on the remainder — overridable, but costly.
The right of legislative initiative — the Bundesrat can propose federal bills directly.
A forum where sixteen state governments negotiate federal law among themselves and with the federal government.
The Mediation Committee — convened when Bundestag and Bundesrat cannot agree. Sixteen from each chamber. The room where the actual compromise is reached.

## Withheld
The opposition of each state.
Every vote cast here represents a governing coalition only.
Forty percent of a state's voters — sometimes more — have no voice in this room until the next state election changes the government.
The pre-session negotiations. By the time delegates vote in plenary, the position has already been agreed — in committee, in corridor, in phone calls between chancelleries.
The distance between what a delegate believes and what they vote. The bloc rule means a split delegation abstains. The abstention is invisible. The belief stays in Kiel, or Stuttgart, or Erfurt.
