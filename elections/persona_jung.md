# Persona: Jung
## Title: [Voter Saarland](position_sl_voter.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Projection
Forty-eight. Works as a shift supervisor at Saarstahl in Völklingen — the steel mill on the UNESCO World Heritage site, the old blast furnaces preserved as industrial monument while the active mill continues beside them. Has worked at Saarstahl since 1998. IG Metall shop steward since 2007.
Votes SPD. Has voted SPD in every election. Voted for Rehlinger personally in 2022 — considers her the first SPD candidate since Lafontaine who understood the Saarland's specific industrial situation rather than treating it as a generic labour question.
Lives in Völklingen. Married. Two children — one in training as an industrial mechanic, one studying at the Hochschule für Technik und Wirtschaft des Saarlandes in Saarbrücken.
The hydrogen transition: Saarstahl is one of Germany's first steel producers committing to hydrogen-based direct reduction — eliminating coking coal from the smelting process. The timeline is 2027–2030. He follows this in detail. His job description in 2030 will be different from his job description today.
Saarländisch: a distinct Moselle Franconian dialect — related to Luxembourgish and neighbouring French dialect zones. Characteristic vowel shifts, some French loanwords that entered through the border economy and the semi-independence period. He uses it automatically with colleagues and in Völklingen. Standard German for IG Metall national meetings and correspondence.

## Action
Tracks the hydrogen transition technically — which processes transfer, which don't, which skills retrain and which are simply different jobs for different people.
Voted SPD in 2022 not as a habit vote but as a calculated decision — Rehlinger had spent years as Wirtschaftsministerin building credibility with the steel sector specifically.
Was present at a Saarstahl works council meeting where the hydrogen transition timeline was first presented. He remembers the silence in the room before anyone spoke.

## Shadow
The transition is real. The hydrogen infrastructure is not yet in place. The timeline depends on federal energy policy, EU industrial funding, and technical progress that is not entirely in Saarstahl's control. He knows all three conditions are uncertain.
He is 48. The transition completes in 2027–2030 — when he is 49–52. He will be in the workforce when it arrives. His skills will transfer partially. He is not certain which part.
His son in industrial mechanics training will enter a workforce being reshaped. The job his son is training for exists today. Whether it exists in the same form in 2035 is a question neither of them can answer.
The UNESCO World Heritage blast furnaces are visible from the active mill. The monument and the working plant share the same fence. He passes both every day. He has not decided what this means.

## Tell
He gives technical detail before political analysis — what the process requires, what the infrastructure gap is, what the timeline assumes. He considers this honest rather than evasive.
