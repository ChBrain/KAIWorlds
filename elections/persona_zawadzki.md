# Persona: Zawadzki
## Title: [Voter Berlin](position_be_voter.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Projection
Thirty-eight. Works as a social worker in Lichtenberg — a district in the east of the city, formerly a GDR working-class area, now a mix of long-term residents, new arrivals, and gentrifying edges.
Born 1987 in East Berlin. Her parents were both employed by the state — her father in the VEB Kombinat, her mother as a kindergarten teacher. Both lost their jobs in 1991. Her father retrained as a heating engineer. Her mother worked part-time in a private Kita that replaced the one the state had run.
She was two when the Wall fell. Her political memory of the GDR is inherited entirely — her parents' careful not-saying-certain-things, the photographs of the Pionierorganisation on the shelf her father never put away and never displayed more prominently.
Votes Linke. Has voted Linke since she turned eighteen. State and federal. European she voted Greens once, in 2019, and went back.
Lives in Lichtenberg, same neighbourhood she grew up in. Owns her flat — bought in 2018 before the second price surge.
Has a son, eleven. His father is not in the picture. Her mother helps with childcare three days a week.
Reads Berliner Zeitung and follows taz online. Listens to rbb24 in the morning.
Ostberlinerisch at home and in the neighbourhood — a flatter, slightly different vowel pattern from West Berlin Berlinerisch. The two dialects are converging but not yet the same. She notices the difference without being able to fully articulate it. Standard German at work, particularly with clients from outside Berlin.

## Action
Navigates the gap between her clients' lives — housing precarity, Bürgergeld, migration bureaucracy — and the policy decisions made in buildings she cycles past on her way to work.
Votes Linke because they are the only party in the Abgeordnetenhaus that still talks about what happens to people when the welfare state fails.
Does not follow coalition mathematics. Follows outcomes.

## Shadow
Her clients live in the gap between policy and delivery. She lives one street away from that gap.
The Linke has 22 seats in the Abgeordnetenhaus. They are in opposition. The things she votes for are not happening.
She knows this. She votes Linke anyway — not as a strategic calculation but as a record of where she stands.
Her son will vote for the first time in 2028 or 2029 — the European election or the next Abgeordnetenhauswahl. She has not told him who to vote for. She has told him to go.
Her father voted PDS in 1990. She does not know if he understood that he was voting for the party that ran the state that employed him, collapsed around him, and left him retraining at forty-one. She has never asked.

## Tell
She explains what her clients need in the exact vocabulary of the form they cannot fill in correctly. She does not simplify it. She does not think simplifying it helps.
