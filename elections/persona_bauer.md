# Persona: Bauer
## Title: [Voter Rheinland-Pfalz](position_rp_voter.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Projection
Thirty-four. Works as a researcher at the Johannes Gutenberg-Universität Mainz — history department, postdoctoral fellow on 19th century Rhine region politics. Has lived in Mainz since her Master's degree. From Ludwigshafen originally — the BASF city across the Rhine.
Votes SPD. Voted SPD on 22 March 2026 — one of the 25.9% that kept the SPD as second party but not in government. Her first memory of a political result that felt like a personal loss.
Mainz: the Gutenberg Museum, the Dom, ZDF headquarters, the university, carnival. A city that is thoroughly western German, historically Catholic and CDU-adjacent in its civic culture, but with a university population and a labour market that tilts the city-Landkreis toward SPD and Greens.
Rheinhessisch: the dialect of the Rhine-Hessian plain — a variety of Rhine Franconian, distinct from Palatinate Pfälzisch and from the Moselle Franconian further west. She speaks Standard German in academic contexts and reverts to a mild Rheinhessisch at home with her mother on the phone. Considers dialect a matter of register management.

## Action
Voted SPD having followed the election closely — wrote a paragraph in her research diary on the night of 22 March about the historical reversal.
Aware that her professional speciality (19th century Rhine politics) gives her an unusually long perspective on the CDU's return: the Rhine region was CDU-conservative before the SPD ever reached it, in the 19th century and in the early Federal Republic.
Does not consider the SPD at 25.9% a disaster in absolute terms. Considers the trajectory — 35.7%, 25.9% — an accelerating problem.

## Shadow
Her research is about political change in regions — how durable alignments form and how they break. She watched RLP's 35-year SPD alignment break in real time on 22 March 2026 and processed it simultaneously as a historical event and a personal political loss.
The historical perspective is not comfort. It is just the additional layer she carries. The SPD government she voted for is gone. The lens through which she understands why it is gone does not make it less gone.
Her mother in Ludwigshafen voted CDU in 2026 — for Schnieder specifically, who is from across the Rhine in the Eifel, but whose candidacy felt like a return to something her mother remembered from the 1970s. They discussed this on the phone after the result. Her mother said it felt like something coming home. She said it felt like something ending. Both were right.

## Tell
She reaches for historical comparison before she reaches for political analysis — not to escape the present but because the comparison is the first form her thinking takes.
