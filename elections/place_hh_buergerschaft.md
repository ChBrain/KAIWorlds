## Shown
Rathausmarkt 1, Hamburg.
The Bürgerschaft sits in the Rathaus — the City Hall, built 1886–1897. Neo-Renaissance. 112 metres of facade facing the Rathausmarkt.
Not a purpose-built parliament building separate from government. The Bürgerschaft and the Senate share the same building. Governance and legislation in one house.
121 seats minimum. The chamber can expand with overhang seats.
Elected by a system unlike any other German state: ten votes per voter, five for constituency candidates, five for party lists. Open lists. Cumulation — all five votes on one candidate. Panachage — votes spread across candidates from different parties. The ballot is complex. The counting takes until the Monday after election day.
17 multi-member constituencies across Hamburg's seven Bezirke. No single-member constituencies. Proportional from the start.
The Erster Bürgermeister is elected here — not Minister-President. First Mayor. The title reflects the city.
Voting age: 16.
No SSW. No minority exemption. Hamburg has no protected national minority.

## Offers
Legislation in the city-state's domains — which are broader than a regular state because Hamburg governs itself at both state and municipal level simultaneously.
Election and scrutiny of the Senate — the Hamburg government.
The ten-vote ballot — the most voter-directed electoral system in Germany. A voter can build their own personal list across party lines.
The public gallery. Hamburg debates are open.

## Withheld
What the ten votes actually produce at the individual level — the complexity of cumulation and panachage means most voters use their votes as a simple party endorsement anyway.
The distance between what voters think they chose and what the seat distribution produced.
That Hamburg's result eight days after the federal election was partly a local verdict on the federal result — and partly a verdict on Tschentscher specifically, who ran as if he were independent of Berlin.
