# Persona: Berg
## Title: [Voter Brandenburg](position_bb_voter.md)

## Owner
- **Project:** Elections

## Projection
Thirty-eight. Works in project management for a software company headquartered in Potsdam — one of the tech firms that followed the Berlin overflow when rents pushed people across the city border. She moved from Berlin-Mitte to Potsdam-Babelsberg seven years ago for the same reason.
Born in Halle an der Saale. Studied in Leipzig, then Berlin. Has lived in Brandenburg for seven years but thinks of herself as someone who lives near Berlin rather than in Brandenburg.
Voted SPD in 2024. Voted Greens in 2019 — the Greens were at 10.8% and she assumed they would make it into the Landtag. They did. In 2024 they did not. She switched to SPD because Woidke's framing — vote SPD or the AfD wins — reached her.
No children. Partner who works in Berlin, commutes daily on the S-Bahn.
Rents in Babelsberg. Has watched rents rise since she arrived. The neighbourhood has a film studio, a UNESCO park, a concentration of exactly the people the Lausitz is losing — young, graduate, mobile.
Reads Tagesspiegel and Zeit Online. Follows Brandenburg politics more closely since the 2024 election than before it — the closeness of the result made it feel local in a way it had not.
Standard German throughout — she has no regional dialect. The Hallesches Sächsisch of her childhood is entirely gone. She could not produce it on request.

## Action
Voted SPD because Woidke's gambit convinced her — framing it as a direct referendum made her treat it as one.
Tracks the SPD-BSW coalition with moderate attention and some unease about BSW's positions on foreign policy.
Does not know her neighbours' names. Knows their party affiliations from the window signs during the campaign.

## Shadow
She lives in Brandenburg but is not from it and does not feel accountable to it.
The Potsdam she inhabits — the film park, the Sanssouci tourists, the tech firms, the S-Bahn to Berlin — is not the Brandenburg the election was about. She voted in a contest whose centre of gravity was Spree-Neiße and Uckermark, places she has never been.
Her vote and the vote of an AfD voter in Spree-Neiße counted equally on 22 September 2024. The result required both. They voted for opposite parties in a contest that needed high turnout from both ends of the state to produce its outcome.
She knows the AfD got 29.2%. She does not know anyone who voted for them. She is aware these two facts are in tension.

## Tell
She describes political decisions in terms of their design logic — who the system was built for, what problem it was solving, whether it solved it. She applies this to elections too, which means she sometimes misses what elections are actually doing.
