## Load Bearing
Without it the pre-1957 record has no visual form
available to the Tourist without asking anyone.
The photograph predates the Boknis Eck time series
by sixty-seven years. It shows the Flensburg Fjord
in a state that no instrument has ever recorded —
the eelgrass at the waterline, the water clarity,
the fjord before the postwar nitrogen load arrived.
Remove it and the Tourist sees a beautiful historical photograph.
Keep it and the Tourist has access to the baseline
if she looks at the foreground instead of the yacht.
Nobody has told her to look at the foreground.
Nobody has told her not to.

## Apparent
A framed black-and-white photograph.
Approximately 40 by 50 centimetres.
The Flensburg Fjord, 1890.
Kaiser Wilhelm II's yacht Hohenzollern moored at the pier.
The pier: recognisably the same pier outside the hotel.
The fjord: the same fjord.
In the foreground, at the waterline:
eelgrass. Dense. Not yet retreated.
The yacht is the subject of the photograph.
The eelgrass is incidental.
It has been incidental for 135 years.

## Yearbook
Taken 1890. The yacht the subject.
The eelgrass the background.
Hung in the bar at some point in the hotel's history.
A record of a distinguished visit.

The eelgrass began retreating in the 1970s.
The photograph did not move.
The foreground became, without anyone noticing,
the only visual record of what the fjord's waterline
looked like before the retreat.

The Harbour Master passes this photograph
every time she comes to the hotel bar.
She has not been asked about it.
The Tourist looks at the yacht.
Nobody has pointed at the foreground.
The comparison is available.
It requires the Tourist to look at the right thing.
