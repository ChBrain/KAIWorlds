# Piece: The Thing Brought From Home
## The person's life, placed near the bed.

## Owner
- **Project:** ICU
- **Place:** [The ICU Room](place_the_icu_room.md)

## Place
[The ICU Room](place_the_icu_room.md).
On the windowsill, or the small table
beside the [Bed](piece_the_bed.md),
or propped against the rail.
Somewhere within sight
of whoever is in the bed.

## Load Bearing
Without it the room contains
only what the hospital put here.
The thing from home is the only object in the room
that belongs to the person in the bed —
that knows them from outside this,
from before this,
from the life they were living
when they were still only themselves.
Remove it and the room is entirely
a room about the illness.
The thing from home insists
there is someone here
who is more than the illness.
This insistence is load-bearing.
It holds the room open
to a version of this person
the machines cannot access.

## Apparent
A photograph, usually.
Or a child's drawing.
Or a small object — a stone, a figurine,
the thing that has been on the bedside table
for so many years
nobody remembers where it came from.
It looks ordinary.
It is the most specific thing in the room.
Every other object here
could belong to any patient.
This one belongs to this person.

## Yearbook
It was packed by someone
who stood in the person's bedroom
or kitchen or living room
and looked at their things
and tried to choose the right one.
There is no right one.
They chose anyway.
It has sat beside the bed
through nights when nobody was there
and days when the room was full.
It has been looked at by the nurses
who learned something about the patient
from it that the chart could not tell them.
It has been held by someone
sitting in the [Chair](piece_the_chair.md)
who needed something real to hold.
It has been brought home after.
That was the hardest part of leaving —
picking it up, putting it in a bag,
because picking it up meant
acknowledging what kind of leaving this was.
