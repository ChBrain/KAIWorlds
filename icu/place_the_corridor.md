# Place: The Corridor
## Location: Outside the ICU room. Between the room and the world.

## Owner
- **Project:** ICU

## Shown
- A hospital corridor. Fluorescent light.
  The kind that makes everyone look
  like a different version of themselves.
- The door to [The ICU Room](place_the_icu_room.md).
  Always visible from here.
  The glass panel in it fogs
  when the room is warm.
- Chairs along the wall. Plastic.
  Nobody chose to be sitting in these chairs.
  They ended up here.
- A vending machine somewhere nearby.
  Food that is not food.
  Available at 3am when nothing else is.
- A window at the end, if there is one.
  It shows the outside.
  The outside has weather.
  The outside has continued
  without anyone in this corridor.
- The sound of the ICU beyond the door
  the monitor, the ventilator,
  arriving faintly when the door opens.
- [The Phone](piece_the_phone.md).
  In the pocket of whoever is out here.
  They came out here to use it.

## Holds
- [The Phone](piece_the_phone.md).
  Where the calls get made
  that cannot be made inside.

## Offers
- The distance from the room.
  Not escape. Distance.
  The corridor does not offer resolution.
  It offers the absence of the monitor's sound.
  That is sometimes enough.
- The chairs. To whoever needs to sit
  outside the room without leaving the floor.
- The vending machine. To whoever
  has forgotten to eat
  and remembered suddenly.
- The nurses' station nod.
  A specific communication
  not words, a look
  that means *stable, nothing to report, you can breathe.*
  The family learns to read it.
  It is the most information
  some family members receive
  on some nights.
  The nurses do not know
  how much it carries.
- The door back. Always available.
  Always in sight.

## Withheld
- Whether the person standing here
  is cooling down or leaving.
  From the corridor you cannot tell
  and neither can they.
  The corridor holds that uncertainty
  without resolving it.
- What the nurse is carrying
  between rooms.
  The corridor is the only transit
  between one room and the next.
  Sometimes the nurse who walks it
  has just come from something
  that the room they are entering
  cannot know about
  and must not know about.
  The corridor does not ask
  what is being carried through it.
  It offers the fifteen seconds.
  What arrives at the next door
  is the nurse's work,
  not the corridor's.
  The corridor holds the transit
  without holding what it carries.
  The room holds the answer.
  The room is through the door.
