# Piece: The Chart
## The clinical memory that runs between shifts.

## Owner
- **Project:** ICU
- **Place:** [The ICU Unit](place_the_icu_unit.md)

## Place
[The ICU Unit](place_the_icu_unit.md).
One per patient. One per bed.
At the nurses' station
or at the bedside
or in the hands of whoever
is reading it right now.

## Load Bearing
Without it the clinical world
has no memory between shifts.
The [Handover](process_the_handover.md) has nothing to hand over.
The [Round](process_the_round.md) has no baseline to read from.
The [Blood Draw](process_the_blood_draw.md) results
have nowhere to land.
Every decision made about this patient
was made in relation to
what the chart says today
and what it said yesterday
and the day before.
Remove the chart and the clinical world
must start again from the body
with no record of where it has been.
The chart is the clinical world's
accumulated knowledge of one patient.
It is the most complete record in the room.
It is not complete.

## Apparent
A file. Physical or electronic.
Sections: vitals, medications, observations,
results, notes, plan.
Each section filled in a different hand
or a different login.
The notes are dated and timed.
The times are precise.
What happened at 03:47
is distinguishable from
what happened at 03:52.
The chart does not record
what the nurse thought
while writing the 03:47 entry.
It does not record
what the family said
when the doctor left the room.
It does not record
the name the patient responds to
that is not the name at the top of the page.
It records what can be recorded
in the format available.
The format is precise.
The format has edges.
Everything outside the edges
is not in the chart.

## Yearbook
It has recorded faithfully
through every shift.
It has been read by doctors
who knew the patient
and doctors who did not.
It has been read by nurses
at the start of shifts
who used it to build
a picture of someone
they had not yet met.
The picture was accurate.
It was not the person.
It has been read by a family member —
once, in week two,
when they asked to see it
and the nurse showed them —
who understood some of what it said
and could not parse the rest
and carried both home.
It has recorded the blood results
that changed the plan
and the blood results
that confirmed the plan
and the blood results
that raised a question
the plan did not yet have an answer for.
It has been the thing
the incoming nurse reads first
and the thing the experienced nurse
reads differently —
knowing what the numbers mean
in the context of yesterday's numbers
and the day before's
and the specific trajectory
of this specific patient
which is not visible in any single entry
but is visible across all of them
to someone who has been here
long enough to read it that way.
It has never recorded
what the patient's world feels like
from inside the bed.
That is not its function.
That is the gap it holds
without knowing it holds it.
