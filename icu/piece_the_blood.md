# Piece: The Blood
## The body's report to the clinical world, made without the patient's knowledge.

## Owner
- **Project:** ICU
- **Place:** [The ICU Room](place_the_icu_room.md)

## Place
[The ICU Room](place_the_icu_room.md).
Inside the patient until it is drawn.
In a vial after.
In the lab.
In the results that return to the room
before the family arrives in the morning.

## Load Bearing
Without it the clinical world
cannot read what the patient's body
is actually doing.
The [Monitor](piece_the_monitor.md) reads surface signals
heart rate, oxygen levels, blood pressure.
The blood reads everything underneath:
organ function, infection, electrolytes,
the body's response to treatment,
whether what the clinical world is doing
is working or not.
Remove the blood draw and the clinical world
loses its most honest source.
The patient cannot lie to the blood.
The blood does not spare anyone.

## Apparent
A vial. Small. Dark red.
Several vials sometimes, different tests
require different containers, different colours.
Drawn from a line in the arm or the neck
or a needle in the wrist
in the hour before dawn
while the family sleeps
or sits in the corridor
or is not yet here.
The patient does not feel it
if sedated. May barely feel it
if waking. The nurses are practiced.
The vials are labelled, logged, sent.
The results return as numbers on a screen.
The numbers are not the blood.
They are what the blood said
translated into a language
only the clinical world reads fluently.

## Yearbook
It has reported faithfully through every shift.
The midnight draw that came back wrong
and woke the doctor at 3am.
The morning draw that showed
the first sign of improvement
a number slightly better
than the number from yesterday
and the nurse who saw it first
and knew before the round
that today would be different.
The draw that showed
what nobody had wanted to name
and which made the round
that morning a different round
than any round before it.
It has been drawn from patients
who did not know it was happening.
It has been drawn from patients
who watched it happen
and asked what the results would mean.
The answer was always clinical.
The question was never clinical.
The blood does not resolve this gap.
It simply reports.
