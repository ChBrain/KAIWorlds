# Piece: The Ridge Road

## Owner
- **Project:** Ochsenweg

## Place
The Jyske Højderyg, Cimbrian Peninsula.

## Load Bearing
Without it there is no overland route between Jutland and the Elbe
that avoids the bogs. Remove it: the drover has no path south.
The pilgrim has no line to follow. The trader cannot move goods
overland without becoming lost in marsh. The toll keeper has
no one arriving. The innkeeper's door faces the wrong direction.
The market at Wedel receives no cattle.
True in 1700 BCE. True in 1600 CE. True until the railway.

## Apparent
A worn path along high ground. Never built - compressed by weight
and repetition into ground harder than the surrounding heath.
Visible as a slight hollow in places; a raised causeway in others
where travellers have walked beside the original line
rather than in the mud of it.
Rutted where carts have used it. Smooth where only feet have.
Burial mounds visible from the ridge on clear days, in both
directions. Sky larger than expected.
In wet seasons: the only ground that holds.

## Yearbook

**c. 1700 BCE - Early Nordic Bronze Age.**
Copper and tin moving south along the ridge.
The burial mounds being built beside it -
the dead placed where they can watch the road.
The road is not yet named. It is the only dry line
north to south on the peninsula.

**c. 1500–500 BCE - Late Bronze Age into Iron Age.**
Amber going south. Iron goods beginning to move.
The corridor widening - several parallel tracks
where the main line becomes too churned to walk.
The first defensive earthworks visible from the ridge.

**c. 500 BCE–0 CE - Pre-Roman Iron Age.**
Bog iron worked in southwest Jutland.
Iron moving south in volume for the first time.

**c. 500–737 CE - First Danevirke.**
The road reaches a wall for the first time.
Earliest earthworks across the isthmus dated to c. 500 CE,
timber palisade phase built 737 CE.
The road must now pass through a controlled point.

**c. 737–1000 CE - Viking Age. Hedeby.**
The road is the spine of the most important
trading network in northern Europe.
Hedeby sits at the crossing of the Schlei -
the essential hub between the North Sea and the Baltic.
The Danevirke expanded multiple times.

**c. 1200–1500 CE - Medieval pilgrimage era.**
Pilgrims walking to Santiago de Compostela, Rome, Jerusalem.
Infrastructure accumulates beside it: inns, hospices, churches.
The Bronze Age burial mounds are passed by people
who do not know who built them.

**c. 1423–1618 CE - Peak cattle trade.**
Danish ox exports: 2,000 head in 1423.
45,500 in the late 1560s.
Up to 30,000 traded at Wedel in a single spring market around 1600.
The road is at maximum use. It will never be this busy again.

**1618–c.1750 - Thirty Years' War and rinderpest.**
Three rinderpest panzootics: 1709–1720, 1742–1760, 1768–1786.
The toll keeper is now also a quarantine officer.
The market at Wedel contracts to a small-town livestock fair.
Still 700 animals sold in 1885.

**1848–1864 - The Schleswig Wars.**
The road runs through contested territory.
The border the road has crossed for a thousand years
is no longer agreed upon.

**c.1920–1939 - Heritage.**
The market rediscovered as local identity.
The road remembered as history.

**1949 - Resumption.**
250 oxen. Wedel proving it still exists.

**c.1950–1979 - Last resemblance.**
Cattle still driven through the streets to market.

**2022 - Cancelled until further notice.**
The road is now a hiking trail.
The road is older than everything that walked it.
