# References

## Ochsenweg / Hærvejen

Hærvejen (The Army Road / Ox Road). The ancient north-south route along the Jutland Ridge, Denmark.
Used from the Bronze Age. Named for the cattle drives that defined its use from the medieval period onward.
The German name Ochsenweg refers specifically to the cattle trade era.
The road was not constructed - it formed through repeated use along the highest, driest ground.

Jyske Højderyg (The Jutland Ridge) - the spine of the Cimbrian Peninsula.
The only reliable dry route for north-south movement before modern drainage.

## The Ochsenmarkt at Wedel

Wedel-on-Elbe. The terminus of the cattle drives since the medieval period.
Peak trade: 40,000-45,000 head of cattle per year, 1610-1620.
2003: 88 animals.
March 2022: the Ochsenmarkt was cancelled. The final market did not take place.

## The Roland Statue

Roland figures mark medieval market rights and civic freedoms in northern German towns.
The Roland at Wedel is the smallest Roland figure in Germany.
It marks the market ground and the civic right to hold a market.

---

## Historical trade volumes

Toll records at Gottorf and Schleswig provide the primary quantitative evidence for cattle volumes.
Key data points used in this world:
- c. 1485: 2,491 oxen at Gottorf toll station
- 1545-1547: 39,000 head at Schleswig toll
- 1610-1620: estimated peak of 40,000-45,000 head per year
- 1885: 700 animals at Wedel market
- 2003: 88 animals

## Pilgrimage on the Hærvejen

The road served pilgrims travelling to Santiago de Compostela as well as drovers.
The pilgrimage route enters the European network at Ribe or Schleswig,
continuing south toward Flensburg and Hamburg.
The Pilgrim's Credential (credencial) was issued by church authorities
and stamped at recognised waypoints.

---

## The Contracte

The formal written agreement used in the Wedel cattle market,
specifying animal count, agreed price, transfer of horn marks, and payment terms.
The Handshake accompanied the Contracte as the performative completion of the transaction.
