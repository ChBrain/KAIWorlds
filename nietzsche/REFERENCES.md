# References

## Nietzsche's Works

**Thus Spoke Zarathustra** (1883-1885)
The architecture of this world is drawn from Part One, "On the Three Metamorphoses."
The camel. The lion. The child.
Public domain in all jurisdictions.

**Beyond Good and Evil** (1886)
Appears in this world as a piece - a book the philosopher is writing.
Referenced directly as source for the perspectivism that shapes how positions are structured.
Public domain in all jurisdictions.

**Twilight of the Idols** (1889)
Appears in this world as a piece. Written in the final productive year before the collapse.
The title is the philosopher's own - a deliberate echo of Wagner's Twilight of the Gods.
Public domain in all jurisdictions.

## Interpretive Note

The three metamorphoses are used here as an architectural framework, not a summary of Nietzsche's philosophy.

The world does not claim that Nietzsche intended this reading, that it is complete, or that the transformation sequence is linear in the way the world renders it. Nietzsche resists linearity. The architecture borrows the sequence while acknowledging that the sequence is a simplification.

The positions, pieces, processes, and places in this world are original constructions.
They are not reproductions of Nietzsche's text.
They are built on the architecture the text provides.

---

## The Collapse

Nietzsche's productive work ended in January 1889 in Turin.
The cause of the collapse is disputed.
The work was already complete.

This world does not address the collapse.
It addresses what the work built.
