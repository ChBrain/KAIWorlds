# References

## Aristotle (384-322 BCE)

Born in Stagira, Chalcidice. Son of the royal court physician to the King of Macedon.
Studied at Plato's Academy in Athens for twenty years.
Tutor to Alexander the Great, 343-335 BCE.
Founded the Lyceum in Athens, 335 BCE.
He wrote on logic, biology, physics, metaphysics, ethics, politics, and rhetoric.
He died in Chalcis, aged 62.

## Rhetoric (c. 350 BCE)

Written in Athens during Aristotle's second period at the Lyceum.
The work is organized in three books.
Book I identifies three means of persuasion available to the speaker: Ethos, Logos, and Pathos.
Book II develops the emotional states of the Audience and the character of the Speaker.
Book III addresses style and arrangement - the parts of the speech and their sequence.

The Rhetoric is not a manual for producing persuasion. It is an analysis of the means that are available and why they work when they work. Aristotle distinguishes between the artistic proofs - Ethos, Logos, Pathos, deployed by the Speaker - and the inartistic proofs: laws, witnesses, contracts, existing before the speech.

The question the Rhetoric asks is not: how do you persuade? The question is: what are the available means in this occasion, for this audience, and which of them are you using?
