# References

## G. H. Hardy (1877–1947)

Born in Cranleigh, Surrey. Mathematician at Trinity College, Cambridge. Received Ramanujan's unsolicited letter on January 31, 1913. Brought him to Cambridge in 1914. Watched him die. Wrote *A Mathematician's Apology* in 1940, in decline, unable to do mathematics anymore. The taxi-cab number story is in the foreword C. P. Snow added to the 1967 edition.

**The work this world draws from:**

*A Mathematician's Apology* — G. H. Hardy
First published 1940. Cambridge University Press.
Recommended edition: Cambridge University Press paperback with C. P. Snow's foreword (1967).

---

## Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887–1920)

Born in Erode, Tamil Nadu. Self-taught. Wrote to Hardy from Madras on January 16, 1913. Arrived in Cambridge March 1914. Fellow of the Royal Society, 1918. Returned to India, 1919. Died April 26, 1920, aged thirty-two. Sent his last letter — four pages, seventeen functions — from Kumbakonam in January 1920. Hardy could not solve them. Nobody could. Not for eighty more years.

**Primary source:**

*The Letters of G. H. Hardy and S. Ramanujan* — 1913–1920
Collected in: Robert Kanigel, *The Man Who Knew Infinity* (1991), appendix and notes.
The January 1913 letter and the January 1920 letter are the spine of this world.

*Notebooks* — Srinivasa Ramanujan
ca. 1904–1920. Three volumes, plus the lost notebook found in the Wren Library,
Cambridge, in 1976 by George Andrews. Still being read.

## Secondary sources

**Robert Kanigel** — *The Man Who Knew Infinity: A Life of the Genius Ramanujan* (1991)
Scribner. The definitive biography. The primary source for the shape of the
relationship, the letters, and what Cambridge cost.

**Srinivasa Ramanujan** — *Collected Papers* (1927)
Ed. Hardy, Seshu Aiyar, Wilson. Cambridge University Press. The theorem that carries
both names — Hardy-Ramanujan, 1917 — is here.

**Bruce C. Berndt** — *Ramanujan's Notebooks*, Parts I–V (1985–1998)
Springer. The scholarly edition of the notebooks. Proof of what the world needed
a century to catch up with.

**Note on mock theta functions:** The seventeen functions in Ramanujan's last letter
were not fully understood until 2002, when Sander Zwegers completed his doctoral
thesis at Utrecht. They belong to a theory nobody knew existed when Ramanujan wrote
them down.
