# References

## Socrates (470–399 BC)

Born in Athens. Son of a stonemason and a midwife. He served as a soldier at Potidaea, Amphipolis, and Delium. He was ugly by his own account. He charged nothing for his conversations. He wrote nothing.

He was tried in 399 BC before a jury of 501 citizens on charges of impiety and corrupting the young. The vote to convict: 280 to 221. The vote on the death penalty: 360 to 141. He was executed by hemlock. He was seventy years old.

Everything we know about what he said and thought arrives through others — primarily Plato, but also Xenophon, Aristophanes, and Aristotle. These accounts are not consistent. The project of recovering the historical Socrates from beneath the literary Socrates has not succeeded and is not expected to.

## Primary sources

**Plato** — the dialogues, written across approximately forty years following the trial. The early dialogues (Apology, Crito, Euthyphro, Ion) are generally considered closest to the historical Socrates. The middle and late dialogues increasingly reflect Plato's own philosophical development, with Socrates as vehicle. The line between the two is disputed and cannot be drawn with certainty.

*Apology* — the defense speech. Written within a few years of the trial. Plato was present. The closest account of what Socrates said in the courtroom. Still a reconstruction.

*Crito* — the prison dialogue. Crito brings the plan for escape. Socrates refuses it. The argument is about obligation to the city's laws.

*Phaedo* — the death scene. Written forty years after the event. Plato was not present — he names himself as absent due to illness. The most philosophically complete account of the death. Its composition and the distance from the event are facts the world holds without resolving.

*Symposium* — the dinner dialogue on love. Alcibiades arrives late, drunk, uninvited. His speech on Socrates is the most personally vivid account in the corpus.

**Xenophon** — *Memorabilia*, *Apology*, *Symposium*. A different Socrates: more practical, less abstract, easier to live alongside. Useful as a check on the Platonic version.

**Aristophanes** — *The Clouds* (423 BC). A comic portrait of Socrates as a sophist and a fraud, performed twenty-four years before the trial. Socrates named it in his defense as a source of popular prejudice against him. It is a primary document of what Athens thought of him before Plato wrote.

## Secondary sources

**Gregory Vlastos** — *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher* (1991). The standard modern treatment of the distinction between the Socratic and Platonic layers of the dialogues. Essential for the question of what Socrates actually held.

**I.F. Stone** — *The Trial of Socrates* (1988). A contrarian reading: Athens was not wrong to be threatened; Socrates was not a simple martyr. Useful pressure on the standard account.

**Robin Waterfield** — *Why Socrates Died* (2009). Reconstructs the political context of 399 BC. The trial was not only about philosophy.

**A.A. Long** — *Socratic Studies* (various). On the daimon, on the early dialogues, on the problem of Socratic irony.

## On Alcibiades

Alcibiades (c. 450–404 BC) was a student of Socrates, a general, and the most controversial political figure of late fifth-century Athens. He defected to Sparta, returned to Athens, was exiled again, and was killed in Phrygia in 404 BC — seven years before the trial. The accusation of corrupting the young was widely understood to refer to him, though his name was not spoken in court. Plato's *Symposium* gives him the final and most revealing speech.
