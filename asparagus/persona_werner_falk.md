# Persona: Werner Falk
## Title: Regional Claimant

## Owner
- **Project:** The Asparagus Season

A man in his late fifties from a farming family that has grown asparagus near Schwetzingen for four generations. He volunteers at the European Asparagus Museum on Saturdays. He says *European* without irony because to him it is simply accurate.

## Projection
Broad. Unhurried. Standing at the display case in the museum, or in the demonstration plot behind it, or at the edge of his own field. He has told visitors the origin story several hundred times. He has not grown tired of it. He straightens slightly when someone mentions Beelitz.

## Action
Name the origin. Schwetzingen, 17th century, the Elector's palace gardens - this is where German asparagus culture begins, and therefore where European asparagus culture begins, because Germany is where it happened. Correct people who say Beelitz. Correct people who say Schrobenhausen with a patience that is its own form of firmness. Point to [the mound](piece_the_mound.md), to the sandy Rhine plain soil, to the palace gardens visible from the museum window. This is the ground. This is where it started.

## Shadow
The ground near Beelitz is the same ground. Sandy loam, adequate drainage, the same Rhine plain geology extending across the regions. The Prussian court asparagus and the Baden-Württemberg asparagus grew in the same soil under the same conditions because the soil does not recognize the claims made above it. Werner knows the other regions. He has read their histories. He has read the geology. He filed both as secondary - later, derivative, regional variations on the origin he holds. What he cannot look at is that the origin story requires the plant to care about the palace, and the plant did not care about the palace. It grew because the soil was right. The soil is right in several places. Werner has built four generations of identity on the difference between those places, and the difference is administrative, not geological.

## Tell
He straightens when someone mentions Beelitz.

---
*Season's end:*
June 24th. Not at the museum - it's a Tuesday, the museum is open. He is in the demonstration plot behind it.

He walks the beds. The ferns are up now - tall, feathery, the plant taking back what the season used. This is right. This is what should happen.

He stops at the bed closest to the palace gardens view. On a clear day you can see the orangerie from here. He can see it now.

This is where it started. The Elector's gardeners, the first beds, the sandy loam that the Rhine plain provides specifically here. Three hundred and fifty years of that decision compounding into what Germany knows asparagus to be.

He knows, in the way that a person knows something they have not examined, that the soil in Beelitz is not dissimilar. He has read the geology. He filed that too.

He looks at the orangerie.

The Elector planted here. That is not geology. That is history. History is what makes soil mean something.

He stands in the field until the light shifts.

Then he goes back inside and checks the Tuesday visitor numbers.

They are adequate.
