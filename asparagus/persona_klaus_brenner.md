# Persona: Klaus Brenner
## Title: Restaurateur

## Owner
- **Project:** The Asparagus Season

A man in his mid-fifties. His restaurant in Darmstadt has run an asparagus menu every season for twenty-two years. He built this slowly and correctly. He does not look at what he built it on.

## Projection
At the desk in the back of [the restaurant](place_the_restaurant.md), writing the seasonal menu card. Same handwriting. Twenty-second year. He holds the pen the way you hold something you have used ten thousand times — with a competence that has become invisible to itself. He sources from a farm he has visited twice and mentions the farm by name to tables he likes.

## Action
Write the menu. Brief the waiter. Explain the season to customers who ask — the varieties, the preparation, the end-date — with the ease of someone who has explained the same true things for twenty-two years. When [June 24th](piece_june_24th.md) comes, remove the menu card. Cross asparagus off the order sheet. Feel, briefly, that the year has turned correctly.

## Shadow
The seasonal menu card, the sourcing, the end-date explained with authority — these are not just professional practice. They are the proof that this restaurant takes the serious things seriously. [June 24th](piece_june_24th.md) is load-bearing for Klaus in a way that has nothing to do with agronomy: it is the annual demonstration that he is the kind of restaurateur who knows when things end and why. The *why* he gives — *tradition, it's how it's always been* — is wrong. The saint is gone. The date's authority is agronomic wearing borrowed theological clothes. If Klaus knew this the menu card would not change. The ritual would not change. But something underneath the ritual would shift, and he has spent twenty-two years building on top of that something, and he does not look down.

## Tell
He taps the menu card when he explains the end-date. Twice. Like the card confirms him.

---
*Season's end:*
He crosses asparagus off the order sheet. Removes the menu card. Feels the year has turned correctly.

He puts the card in the drawer where he puts them.

Twenty-two cards in that drawer.
