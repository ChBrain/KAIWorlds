# References

## Aristotle (384-322 BCE)

Philosopher. Student of Plato. Founder of the Lyceum in Athens.
The Poetics is his systematic account of Greek tragedy - its structure, its elements, and the conditions under which it produces catharsis.
Written circa 335 BCE. The surviving text is the first part; a second part on comedy is lost.

**The work this world is built on:**

*Poetics* - Aristotle
Circa 335 BCE. Translation consulted: Penguin Classics edition, translated by Malcolm Heath (1996).
The primary source for all structural elements in this world: mythos, desis, catastrophe, lysis, hamartia, peripeteia, anagnorisis, catharsis.

## Secondary sources

*Aristotle's Poetics* - Leon Golden and O.B. Hardison Jr.
Published 1968, Prentice-Hall, New Jersey.
Commentary and analysis. Particularly useful on the catharsis debate.

*The Art of Fiction* - David Lodge
Published 1992, Penguin, London.
Modern application of Aristotelian principles to prose fiction. Chapters on peripeteia and anagnorisis.
