# Piece: Die Doppeleiche
## Two trunks, one crown, forever undivided.

## Owner
- **Place:** Kiel

## Place
Kiel. The Schützenpark.
One Doppeleiche under cultural heritage protection.
Also: approximately 150 remaining across Schleswig-Holstein,
planted in village squares, outside schools, beside inns.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the Schleswig-Holstein position loses its image of itself.
The position is two cultures that are neither fully the same nor fully separate.
The piece is the tree that cannot be divided
without both trunks dying.

## Apparent
An oak with two trunks growing from one root.
Sometimes joined at the base, sometimes diverging from the first growth.
A shared crown above both.
*Up ewig ungedeelt* - forever undivided -
carved on the stones beside them.
About 150 still standing from the mass plantings of 1898.
Most recognised by local name only.
A few under heritage protection.

## Yearbook
1460: the Privilege of Ripen.
Schleswig and Holstein to remain together under one ruler.
*Up ewig ungedeelt.* The lawyers later noted
the phrase applied only to the nobility's estates.
The phrase escaped the lawyers.
1844: the Schleswig-Holstein-Lied sung for the first time.
Seventh strophe: *Teures Land, du Doppeleiche,*
*unter einer Krone Dach.*
Dear land, thou double oak, beneath one crown's roof.
The symbol named in song before it was planted in ground.
1848: the Schleswig-Holstein Uprising.
The Doppeleiche becomes the symbol of the movement.
1864: Prussia defeats Denmark. The duchies annexed.
The symbol survives the victory it stood for.
1898: the 50th anniversary of the Uprising.
Hundreds of Doppeleichen planted across the region.
1920: the border drawn. Northern Schleswig returned to Denmark.
The Doppeleichen in the north quietly removed.
The ones in the south remain.
Today: the border settled. The symbol stays.
Not political now - regional.
Two trunks. One crown.
The position the piece holds
is the position Kai carries personally.
