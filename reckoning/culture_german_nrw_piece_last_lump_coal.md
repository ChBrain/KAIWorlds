# Piece: The Last Lump of Coal
## Handed to the Federal President on Christmas Eve 2018. The end of something that built Germany.

## Owner
- **Place:** Essen

## Place
Essen. The Zeche Zollverein.
UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2001.
A coal mine that closed in 1986.
Now a museum, a design centre, an ice rink in winter.
The headframe still stands.
The machinery is still inside it.
The last lump of coal from the Ruhr:
Prosper-Haniel colliery, Bottrop.
Christmas Eve 2018.
Frank-Walter Steinmeier, Federal President,
received it by hand.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the NRW position loses its anchor.
The Ruhr was not just an industry.
It was the operating system
on which the Federal Republic was built.
German reconstruction after 1945:
Ruhr coal and steel.
The economic miracle: Ruhr labour.
The social market economy:
Ruhr unions in negotiation with Ruhr management,
a model for the republic.
When the coal ended
it was not just an industry closing.
It was the retirement of the engine
that had run everything.
The last lump is load-bearing
because it names the end
of something that was never supposed to end.

## Apparent
The Zeche Zollverein. Essen.
The most beautiful coal mine in the world -
the architects Schupp and Kremmer designed it
in Bauhaus style in 1932.
The Förderturm: the headframe, twelve stories.
Still standing. Open to visitors.
The Ruhr ground has sunk by up to 25 metres
from a century of mining.
The river Ruhr no longer flows naturally -
it must be pumped into the Rhine
24 hours a day.
If the pumps stop, the valley floods.
The pumps run on electricity.
They will run as long as there are towns here.
The last lump of coal:
held in Steinmeier's hands on Christmas Eve.
Filmed. Broadcast. Remembered.
The miners who carried it:
some of their fathers mined this coal.
Some of their fathers' fathers.
The mine's name: Prosper-Haniel.
*Prosper.* The name is in the record.

## Yearbook
1800s: coal mining begins at scale in the Ruhr.
The landscape transforms within decades.
The population grows fifty-fold.
1871: the German Empire. Ruhr steel and coal
at its industrial centre.
1923: French occupation of the Ruhr.
Germany defaults on reparations.
France sends troops.
The Ruhr workers strike.
The German government prints money to pay them.
Hyperinflation follows.
1945: the Ruhr bombed to rubble.
It rebuilds within years.
The urgency is not manufactured.
Without the Ruhr, there is no West Germany.
1957: the coal crisis begins.
Oil replaces coal.
The slow ending starts.
1968: the first steel crisis.
1973: the oil shock accelerates the transition.
The transition takes fifty years.
2018: the last mine closes.
Christmas Eve. Bottrop.
The Federal President receives the last lump.
The pumps continue.
They will continue.
