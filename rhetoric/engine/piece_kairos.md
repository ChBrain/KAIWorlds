# Piece: Kairos

## Owner
- **Project:** Rhetoric
- **Process:** [Techne](process_techne.md)

## Place
The occasion itself. Not carried by the [Speaker](position_speaker.md) - found by them.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and [Techne](process_techne.md) produces a speech without an occasion. The right argument at the wrong moment does not persuade. The same claim that fails in one context lands in another. Ethos, Pathos, and Logos are the means. Kairos is the condition under which the means work. It cannot be manufactured. It can only be seen and used.

## Apparent
The right moment. The specific convergence of speaker, audience, claim, and occasion that makes persuasion possible now and not at another time. Present or absent before the speech begins. The Speaker who reads it correctly deploys the available means at the moment they will land. The Speaker who misreads it deploys the same means into ground that will not receive them.

## Yearbook
Aristotle does not name Kairos as a technical means of persuasion alongside Ethos, Pathos, and Logos - but the concept runs through the Rhetoric as the condition of all three. The right emotion at the wrong moment produces no Pathos. The right argument before the Audience is ready produces no conviction.
Later rhetoricians made it explicit. Purdue's treatment places it alongside the three means as the setting that makes them available.
The piece travels into every publishing decision: when to post, when to pitch, when to speak. The Substack writer who publishes the right essay the day the conversation shifts - Kairos found them, or they found it.
