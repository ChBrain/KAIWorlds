# Piece: Logos

## Owner
- **Project:** Rhetoric
- **Process:** [Pistis](process_pistis.md)

## Place
[The Agora](place_the_agora.md). The structure of the speech itself.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and [Pistis](process_pistis.md) has no body. Aristotle names the enthymeme - the rhetorical proof - as the body of persuasion. Ethos and Pathos support it. They do not replace it. A speech without Logos moves the Audience but gives them nothing to hold. Conviction built on emotion alone does not survive scrutiny.

## Apparent
The argument - its structure, its content, its logical connections. Not formal logic: the enthymeme works by connecting the claim to premises the Audience already accepts, leaving the conclusion for the Audience to reach just before the Speaker states it. Aristotle notes this produces pleasure: the Audience congratulates itself for following. That pleasure is part of the persuasion.

## Yearbook
Aristotle calls the enthymeme the body of persuasion and places it at the center of the art.
Logos is not synonymous with logic. It is the rhetorical version - it leaps gaps, works with probability rather than certainty, and is always addressed to a specific Audience with specific existing convictions.
The piece travels into every structured argument: the op-ed, the business case, the Substack essay that builds a thesis paragraph by paragraph. When the reader finishes a section thinking they had already seen where it was going - Logos is running.
