# Place: The Station
## Location: Boknis Eck. 54°31.77'N, 10°02.36'E. 28 metres depth. 30 April 1957.

## Owner
**Project:** Baltic Sea Timeline
**Persona:** Johannes Krey

## Shown
- The research vessel at anchor.
  The cable running over the stern.
  The water sampler descending — brass fittings,
  Niskin bottles, the smell of salt and diesel.
  The same smell that will be on this deck
  every month for the next sixty-seven years.
- The water column. 28 metres.
  Cold below twelve metres, warmer above.
  The thermocline not yet named as the problem it will become.
  The oxygen in the deep water: adequate.
  The nitrogen: elevated above the pre-loading baseline,
  though the pre-loading baseline
  is not in any record that Krey can read.
- The Eckernförde Bay shore.
  Two kilometres away.
  The town visible. The German Navy torpedo range
  on the same shore — both present,
  neither acknowledging the other.
- The instruments. Precise.
  They will measure everything they can measure.
  They cannot measure what happened before they arrived.
  They cannot measure the world in the sediment below them.
  They measure the water column, starting now.
- The sky. April. Cold and clear.
  The kind of morning that feels like beginning.
  It is beginning.

## Holds
- [The First Measurement](piece_the_first_measurement.md).
  The first row of data.
  Everything starts here.

## Offers
- The record. Starting now.
  Available to every scientist, policymaker,
  journalist, and tourist who will ever
  need to know what happened to this water.
  The record is the offer the Station makes
  to the future from this morning forward.
- The depth profile. 28 metres of water column,
  measurable at every depth.
  The thermocline. The oxygen gradient.
  The nutrients. The temperature.
  Available monthly. Available now.

## Withheld
- The pre-loading baseline.
  The oxygen in August 1800, before the tile
  network reached its full extent.
  The eelgrass at full depth, before the retreat.
  The nitrogen load before Haber-Bosch.
  All of this is in the sediment
  eight metres below the station's anchor.
  Krey does not know to look there.
  The instruments are measuring the water column.
  The sediment record requires a different question —
  one that will not be asked
  with the right instruments
  for another fifty years.
- What the record will show.
  Krey is starting a record.
  He does not know it will produce the paradox.
  He does not know about the paradox
  because the paradox requires sixty years of data
  to become visible.
  The paradox is already running —
  in the water column, in the tile network,
  in the warming atmosphere.
  The Station will record it.
  Not today. In sixty years.
