## The Boknis Eck Time Series

GEOMAR Helmholtz Centre for Ocean Research Kiel.
Monthly sampling initiated by Johannes Krey on 30 April 1957.
The longest continuous oceanographic time series in the Baltic Sea.
https://www.bokniseck.de

## Johannes Krey

German marine biologist and oceanographer (1914–1986).
Kiel University / Institut für Meereskunde.
Initiated the Boknis Eck time series on 30 April 1957.
Developed methods for measuring particulate organic matter
in marine systems. Died in Kiel, 1986.

## The Ertebølle Culture

Hunter-gatherer-fisher culture, southern Scandinavia and northern Germany.
~5400–3900 BCE. Littorina Sea phase — the Baltic at maximum salinity and extent.
Named after the shell midden site at Ertebølle, Limfjord, Denmark.
Shell middens (køkkenmøddinger) are the primary archaeological record —
accumulated deposits of oyster, cockle, and mussel shells, fish bones,
and material culture.
The transition to the Funnel Beaker culture (~3900 BCE) marks the
adoption of agriculture in the region.

---

## The Ancylus Lake

Freshwater phase of the Baltic basin, ~8500–7000 BCE.
Named after the freshwater limpet *Ancylus fluviatilis* found in sediments
from this period. Formed after the Baltic Ice Lake drained through the
Öresund region c. 10,000 BCE. Followed by the Yoldia Sea (brief marine
incursion), then the Ancylus Lake (freshwater highstand), then the
Littorina Sea transgression (~7000–6000 BCE) as Atlantic waters
entered through the Danish straits.

## Haithabu / Hedeby

Viking-age emporium (Handelsplatz) at the head of the Schlei fjord,
Schleswig-Holstein, Germany. Active ~700–1050 CE.
One of the largest trading centres in northern Europe in its time.
Located at the crossing point between the North Sea and Baltic trade routes.
Named Haithabu in Danish / Hedeby in German.
Abandoned after destruction c. 1050 CE. Hedeby Noor (lake) marks the site.
UNESCO World Heritage inscription 2018 (as part of the
Danevirke and Hedeby site).
https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1669
