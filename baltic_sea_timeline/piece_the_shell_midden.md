# Piece: The Shell Midden
## The archive that does not know it is an archive.

## Owner
**Project:** Baltic Sea Timeline
**Persona:** The Fisher

---

## Place
[The Lake](place_the_lake.md).
The slight rise above the waterline.
The eating place of the settlement.

## Load Bearing
Without it the freshwater world leaves no record.
The lake, the fish, the culture, the knowledge —
all of it ends when The Salt arrives.
The midden is the only thing that survives.
Not because it was built to survive —
because the salt water that destroys
the freshwater world also preserves
the organic matter in the midden.
The archive is created by the eating.
It is preserved by the catastrophe.
Remove it and the world of The Lake
has no evidence it existed.
With it, eight thousand years later,
the Boknis Eck scientists can read
the pre-disturbance baseline
the monitoring began too late to measure.
The midden is the Pre-1957 Record
of the Pre-Salt world.

## Apparent
A low mound. Oyster and mussel shells,
fish bones, the occasional antler or flint.
Stratified — the oldest layers at the bottom,
each season's eating above the last.
The Fisher does not see the stratification.
She sees the place where eating happens.

## Yearbook
Built over generations at the lakeshore.
Each meal a layer. Each layer a season.
Each season the same species, the same shore,
the same water.

When The Salt arrives the settlement moves
or adapts. The midden stays.
The salt water rises over it.
The anaerobic conditions below the sediment
preserve what the air would have rotted.

The Ertebølle middens of Denmark:
excavated from the nineteenth century onward.
The shells of *Ancylus fluviatilis* —
the freshwater snail that named the lake —
in the lower layers. *Cerastoderma edule* —
the cockle, a saltwater species — in the upper.
The transition layer is a few centimetres thick.
It is the moment The Salt arrived,
compressed into sediment.
The Fisher tasted it. She had no word for it.
The midden recorded it anyway.
