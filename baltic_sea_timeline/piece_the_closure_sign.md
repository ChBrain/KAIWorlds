# Piece: The Closure Sign
## The infrastructure of adaptation.

## Owner
**Project:** Baltic Sea Timeline
**Persona:** The Inheritor

---

## Place
[The Future — Branch B](place_the_future_branch_b.md).
At the beach entrance.
Before every August visit.

## Load Bearing
Without it the degradation is invisible
to the person at the shore.
The Closure Sign makes the water quality
into a system — checkable, plannable,
adaptable around. Remove it and the degradation
is unmanaged. Keep it and the degradation
is managed — which is not the same as reversed.
The Sign is the piece that makes
Branch B liveable. That is why it is darker
than catastrophe would be:
Branch B is liveable.
The Closure Sign is the infrastructure
that makes it liveable.
The adaptation works.
The shore is still a shore.
The water is still water.
The Sign is not a warning.
It is a tool.

## Apparent
A yellow board at the beach entrance.
Standard municipal format.
Water quality rating for today:
— Excellent (blue)
— Good (green)
— Sufficient (yellow) — today
— Poor (red) — do not swim

A QR code linking to the weekly forecast.
The Inheritor has it saved on her phone
as a widget. Updates automatically.
She checks it the way she checks the weather.
She has always checked it.
Her mother checked it before her.
Her grandmother remembers when
you did not need to check it.
The Inheritor does not share this memory.
She checks the sign.

## Yearbook
The first beach closure in this timeline:
not recorded. It happened before
the systematic monitoring began.
The first recorded closure at a western Baltic beach
for water quality: 1990s.
An algal bloom event. Temporary.
A warning. Not yet a system.

2010s: closures becoming more frequent.
The bloom season lengthening.
The monitoring improving.
The notification systems developed.
The Sign professionalised — no longer
an ad hoc warning, a permanent infrastructure.

2026: the branch point.
In Branch A: the closures become less frequent
through the 2030s as the recovery runs.
By 2045 the Sign is still there —
the system is maintained —
but the yellow rating appears
three weeks per summer instead of eight.
In Branch B: the closures stabilise
at eight weeks per summer.
The infrastructure handles it.
The infrastructure was built for this.

The Inheritor checks the sign.
The sign says: sufficient. Acceptable.
She goes in anyway.
The water closes over her.
She does not know that on another shore —
in a future that ran from the same branch point —
the sign says excellent today
and the eelgrass is at three metres
and the Restorer is watching the trend line
move in the right direction.

The Inheritor does not know about the Restorer.
The Restorer does not know about the Inheritor.
They are in different futures.
They are on the same shore.
The branch point that separated them
is invisible to both of them now —
celebrated in one future,
forgotten in the other.
