# References

## On Dante's Commedia

Dante Alighieri's *Commedia* (c. 1308-1321) - commonly known as the *Divine Comedy* -
is divided into three canticles: *Inferno*, *Purgatorio*, and *Paradiso*.
The work follows a traveller through the three realms of the afterlife,
guided first by the Roman poet Virgil, then by Beatrice, then by Bernard of Clairvaux.
It ends on the word *stelle* - stars - at the close of each canticle.

---

## On the two guides

Virgil (70-19 BCE) was the author of the *Aeneid*. Dante chose him as the
guide through Hell and Purgatory: the voice of reason, classical learning,
and human understanding taken as far as it can reach.
Virgil cannot enter Paradise. His limit is not a failure. It is the edge of what reason holds.

Dante-as-character is not Dante-as-author. The traveller who walks the road
carries the weight of the living man who entered the wood.
The author placed himself in the poem as the one who remembers.
This world uses that distinction: the traveller walks; the Guide of Memory carries it back.

---

## On the structure

The nine circles of Hell, the seven terraces of Purgatory, and the nine spheres of Heaven
provide the architecture. This world does not reproduce them in detail.
It extracts the mechanism: a road that moves, a traveller who is changed by it,
and two guides whose limits are built into what they are.

The *P's* in this world refer to the seven *P*s (for *peccato*, sin) that the angel
marks on the traveller's forehead at the gate of Purgatory.
Each one is erased as a terrace is passed.

---

## On the final word

*Stelle* - stars. The last word of each canticle.
Not a destination. A direction the road was always moving toward.
