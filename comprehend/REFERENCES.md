# References

## Johann Sebastian Bach (1685-1750)

Born in Eisenach. Orphaned at ten. Walked from Lüneburg to Hamburg as a teenager to hear Johann Adam Reinken play. Walked five hundred kilometers from Arnstadt to Lübeck in the winter of 1705 to hear Dieterich Buxtehude. The institution gave him four weeks. He stayed four months. He came back owing an explanation to a consistory court. He gave them one. It did not mention what the walk had done.

Appointed Thomaskantor in Leipzig, 1723. He was the council's third choice. He held the position for twenty-seven years and produced, in the gaps between funerals and choir rehearsals and filing invoices, the Mass in B Minor, the St. Matthew Passion, the Art of Fugue, the Well-Tempered Clavier, the Goldberg Variations, the Brandenburg Concertos. The council never knew what it had hired.

**Primary historical sources used:**

*Johann Nikolaus Forkel* - *Über Johann Sebastian Bachs Leben, Kunst und Kunstwerke* (1802). The first Bach biography. Written with assistance from Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach. The document that began the public recovery.

*Bach-Gesellschaft Ausgabe* - Complete works edition, 49 volumes, 1851-1899. Edited by various scholars under the auspices of the Bach-Gesellschaft, Leipzig. Founded 1850, centenary of Bach's death. The infrastructure that made modern performance possible.

*Wolfgang Schmieder* - *Thematisch-systematisches Verzeichnis der musikalischen Werke von Johann Sebastian Bach* (1950). The BWV catalogue. Every known work assigned a number. Findability achieved.

## Felix Mendelssohn (1809-1847)

Born in Hamburg. Grandson of Moses Mendelssohn. His grandmother Bella Salomon - Sara Levy's sister - gave him the manuscript of the St. Matthew Passion when he was fifteen. He read it in one sitting. Five years of preparation followed. On March 11, 1829, at the Singakademie in Berlin, he conducted the first public performance of the St. Matthew Passion since Bach's lifetime. He was twenty years old. Hegel was in the audience. The King of Prussia was in the audience. The work was repeated on Bach's birthday, March 21. Then again on Good Friday.

He cut approximately one hour of the work. It survived the cut. The question of what the cut cost arrives later, with the scholars.

## Sara Levy (1761-1854)

Born Sara Itzig in Berlin. Studied harpsichord with Wilhelm Friedemann Bach - J.S. Bach's eldest son. Collected manuscripts and scores by Bach and his sons across her lifetime. Hosted a salon in Berlin where musicians and intellectuals gathered to play and discuss music. Her sister Bella Salomon inherited part of her collection. Bella's grandson was Felix Mendelssohn.

The direct line of transmission from Bach's desk in Leipzig to the Singakademie in 1829 runs through Sara Levy's hands. She did not know this was the line she was holding.

## Selected secondary sources

**Christoph Wolff** - *Johann Sebastian Bach: The Learned Musician* (2000, W.W. Norton). The standard modern Bach biography in English. Essential for the Leipzig years and the institutional context.

**Peter Wollny** - research on Sara Levy and the Berlin Bach tradition. Various publications through the Bach-Archiv Leipzig.

**R. Larry Todd** - *Mendelssohn: A Life in Music* (2003, Oxford University Press). The standard Mendelssohn biography. Detailed on the 1829 revival and the institutional context.

**Celia Applegate** - *Bach in Berlin: Nation and Culture in Mendelssohn's Revival of the St. Matthew Passion* (2005, Cornell University Press). The most thorough account of the 1829 performance and its cultural significance.
