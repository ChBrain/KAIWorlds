# Process: Find the Score
## The work returned to the condition it was always in.

## Owner
Project: Comprehend
Position: The Conductor
Position: The Scholar
Piece: The Score — St. Matthew Passion
Piece: The Complete Works — Bach-Gesellschaft Edition
Place: Singakademie, Berlin
Place: Sara Levy's Salon, Berlin
Persona: Felix Mendelssohn

## Initiates
The score exists in Sara Levy's collection in Berlin.
A boy of fifteen sees it for the first time.
His grandmother Bella Salomon — Sara Levy's sister — gave it to him.
He knows immediately what it is.
Five years follow: Zelter's resistance overcome,
158 singers assembled,
the Singakademie secured,
the work cut to two hours
because the era cannot yet hear three.
March 11, 1829.

## Direction
Toward the first public room.
The work has survived privately for seventy-nine years.
Now it requires an audience.
The Conductor does not restore the work.
He returns it to the condition it was always in —
heard by people in a room together.
This is what it was written for.

## Lever
The gap between the work existing on paper
and the work existing in air.
Carrying force: the Conductor's certainty —
at fifteen he saw the score and knew.
Five years of rehearsal is not doubt.
It is preparation.
Resistance: the era's preference for new music,
the scale of the work,
the need to edit it for an audience
that has never heard anything like it.
Remove the Conductor: the score remains in the collection.
The cheese shop was survived.
This is the second survival.

## Echo
The St. Matthew Passion has been heard
in a public room for the first time in a century.
Hegel is in the audience.
The King of Prussia is in the audience.
The concert is repeated on March 21 — Bach's birthday.
Then again on Good Friday.
The work is no longer private.
What was held in a salon
is now held by a city.
The thread pulled taut
does not slacken again.
