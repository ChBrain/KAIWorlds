# Piece: The BACH Motif

## Owner
Project: Comprehend
Persona: Johann Sebastian Bach

## Place
Everywhere a composer has written
B — A — C — H
since 1722.

## Load Bearing
Remove it and the work loses
its most portable form.
The Score requires a building.
The Organ requires a church.
The BACH Motif requires four notes
and any instrument willing to play them.
It cannot be destroyed.
It cannot be held in an archive.
It travels without infrastructure.

## Apparent
Four notes: B-flat — A — C — B-natural.
In German notation: B — A — C — H.
A descending minor second,
a minor third up,
a major second up.
Playable on anything.
Recognizable once heard twice.

## Yearbook
1722: Bach uses his own name as a musical subject
in the Art of Fugue.
The signature is embedded in the work itself.
1750–1829: the motif travels privately.
Composers who know the work
carry it in their hands.
1845: Schumann writes Six Fugues on the Name BACH.
He is the first to publish the acknowledgment.
Late 19th century: Liszt. Brahms.
The motif appears in new work
as an act of respect, or argument,
or conversation with the dead.
20th century: Shostakovich writes
the DSCH motif — his own name in notes —
in direct answer to Bach's BACH.
The conversation crosses two centuries
and a political border.
Now: the motif appears in jazz, in film scores,
in pieces written by composers
who have never set foot in Leipzig
and never will.
The most traveled piece in the world.
It does not know it is traveling.
