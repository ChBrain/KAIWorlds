## Eunice Newton Foote (1819-1888)

Born in Goshen, Connecticut. Attended the Troy Female Seminary in New York.
She was a scientist and women's rights campaigner.
She signed the Seneca Falls Declaration of Sentiments in 1848.
In 1856 she published "Circumstances Affecting the Heat of the Sun's Rays" in the American Journal of Science and Arts.
The paper demonstrated that CO2 absorbs more heat than ordinary air and that a CO2-rich atmosphere would warm the planet.
She did not present the paper herself at the American Association for the Advancement of Science meeting. A male colleague read it on her behalf.
Her contribution was not credited in the subsequent history of climate science until researchers recovered it in 2011.
She died in Lenox, Massachusetts, aged 68.

## The Ice-Albedo Feedback

The phenomenon by which retreating sea ice exposes darker ocean and land surfaces that absorb more solar radiation than the reflective ice they replace.
First identified in the nineteenth century. Quantified through satellite observation from the 1970s onwards.
The Arctic is warming approximately four times faster than the global average, in part because of this feedback.
The feedback loop is self-reinforcing: warming melts ice, exposed surface absorbs heat, absorbed heat produces more warming.

## The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)

A system of ocean currents that carries warm surface water from the tropics northward and returns cold deep water southward.
It distributes heat across the North Atlantic and moderates the climate of Northern Europe and the eastern coast of North America.
Research published in Nature Climate Change (2021) indicated that the AMOC has been weakening since the mid-twentieth century and is approaching a critical threshold.
The circulation is driven by differences in the density of warm and cold water. Melting ice sheets dilute the cold dense water that drives the return current.
A significant weakening or collapse would alter precipitation patterns, sea levels, and temperatures across the Atlantic basin.

## The Amazon Carbon Sink

The Amazon rainforest has historically absorbed a significant portion of the CO2 released by human activity.
Research published in Nature (2021) found that the eastern Amazon is now emitting more carbon than it absorbs, driven by deforestation and drought.
The shift from sink to source is driven by a combination of land-use change and climate-induced stress.
The forest contains approximately 150-200 billion tonnes of carbon in its biomass and soil.
The tipping point for large-scale dieback - where the forest can no longer maintain its own rainfall cycle - is estimated at 20-25% deforestation.
Current deforestation stands near 20%.

## Permafrost Carbon

Permafrost - ground that remains frozen year-round - covers approximately 15% of the Northern Hemisphere land surface.
It contains an estimated 1.5 trillion tonnes of organic carbon, accumulated over thousands of years.
As permafrost thaws, microbial decomposition releases this carbon as CO2 and methane.
Methane is approximately 80 times more potent as a greenhouse gas than CO2 over a 20-year period.
The permafrost carbon feedback is not included in most standard climate model projections.

## The Paris Agreement (2015)

Adopted at COP21 in Paris on 12 December 2015.
196 parties to the UNFCCC agreed to hold global average temperature increases to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the increase to 1.5 degrees.
The Agreement relies on nationally determined contributions (NDCs) - voluntary pledges from each party.
The NDCs submitted as of 2025 collectively project warming of approximately 2.5-3 degrees Celsius.
The Agreement entered into force on 4 November 2016.

## Earth Day

First observed on 22 April 1970 in the United States, organised by Senator Gaylord Nelson following a major oil spill off Santa Barbara, California in 1969.
Approximately 20 million Americans participated in the first event.
It is now observed annually in more than 190 countries.
Earth Day 2026 falls on 23 April 2026.
