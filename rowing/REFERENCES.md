# References

## Rowing

Rowing - propulsion of a watercraft using oars as levers, the oarlock as fulcrum, and the rower's body as engine. Distinct from paddling in that the oar is fixed to the boat at a pivot point.

Sculling and sweep rowing - the two primary forms. In sculling, each rower holds two oars (one per hand). In sweep rowing, each rower holds one oar with both hands. The coastal shell in this world is a sculling vessel.

The stroke cycle - catch, drive, finish, recovery. The fundamental unit of rowing technique. Every coaching system in competitive rowing organises around these four phases. The sequence is invariant: altering the order produces a different movement, not a modified stroke.

The puddle - the disc of disturbed water left on the surface at the point of blade extraction. Used by coaches and rowers as a diagnostic tool: the size, position, and shape of the puddle indicate the quality of the drive and finish. The puddle is the only persistent mark of a stroke after it has ended.

Coastal and open water rowing - World Rowing recognised coastal rowing as a formal discipline and introduced beach sprint events to the World Rowing Championships in 1997. The discipline is distinct from flat water racing in equipment, course design, and conditions. Coastal shells are built to be rowed in open sea conditions including swell, tidal current, and variable wind.

The coxswain - the position in the boat that steers and directs the crew without rowing. Present in coxed fours and eights. The coxswain is the only crew member facing the direction of travel. In a racing eight, the coxswain lies in the stern and communicates via a small amplifier system. The role is tactical, navigational, and motivational simultaneously.
