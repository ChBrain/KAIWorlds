# Persona: Morris
## Seat 1. Bow. 1936 Olympic gold. Seattle, Washington.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Bow Seat](position_bow_seat.md)

## Projection
He sits at the front of the boat, the bow, the seat that crosses the finish line first. He is lighter than the engine room, technically precise, responsible for the balance the rest of the crew rows inside. He grew up rowing a small boat on Bainbridge Island in the summers. At twelve he rowed across Puget Sound and through the Ballard Locks to his family home in Fremont. He did not announce this. He simply arrived. The water was always the way he got from one place to another.

## Action
The race at Berlin is over. The accounts of it begin:
the stroke who rowed ill, the coxswain who pounded the hull, the come-from-behind win, the gold.
Morris's name appears at the end of every list of the crew.
Seat 1 is the last seat named.
He reads the accounts.
He does not correct them.
He does not add himself to them.
He was at the front of the boat.
He crossed the line first.
He knows this.
He was the last surviving member of the 1936 eight.
He died in 2009, at 94, in Maple Valley.

## Shadow
He believes the bow seat's work is the work — that keeping the boat balanced and moving cleanly through the water is the contribution, and that the contribution is sufficient without being named.
What he cannot see:
the bow seat's contribution is precisely its invisibility.
The boat only notices the bow when it is wrong.
When Morris is doing his job,
the shell moves cleanly, the crew rows inside the balance he holds,
and nothing is attributed to him.
He has internalised this so completely
that he does not think of it as a cost.
The last surviving member of the crew
was the one the accounts named last.
He outlived them all.
He did not write a memoir.

## Tell
When his teammates are praised —
nods once,
does not add to what was said.
