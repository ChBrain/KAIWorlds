# Piece: Blade
## The point of contact.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing
- **Process:** [Stroke](process_stroke.md)

## Place
Where the rower rows.

## Load Bearing
Remove the blade and the stroke cycle has no anchor. The catch, the drive, the finish — all of these depend on the blade being in the water and held there by the water's resistance. Without the blade, the rower pushes against nothing. The boat does not move. The process is not modified — it does not exist.

## Apparent
A shaft of carbon or wood, two to three metres long depending on discipline. At one end: the handle, wrapped in grip tape or rubber. At the other: the spoon — a flat, curved face roughly 50 by 30 centimetres. The spoon is the working surface. Everything else is the lever that positions it. In the water, the spoon is black and featureless. Out of the water, it carries the club colours. At the finish, when the hands press down and the spoon lifts clear, water sheets off the face in a single motion and the blade is feathered — rotated parallel to the surface — before it has fully left the water.

## Yearbook
The blade is as old as rowing. Egyptian oarsmen used a version of this geometry. Greek triremes ran on it — the spoon fixed to the thole pin rather than a swivel, but the principle unchanged: a surface held perpendicular to the water, pulled through an arc, releasing the boat forward. Viking longships. The Thames watermen who staged the first professional regattas in the early 17th century. Every boat that has moved by human effort on water has moved because something held the water still long enough for everything else to push against it.

The modern blade — carbon fibre, asymmetric spoon, swivel oarlock — arrived in the mid-20th century. The asymmetric spoon came later still, introduced in the 1990s. It enters the water more cleanly and exits more cleanly than the symmetric blade it replaced. The stroke did not change. The blade became better at the same job.
