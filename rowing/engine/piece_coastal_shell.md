# Piece: Coastal Shell
## Built for conditions that will not cooperate.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing
- **Process:** [Stroke](process_stroke.md)
- **Place:** [Open water](place_open_water.md)

## Place
[Open water](place_open_water.md).

## Load Bearing
Remove the coastal shell and open water rowing does not exist in its current form. A flat water racing shell — narrow, low, built for controlled conditions — is unstable in swell and cannot self-bail when water comes over the gunwale. The coastal shell is the piece that makes the open water a viable place to row. Without it, the stroke cycle stops at the shore.

## Apparent
Wider than a racing shell — typically 30 to 40 centimetres broader at the beam. Higher sides. The hull is heavier and more robust, built to absorb impact with waves rather than to minimise displacement. Self-bailing: drainage holes in the cockpit floor allow water that enters the boat to exit without swamping it. The riggers are set lower and angled differently to keep the blades close to the surface in chop. The finish on the hull is typically matte rather than polished — a racing shell is built to be fast in ideal conditions; the coastal shell is built to survive the conditions it will actually encounter. In the water it sits visibly higher and broader than a flat water boat. A rower stepping from a single scull into a coastal single notices the width immediately. The catch feels the same. The boat under the catch does not.

## Yearbook
Coastal and open water rowing predate the racing shell. Traditional working boats — fishing vessels, ferries, lifeboats — were rowed on open water by necessity long before the sport formalised its equipment. The evolution of the dedicated coastal rowing shell as a distinct competition class is recent: World Rowing recognised coastal rowing as a formal discipline in the late 20th century, with beach sprint events added to the World Rowing Championships in 1997. The discipline grows steadily — partly because the boats are cheaper and more accessible than flat water racing shells, partly because open water rowing reaches places and communities that flat water courses do not.
