# Piece: Slide
## The mechanism that gives the legs their range.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing
- **Process:** [Stroke](process_stroke.md)

## Place
Inside the shell. The shell lives in [the boathouse](place_boathouse.md).

## Load Bearing
Remove the slide and the stroke loses its primary power source. The legs account for the majority of the force in each drive. Without the slide, the legs cannot travel through their full range — they push once from a fixed position and stop. What remains is an arm and back stroke. The boat moves, but at a fraction of the speed. The sliding seat is not a comfort feature. It is the mechanism by which the body's largest muscles enter the stroke at all.

## Apparent
Two parallel runners set into the hull, roughly 60 to 80 centimetres long. A seat mounted on small wheels that travel along them. The seat is moulded, unpadded, hard — designed for function, not comfort. At the catch, the seat is at the stern-most position of its travel. At the finish, it is at the bow-most. The wheels are silent when clean. When grit or water gets into the runners the seat skips slightly at the catch — a sound the rower knows immediately and does not like. The foot stretcher sits at the bow end of the slide, fixed to the hull, holding the rower's feet in place while the seat moves beneath them.

## Yearbook
Fixed-seat rowing is the older form. The sliding seat was introduced to competitive rowing in the 1870s, first in the United States, then adopted rapidly across the sport. Before it: the rower was rooted at the hips, the stroke driven by back and arms with a fixed leg position. The slide changed the geometry of the human body in the boat entirely. The legs became the engine. The boat became faster. The technique built around the slide — catch, drive, finish, recovery as a four-part cycle with a moving seat at its centre — followed from the invention, not the other way around.

Every drill that isolates parts of the stroke — arms only, arms and body, full slide — is an exercise in removing the slide's range temporarily in order to understand what it gives. The rower who drills without slide and then returns to full slide has a sharper sense of what the legs contribute. The slide is most understood in the moment of being given back.
