# Piece: Puddle
## The stroke's only record.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing
- **Process:** [Finish](process_finish.md)

## Place
[Flat water](place_flat_water.md). On the surface at the point of extraction, briefly, before the water closes.

## Load Bearing
Remove the puddle and the stroke has no readable consequence. The coach on the bank reads the spacing between puddles to know whether the boat is running or checking. The coxswain reads them to know whether the crew is together. The rower in bow can glance back and read them to know whether the last stroke was long or short. Without the puddle, the stroke is opaque — it happened, but there is no way to know what it produced. The puddle is not decoration. It is the evidence.

## Apparent
A circular disturbance on the water surface, roughly the diameter of the spoon, left at the point where the blade exited the water. In the moment of extraction it is bright and defined — water still spinning from the blade's arc, surface broken and catching light differently from the flat around it. Within a few seconds it flattens and spreads. The boat has moved on before the puddle has finished forming. Looking back from the bow, the puddles appear in a line — closer together when the recovery was rushed, further apart when the boat ran well between strokes. On flat water in still conditions they are readable for ten to fifteen seconds. On open water, a wave erases them immediately.

## Yearbook
The puddle has no manufacturer and no date of introduction. It is a consequence of the blade's exit, not a designed feature. Coaches have read puddles as long as coaches have stood on banks. The language built up around it — *spacing*, *run*, *check* — is old and practical.

No instrument replaced it because no instrument gives the same information as cheaply or as immediately. GPS, accelerometers, stroke rate monitors — all of these measure what happens inside the boat. The puddle measures what the boat gives to the water. These are different questions. The instruments answer one. The puddle answers the other.

A well-spaced line of puddles on a still morning is the most direct proof available that a crew is moving together and the boat is running free.
