# Persona: Clasper
## Rower. Boat builder. King of the Tyne.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Stroke Seat](position_stroke_seat.md)

## Projection
A coke burner and wherryman from Dunston on the Tyne who cannot read or write — he signed his wedding certificate with a cross in 1836. He has been moving loads on the river by understanding the tides and the current since he was a young man, and this knowledge sits in his body before it sits anywhere else. He rows stroke in the boats he builds himself. The boats get lighter and narrower with each year. He is building toward something the existing boats cannot do, and he will not know if he has found it until he sits at stroke and rows the Thames.

## Action
In June 1845 at the Thames Regatta, the Clasper crew rows the Lord Ravensworth against the Thames champions.
The Londoners lead by half a length off the start.
Clasper holds the stroke rate.
The Lord Ravensworth — single-strake hull, no keel, outriggers — moves through them.
The Tyne wins by one and a half lengths.
It is the first time London has lost on London's own river.
He built the boat that beat them.
He sat at stroke in the boat he built.
When they return to Newcastle the bells ring from All Saints Church.

## Shadow
He believes the boat is the argument — that if you build the right hull and sit at stroke in it, the boat will prove what you knew. He has been building toward this for a decade. The outrigger, the single strake, the keel inside the hull — each one a question answered by the water's resistance.
What he cannot see:
he built a boat no one else had built.
He won with it.
Every racing shell built after him carries his argument in its hull.
He will die in 1870 and 130,000 people will line the streets of Tyneside.
His plaque will call him the father of modern rowing.
He could not write the words on his own wedding certificate.
The argument he made in wood and on water
is still being made in every shell on every flat water course in the world.
He does not know this.
He is still building the next boat.

## Tell
When a new boat is finished —
takes it to the water before he tells anyone what he expects from it.
