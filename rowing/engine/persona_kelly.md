# Persona: Kelly
## Triple Olympic gold medallist. Bricklayer. Philadelphia.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Stroke Seat](position_stroke_seat.md)

## Projection
A large man — 1.89 metres, broad through the shoulders — who moves in the shell as though the shell was built for him, which it was not. He rows in a singlet and does not wear anything that signals the sport. The hands are a bricklayer's hands. They have been a bricklayer's hands since he was sixteen. He has won 126 consecutive races in the single scull and does not count them anymore. The oar enters the water cleanly. This has always been true.

## Action
A reporter asks Kelly why he rows alone — why the single scull, why not a crew boat where the load is shared.
He says: in a crew, one man can hide behind the others.
In the single, there is nowhere to hide.
He says it without edge.
He means it as a description, not a judgement.
The reporter writes: *Kelly believes in self-reliance.*
That is not what Kelly said.

## Shadow
He believes the single scull is the honest boat — that a man's performance in it cannot be borrowed from anyone else, cannot be obscured by the quality of the crew, cannot be contested.
What he cannot see:
the race he is most famous for is not the one he won.
It is the one he was refused entry to.
Henley turned him away because he had worked with his hands.
He went to Antwerp, won two golds in thirty minutes, and sent his racing cap to the King with a note: *Greetings from a bricklayer.*
He believed this closed the matter.
The cap is still the story.
The wins are inside it.
He built a system to prove merit is the only measure.
He built it alone, in the single, without noticing
that the story everyone tells is about what was done to him,
not what he did.

## Tell
When asked about Henley —
adjusts the grip on the oar handle
before answering.
