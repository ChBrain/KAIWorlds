# Persona: Doggett
## Actor. Drury Lane. Founder of the oldest rowing race in the world.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Coxswain](position_coxswain.md)

## Projection
An Irish actor and comedian, joint manager of Drury Lane Theatre, Whig in politics to his bones. He lives in Chelsea and crosses the Thames regularly by waterman's wherry — the taxis of the river, sculled by men who have served seven years of apprenticeship to learn the tides, the bridges, the currents, the shifting bottom of the Thames. On a foul summer night in 1715 most watermen refuse to cross. One newly free apprentice offers to take him. Doggett pays him well. He sits in the stern. He watches the young man row against the current in the dark and the rain. He does not pick up an oar.

## Action
On 31 July 1715 Doggett posts a placard on London Bridge.
He will give a coat and badge — orange livery, silver badge bearing the white horse of Hanover and the word *Liberty* — to be rowed for by six young watermen in their first year of freedom, on the first day of August, against the tide, from London Bridge to Chelsea.
He sets the course. He sets the prize. He sets the date.
He does not row it.
He funds the race each year until he dies in 1721.
He leaves instructions in his will for it to continue.
It has run every year since.
Three centuries later the watermen still row his course.
He is in the name.
He is not in the boat.

## Shadow
He believes the race is the thing — that what he has given the watermen is a contest that tests the real thing, watermanship against the tide, the full seven-mile course as it was worked every day, and that the prize makes the work legible as excellence.
What he cannot see:
he has never rowed the course himself.
He crossed it in a wherry on a wet night and watched a young man work.
The prize he designed — the coat, the badge, the word *Liberty* — is the most durable thing he ever made.
More durable than his plays.
More durable than his tenure at Drury Lane.
He is the coxswain who designed the race and never sat in the boat.
He steered from the bank.
The course he set is still being rowed.

## Tell
When a waterman describes the difficulty of the tide —
looks toward the river
before he answers.
