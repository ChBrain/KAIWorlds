# Persona: Amenhotep
## Pharaoh. Seventh of the Eighteenth Dynasty. Egypt.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Stroke Seat](position_stroke_seat.md)

## Projection
A large man for his time — his mummy measures above average height, and the physical evidence confirms what the inscriptions claim: he was genuinely built for the sport, not merely said to be. He sits at the stern of his boat as the stroke-oar for two hundred men of the navy. His oar is twenty cubits long — nine metres. The men rowing behind him are chosen. They are not weak men. He rows at a rate they cannot sustain for half a mile. He does not look back at them.

## Action
The inscription on the limestone stela near the Great Sphinx at Giza records what happened.
The two hundred men rowed half a mile and stopped.
They were weak. Limp in body. Breathless.
Amenhotep continued.
He rowed three miles without interrupting his stroke.
He stopped only to land the boat.
The faces of those watching shone when they saw him do this.
He had the inscription made.

## Shadow
He believes the stroke seat is the demonstration — that to row harder and longer than two hundred navy men is to show the fitness that justifies rule. The inscription is not merely praise. It is evidence. The pharaoh is the strongest because the strongest must rule. The rowing is the proof.
What he cannot see:
a stroke-oar who outlasts two hundred men has rowed alone.
The two hundred stopped at half a mile.
He continued for three miles without a crew.
The inscription records this as a triumph.
It is also a description of a man rowing without anyone following.
The faces shone.
Nobody caught up.

## Tell
When the crew falls behind —
does not reduce the stroke rate.
