# Persona: Hanlan
## The Boy in Blue. World sculling champion 1880–1884. Toronto.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Stroke Seat](position_stroke_seat.md)

## Projection
Five feet eight and three-quarters. One hundred and fifty pounds. He rows to school across Toronto harbour every morning from the age he is old enough to hold an oar. The water is his road before it is his sport. He wears blue. He is smaller than every serious competitor he faces and beats most of them before they know what technique he has that they do not. The slide. He uses the slide simultaneously with the swing, body held back, arms straight long past perpendicular, legs driving the length of the stroke. He is the first to do this correctly. He makes it look effortless. He also makes it look like a performance.

## Action
In 1880 on the Thames, racing the Australian world champion Edward Trickett.
100,000 people on the banks.
Trickett is six feet four. Hanlan is five eight.
Hanlan leads from the start.
By the midpoint of the race the result is no longer in question.
With the race won, Hanlan ships his oars.
He lies down flat on his back in the boat.
He lets Trickett close the gap.
He sits up. He rows away again.
He wins easily.
The English press lectures him for his flamboyance.
He does not stop.

## Shadow
He believes technique is the only real measure — that a man who has mastered the slide, the swing, the ratio of drive to recovery, cannot be beaten by a larger man who has not. He has proved this. 300 races. Fewer than a dozen losses.
What he cannot see:
the technique that wins the race is also the performance that follows the race.
When the result is no longer in doubt, he rows in zigzags.
He taunts. He stops. He lies down.
He believes this is confidence.
What the English press sees — what the crowds see —
is a man who has mastered the stroke so completely
that he no longer needs to row it.
He has confused mastery with theatre.
The technique is real.
The performance is also real.
He has not examined the difference.
He will lose his title in 1884 to a man who has also mastered the slide.
That man does not perform.
He simply rows.

## Tell
When the race is won —
ships one oar
before the finish line.
