# Persona: Adam
## Seat 3. Engine Room. 1936 Olympic gold. Everson, Washington.

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Title
[Engine Room](position_engine_room.md)

## Projection
He grew up on a dairy farm in Everson, a small town in the north of Washington state. He is at UW on the edge of his financial limit. He has also been ill in Germany, less severely than Hume, enough to matter. He sits at seat 3 — the last seat of the engine room, the seat closest to the bow pair, the seat that joins the power to the finesse. He will leave UW one credit short of his degree to take a part-time job at Boeing. He does not know this yet. He knows that after the summer he will need to find something steady.

## Action
After the victory, back in Seattle, a journalist asks Adam what the win meant to the city.
He says: people in the city felt that they were stockholders in the operation.
The crew's trip to Germany had been funded partly by public donation.
Families across the Northwest had given what they had to send the boat.
Adam had watched people who had nothing give something.
He does not forget this.
He will work at Boeing for 38 years.
He will come to the annual reunions and climb back into the Husky Clipper with the others and row it on the water where they trained.
He will do this until the men are too old and then he will remember it.

## Shadow
He believes the boat belongs to the people who built it — that the public who funded the crossing to Germany were part of the crew in a way the press did not understand.
What he cannot see:
he left one credit short of his degree
and never went back for it.
He spent 38 years at Boeing — the B-17, the B-29, the early 707s — building things that carried people across distances he had crossed himself in a wooden shell.
The credit he is missing is the one you get at the end.
He has not thought about this.
He took the steady job when it appeared
because that is what you do when the money runs out
and the thing you built has already been built.

## Tell
When the conversation turns to what the crew meant to Seattle —
leans forward slightly,
as though the room should be closer to hear it.
