# Place: Open Water

## Owner
- **Project:** Rowing

## Shown
Sea, large lake, or coastal fjord. No lane markers. The course is defined by turning buoys that must be navigated, not a straight line from start to finish. Wind direction and strength are variable and cannot be known in full before launch. Current runs where it runs. The surface changes between strokes — a swell that was not there a minute ago is there now. The [coastal shell](piece_coastal_shell.md) sits wider in this water than a racing shell would. The horizon is the only fixed reference.

## Holds
- [Coastal shell](piece_coastal_shell.md) — built for this place. A flat water racing shell cannot safely enter open water conditions.
- [Blade](piece_blade.md) — enters and exits a surface that is never the same twice. The catch must adjust to what the water is doing, not what it was doing on the last stroke.

## Offers
Navigation. The rower or crew must read the water, choose the line, manage the turn at the buoy, and decide whether to take the wave or let it pass. Open water offers the stroke cycle a context that the stroke cycle alone cannot account for. Technique is necessary but not sufficient.

## Withheld
The flat water's certainty that effort produces a proportional result. On open water a headwind can negate a strong drive entirely. A favourable current can carry a tired crew faster than a fresh one fighting the water on the other side of the course. The relationship between work and outcome is real but no longer simple. The open water does not promise fairness. It promises conditions.
