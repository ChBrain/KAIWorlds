## What this world is

A shell on the water. A boathouse at the edge. A crew facing backwards.
The stroke cycle runs. The boat moves. The rower returns to the same starting position at a different point on the water.

Fourteen personas. Four pieces. Three places. Four positions. Seven processes.
This world does not resolve. It cycles.

The stroke is indivisible: catch forces drive, drive forces finish, finish forces recovery, recovery forces the next catch. Remove any link and what remains is not a modified stroke - it is no stroke at all. Every interruption costs the boat speed it cannot recover within that stroke.

The coxswain is the only one facing forward. Everyone else faces the direction the boat came from.

## The places

**The Boathouse** - a long, low building at the water's edge. The doors are wide enough for an eight. Everything inside is organised around the longest boat. The last moment of not knowing what the water will be. Only the water knows, after launch.

**Flat Water** - a controlled surface. A course, a lane, a measured distance. The conditions that racing shells are built for. The world where technique is the only variable.

**Open Water** - conditions that will not cooperate. Swell, chop, wind, tidal current. The coastal shell is the piece that makes this a viable place to row. Without it, the stroke cycle stops at the shore.

---

## The pieces

**The Blade** - the oar. The only part of the rowing system that enters the water. The catch begins when the blade enters. The finish ends when it exits. Everything between is leverage.

**The Coastal Shell** - wider, higher-sided, self-bailing. Built to survive the conditions it will actually encounter rather than to be fast in ideal ones. The piece that extends rowing beyond the flat water course.

**The Puddle** - the disc of disturbed water left on the surface at the point of blade extraction. The only visible record of a stroke after it has been completed. It is already behind the rower before they can look at it.

**The Slide** - the seat on rails. The mechanism that allows the legs to drive the stroke. The most interrupted part of the stroke cycle: rushing the slide on recovery is the most common technical error, and it checks the boat before the catch has been taken.

---

## The positions

**Stroke Seat** - the rower closest to the stern, directly in front of the coxswain. Sets the rating and the rhythm for the entire crew. Every other rower follows this blade. The stroke seat does not look at the water; they look at the blade behind them, which follows a blade they cannot see.

**Bow Seat** - the rower closest to the bow. The last to enter the water on the catch, the first to exit on the finish - or the reverse, depending on the boat. The position that is farthest from the stroke seat and must follow a rhythm it receives at a slight delay.

**Engine Room** - the middle seats of the crew. The positions that generate the majority of the boat's power. Less visible than the stroke seat, less exposed than the bow seat. The engine room rows in the middle of everything without leading or anchoring. It drives.

**Coxswain** - the only one facing forward. Holds the steering, the race plan, the calls, and the knowledge of where the boat is. Rows nothing. Holds everything. Discovers that seeing everything is not the same as being able to use what you see: at race pace, the crew cannot hear nuance. The call must strip to one word, timed to the catch.

---

## The processes

**Stroke** - the indivisible cycle. Catch, drive, finish, recovery. Runs continuously until the boat stops. The direction is circular: the rower returns to the starting position at a different point on the water.

**Catch** - the entry. The blade enters the water at the forward end of the stroke. The compressed body, the extended arms, the blade square and on the surface. The moment before the drive. Does not tolerate hesitation.

**Drive** - the power phase. Legs push, back swings, arms pull. In this sequence. Never out of sequence. The legs are the engine. The arms are the transmission. The back connects them. Reversed, the chain breaks.

**Finish** - the extraction. The blade leaves the water. The handle drawn to the body, the trunk past vertical, the blade lifted and feathered. The end of the power phase and the beginning of recovery.

**Recovery** - the return. Arms away, body over, slide forward. The body returns to the catch position. The slide is the most interrupted part: rushed, it checks the boat; controlled, it allows the boat to run under the rower.

**Training** - the repetition that makes the stroke cycle automatic. What is built in training is used once in a race and cannot be rebuilt between the start and the finish line.

**Race** - the process that runs once, in one direction, and ends. Unlike the stroke cycle, it does not return to its starting condition. The finish line is terminal. What was built in training has been used and cannot be used again on this day.
