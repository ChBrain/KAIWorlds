# Position: Hubris
## The one who has placed their own law above the boundary.

## Owner
- **Project:** Antigone
- **Process:** [Nomos](process_nomos.md)
- **Process:** [Dike](process_dike.md)

## Has
The absolute certainty that the law they hold is the law that counts.
Not arrogance — conviction.
The position does not feel like overstepping.
It feels like standing exactly where one must stand.
The boundary that has been crossed is not visible from inside the position.
It is only visible from outside it —
from [The Witness](position_the_witness.md),
from [The Seer](position_the_seer.md),
from the audience watching both sides hold the same position
without knowing the other is there.

## Orders
Hold. The law requires it.

## Loses
The knowledge that holding is the crossing.
The position was certain it was defending a boundary.
What the play reveals:
the boundary being defended is the boundary being violated.
Antigone crosses the limit of mortal authority over the gods' domain.
Kreon crosses the limit of mortal authority over the gods' domain.
The same position. The same crossing. The same certainty.
Neither can see the mirror.

## Drives
On the loss —
does not yield.
The position drives forward because yielding would mean
the law it holds was not absolute.
And if the law is not absolute, everything built on it collapses.
The position cannot afford to be wrong.
This is why it cannot stop.
This is why the collision is not an accident.

## Yearbook
Aristotle named hubris the act of shaming another for one's own pleasure.
That reading is too narrow for what this position carries.
The classical Greek sense: overstepping — crossing from the mortal into the divine domain.
Kreon's hubris is widely accepted: he forbids what the gods require.
Antigone's is disputed.
Some read her as the one who holds the line the gods themselves defend —
how can she overstep by doing what the gods demand?
Others: her absolute certainty, her refusal of all compromise,
her willingness to take Ismene down with her —
this is the crossing. Not the act. The manner.
The position does not resolve the dispute.
It is built on the symmetry.
The symmetry is stronger if Antigone's hubris remains arguable.
A position that is only Kreon's is a moral.
A position that is both is a tragedy.
