# References

## Sophocles (c. 496–406 BCE)

Born in Colonus, near Athens. Son of a wealthy weapons manufacturer.
Served as a general under Pericles. Held civic and religious offices throughout his life.
He is said to have won first prize in the Athenian dramatic competitions more than twenty times
and never to have finished lower than second.
He wrote approximately 120 plays. Seven survive complete.
He died in Athens, aged ninety, reportedly still writing.

## Antigone (442 BCE)

First performed at the Festival of Dionysus in Athens, c. 442 BCE.
The play is the earliest of the three Theban plays in composition,
though its events occur last in the mythological sequence —
after *Oedipus Rex* and *Oedipus at Colonus*.

The immediate source is the Theban legend cycle, predating Sophocles.
The story of Antigone's burial of Polynices was Sophocles' own emphasis —
earlier treatments focused elsewhere in the myth.

The play is classified as a tragedy, but it resists the single-protagonist structure
Aristotle later codified. Both Antigone and Kreon sustain tragic falls.
Both hold positions of structural legitimacy.
Neither is simply wrong.
George Steiner noted that between 1790 and 1905 it was widely held
to be the nearest thing to a perfect work of art the human spirit had produced.

The central question: when divine law and human law collide directly,
which claim holds? Sophocles does not answer it.
He structures the play so that both readings are simultaneously present
and simultaneously costly.

## Primary source

Sophocles, *Antigone* (c. 442 BCE).
Recommended translations: Richard Jebb (1891, literal, scholarly);
Robert Fagles (1982, performable, reliable);
Anne Carson, *Antigonick* (2012, radical, structurally illuminating).

## Secondary sources

**George Steiner** — *Antigones* (1984). The definitive study of the play's reception
across two and a half millennia. Essential for understanding why the play
has been returned to in every era of political crisis.

**Bernard Knox** — *The Heroic Temper* (1964). On the Sophoclean hero's
characteristic immovability — the structural feature that makes both Antigone
and Kreon incapable of the yield that would save them.

**Martha Nussbaum** — *The Fragility of Goodness* (1986).
The chapter on Antigone as a study in the collision of legitimate moral claims.
The philosophical framing that best supports the architecture of this world.

## On the Greek terms

**Nomos** — constructed law, social order, the rules the polis makes.
Opposed in Greek thought to *phusis* (natural law, the order underlying the universe).
Kreon's law is nomos. It is real. It is legitimate. It is not the only law.

**Dike** — divine justice, the right order of things, the claim of the gods.
Antigone appeals to Dike as the supporter of the unwritten laws
that require burial of the dead. These laws were not written because they did not need to be.

**Hubris** — the act of overstepping the boundary between mortal and divine jurisdiction.
Not pride as character trait. The specific transgression.
Both Antigone and Kreon commit it. Neither knows it at the time.
The play is built on that symmetry.

## Scope note

This world is built from the full arc of the play —
from the scene before dawn at the gates
to Kreon standing alone at the end.
It does not adapt or modernise the action.
It holds the pressure of the collision and makes it available as a running world.
The source text is in the public domain. The architecture is original.
