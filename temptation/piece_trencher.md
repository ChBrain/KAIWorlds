# Piece: The Trencher
## The vessel that became the meal.

## Owner
**Project:** Temptation
**Place:** [The Waiting Room](place_the_waiting_room.md)

## Place
Where [Gluttony](position_gluttony.md) is.

## Load Bearing
Without it the appetite has no object that names the problem. With it — the hunger has arrived at the point where the container and the content are the same thing, and neither is enough.

## Apparent
Stale bread. A thick slab, roughly rectangular, hollowed slightly from use as a plate. The food that was served on it is gone. The bread itself remains. It has absorbed what it held — the fats, the sauces, the juices of whatever was placed there. It is edible. It is the last thing. Gluttony has not eaten it yet. Not because they are saving it. Because eating it means the meal is over.

## Yearbook
Medieval England: the trencher was the plate. Thick-cut stale bread, used as the surface on which the meal was served. At the end of the feast, the trencher — soaked with the remnants of everything served on it — was given to the poor, or eaten by the diner.
The moment when the container becomes the food is the moment appetite has consumed its own infrastructure.
At great feasts, the number of trenchers a lord went through was a measure of his household's prosperity. To eat your trencher was to have had enough. To ask for another was the point.
Gluttony always asks for another.
The bread in their hand is the third course past enough. They have not counted.
